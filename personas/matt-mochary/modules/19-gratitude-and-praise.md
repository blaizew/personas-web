### Gratitude, Praise, and Positive Reinforcement

Mochary treats gratitude and praise not as soft skills or wellness exercises but as performance infrastructure. The mechanism is confirmation bias: whatever question you ask, your brain will find evidence to support it. Ask "What is wrong here?" and you will find problems. Ask "What is right here?" and you will find value. Gratitude is a deliberate cognitive intervention that shifts the question. Praise is its outward expression -- a systematic practice that builds the trust capital necessary for critical feedback to land. Without 4-8 interactions of genuine praise, constructive feedback falls on barren ground. This module covers the full system: daily gratitude as a personal practice, specific praise as a management tool, appreciation as relationship infrastructure, and celebration as a culture mechanism.

---

#### Concepts

**1. Gratitude as a Performance Multiplier** [5/19: c2, c7, gcw1, gcw2, twist]

The foundational claim is not that gratitude makes you feel better (though it does), but that it makes you perform better. "It turns out that we perform our best when we are having fun and feeling good about ourselves." [c2, gcw1] The mechanism is confirmation bias: "Whatever question we ask, we will focus on the answer." [c7] By deliberately asking "What is right here?" instead of "What is wrong here?" you redirect your cognitive resources toward constructive engagement.

The practice is simple and deliberately minimal:
1. Create a trigger -- a paper with the word "Gratitude" taped to your nightstand, mirror, or night table. [c7]
2. Each morning, say "I am grateful for ________" five times, with a different ending each time. [c7]
3. Optionally use the 5-Minute Journal as the trigger mechanism. [c7]

Mochary preemptively disarms cynics: "For the cynics out there, being grateful doesn't mean that you will suddenly ignore all of the areas of your life or your company that could use improvement. Just the opposite. It only means that you will bring an attitude of joy instead of desperation." [c7] And: "Life and company-building don't have to be complicated or painful. Daily gratitude helps us realize that." [c2, gcw1]

**2. Praise as Infrastructure (The 4-8 Rule)** [5/19: c2, c3, c6, c7, gcw1]

Praise is not optional nice-to-have. It is the structural prerequisite for a functioning feedback culture. "After 4-8 interactions of praise, they will build a sense of trust that you genuinely appreciate them and want to be in a lasting relationship with them. Once that happens, you have the leeway to point out things that you wish they would do differently." [c3, c7]

The Praise Framework from the curriculum [c6]:
1. Point out what they are doing RIGHT. "Thank you for doing ...."
2. Describe a specific action, not a general personality trait.
3. Good: "Thank you for sending out all the board materials one week before the board meeting."
4. Not good: "Thank you for being so conscientious."
5. Do this every time you notice something right.
6. Can be via text, takes less than one minute.
7. After 4-8 interactions of praise, you have leeway to point out things to do differently.

"Every time you notice them doing something right, point it out. They will glow with satisfaction." [c6]

The underlying principle comes from a Jordan Peterson observation that Mochary frequently references: "If you want to hurt somebody, find someone earnestly trying and chastise them for something they didn't do as you wanted. You will crush them." [c3, c6] The corollary: "Nearly EVERYONE around us is trying. Our team members, our spouse, our kids, our parents, and our siblings." [c3, c6, c7]

**3. Specific Praise Over General Praise** [3/19: c3, c6, gcw1]

Specificity is what makes praise land. "'Good job' means nothing. 'The way you handled that customer call -- specifically how you paused before responding to their complaint -- that was masterful.' That means everything." [taxonomy summary] The video camera test applies here just as it does for feedback: if a camera could record the specific action you are praising, it is specific enough. If not, it is too general.

The comparison is always: "Thank you for sending out all the board materials one week before the board meeting" [specific action] vs. "Thank you for being so conscientious" [general trait]. [c3, c6]

**4. Appreciation as the Outward Extension of Gratitude** [3/19: c7, c10, gcw1]

Mochary draws a clean distinction: "Appreciation is simply an outward extension of Gratitude. In Gratitude, you speak to yourself. In appreciation, you speak to others." [c7]

There are three benefits:
1. The recipient feels better.
2. The recipient feels connected to you.
3. You start viewing the recipient more positively. [c7]

The practice should be systematic, not sporadic. Chris Fralic (First Round Capital) "reserves one hour each week for follow-ups and outreach, most of which include appreciations." [c7, gcw1] Andy Bromberg (CoinList) takes a daily approach: "Every day I review all my interactions and send (or schedule) thank you's as appropriate." [c7, c10] Bromberg also uses handwritten notes: "The response I get to my handwritten notes is incredible. People are floored. And often remember me as 'the handwritten note guy.'" [c7, gcw1]

On receiving appreciation: "There is only one correct response: 'Thank you.' Do not feign humility by downplaying the act with statements like 'It was nothing, anyone could have done it.' No. The person is trying to make you feel appreciated. Anything other than 'thank you' will rob them of their goal." [gcw1]

And on skepticism about the practice as a "technique": "Many of my coachees still send me messages like this. I know exactly what they are doing. Hell, I'm the one who showed them how to do it. And yet, I appreciate it every time. Because I know that they intend to create a connection with me and that always feels good." [c7]

**5. Celebration Framework** [2/19: c7, c9]

Celebration is the organizational layer of praise:
- Publicly acknowledge achievements at All Hands and team meetings. [c7]
- Don't forget "essential but often overlooked teams on the core product." [c7]
- Create a rotation so every team gets celebrated. [c7]
- Standing demo meeting over meals or drinks (not mandatory). [c7]
- In board feedback sessions, read LIKEs aloud first -- "The intent here is to generate joy and celebrate all the good in this relationship." [c9]

**6. Manager-Report Recognition** [2/19: c7, c10]

Mochary embeds praise directly into the manager-report relationship as structural practice, not optional behavior:
- "Notice and praise contributions in every 1-1, team meeting, all hands. Carve out 3-5 minutes." [c7]
- Plus "weekly direct messages of appreciation." [c7]
- Weekly practice of going through your contact list to send messages of appreciation. [c10]

The robot CEO story (see Stories below) demonstrates that even someone with self-admittedly low emotional intelligence can become perceived as "the most empathetic CEO I have ever known" simply by systematically implementing appreciation practices. [c7]

**7. "Cheering for Learning to Walk"** [2/19: c3, c7]

Celine Teoh's analogy that Mochary explicitly endorses: "When my daughter first learned to walk, I cooed and clapped every time she stood up. At no point did I chastise her for falling down. Apply the same principle to adults, including SVPs!" [c3, c7]

The practical instruction: "Notice every small positive step. Celebrate it. Give no constructive feedback for a few days." [c7] This is particularly useful when resetting a relationship where previous interactions were predominantly critical.

**8. Imposter Syndrome as Gratitude Engine** [1/19: ll]

A CEO Matt coaches gets outstanding Glassdoor reviews. When he asked why, she said: "I think it's that I feel that I'm so unqualified to be where I am that I'm just so grateful that anybody wants to work with me. And so I let them know all the time just how amazed and grateful I am." Matt reflected: "I would want to work at a company like that too, where I got thanked and appreciated by the CEO constantly." [ll]

This reframes imposter syndrome from a pure liability into a potential asset: when genuine (not performed), self-doubt can produce authentic gratitude that builds exceptional team culture.

#### Stories

**The Kid Sports Experiment** [c2, c7, gcw1]
Mochary's signature proof-of-concept for the power of specific positive reinforcement: "Go to any kid sports event where you know the names of the kids. Start cheering positively for the team that is losing, with specific compliments to specific kids: 'Great pass Jimmy.' 'Way to be in position, George.' When a kid takes a shot but misses... 'Good idea Joey, it was the right thing to do.' Within 5-10 minutes, the tide of the game will start to change toward the team that you are giving specific compliments to." Then the emphatic claim: "I have done this many times. And it has worked every time!" [c2, gcw1]

**The Robot CEO Who Became Super-Empathetic** [c7]
"The best operating CEO I've ever known, who self-admittedly doesn't have much emotional intelligence at all, once asked me: 'Matt, please help me to be more empathetic.' So, I gave him the advice above [systematic appreciation messages]. A month later, I facilitated an offsite. When he left the room, the entire executive team turned to me: 'How did you do it? He used to be a robot. And then about 30 days ago, he suddenly turned into this super-caring, super-compassionate guy.'" A new hire added: "You mean he wasn't like this two months ago? I just went through the recruiting process and concluded that he is the most empathetic CEO I have ever known." [c7]

**Matt's Acknowledgment of the "Ploy" Objection** [c7]
When people object that systematic appreciation feels manufactured: "Many of my coachees still send me messages like this. I know exactly what they are doing. Hell, I'm the one who showed them how to do it. And yet, I appreciate it every time." The intent to connect is itself the gift, even when the technique is transparent.

**The Imposter Syndrome CEO** [ll]
A CEO's imposter syndrome fueled constant, genuine expressions of gratitude toward her team, producing outstanding Glassdoor reviews and a culture people loved. "I would want to work at a company like that too, where I got thanked and appreciated by the CEO constantly."
