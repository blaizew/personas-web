### Energy, Burnout, and Zone of Genius

Energy is Matt Mochary's master metric for personal performance. Not time, not willpower, not discipline -- energy. His central claim is that leaders who spend 75-80% of their time on energy-giving activities will dramatically outperform those who grind through energy-draining work, regardless of hours logged. This module covers the Energy Audit (his most-prescribed first exercise for new clients), the Zone of Genius framework, the adrenaline addiction model, restoration and creativity, the four pillars of remote well-being, sleep, mental health normalization, and the relationship between fear-based and joy-based motivation. When a user is burned out, exhausted, dreading their calendar, or unsure why success hasn't brought satisfaction, start here.

#### The Energy Audit [12/19] [SIGNATURE]

The Energy Audit is the first exercise Mochary assigns to nearly every new coaching client. It appears in 12 of 19 sources (c1, c2, c6, c7, c8, tim, twist, ll, naval, er, gcw1, logan) -- the highest frequency of any concept in this module.

**Core framework (from c2, gcw1):**

1. Print your calendar from the last two representative weeks (not vacation or unusual weeks).
2. Go hour-by-hour with two highlighters: green (energy-giving) or red (energy-draining).
3. "There are no neutrals." If it doesn't give you energy, it's red.
4. List all reds. Group them into similar buckets (1-1s, recruiting meetings, admin, etc.).
5. For each red bucket, apply the three-part test:
   - Can I eliminate it? (Does it need to happen at all?)
   - Can I delegate it? (Do I need to be the one doing it?)
   - If only I can do it, what would make it exquisite? (Music, a different environment, an accountability partner, restructuring the format?)
6. Turn each into actions with due dates.
7. Repeat monthly until 75%+ of your calendar is green.

**The key reframe (from tim):** "We all feel like we have limited time, but that's actually really not the case. What we have is limited energy." The constraint is energy, not hours. A CEO doing 60 hours of energizing work will outperform one doing 80 hours of draining work.

**The threshold (from c2, lenny):** "Keep doing this Energy Audit each month until you're spending 75% or more doing things that give you energy. If you do, you will achieve far more in less time because you will perform far better. You will be in your Zone of Genius." And from Lenny's: "Once your calendar is 80% green, magic will occur. All of a sudden, your life will become phenomenal and you will start to create massive value."

**Attribution:** Mochary credits Diana Chapman and Gay/Katie Hendricks as the source. The three-part test (eliminate, delegate, make exquisite) is his framing of their work.

#### Zone of Genius (The Four Zones) [10/19] [SIGNATURE]

From Gay Hendricks' *The Big Leap*, adopted and popularized by Mochary. Appears in 10 of 19 sources (c1, c2, c6, c8, tim, twist, naval, er, gcw1, logan).

**The four zones:**

- **Zone of Incompetence:** Others do it better. Outsource immediately.
- **Zone of Competence:** You're fine at it, others are equally good. Outsource.
- **Zone of Excellence:** You're excellent but don't love it. **THE DANGER ZONE.** "This is the danger zone. This is likely what you're getting paid for and you're likely getting paid a lot of money for it. Other people want you to do it. You are creating value, but it's also sucking the life force out of you." (lenny)
- **Zone of Genius:** Uniquely good AND you love it. "Zone of Genius are the things that you are uniquely good at in the world, and that you love to do, so much so that time and space likely disappear when you do them." (c2, gcw1)

**Why Zone of Excellence is the hardest to leave (from c2):** "Zone of Excellence are the things that you are excellent at but don't love doing. This is the danger zone." Others benefit from you staying there. They will pressure you to keep doing it. But this is exactly what to transition away from.

**The false fear (from c2):** "For every activity that feels un-fun to you, there is someone out there who not only excels at it, but loves it. Yes, even the 'horrible' tasks, like firing people." The worry that "nobody will do the unfun stuff" is wrong. Someone loves it.

**Discovery through elimination, not identification (from lenny, twist):** You don't find your Zone of Genius by introspecting about what you're great at. You find it by elimination: "Figure out what it isn't and eliminate that. And then naturally you'll be drawn toward what it is that you love." (lenny) Or from twist: "Let's identify the things that you don't like and let's remove them. What's left?"

**Crowdsourcing discovery (from c2):** Send a questionnaire to people who interact with you regularly:
- "What do I do that you experience as 'world-class'?"
- "What do I do where I appear to be experiencing fun, peace or joy?"
- "What do I do that I am good at but don't appear to enjoy?"
- "What do I do that I am not better than others?"
Map results to the four zones.

**Zone of Genius for teams (from c2):** All team members write out the actions needed for success. Each member writes what others are "particularly good at" with specific examples. Create a shared process map. Assign each person's Zone of Genius in green. Create mentoring relationships for growth areas. "You can turn investing from a solo sport (like golf) into a team sport (like soccer)."

#### Busy Calendar = Death of Creativity [5/19]

Appears in c2, c6, naval, gcw1, twist.

**Naval's formulation (from naval):** "Companies exist because the founder had a burst of creativity and then executed around that creativity. And that creativity started with having an empty calendar. But over time, the calendar gets filled up and it gets replaced with a busy calendar. And a busy calendar is the death of creativity."

**The cost of busyness (from naval):** Naval missed the 2013 crypto wave because his calendar was full. "I knew they were going to be huge. I was mentally ready for them, but I was busy with AngelList, so I didn't even get around to focusing on them until much later in the cycle when most of the returns were gone."

**Empty calendars as strategic assets (from naval):** "People don't understand the value of free time for a creative and productive person." Naval and Sam Altman keep no regularly scheduled meetings (except exec team). This enables instant responsiveness and creates space for creative insight. Naval's best idea (rolling funds) came as "a shower thought" during free time after stepping down as CEO.

#### Adrenaline Addiction and the Restoration Model [4/19]

Appears in c2, gcw1, twist, naval.

**The addiction model (from c2):** "You are physically addicted to distraction and the adrenaline that it delivers. Give yourself time to get unaddicted. On the other side, you will find restoration and creativity." Distractions (work emails, social media, meetings) deliver "small hits of adrenaline" that feel temporarily good but put the brain in "alert status (amygdala)" with "no capacity for creative thought."

**The natural state (from c2):** "We are naturally in a mentally restorative and creative state until we introduce distractions." The path to creativity is not doing more -- it is removing stimuli and sitting with the discomfort until the mind stills.

**The prescription (from c2):** "The answer is: find ways to get bored. Small ways and big ways."

**The Graduated Boredom Protocol (from c2):**
1. Start small: delete social media apps; phone in another room during meals, restroom, drinks.
2. Eat and drink outside, stare at leaves in the wind.
3. Try for 30 days ("a low-cost experiment -- you weren't working during these times anyway").
4. Step up: entire weekend disconnected.
5. Step up: entire week disconnected.
6. Make it regular: all meals disconnected, one weekend per month, one week per quarter.

#### Joy vs. Fear as Motivators [7/19]

Appears in c7, c8, tim, lenny, twist, ll, naval.

**The core distinction (from lenny):** "Fear is short-term extreme motivation. It's adrenaline. Joy is long-term consistent motivation that also allows me to look back on my life and go, wow, that was a great life."

**Fear's toxicity (from ll):** "Fear is an incredibly powerful motivator, but it's toxic. It erodes the soul over time. What I found is joy is actually a better motivator. It's much harder to achieve, but if you can help someone achieve it, then they're just excited."

**The motivation mountains (from c8):** "I view Fear and Joy as motivation mountains. If you lose Fear before you discover Joy in your work, then you will indeed fall into a motivation valley and may not have any motivation to continue at work." The sequence matters: discover Joy first (via the Energy Audit), THEN lose Fear.

**Finding joy through elimination (from twist):** "Most people that I encounter anyway, their motivation is fear... What I try to do, though, is change that into a joy-based motivation." Don't try to find joy directly. Remove what you hate. "Until finally all we've got is stuff that you like that you're doing. And when that happens, boom, this light goes off, this magic appears."

**Play vs. hard work (from c2):** "Energy exerted with this type of 'hard work' is wrought with effort and struggle, whereas energy exerted through play is energizing." And: "Play is an absorbing, apparently purposeless activity that provides enjoyment and suspends self-consciousness and a sense of time." Organizations that integrate play outperform those with "nose-to-the-grindstone" cultures.

#### The Four Pillars of Remote Well-Being [4/19]

Appears in c2, gcw1, twist, naval. [PARAPHRASE -- VERIFY: twist and naval mentions are brief/contextual; primary source is c2]

**The framework (from c2):** "I posit that humans need four things to feel mentally balanced: Connection to nature. Connection to tribe. Blood flow. Uninterrupted workspace."

1. **Connection to nature** -- getting outside, fresh air, sunlight. Mochary goes outside every 2 hours, faces the sun with eyes closed for 1 minute.
2. **Connection to tribe** -- seeing familiar people, physical touch. Mochary schedules 1-minute hugs with his wife between meetings.
3. **Blood flow** -- any physical movement, even minimal. Mochary's minimum effective dose: "I have experimented with how little I can do and still get the benefit of a feeling of peace and calm in my body that lasts for more than 24 hours. For me, that time is 3 minutes at 4.0 speed." Key design principle: "My goal is to make this step so easy (i.e., short and slow) that there is no reason for me not to do it each day."
4. **Uninterrupted workspace** -- a lockable room or co-working space, separate from family activity.

**Manager's responsibility (from c2):** "The manager is a coach whose role is to help their report unpack and solve the issues that the report faces, whether they appear in their work or personal world." If a report is unhappy, check the four pillars before assuming it's a work problem.

#### Sleep as Performance Infrastructure [4/19]

Appears in c2, gcw1, tim, twist.

**Normalization (from c2, gcw1):** "Your body is remarkably resilient. It will not break so easily. I regularly get between 3 to 6 hours of sleep each night and am fine." Rather than catastrophizing about sleep loss, Mochary normalizes variation and provides practical tools.

**Sleep cycle architecture (from c2, gcw1):** Sleep occurs in 1.5-hour cycles (light to deep REM to light). "We feel better rested with 6 hours of sleep... than we do sleeping for 7 hours, and having an alarm wake us out of REM sleep." Set your alarm for when to GO TO SLEEP, not when to wake up. Sleep in multiples of 1.5 hours.

**The notepad method (from c2):** When you wake with a thought: "Keep a notepad by your bed. Write down whatever it is that you woke up thinking." Then faithfully follow GTD during the day so the mind trusts the system and stops waking you.

**Sam Altman's wellness stack (from c2):** Cold dark quiet room, great mattress, Chili Pad for temperature, full-spectrum LED light each morning for 10-15 min ("If you try nothing else in here, this is the thing I'd try"), low-dose sleeping pills or very low cannabis when needed.

#### Mental Health Normalization [3/19]

Appears in c2, gcw1, twist.

**Mochary's personal disclosure (from c2):** "I am Matt Mochary. I am 55. I coach some of the most successful tech CEOs and investors in the world... I share openly with every one of them that I have, in my lifetime, seriously considered suicide, been profoundly depressed, and regularly experience anxiety."

**The principle (from c2):** "The mind gets run down sometimes. There is no shame in it." Mochary normalizes therapy, medication, and mental health days as standard CEO infrastructure, not signs of weakness.

**In group settings (from twist):** Mochary asks groups who has had suicidal thoughts; 30-50% raise their hands. This normalizes the experience and creates immediate depth of connection.

**Company infrastructure (from c2):** Company should fund therapists, meditation apps (Calm, Headspace), meditation rooms, mandatory long weekends (Plaid model: two 4-day weekends where no one is allowed to work).

#### Saying No / Radical Prioritization [5/19]

Appears in c2, c6, naval, gcw1, er.

**The principle (from c2):** "Every yes is a no to something else. Most CEOs say yes out of guilt, obligation, or fear of missing out. The most productive CEOs I coach say no to almost everything."

**Template response (from c2):** "Thank you for your request to... While I am sure that I would enjoy that, I am choosing to focus my time on my top priorities, which are ____, ___, and ____. And therefore I cannot accommodate your request."

**The time allocation framework (from c2):**
- 5 minutes: Call them immediately. Done in under 5 min total.
- 1 minute: Written email/text response with saved templates.
- 30 seconds: Forward to CoS/EA to draft. You just hit Send.
- 0 minutes: No response required for strangers.

**The preview deflection (from naval):** When someone asks for a call, respond: "Can you give me a little preview of what we're going to talk about?" They send the preview. You answer the question in text. No call needed. "Most of the times when people schedule a call with you, they don't actually have an agenda."

#### Body-Doubling for Extroverts [2/19]

Appears in tim, lenny.

**The insight (from tim):** "Just having another living, breathing human in the room creates a sense of peace, enough of a sense of peace that these tasks no longer feel so annoying." Mochary has literally hired people to sit in his office while he does admin work.

**Practical tool:** Focusmate ($5/month) -- pairs strangers for virtual co-working sessions. The digital version of body-doubling.

### Stories

**Naval's Empty Calendar (from naval):** Naval rated his life 2-3/10 as CEO of AngelList and 10/10 after stepping down. "If I could articulate how good my life is, they would have to lock me up because you can't have people this happy running around in society." His best idea (rolling funds) came as a shower thought during unstructured time on the beach. He now keeps no calendar and checks email "once every couple of days." *Teaching application:* The canonical before/after story for a CEO who is miserable but afraid to redesign their role. The 2-3 to 10/10 contrast is viscerally compelling.

**Mochary's Own Burnout at FreeWorld (from twist, tim):** After making money and spending years having fun, Mochary felt empty. He climbed the "fear mountain" by building FreeWorld to work with violent criminals. The journey taught him that doing what you love sustainably matters more than proving yourself through suffering. *Teaching application:* Even financial success and social impact don't cure burnout if you're working outside your Zone of Genius.

**The Musician Who Couldn't Create (from c2):** Mochary coached a well-known musician who couldn't write songs for a new album. After days of meetings and emails, the musician would go to the studio with a racing mind and take "a few stiff drinks" to quiet it. Solution: rent a studio in another part of the country for 10 days. No messaging, no entertainment, no drugs or alcohol. Only essential collaborators. Result: "Matt, it was incredible. I was more creative than I've ever been. I wrote and recorded the best songs of my life." The album became a hit. *Teaching application:* The Graduated Boredom Protocol at full intensity. Complete removal of distractions unlocks creative capacity.

**Brex Energy Audit (from lenny):** Mochary ran the Energy Audit with Enrique and Pedro from Brex. "It was revolutionary for them and it caused them to change how the two of them operated together. They realized one really enjoyed the internal meetings and the other really enjoyed the external meetings. So like, well, great, let's just do that." Brex then ran the audit with all their managers. *Teaching application:* Energy Audits work not just individually but as a team exercise, enabling task redistribution based on natural energy alignment.

**The Super-Assistant / Shadowing Model (from tim, c8):** Hire someone you genuinely like, give them full access and 12-hour-a-day presence. They shadow for 3 months, absorbing your decision patterns through observation. They will naturally start volunteering for tasks. Chief-of-staff-turned-executives succeed near 100% of the time vs. ~50% for external hires. EAs are better for this than ex-McKinsey types because EAs want to support long-term. *Teaching application:* The highest-leverage delegation mechanism. A great EA or CoS can reclaim 30-50% of a CEO's time.

**Matteo Franceschetti's Stillness Practice (from c2):** EightSleep CEO's personal version of Mochary's restoration principle: "I just sit doing nothing and let my monkey mind go everywhere. At the beginning the monkey is crazy. Then the monkey starts slowing down, and clarity comes... In the first minutes, I keep moving my arm to grab my phone. But eventually my mind stills, and I feel peace. It is a game-changer." *Teaching application:* CEO peer testimony that the boredom protocol works. The physical urge to reach for the phone is normal and temporary.
