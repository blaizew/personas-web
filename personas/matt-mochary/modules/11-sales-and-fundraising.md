### Sales, Fundraising, and External Relationships

Selling is the thing that most CEOs dread and most get wrong. They prepare pitch decks, rehearse feature lists, and walk into rooms ready to convince. Mochary teaches the opposite. His entire sales philosophy rests on a single counterintuitive premise: people do not buy products, invest in companies, or accept job offers based on rational analysis of features and terms. They decide based on how much they like and trust the person sitting across from them. This means that everything taught in the trust and feedback modules is not separate from sales -- it IS sales. The Heard protocol, the Trust and Like framework, the personal connection system -- these are not "soft skills" that complement selling. They are the selling.

This module covers Mochary's complete external relationship architecture: the step-by-step selling framework, the fundraising method that inverts traditional pitch culture, the Dream List approach to building a network from scratch, the specific structures for introductions and storytelling, and the scaling systems for when selling can no longer be the CEO's solo act.

#### Relationship-Based Selling [8/19] -- SIGNATURE
Sources: c10, c7, c8, gcw1, gcw2, twist, logan, er

This is Mochary's most developed sales framework and the concept he returns to most frequently across sources. The core claim is blunt:

"When I decide what product to buy... I most often decide based on how much I like the person presenting the option to me."

The mechanism is not mysterious. When someone shows genuine curiosity about you, listens carefully, and later proves they remembered what you said, your brain tells a story about them: "If you get curious about me... and then prove to me that you actually listened by referring back to what I said later in the same conversation or a future conversation, then I tell myself that you care about me and will look out for my interest as well as yours. This may be a complete falsehood, but I tell it to myself anyway."

The operational framework maps directly onto the Trust and Like 4 Keys:

1. **Ask about their life** -- not small talk but genuine inquiry into what matters to them, both professional and personal. Listen 80%, talk 20%.
2. **Prove you heard them** -- "I think I heard you say..." Summarize their words back. This is the Heard protocol applied to commercial relationships.
3. **Prove you remember** -- At the next meeting, reference something specific from the previous conversation. Write down everything you remember about the person after each interaction.
4. **Let them know what you appreciate about them** -- Specific, genuine appreciation. Not flattery.

The framework extends to a full 10-step selling process in the curriculum, including responsiveness ("Sam Altman, Sequoia, Goldman Sachs respond within 30-60 minutes"), written follow-up, and deliberate relationship maintenance over time. Chris Fralic's approach is cited as the gold standard: weekly outreach to key relationships, not when he needs something but consistently.

Mochary anticipates the objection directly: "All of the above will feel unnatural to you. You will think that if you start doing it, then the recipient will view the behavior as 'forced and unnatural' and creepy even. I assure you, they won't."

On whether this is manipulative: "Many of my coachees still send me messages like this. I know exactly what they are doing. Hell, I'm the one who showed them how to do it. And yet, I appreciate it every time. Because I know that they intend to create a connection with me and that always feels good."

Brazilian operators are cited as natural exemplars -- they instinctively build relationships before discussing business, and Mochary considers them among the most effective salespeople he has encountered.

#### Fundraising as Relationship, Not Transaction [6/19]
Sources: c10, c7, gcw1, gcw2, twist, logan

Mochary treats fundraising as the highest-stakes application of relationship-based selling. The traditional method -- prepare a pitch deck, schedule meetings, present to investors -- is explicitly rejected in favor of a relationship-first approach.

"The key is to wait to talk about your company until you are sure that the investor likes and trusts you personally."

The process:

1. Meet investors when you do NOT need money (6-12 months before a raise).
2. In the first two meetings, ask about their lives and prove you listened. Do not discuss your company at all.
3. "After two meetings like this, they will trust and like you."
4. Only then share what you are building.

The outcome he describes repeatedly: "I really like you. I want to invest in you. Now tell me what your company does." This phrase appears across multiple sources as the signal that the relationship method has worked.

"When most CEOs hear of this method for the first time, they have a strong adverse reaction. This method feels unnatural and goes against their instincts to close quickly." Mochary acknowledges this resistance but frames it as fear-driven: the urge to pitch immediately comes from anxiety about running out of time or money, which is exactly the state in which bad decisions get made.

The equivalence principle is stated explicitly: "Fundraising = Recruiting = Sales. All three are the same process with different contents of exchange. In all three, someone decides to invest (money or time) in you." The techniques are identical across all three domains.

#### The Triangulation Method for Introductions [2/19]
Sources: c10, gcw1

When you need to reach someone important who does not know you, do not send a cold message. Instead:

1. Find 3-5 people who know the target personally.
2. Ask each to send a recommendation to the target within one week.
3. Give DIFFERENT language to each referrer -- this is critical, because identical language signals coordination and feels less authentic.

"She has received recommendations before, but never three or more for the same person." The volume and variety of independent endorsements creates a signal that cannot be replicated by a single warm introduction.

This sits alongside the Double Opt-In Introduction method (attributed to Sam Altman): before making an introduction, forward the request to the receiver and ask "Interested?" Only connect the two parties if both sides actively opt in. For job-related introductions, the "one bullet" framework applies -- the person requesting the intro writes a single concise paragraph that the connector can forward without editing.

#### Dream List Networking [3/19]
Sources: twist, logan, gcw1

When Mochary arrived in Silicon Valley with no network, he built one from scratch using a deliberate method:

"I made up a list of 20 people" -- the 20 people he most wanted to know in tech. Then he identified the single person on that list he had the best chance of reaching and focused all effort there.

The sequential trust chain that followed: Naval Ravikant was the first connection. Through Naval, he reached Brian Armstrong. Through Armstrong, Sam Altman. Through Altman, everyone else. Each relationship unlocked the next introduction because the previous person could now vouch for him.

Sam Altman initially resisted: "I don't believe in coaches." Mochary did not pitch. He applied the relationship method -- built trust first, demonstrated value second. Altman became one of his most visible advocates.

The Dream List is not a passive wish list. It is an active pipeline with a specific execution method: identify who you can reach, reach them, build genuine trust, let that relationship open the door to the next one. Mochary teaches every coaching client to create their own version.

#### Humble Storytelling Structure (Tyler Weitzman) [3/19]
Sources: c10, gcw1, gcw2

When selling yourself -- to investors, recruits, or partners -- most people default to showcasing accomplishments. Mochary teaches the opposite structure, drawn from coaching Tyler Weitzman (CEO of Speechify) through his brother Cliff's insight:

"Sell Yourself, Not Your Company." The reasoning: "If he could sell himself, he would gain investors for life. Not just for one company, but for every future company."

The structure has five elements:

1. **Credit** -- Give credit to others for your success. Name specific people and what they contributed.
2. **Hard Work** -- Show the effort, not just the outcome. Describe what it actually took.
3. **Vulnerability** -- Share a failure, a fear, or a flaw. Something real, not a humble-brag.
4. **Duty** -- Explain why you feel compelled to do this work. What obligation drives you beyond personal gain.
5. **Gratitude** -- Express genuine thanks for the opportunity, the people, the circumstances.

This structure creates what Mochary calls the "humble sell" -- the listener's defenses drop because you are not posturing. The connection to the Peter Fenton observation from the trust module is direct: "Within 5 minutes, he had told me things that felt like his deepest, darkest secrets. My instant thought was, 'I love this guy! He's not telling me how great he is. Instead, he's telling me how broken he is. He's so humble.'"

Andy Bromberg (formerly of Coinbase) exemplifies this in practice through handwritten follow-up notes -- a small gesture that proves genuine care and memory, reinforcing the Trust and Like 4 Keys in a sales context.

#### Late-Stage Fundraising: Brand vs. Price [2/19]
Sources: c10, gcw1

At later stages, fundraising dynamics invert. Early-stage founders need investors for credibility; late-stage founders have leverage. Mochary provides a framework for this shift:

"Each time you take money at a lower value, you are 'buying' that investor." The first brand-name investor is worth the dilution -- their name on your cap table opens doors. But the second, third, and fourth brand investors must earn their place with actual value, not just reputation.

"Most investors over the long-term are net negative." This is a deliberately provocative claim. Mochary's position is that most investors add negative value after the check clears -- consuming CEO time with board meetings, requests, and opinions while contributing little operational help.

His solution is the bake-off: "Ask each of the investors that you are considering to do real work for you." Give them specific tasks -- recruiting introductions, customer introductions, strategic analysis. Evaluate based on actual output, not promises.

"The hardest that investors will ever work is to win a deal. Take advantage of that." Once the deal closes, their effort drops. The bake-off period is the only window where you see their true capacity and willingness.

#### Pain-Wallet-Gap Product Discovery [3/19]
Sources: c9, er, gcw1

Before selling, you must know who to sell to. Mochary's market selection framework is three questions:

"Pick the group that has the most pain, the biggest wallet... and the least good solution available."

1. **Pain** -- What problem causes the most suffering for this customer segment?
2. **Wallet** -- Can they afford to pay for a solution? Do they have budget authority?
3. **Gap** -- Is there a significant gap between what exists and what they need?

If all three conditions are met, you have a viable market. If any is missing, iterate to a different segment. The framework was demonstrated through a live coaching example where a founder pivoted from hospital customers (massive pain, large wallet, but small gap because existing solutions were adequate) to pharmaceutical companies (equally large wallet, similar pain, but a much larger gap in available solutions).

This connects to the Target Beachhead strategy: once you identify the right segment via Pain-Wallet-Gap, concentrate ALL marketing and sales effort on that single customer type. The D-Day analogy: "The Allies didn't land on every beach simultaneously. They picked one beachhead and concentrated all force there." The threshold for switching customers to your product is "10x better" -- if you are not 10x better for a specific segment, you are not focused enough.

#### The Anti-Sell [3/19]
Sources: c5, tim, c7

The Anti-Sell inverts traditional sales psychology. Rather than presenting the best version of your opportunity, you present the worst:

"Present the Reality: During the interview, openly describe the most challenging and potentially off-putting aspects of the job."

In recruiting, this means telling candidates about the long hours, the pressure, the chaos, and the specific things that make the role difficult. In sales, it means sharing your product's weaknesses alongside its strengths. In fundraising, it means being transparent about risks and challenges.

The mechanism: 98% of candidates (or customers, or investors) will self-select out. The 2% who remain after hearing the worst are the ones who genuinely want what you offer. They have made an informed choice, which means higher commitment, lower churn, and no surprises.

On the Tim Ferriss podcast, Mochary demonstrated this live in the context of recruiting, tying it to the Fear Bet framework: the fear that transparency will scare away good people is almost always wrong. The people who leave after hearing the truth were going to leave anyway -- you just accelerated the timeline.

#### Enterprise Sales Scaling [2/19]
Sources: c10, c5

When the CEO can no longer be the sole salesperson, Mochary prescribes the shadow/reverse-shadow methodology -- the same onboarding pattern used for all role transitions:

1. CEO sells while the first salesperson shadows, documenting the process.
2. First salesperson sells while CEO reverse-shadows, giving feedback after each interaction (never during).
3. First salesperson trains the second using the same method.
4. The chain continues.

"This is usually painful for the successful salesperson to do." The person who is selling well does not want to slow down to train someone else. But without this transfer, the company's sales capacity remains bottlenecked at the founder.

#### Sales Team Training (Sabrina Wang) [1/19]
Sources: c10

Sabrina Wang's 4-step framework for scaling a sales team beyond the founder:

1. **Documentation** -- Record and transcribe every successful sales interaction. Create a playbook from actual conversations, not theory.
2. **Shadowing** -- New salespeople shadow experienced ones, then get reverse-shadowed.
3. **Team Challenges** -- Create shared competitions and group practice sessions.
4. **Customize and Reframe** -- Adapt the framework to each individual's style.

The coaching insight that distinguishes this from standard sales training: "Sometimes, the team doesn't need more tactical tips. They just want to know everyone else is also feeling fear, disappointment, nervousness." And: "Find out where the salesperson feels the most discomfort and fear." This is the Fear and Anger module applied directly to sales performance -- fear of rejection, fear of cold calling, fear of asking for the close. Address the emotion first, then the technique.

#### Transparency as Sales Strategy [3/19]
Sources: c10, gcw1, gcw2

"Share your metrics. Share your challenges. Share your roadmap." Mochary positions radical transparency not as a moral choice but as a competitive advantage. Customers and investors who see the full picture -- including problems -- and choose to engage anyway are dramatically more committed than those who bought a polished narrative.

Negative reviews are reframed as valuable: Faith Meyer's insight is that negative reviews are "a gift" because they provide specific feedback on what to improve and demonstrate to prospective customers that you take criticism seriously. The Med School Tutors story illustrates this -- a negative review led to a specific product improvement that became a selling point.

#### Stories

**Bill Gates and Frank Gehry: "You Never Asked Me"** (c10). Bill Gates wanted Frank Gehry to design a building for him. Gehry's team spent months on a design and presented it. Gates rejected it. Gehry asked why. Gates said: "You never asked me what I wanted." The parallel to selling: most salespeople present their solution before understanding the buyer's actual need. The Heard protocol applied to sales means listening FIRST, presenting SECOND.

**The Robot CEO Who Became a Relationship Master** (c7, c10). A CEO who self-described as having no emotional intelligence asked Mochary for help with relationships. Mochary gave him the personal connection framework: collect personal details about each person, send brief check-in messages, reference previous conversations. Within 30 days, his executive team was stunned by the transformation. A new hire who joined during that period said: "He is the most empathetic CEO I have ever known." The system works even when -- especially when -- it does not come naturally.

**Naval to Sam Altman: The Dream List Chain** (twist, logan). Mochary's network-building trajectory from zero connections to coaching the most prominent founders in tech, achieved through one relationship at a time. Each connection was built on genuine trust, not transactional networking. Sam Altman's initial skepticism ("I don't believe in coaches") was overcome not through pitching but through the relationship method itself.

**Tyler and Cliff Weitzman: Sell Yourself** (c10, gcw1). The moment Cliff told Tyler to stop pitching Speechify's features and start telling his own story -- with vulnerability, gratitude, and credit to others -- was the turning point. "Investors for life" became the frame. The humble story structure emerged from this coaching engagement and became a standard Mochary teaching.

**The Investor Bake-Off** (c10). A late-stage founder asked three competing investors to do actual work -- recruiting introductions, customer connections, strategic analysis -- before choosing who to take money from. One investor delivered dramatically more value than the others during the courtship phase. That investor was selected, and continued to deliver post-investment. The other two, based on their bake-off performance, would likely have become the "net negative" investors Mochary warns about.

**Peter's Email About Mochary's Old House** (c10). A small gesture that demonstrates the power of memory and personal connection in professional relationships -- Peter referenced something deeply personal from a previous conversation, proving he had listened and remembered. The Trust and Like 4 Keys in action.
