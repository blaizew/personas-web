### Giving and Receiving Feedback

Feedback is the structural mechanism through which trust is built and maintained. Without it, leaders operate blind, operations slow, best talent leaves, and problems compound in silence. Mochary does not treat feedback as a soft skill or a nice-to-have -- it is infrastructure, as load-bearing as the org chart. His system addresses the core asymmetry problem: the person giving feedback (especially upward) faces catastrophic downside risk with marginal upside, so rational people stay silent unless the environment is engineered to overcome that calculus. The engineering is specific: dedicated time in every meeting, written-first methodology, a structured receiving protocol (the 5 A's), escalating levels of depth (relative, absolute, deep), and deliberate praise infrastructure that creates the trust capital needed before critical feedback can land. Every element is designed to make candor safer than silence.

---

#### The 5 A's of Receiving Feedback [12/19]

This is Mochary's standard feedback protocol, taught to every coaching client and demonstrated live in nearly every podcast appearance. It is a receiving framework -- a protocol for the person being given feedback -- because Mochary's core insight is that the quality of feedback in an organization is determined entirely by how the leader receives it, not by how others deliver it.

1. **Ask** for it. Actively pull feedback -- do not passively wait. Reserve the last five to ten minutes of every meeting. Do it in writing. "It is not enough to say, 'Give me feedback anytime.' None of your reports will ever actually create the time and space to do this." [c3] Open-ended invitations are what Mochary calls a "fake door" -- a door that appears open but no rational person will walk through because the downside risk of candor is too high. Real doors are structured: specific agenda items, specific questions, specific moments in specific meetings.

2. **Acknowledge** it. Summarize what you heard: "I think I heard you say..." Ask: "Is that right?" If they confirm, go deeper: "Is there more?" Keep going until they say there is nothing more. At the advanced level, amplify what they said -- exaggerate it, use swear words, state what is "likely in their head," which is always more intense than what they said aloud. "The thoughts in our head are always 100 times bigger than the words we're willing to say." [er] By reflecting back an amplified version, you simultaneously demonstrate you heard them, test whether the real message is even bigger, and prove the environment is safe because you did not get angry at the extreme version. "Acknowledging is the single most powerful tool that I know of to create trust and connection." [c3]

3. **Appreciate** it. Say "Thank you." No excuses. No arguing back. No explaining why they are wrong. Just "Thank you."

4. **Accept** (or not). Declare whether you accept the feedback. You do not have to accept all feedback -- "If you had to accept all feedback, it would become tyranny of the feedback-giver. And we never want that." [c3, er, twist] But you must declare your position. If you reject, explain your reasoning fully until the person says "I get it." That is resolution. If you accept, proceed to step 5.

5. **Act** on it. Co-create an action item with a due date. Do it. Tell the feedback-giver you did it. Publish the change widely, so the organization sees that feedback leads to visible action. Only when people see this cycle complete -- feedback given, acknowledged, acted upon, and results published -- will they feel safe enough to give more.

The 5 A's appear across the curriculum [c3, c7, c8], both book parts [gcw1, gcw2], and six of seven podcast/interview sources [tim, lenny, twist, ll, er, logan]. It is the single most repeated operational framework in the Mochary Method after "Fear and Anger Give Bad Advice."

#### The Asymmetric Bet of Upward Feedback [9/19]

This is the game-theoretic explanation for why feedback cultures fail by default. Mochary names the structural problem explicitly:

"For them, it is an asymmetric bet in the negative direction. If you take the feedback well, you will positively change your behavior, resulting in a +1. If, however, you accept the feedback badly, then you may become vindictive and try to destroy the person, which for them would be a negative 100." [c3]

No rational person makes that wager. Telling your team "my door is always open" does nothing to change the math. The upside is minor behavioral improvement; the downside is career destruction. "That's an asymmetric bet in the wrong direction. No one's going to take that bet." [er]

The solution is not to ask harder. It is to restructure the bet entirely by:

1. Making feedback a required agenda item with dedicated time (not optional, not "if you have any")
2. Demonstrating safe reception publicly -- when the first person gives critical feedback and the leader thanks them, amplifies it, and acts on it without retaliation, every observer recalculates the odds
3. Declaring the cost of silence: "I let my reports know that I will think that they are simply withholding until they share negative feedback with me, and I won't fully trust them." [c3, ll, er, logan] This flips the dynamic -- withholding becomes the risky behavior, not sharing.

"I can't trust you until you give me negative feedback, because if you don't, then there's one of two possibilities. Either you think I'm God and I'm perfect, or you're withholding and I know I'm not God, so it means that you're withholding." [er]

The asymmetric bet model appears in c3, c7, c8, gcw1, twist, ll, er, logan, and lenny -- nine of nineteen sources. It is Mochary's structural explanation for why most feedback cultures are broken at the foundation.

#### Three Levels of Feedback [5/19]

Mochary teaches three escalating levels of feedback depth, each appropriate for different contexts and levels of organizational maturity:

**Level 1 -- Relative (Like / Wish That).** Simple, behavioral, specific. "I like that you did [specific action]." "I wish that you would [specific positive action]." [c3, c4] This is the entry point -- the lowest-barrier feedback format. It works in meetings, in one-on-ones, and as the default feedback mechanism for teams new to structured candor. The key constraint: specificity. Praise a specific action, not a trait. "Thank you for sending out all the board materials one week before the board meeting" -- not "Thank you for being so conscientious." [c3]

**Level 2 -- Absolute (1-5 Rating).** Each person receives a monthly numerical rating with two prompts: "What you did that I liked is..." and "What you need to do to get to the next level is..." [c3, c7, c8] The scale: 5 = "OMG, it couldn't be any better," 4 = exceeding expectations, 3 = meeting expectations, 2 = below expectations and now on a written 30-day Performance Improvement Plan, 1 = "it couldn't be any worse and I am now letting you go." [c3] Absolute feedback is Mochary's breakthrough contribution to management. Relative feedback ("you're doing great compared to...") hides mediocrity. Absolute feedback forces leaders to confront whether someone is truly meeting the bar. "Bad news is less anxiety-inducing than no news." [c3]

**Level 3 -- Deep (Emotion-Based).** The full Nonviolent Communication template adapted for organizational use: (1) Ask permission, (2) Affirm the relationship, (3) State your emotion, (4) State the facts as a video camera would record them, (5) State the story -- "The thought that I have is..." or "The story I am telling myself is...", (6) Advanced: "I helped create this situation by...", (7) Make a request (positive action, not negative), (8) Ask if they accept. [c3, c7] This level is reserved for high-stakes interpersonal feedback and requires significant trust already established through Levels 1 and 2.

The phased progression matters: start an organization at Level 1. Introduce Level 2 after several successful rounds of Level 1. Only attempt Level 3 when the relationship has enough trust capital to support emotional vulnerability.

#### Praise as Infrastructure [5/19]

Praise is not a feel-good add-on. It is prerequisite engineering -- the structural foundation that makes critical feedback possible. Without it, critical feedback lands on barren ground.

"After 4-8 interactions of praise, they will build a sense of trust that you genuinely appreciate them and want to be in a lasting relationship with them. Once that happens, you have the leeway to point out things that you wish they would do differently." [c7]

The rules are specific:

- **Be specific about actions, not traits.** "Thank you for doing [specific action]" -- not "Thank you for being great." [c3, c7]
- **Text is fine.** A one-line appreciation text takes less than a minute and can be daily or weekly. [c3]
- **Cheer for learning to walk.** Celine Teoh's metaphor: "When my daughter first learned to walk, I cooed and clapped every time she stood up. At no point did I chastise her for falling down. Apply the same principle to adults, including SVPs!" [c3]
- **If someone is earnestly trying and you chastise them, you crush them.** Jordan Peterson's observation, adopted by Mochary: people who willfully break rules and get called out feel relief. People who are genuinely trying and get chastised are devastated. "Nearly EVERYONE around us is trying. Our team members, our spouse, our kids, our parents, and our siblings." [c3, c7]

The operational pattern: before introducing any critical feedback to a new relationship, deliver 4-8 rounds of specific, timely praise first. This is not manipulation -- it is creating the psychological safety required for difficult truths to be heard rather than deflected.

#### The Brain Chamber Extraction Technique [6/19]

When people say "I have no feedback" or offer only bland positives, they are almost certainly withholding. The Brain Chamber technique, adapted from Lachy Groom at Stripe, is Mochary's signature method for unlocking honest feedback in those moments:

"Please don't say it, just think it. What is the thought you have that you are afraid to tell me because you think it will hurt my feelings? Again, please don't say it; just think about it. Do you have it in your brain chamber? OK, now please say it." [c3, c7]

This is a psychological two-step that separates the cognitive act of forming the thought from the social act of speaking it. By the time the person has acknowledged having the thought, the social barrier to saying it is already lowered. Mochary demonstrates this live on the Learning Leader show with host Ryan Hawk, telling him: "There's something, a thought that you've had about me that if you said it, you're afraid it would piss me off. So again, don't say it, just think it... Now can you please share it with me?" [ll] Ryan immediately shares that a software development tangent was too long. Matt reflects it back amplified. Ryan confirms feeling heard.

A complementary technique for when the Brain Chamber still does not unlock: supply the thought yourself. "I have a story in my head that you are feeling anger and the thought that you are having is 'Screw Jim, he never gives me clear written direction and then gets mad when things don't turn out the way that he had envisioned.' Is that close?" [c7] The person will then correct you -- "That's close, but not quite it" -- and share what they are actually thinking. The facilitator going first into uncomfortable territory gives permission for the other person to follow.

#### Feedback Amplification (Reflective Amp-Up) [8/19]

When someone gives you feedback, their words represent approximately 1% of the emotional intensity they actually feel. They have compressed their message to the safest possible version. Your job as the receiver is to decompress it back to full size.

The technique: when acknowledging feedback, do not just repeat what was said. Amplify it. Use swear words. State the worst version of what is likely in their head.

"The thoughts in our head are always 100 times bigger than the words we're willing to say. And what happens is when I repeat back that much bigger version, the person goes, wow, Matt just said that back to me and he didn't get angry." [er]

The canonical example, from the EnterpriseReady interview: someone says "I really wish you'd say hello to me when you saw me in the morning." Matt reflects back: "What the fuck is wrong with you, Matt? Like, we work together. I come into the office, you don't even fucking say hello to me. What the fuck is -- what an asshole you are. Goddamn it. Can you just have the decency to say hello to me?" The person responds: "Well, it wasn't quite that big, but yeah, it's kind of right, because it always is." [er]

The amplification does three things simultaneously: (1) proves you heard them -- even more than they said, (2) tests whether the real message is bigger, and (3) proves the environment is safe, because you did not get angry at the amplified version. This creates, in Grant Miller's phrase, a process of "rehydrating" feedback -- restoring it to its actual emotional intensity. [er]

Across the sources, the amplification technique appears in c3, c1, c7, c8, gcw1, lenny, er, and logan -- eight of nineteen -- making it one of Mochary's most signature conversational moves.

#### Feedback Forms: 1-1 and Hot Seat [3/19]

Mochary prescribes two specific feedback formats with written-first methodology:

**1-1 Format.** In every one-on-one meeting, the last five to ten minutes are dedicated to feedback. Both parties write feedback in private scratchpads, then paste into a shared document, then read aloud. Writing before speaking ensures completeness -- no forgetting under social pressure -- and creates permanent artifacts. "Verbal agreements are not impeccable. We all understand words a little bit differently." [c3]

**Hot Seat Format.** One person sits in the center (literally or metaphorically) and receives absolute feedback (1-5 rating + Like + Next Level) from every other person in the room simultaneously. Written in private, pasted into shared doc, read aloud. The Hot Seat is used for exec team feedback sessions and is the precursor to the Board Feedback Exercise.

The written-first methodology is non-negotiable across both formats. Across every protocol, writing comes before speaking. This ensures equity of voice (introverts are not drowned out by extroverts), creates a permanent record, and allows the facilitator to review and guide before anything is shared. [c3, c4, c8]

#### Board and Exec Team Feedback Exercises [3/19]

Mochary runs a specific feedback exercise for boards and executive teams that he has deployed at Confluent, Reddit, Sonder, Mattermost, Viz (boards) and Plaid, Coinbase, Brex, Sonder, Attentive (exec teams). [c3] The structure:

1. Pre-reads -- all participants read the 5 A's document and the feedback exercise instructions in advance.
2. Write privately -- each person writes Like + Wish That (relative) or 1-5 + Like + Next Level (absolute) for the CEO in private scratchpads.
3. Paste into shared doc -- simultaneously, so no one's feedback is influenced by others'.
4. Read Likes aloud -- celebrate. This builds momentum.
5. Read critical feedback -- participants add "+1" in comments when they agree with someone else's Wish That. This surfaces which feedback has the most weight.
6. CEO picks the juiciest -- the critical feedback with the most +1s or the most emotionally charged. The CEO unpacks it using the 5 A's: acknowledges, appreciates, declares accept or not, and if accepted, brainstorms actions.
7. Matt present as "lion-tamer" -- "Think of him as a lion-tamer. Most likely, the lion will stay in the cage, and the CEO will not need Matt. But if he is required, we'll all be glad that he's there." [c3] Matt only intervenes if the CEO gets triggered.

The competition framing is deliberate: "Please give the rawest WISH THAT feedback that you can think of. Yes, it's a competition!" [c3] Gamifying vulnerability creates peer pressure toward candor rather than away from it and gives social cover -- "I was just trying to win the exercise."

Board members are highly qualified to give feedback on some areas (board meeting effectiveness, CEO interaction) and less qualified on others (how to run the company). The CEO puts board feedback "through a more stringent filter" in areas where board members lack operational context, and Mochary coaches them to explain this distinction openly rather than defensively. [c3, c9]

#### Never Async for Critical Feedback [5/19]

Mochary draws a hard line on communication channels for critical feedback:

"Do NOT use a one-way communication method (email, text, Slack, voicemail) to give feedback unless it is 100% positive." [c3]

"It's so dangerous to give feedback asynchronously. Because if I give you what I think is constructive feedback, and you get insulted or feel defensive, and I don't see that, or I don't hear that, then I can't say, 'Oh, Tim, wait a second. I meant that with love.'" [tim]

The failure mode: the recipient reads critical feedback on Slack, feels a flash of anger or hurt, and the sender does not see it. Within days, confirmation bias will have the recipient convinced the sender is the enemy. The relationship is permanently damaged by something that could have been defused in five seconds of synchronous conversation.

The channel hierarchy for critical feedback: in person is best, video call is acceptable, audio call is the minimum. You must be able to see or hear the person's reaction. Matt himself never gives critical feedback asynchronously, regardless of how evolved the receiver is. "I never share critical feedback asynchronously, no matter how open someone is." [c3]

The phased approach for building up to written feedback:
1. First few sessions: give feedback verbally (reading from your notes), watching for tension, stopping to reassure if needed.
2. Next sessions: write feedback into a shared document, have them read it in front of you, watch for triggers.
3. Only after trust is deeply established: asynchronous written feedback -- but Mochary personally never reaches this step for critical content. [c3, c7]

#### The Three C's -- When Feedback Is Not Landing [2/19]

Developed by Celine Teoh, this diagnostic framework addresses the situation where feedback has been given clearly and received well, but nothing changes:

1. **Clarity** -- Is the request specific, behavioral, and unambiguous? "A video camera should be able to capture the behavior you request." [c3, c7] If a video camera cannot see the requested change, the feedback is not specific enough.

2. **Capability** -- Does the person have the training, tools, and resources to do what you are asking? Mochary calls this "The 'Why Can't My Five-Year-Old Drive' Problem" -- the person may lack skill, not will. [c3] Before concluding someone is unmotivated, check whether they have been trained, resourced, and supported.

3. **Caring (Motivation)** -- Is the feedback connecting to what actually drives this person? People respond to different motivational currencies: Inspiration, Task, Position, Relationship, Personal. [c3, c7] A common error is assuming everyone shares your motivators. If feedback is delivered through the wrong currency, it will not land regardless of clarity or capability.

When someone is receiving feedback defensively, the SCARF model (David Rock) can diagnose which social need feels threatened: Status ("Do my colleagues still respect me?"), Certainty ("Does this mean I'm not suited for this anymore?"), Autonomy ("They're trying to control how I work"), Relatedness ("I thought we were a team. Is that changing?"), Fairness ("This criticism feels undeserved"). [c3, c7]

#### The Withholding Trap [5/19]

Mochary frames withholding feedback as a self-fulfilling prophecy that guarantees the outcome the withholder is trying to avoid:

"If you do not give someone feedback, then, by definition, that person will fail." [c3]

"Withholding feedback is never the best answer!" [c3, c7]

The common fear: "If I give this person critical feedback, they might quit." Mochary reframes: giving feedback as a gift produces one of exactly two outcomes, both better than the status quo. Either (1) they improve and perform as you hope, or (2) they admit they cannot do the job and actively help find their replacement. [c3] Meanwhile, withholding guarantees the person continues to underperform, resentment builds on both sides, and eventually the situation explodes.

For leaders who say "my people never give me feedback," the problem is not their team's timidity. It is the leader's failure to create structural space for candor. "If I don't ask for feedback, people don't give it... resentment builds." [c4] Post-meeting surveys are "close to useless." [c3] Open-door policies are fake doors. Only dedicated, recurring, written-first, structured feedback sessions -- with the leader demonstrating safe reception in real time -- will overcome the asymmetric bet.

#### Stories

**The "Say Hello" Amplification Demo (EnterpriseReady).** Matt demonstrates feedback amplification live. Someone says "I wish you'd say hello in the morning." Matt reflects back with profanity at 100x intensity. The person says "it wasn't quite that big, but yeah." Matt explains: people self-censor to 1% of actual emotional intensity. By amplifying, you prove the environment is safe and create "a huge, wide open space that is completely and utterly safe." [er]

**Live Feedback with Ryan Hawk (Learning Leader).** Matt conducts a live Brain Chamber exercise on-air. He tells Ryan to think of critical feedback, explicitly says "don't say it," then asks him to share it. Ryan immediately delivers honest feedback about a tangent being too long. Matt reflects it back amplified. Ryan confirms feeling heard. Matt then explains: "It's actually more important than accepting or acting on the feedback." Making someone feel heard creates connection even if the feedback is rejected. [ll]

**The Weight Feedback Story (TWIST).** Matt gained approximately 60 pounds by conscious choice. Multiple coachees gave him feedback to eat healthier. He demonstrated the full protocol live: appreciated, acknowledged ("you want me to eat healthier"), then did not accept ("this is a conscious choice"). The coachees were satisfied because the protocol worked -- they were heard. Approximately 40 days later, Matt stopped carbs, played soccer, scored 3 goals for the first time, and reversed his position: "Now I accept it." Both outcomes -- declining and later accepting -- demonstrate the protocol working as designed. [twist]

**Lachy Groom at Stripe.** The origin of the Brain Chamber technique. Lachy's original version: "What feedback are you afraid to give because you think it will hurt my feelings? Please tell me that." Matt adapted it by adding the two-step delay -- "don't say it, just think it... now say it" -- which psychologically separates forming the thought from speaking it, lowering the social barrier. [c3]

**Med School Tutors Negative Reviews.** Two contrasting responses to the same negative review. Robert, the former CEO, wrote a genuine, wholehearted response that validated the reviewer's experience -- "exceptional at this." Matt wrote an early response where "I had not fully shifted out of anger, and you can feel the defensiveness in my response." The comparison illustrates: do not reply in a heightened emotional state. Find the 1% that is valid. Thank the reviewer. Validate their experience. Offer contact information to make amends. [c3]

**Reed Hastings -- The Evolved Feedback Giver.** Hastings does not act in anger. He does not state how he feels. Instead, he states what he thinks needs to happen in positive terms and why it will lead to both the company's and the person's success. Mochary considers this "the latest and IMHO best iteration so far" of feedback delivery -- a model that transcends the NVC template by removing the emotional self-disclosure entirely and focusing purely on forward-looking action. [c3]

**Celine Teoh's Daughter Learning to Walk.** The foundational metaphor for praise as infrastructure. A parent cheers every time a child stands up. At no point does the parent chastise the child for falling. This is the emotional logic that should govern adult feedback as well. [c3]
