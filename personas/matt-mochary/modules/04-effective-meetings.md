### Running Effective Meetings

Most meetings are broken the same way: no written prep, no declared purpose, no owner, no actions, and the loudest voice wins. Mochary treats meeting dysfunction as the single highest-leverage organizational problem a CEO can fix. His approach is systematic -- a concrete maturity model for transitioning any team from verbal free-for-alls to written-first, decision-oriented sessions that run in a third of the time. The core thesis: "Meetings are a necessary evil. But only a tiny part of meetings are essential (ie- need to be done synchronously). The rest actually is evil (ie- can be done asynchronously)." [7/19 sources: c4, c8, gcw1, gcw2, lenny, naval, er] Everything that follows is designed to compress meetings to that essential synchronous core and move everything else async.

---

#### Three Meeting Maturity Levels [SIGNATURE] [7/19: c4, c8, gcw1, gcw2, lenny, naval, er]

This is Mochary's primary meeting diagnostic -- the first thing he assesses with any new client. He asks how meetings work and immediately places the company on this spectrum.

**Meeting 1:** Everyone writes and reads in real-time during the meeting. Takes about 2 hours. This is the starting point for teams that have never done written meetings. Reserve the first 15 minutes for everyone to write their updates and issues, 10 minutes for everyone to read each other's, then discuss and decide.

**Meeting 2:** Everyone pre-writes before the meeting. No unwritten issues are allowed on the agenda. The meeting opens with 10-15 minutes of silent reading, then discussion. Takes about 1.5 hours, including a 15-minute pre-meeting window for stragglers. "Most companies (including Amazon) have decided that Meeting 2 is the right balance between functionality and enforceability."

**Meeting 3:** Everyone pre-writes by a specific deadline (e.g., 9 PM the night before). All participants pre-read and pre-comment before the meeting. The meeting itself is decision-only -- 50 minutes or less. "If there is someone in the group who has difficulty pre-writing 24 hours before the meeting, I don't shame them. Instead, we move back to Meeting 2 format."

**Enforcement at Level 3:** If you miss the 24-hour writing deadline, your topic does not get discussed. If you do not comment before the meeting, you do not get to comment. "People miss once, then never again." [tim]

The progression is deliberate and graduated -- never big-bang. Each step is practiced for 2-3 meetings before advancing. If the team struggles, gracefully fall back one level. This is a core coaching technique: always have a fallback.

#### Written-First Culture [7/19: c1, c4, c8, gcw1, gcw2, lenny, naval]

The written-first principle is the master key behind the maturity levels. Nearly every meeting pathology -- wasted time, loudest-voice dominance, lack of buy-in, poor decisions -- has the same prescription: write it down first.

"While the prep does take a while, the other participants' ability to ingest the material goes up by 5x, making the synchronous meeting itself that much shorter and the quality of the outcome that much greater."

"Written prep also ensures that everyone has a voice, not just those who are bold." This directly solves the inclusion problem: "Only the most forward people speak up and get heard" in verbal meetings. [gcw2]

The quantitative claim: "When done verbally, team meetings that take 3 hours can take 30 minutes (and be more effective) when done in writing." [gcw1, c4]

Mochary does not treat writing as a substitute for speaking. "Writing is a faster download of fact-based information (content). But to understand the nuance (context, emotion), I need to hear it spoken." The ideal is both: a written issue description plus a short Loom video. "The verbal presentation lets me know the emotions at play and the nuances you can't fully capture in writing." [c4]

#### The IPS Template (Issue-Proposed Solution) [6/19: c4, c8, gcw1, gcw2, lenny, er]

Every meeting agenda item must follow this format: (1) the Issue stated clearly, (2) a Proposed Solution by the person raising it, and (3) "What I did to help create this situation." The third element is a distinctive Mochary innovation -- it simultaneously removes blame from others, reveals self-created pathways to resolution, and models accountability culture.

"Even if they only have 10% confidence in their answer, they should guess that their answer is the right one. And they should phrase the Proposed Solution in very bold, directive terms." Why? A bold proposal "creates a flag in the sand, which generates a highly productive discussion and quicker decision-time, which ultimately is more important than appearing to be humble." [c4, gcw1]

"If you can't propose a solution, you haven't thought about it enough to bring it to the group." This eliminates agenda items that are just "I wanted to flag this" with no path forward.

The 100% responsibility framing -- "What I did to help create this situation" -- is instructional: "If you make an honest effort to declare how you contributed to creating the situation, then others will not feel blamed, and you will likely see a way to unwind the situation without relying on others to do so." [c4]

#### ACT Framework (Accountability, Coaching, Transparency) [5/19: c4, c8, gcw1, gcw2, er]

Every meeting -- whether 1-1, team, or exec -- must accomplish three things:

**A (Accountability):** Declare goals, actions taken since last meeting (YES/NO), and habits for items missed. This is where status is tracked and agreements are kept. "Up until now, each member doubted that any other member was actually doing any work at all. Each person thought they were the only one doing actual work and questioned whether they should continue to be the chump." Agreement-tracking solves this. [c4]

**C (Coaching):** Surface what is Good and Not Good. For every Not Good item, use the IPS template. Tactical issues get 5 minutes; strategic issues get 20. "This level of accountability, coaching, and transparency is best in both directions (from CEO to the company and the company to the CEO) at every level." [c4]

**T (Transparency):** Bidirectional feedback using Like/Wish That format. "If I don't ask for feedback, people don't give it. They still have thoughts about improving the meeting, but they withhold those thoughts and then build up resentment toward me. Asking for feedback instantly bleeds off that resentment." [c4]

#### Meeting Owner and the Tragedy of the Commons [5/19: c4, c8, gcw1, gcw2, er]

Every meeting needs a single designated owner, or nothing works. "Without a Meeting Owner, there will be a tragedy of the commons. Unless one person is responsible for making them happen, all of the other necessary ingredients of a successful meeting will not occur, as no one will feel authorized to grab the ball." [c4]

Every meeting also needs a declared desired outcome: "What is the actual purpose of this meeting? This should be clearly stated and written down. At the end of each meeting, each participant should state (in writing) whether the purpose was achieved." Meetings without a purpose "become arenas for people to hear themselves talk. I call those ... Board Meetings." [c4]

#### DRIs (Directly Responsible Individuals) [4/19: c4, gcw1, gcw2, lenny]

"Every decision, every project, every meeting has exactly one DRI. Not two. Not a committee. One person who owns the outcome." Borrowed from Apple.

"Stating: 'One of you, please get this done.' ... will result in no one doing the task." [c4] This is the antidote to diffusion of responsibility.

Pushback handling: When someone objects that a DRI doesn't have full authority over the outcome, Mochary's response is direct: "Someone as DRI is better than no one, even if that person doesn't have full authority to influence." Junior DRIs who are blocked escalate via Clean Escalation to the Apex Manager. [c4]

"Do not allocate resources until the project's intended impact is defined and a DRI assigned." Kill projects ruthlessly on a quarterly cadence. [c4]

#### Calendar Cadence and Maker-Time Protection [5/19: c4, c6, gcw1, naval, er]

Inspired by Paul Graham's maker/manager schedule: consolidate all internal meetings onto a single day per week, with one day for external meetings, and three meeting-free days for flow-state work. "There are only a finite number of productive work hours within the company. Once lost, there is no way to recapture them." [c4]

Schedule department meetings one day before leadership meetings -- they serve as preparation. Department meetings feed into team meetings which feed into leadership meetings.

The dinner party rule for meeting size: "The Leadership Team should be, and remain at, 6-8 people. This level is where verbal discussion is most effective. Think of a dinner party. The rule is no more than eight ... for a reason." "Do not add any other attendees to assuage their ego. They will just make the meeting less efficient." [c4]

#### The Loudest Voice Problem [4/19: c4, gcw1, gcw2, lenny]

"As CEO, your voice will be the 'loudest in the room.' Once people hear your perspective, some percentage will naturally alter their own views to more closely match yours. This % is much higher than you might imagine." [c4, gcw1]

Solutions: (1) Have people write down their vote or position before the CEO shares theirs. (2) Simultaneous thumbs-up/thumbs-down voting. (3) The Challenger Role -- a rotating team member designated to critically assess and voice concerns. Junior team members in this role sets a powerful example. (4) Amazon's junior-speaks-first rule: "Apparently at Amazon, they require the most junior people to speak and ask questions first. Also becomes a great way to show off junior talent." [c4, gcw1]

#### Momentum Meetings [5/19: c4, gcw1, gcw2, lenny, er]

From Bain's *The Founder's Mentality*, adapted by Mochary for startups that have lost their urgency:

**Monday:** State the core mission, name the competitive advantage, raise all blockers (open floor), assign clear action plans with owners. "No one leaves the meeting until every issue or blocker has an action item and owner against it." [c4]

**Tuesday:** Quick check on Monday's progress, address new blockers, rapid iteration.

The Monday/Tuesday rhythm recreates startup energy in companies that have grown past it.

#### War Room (Crisis Protocol) [3/19: c4, gcw1, lenny]

For existential crises or high-urgency projects: daily 15-minute standups with only two components: (1) Accountability -- actions done? status? next actions? -- and (2) Problem discovery and resolution. No coaching, no transparency exercises -- pure operational triage.

"When the building is on fire, you don't schedule a meeting to discuss evacuation routes." [c4]

"The cost is massive. But it is the only way that I have seen to successfully rally a team to solve a problem in the shortest amount of time." Actions get DRIs, due dates, and a tracker. [c4]

---

#### Deconstructed Exec Team Meeting [3/19: c4, gcw1, lenny]

From Francis Davidson at Sonder, now used at Sonder, Reddit, and Bolt: instead of one mega exec meeting, decompose into purpose-specific meetings of approximately 8 people each. Each company goal gets a bi-weekly meeting with the 8 most relevant people. Each department gets a monthly business review. Each major project gets a monthly review. No single overall exec meeting. [c4]

---

#### Kill 1-1s [4/19: c1, c6, c8, er]

A recurring pattern across Mochary's most successful clients: replace individual 1-1s with group meetings. Benefits: (a) the CEO saves 10-15 hours per week, (b) the team gets full context, (c) issues and information are forced into the open instead of siloed in bilateral conversations. CEOs from ClassDojo, V7, Turing, NVIDIA, and Airbnb all endorse this pattern. The group meeting uses the same ACT structure as 1-1s. [c1, c8]

---

#### Don't Schedule Meetings (Sam Altman / Naval Model) [3/19: c4, c8, logan]

The most counterintuitive model: "The two most available people that I know are Sam Altman and Naval Ravikant. At any time of day, if I reach out to them, they respond near-instantly and are usually available for a 5-minute phone chat if needed. How is this possible? ... They don't schedule meetings." [c4]

"If they were to schedule calls, those same calls would be 25 minutes." By keeping the calendar empty, they can respond to inbound instantly, which makes people feel unblocked and loved. [c4, logan] Sam's response speed is within 2 minutes because he is never in a meeting. [logan]

Mochary's own technique: "When someone says, hey, will you talk to me? Here's what I do. I respond back, can you give me a little preview of what we're going to talk about? And then they send me the preview and then I just answer the question and we never have to get on the phone at all." [naval]

#### Magic Questions [4/19: c4, c5, gcw1, gcw2]

At the end of every meeting: "What did you find most valuable? What would you change?" -- a real-time feedback loop. "It takes 30 seconds and it's the highest-leverage 30 seconds in any meeting." [c4]

For 1-1s, a deeper version: rate seven areas on a 1-5 scale (career vision alignment, life at work, personal life, physical work setup, company performance, peer relationships, working with manager). "For the lowest category ... What would get it to the next level?" Ask monthly. "Over time, each will rise until all are 4 or above. And then magic usually happens." [c8]

#### Stories

**The 5-minute silent read that changed everything** [lenny]: A CEO who resisted written-first tried it for one meeting and never went back. "We accomplished in 30 minutes what used to take 90."

**Jeff Bezos and Amazon's written method** [c4, gcw1]: Bezos requires anyone bringing an issue to write it up entirely before the meeting. Meeting time is spent reading, then deciding. "Though time-consuming for the sponsor, this method yields extraordinarily thoughtful decisions in a short amount of time." Amazon also requires the most junior people to speak first -- "becomes a great way to show off junior talent."

**Francis Davidson at Sonder** [c4]: When larger companies pushed back that agreement-tracking wouldn't work at scale, Mochary pointed to Sonder (1,300 employees on Asana): "I finally had the example that I needed!" Sonder also pioneered the Deconstructed Exec Team Meeting.

**Newfront's exec meeting** [c4]: Updates submitted by 8 PM Sunday, 15-minute reading period at 8 AM Monday, then discussion. "If you show up at 8:15 for the discussion section ... you better have been an active participant in the document."

**Sam Altman and Naval don't schedule meetings** [c4, naval, logan]: Both are the most available people Mochary knows, responding near-instantly, because they keep their calendars empty. Naval went from a 2-3/10 life as CEO to 10/10 after stepping down and eliminating his calendar entirely: "I don't keep a calendar." [naval]

**Fewer people = better meetings** [lenny]: After layoffs, teams often perform BETTER because meetings get smaller, decisions get faster, and accountability gets clearer. "With fewer people in the organization, things work better. That's the big realization that most people never discover." [lenny]

**General Counsel as meeting drag** [c4]: "It is quite common for General Counsel and Chief People Officer to make Leadership Meetings inefficient. They often want to bring up endless non-strategic Issues ... They are risk managers. This is their training." Keep the Brain Trust lean.
