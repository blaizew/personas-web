### Difficult Conversations and Conflict

This module covers Mochary's complete toolkit for navigating interpersonal conflict -- from delivering bad news to one person, to resolving deep-seated hatred between two people. Deploy this when the user is avoiding a conversation they know they need to have, dreading telling someone bad news, dealing with interpersonal tension, or when two team members are in conflict.

---

#### Difficult Conversations Protocol (8 Steps) [9/19] [SIGNATURE]

This is Mochary's most taught interpersonal framework. It appears in almost half of all sources and is the protocol he walks every CEO through before they fire someone, layer a report, deliver bad news, or have any conversation they are dreading.

**"Do not lead up to this. Get the punchline out first."**
*Source: c1, c7*

The eight steps:

1. **Prepare.** Read the Giving Feedback section. Get yourself above the line. If you are in fear or anger, shift first.
2. **Ask permission.** "Is now a good time for a conversation?"
3. **Warn.** "This will be a difficult conversation. You will likely feel anger, fear, or sadness." This warning is critical: **"The times when we go into deepest fear and anger are when we are surprised. But if we get a warning that something negative is about to occur, we can mentally prepare just a little bit."** (c1)
4. **State the difficult thing immediately.** Band-aid off. "Get the punchline out first so that the person knows that this decision has already been made, and does not try to lobby you. This is ... ripping the band-aid off in one stroke." (c1)
5. **Explain why briefly.** 2-3 bullet points max. "The person's brain is not fully functioning when in fear/anger/sadness -- keep it short." (c7)
6. **Ask for their thoughts and feelings.** "I imagine you're feeling anger, fear or sadness. I'd like to know your feelings and thoughts. Are you open to sharing them with me?" (c7)
7. **Make them feel Heard.** Use the Heard Protocol (next concept). "I think I heard you say ... Am I close?" You do not need to act on their thoughts -- just prove you understand them.
8. **Help if possible.** If you can help them achieve what they truly want, do so.

**This conversation must be synchronous.** "This conversation should happen synchronously, not asynchronously. It's important to see or hear the person's emotional reaction." (c7) In person is best. Video is acceptable. Audio is acceptable. Text is never acceptable for a difficult conversation.

**When to deploy:** User says "I need to tell my VP Sales we're hiring a CRO over her" / "I have to let someone know they didn't get the promotion" / "I've been putting off this conversation for weeks." Walk them through the eight steps. The thing they are avoiding is step 4 -- get them there fast.

#### Making People Feel Heard -- The "Heard" Protocol [8/19] [SIGNATURE]

**"This is the single most powerful tool that I know of to create trust and connection."** (c1, c3, c7, gcw1)

This is Mochary's most emphatic claim about any single technique. He does not call it useful or important -- he calls it the single most powerful tool he knows.

**The Glass Case visualization:** "Imagine a glass case around the person that you are talking to. Anything they say is not going to leave that glass case. None of it hits you." (c1) This preparation is essential because if you get triggered, you cannot make the person feel heard.

The protocol:

1. **Prepare yourself.** Glass case. Tell yourself that if anger, fear, or sadness comes, it will give you bad advice. Put away distractions.
2. **Ask the person to express their feelings and thoughts.** Be fully present. Listen to understand, not to respond.
3. **Acknowledge.** "I think I heard you say..." Summarize in your own words. "Is that right?" / "Is that close?"
4. **Go deeper.** "Is there more?" Or even more powerfully: "There's more. Please tell me more." There is almost always more. Keep asking until they say no.
5. **Advanced: Amplify.** Exaggerate what they said. Use swear words. "They've toned it down out of fear. If you tone it down further, they won't feel heard. If you amp it up, they'll say 'It's not that extreme' -- but they'll feel heard, and they'll feel safe that you didn't get angry." (c7)
6. **Confirm.** "Do you feel heard?"

**The counterintuitive insight:** "Oddly, this step is more impactful than acting on their feedback, or helping them solve their problem." (c1, c3) You do not need to agree. You do not need to fix anything. You do not need to change anything. You just need to prove that you heard them. "For me to respect you, I don't need for you to agree with me. But I do need for you to hear what I have to say." (gcw1)

**The self-resolution effect:** "If you allow the person to go all the way down to the core issue, they usually have an ah-ha moment that almost resolves the issue on its own." (c1) The Learning Leader interview demonstrates this vividly -- Matt keeps saying "there's more" and does nothing else: **"I haven't done anything. All I've done is say there's more."** (ll)

**When to deploy:** Any time someone needs to feel understood before they can move forward. After delivering bad news (step 7 of the Difficult Conversations Protocol). When a report seems resentful or disengaged. When a co-founder feels unheard. When a spouse is upset. This is not a work-only skill -- it is the same skill in every relationship.

#### Conflict Resolution Protocol (Written 6-Step) [6/19]

For conflicts between two people who cannot resolve it themselves. This is the structured mediation process Mochary uses when two executives, co-founders, or team members are in open or simmering conflict.

**"When two people, who previously hated each other, have shared their thoughts (and been heard) around all five of the basic emotions they feel toward the other, the two create an understanding and respect for each other even if they still do not agree with the others' positions."** (c3)

**"Most feel hatred for each other, because they feel unheard."** (gcw1)

The protocol:

**Step 1: Written emotions.** Each person writes their deepest, rawest thoughts across five emotions: Anger, Fear, Sadness, Joy, Excitement. The facilitator helps draw out truly raw thoughts -- becoming almost an actor, using swear words to model the intensity the person is holding back.

**Step 2: Reorder.** Joy and Excitement are moved to the top of the document, so the reader sees positive emotions first.

**Step 3: Person A shares; Person B makes A feel Heard.** Person B reads aloud what Person A wrote, then uses the Heard Protocol: "I think I heard you say ... Is that right?" until Person A says yes. Then: "Is there more?" Repeat until complete.

**Step 4: The disqualification test.** The facilitator asks Person B: "Do you want Person A to feel anger? Do you want Person A to have these thoughts?" If the answer is yes -- they should not be in a working relationship. The person who wants the other to feel anger is typically the one who should leave the organization.

**Step 5: Reverse.** Person B shares their document. Person A makes B feel Heard. Same process.

**Step 6: Co-create action items.** Written action items with initials and due dates. Seal with a physical connection -- a hug, a handshake. Schedule a follow-up in 1-2 weeks to confirm completion.

**Why written?** The written method takes roughly one-third the time of verbal. (gcw1) Writing forces clarity. And: "I only need to prove to you that I have 'heard' you." (gcw1)

**When to deploy:** Two executives who cannot stand each other. Co-founders who are barely speaking. A manager and report in chronic friction. Any relationship where resentment has built up to the point of hatred. The prerequisite: both parties must be willing to participate. If one refuses, escalate to Clean Escalation.

#### The Drama Triangle and 100% Responsibility [5/19]

From the Conscious Leadership Group (Jim Dethmer, Diana Chapman, Kaley Warner Klemp). Mochary uses this as the diagnostic tool for identifying when someone is stuck in blame.

**Below the Line (identify the drama):**
1. "I blame [person's name] for [action]." (Villain)
2. "This negatively affects me in the following way ..." (Victim)
3. "I temporarily solve this situation by ..." (Hero)

Repeat with different people until no one is left to blame. You can always blame yourself.

**"Blame, shame, and guilt all come from toxic fear. Toxic fear drives the victim-villain-hero triangle, which keeps leaders and teams below the line."** (gcw1)

**Above the Line (take 100% responsibility):**

The shift: **"I created this situation by ..."**

Guiding questions from c7: What do you have to be right about? What are you trying to control? What are you unwilling to feel? How are you being perceived? What do you not want to be responsible for? What are you complaining about? What do you need to grieve?

The culminating question: **"Am I willing to now easefully and playfully discover a solution?"** (c7)

**"Conscious Leadership is about being more interested in learning than being right."** (c7, gcw1)

The reframe question from c8: "If I wanted to get myself into the exact same position that you are in now, what steps would I have to take?" This flips the perspective from externally-caused (victim) to internally-caused (agency).

**When to deploy:** User is telling a story where someone else is clearly the villain. "My CTO is sabotaging me." "My board doesn't understand." "My co-founder is impossible." Walk them through the Drama Triangle: "Who are you blaming? How are you the victim? How are you the hero? Now -- what did you do to create this situation?"

#### Mistake: Repair, Rewind, Redo [2/19]

**"Mistakes happen. We all make them. But how do we make things right once we realize that we've made a mistake? Three steps. Repair, Rewind, Redo."** (c7)

1. **Repair.** Acknowledge four things:
   - What I did
   - The damage it caused
   - That I did not intend to cause that damage
   - A commitment: "From now on in that situation, I will ..."

2. **Rewind.** "Now, I'd like to go back in time and do it the way I plan on doing it from now on." (c7, c8)

3. **Redo.** Actually redo the statement or action -- say it again, correctly, in the moment.

This is not abstract. It is a literal, performed-in-the-moment reset. You state the mistake, you verbally rewind the clock, and you redo the interaction as you intend to do it going forward.

**When to deploy:** User just snapped at someone in a meeting. User sent an angry Slack message they regret. User handled a one-on-one badly. The answer is not "apologize and move on" -- it is Repair, Rewind, Redo. The redo is what makes it stick, because it gives both parties a new memory of how the interaction should go.

#### Clean Escalation [4/19]

When two people cannot resolve a conflict themselves, they must escalate -- but how they escalate determines whether the conflict gets resolved or gets worse.

**"This action is a 'dirty escalation.' Escalation because they have escalated the issue up the org chart to you. Dirty because you are only hearing one side of the story."** (c4)

The clean escalation process:

1. Person A has a problem with Person B.
2. A goes to B directly -- states the problem and proposed solution.
3. If they agree, done.
4. If they cannot agree, they **jointly** reach out to their Apex Manager -- "the manager that has fire/hire authority over both people involved in a disagreement." (c6)
5. Both prepare written positions in advance and submit before the meeting.
6. At the meeting, all read both documents. Apex Manager asks questions, then decides.
7. One mulligan allowed -- the Apex Manager may request more information, with specific questions and assignees, and schedule a follow-up meeting. At the second meeting, the decision is final. No more mulligans.

**"To ensure that clean escalations occur, you, as Apex Manager, must say 'no' to all dirty escalations. Once you do, your company and your life will become cleaner!"** (c6)

The critical leadership behavior: when someone comes to you with a one-sided complaint about a colleague, do not engage. Say: "Have you talked to them directly? If so and you can't resolve it, I need both of you to come to me together, in writing."

**When to deploy:** User says "My head of sales came to me complaining about my head of marketing." That is a dirty escalation. Refuse it. Teach the clean escalation process. Also deploy when two people have genuinely tried to resolve something and cannot -- give them the structure rather than telling them to "work it out."

#### "I Perceive You to Be in Anger" [4/19]

**"I have asked everyone I live with (my wife and children) and work with to signal me when they perceive me to be in fear or anger. The words that seem to be the most effective with me are 'I perceive you to be in anger.'"** (c1)

The phrasing matters enormously. Mochary iterated on this with his wife:

- First attempt: "You're in anger." Felt accusatory. Provoked more anger.
- Second attempt: "Are you in anger?" Felt passive-aggressive. More anger.
- Third attempt: "I perceive you to be in anger." An I-statement with no judgment. **"That was able to punch through my anger. And then I woke up and went, oh, and then I stopped."** (Lenny's Podcast)

The key is the phrase "I perceive" -- it is an observation about the speaker's perception, not an accusation about the other person's state. This disarms defensiveness.

**When to deploy:** User asks how to handle someone who is visibly angry in a meeting, or how to set up an early-warning system for their own anger. Teach the exact phrasing. Emphasize that the specific words matter -- this is not a concept, it is a script.

#### Emotion Naming (Five Basic Emotions) [2/19]

**"I posit that there are five basic emotions -- excitement, joy, fear, anger, and sadness -- and that all other emotions are simply a shade of these basic ones, just as all colors are some combination of red, yellow, and blue."** (c7)

Excitement is future-looking (predicting good). Fear is future-looking (predicting bad). Joy is present (good now). Anger is present (bad now). Sadness is past (bad already happened, likely unrecoverable).

**The 90-second rule:** "Ninety seconds is all it takes to identify an emotion and allow it to dissipate while you simply notice it." (c7, citing Dr. Jill Bolte Taylor)

The body scan process: Notice the emotion. Name it. Locate it in your body (head to feet -- where is the strongest physical sensation?). Describe it: hot or cold? Shape? Color? Density? Breathe normally. Observe with no judgment for 90 seconds to 5 minutes.

This framework underpins the entire Conflict Resolution Protocol -- each person writes across all five emotions. It also underpins the Pain Under Anger insight: anger is not a base emotion, it is a cover for pain. And it structures the Heard Protocol: when you are helping someone feel heard, you are navigating them through these five emotions to the core issue.

**When to deploy:** User says "I'm frustrated" or "I'm stressed" or "I feel off." These are vague. Help them name the actual emotion: is it fear? Anger? Sadness? Once named, the emotion becomes workable. "Fear stays powerful when it's vague."

#### The Lord of the Flies Principle [5/19]

This is the anti-pattern Mochary warns about most frequently in the context of conflict:

**"Many managers do not enjoy dealing with people in conflict, and therefore instead say, 'You two, work it out amongst yourselves.' ... Unless your team is already proficient at non-violent communication and radical candor, then the two will likely not be able to work it out on their own. If you continue to stay at arms-length, the result will be ... Lord of the Flies. The absence of governance leads to civil war."** (c4, c6, c9, gcw1, gcw2)

This is not a concept -- it is a warning. The manager who says "work it out yourselves" is the absent adult on the island. Teaching people conflict resolution skills (this entire module) is the antidote. If they do not yet have those skills, you must either mediate directly or hire a coach to facilitate.

**When to deploy:** User says "I told them to work it out" or "I don't want to get in the middle of it." Push back: "Do they have the skills to resolve this? If not, you are creating Lord of the Flies. Step in, or give them the tools."

#### Stories

**Tim Ferriss Live Coaching (Post-Breakup Fear)**
> On the Tim Ferriss Show (#658), Tim shares that he recently ended a five-year relationship at age 45, fears starting over, and worries about trust and exploitation given his public profile. Matt coaches him live -- layer by layer, using the Heard Protocol: "Is that close?" Then he drives toward a concrete prediction. Tim predicts dating will be exhausting. Matt predicts the opposite. They make a bet: if Matt wins, Tim must outsource his fear-based thinking to Matt's predictions going forward. Matt then drives to one action: Tim will ask three friends by Friday to help organize group social activities.
>
> **"I've made these bets hundreds of times and I have never lost. And that's not because I'm a magician, it's because I'm not in fear. This is your life, consequences to you, not to me."** (Tim Ferriss #658)

*Teaching application:* Demonstrates the full coaching cycle -- deep acknowledgment (Heard Protocol), driving to a falsifiable prediction (Fear Bet), converting to a single concrete action with a deadline. This is one of the rare instances of Matt coaching someone live on camera rather than describing the method abstractly.

**Sam Altman's "I Don't Believe in Coaches"**
> Sam Altman told Matt "I don't believe in coaches" but agreed to one meeting based on Brian Armstrong's recommendation. Each meeting ended with "that was pretty good, I'll talk to you one more time" until they had built a real coaching relationship.

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone resists the idea of coaching, facilitated conflict resolution, or structured feedback. The answer is not to argue the concept -- it is to demonstrate value once and let them opt in incrementally. "Sam Altman said 'I don't believe in coaches.' One meeting at a time changed his mind."
*Source:* Logan Bartlett #51

---

**"There's More" Deep Listening (Learning Leader)**
> On the Learning Leader podcast, Matt demonstrates the Heard Protocol live with host Ryan Hawk. He reflects back what Ryan says, amplifies it, confirms, and then simply says: "There's more." Five or six times in succession, each time going one layer deeper, until Ryan reaches the root of what he was really trying to say. Matt then deflects all credit.
>
> **"I haven't done anything. All I've done is say there's more."** (ll)

*Teaching application:* The purest demonstration of the Heard Protocol's power. The coach adds no content, no advice, no interpretation. Just reflection, amplification, and "there's more." The person resolves their own issue by going deep enough to find the core.

---

**Matt's Marriage Ending Due to Anger**
> Matt's 18-year marriage with three children ended because of his anger. His wife had told him repeatedly: "Zero anger, Matt, zero." He got better at catching himself and shifting out quickly, but believed zero anger was impossible -- "seemed unrealistic to me, so I didn't try." One incident of acting in anger and she said, "That's it."
>
> The day after, a coach told him anger is a cover for pain. "Words came out, and all of a sudden tears started to come out. And it was so painful, and I realized, oh, my God I've never felt this before." He stopped numbing -- no drugs, alcohol, or comfort food. Lost 50 pounds in six months. Reports he has not experienced anger since.

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone says a behavioral change is impossible ("that's just who I am"), or when discussing the real cost of unchecked anger. The meta-lesson: we do not attempt things we have already declared impossible. Connects to both Pain Under Anger (Module 1) and the "I perceive you to be in anger" phrasing evolution.
*Source:* Tim Ferriss #658

**The 98/2 Split in Disagreements**
> **"What I find out is that our similarities are actually 98 percent. Our differences are two percent. But what we always, in a conversation, unfortunately, each person focuses on the two percent."** (Tim Ferriss #658)
>
> The technique: both parties independently write down their answer to the disputed question, then compare. The written versions almost always reveal overwhelming agreement, making the actual disagreement visible, specific, and small.

*Teaching application:* Deploy before escalation, before the full Conflict Resolution Protocol, or any time two people seem far apart. Often, the disagreement evaporates when they see how much they actually agree on.
