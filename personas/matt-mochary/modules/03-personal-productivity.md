### Personal Productivity and Getting Things Done

Mochary's productivity philosophy is not about working harder or managing time better. It is built on a single premise: the constraint is energy, not time. "We all feel like we have limited time, but that's actually really not the case. What we have is limited energy." [tim] Every system he teaches -- Top Goal, Inbox Zero, GTD, Impeccable Agreements -- serves the same function: protecting your most creative hours for work that matters and eliminating the cognitive overhead of open loops. This module covers the tactical infrastructure that transforms a reactive, overwhelmed CEO into a proactive one.

---

#### Top Goal [10/19]
Sources: c1, c2, c4, c6, c8, gcw1, gcw2, tim, twist, er

This is Mochary's single most prescribed productivity technique and his gateway intervention for new coaching clients. The concept is drawn from Greg McKeown's *Essentialism*, but the implementation is pure Mochary -- mechanical, prescriptive, and non-negotiable.

"Schedule two hours each day to work on your Top Goal only. And do this every single workday. Period." [c1]

The rationale is structural, not motivational. "In startups, fires never cease to burn. One of the most common complaints I hear from CEOs is that they seem to have infinite things to do on a day-to-day basis, yet weeks will go by, and they don't feel like they have accomplished anything." [c6] The Top Goal gets the first and best hours of the day because "there is also strong research-backed evidence to show that we have more decision and thought-processing energy early in the day when our brain is freshly rested." [gcw1]

**The ramp-up protocol** for anyone new to the practice: start with 30 minutes tomorrow. Do 30 minutes daily for a week. Increase to 1 hour. Continue increasing until reaching 2 hours. [gcw1] During this time: no emails, texts, calls, or messages. [gcw1]

"Everyone is making requests of you, but if all you do spend all day is responding to other people's requests, you never actually march towards your own priorities." [lenny]

Mochary connects Top Goal to accountability, which he treats as the entire system: "My coaching is all about driving towards an action. And I have a system, and the system is all about writing that down and checking to make sure it got done, and that's it." [tim] Tim Ferriss pushed back: "Accountability beats elaborate planning most of the time." Mochary: "I would say all the time." [tim]

For maintaining focus during Top Goal time, Mochary recommends an accountability partner -- someone physically or virtually present whose only job is to keep you on task. "It's like the same idea as a trainer in a gym. A trainer in a gym, maybe they're teaching you a little bit, but more often than not, they're just forcing you to do the thing that, you know, you need to do. But if they weren't there, you would kind of go, eh, and not do it." [lenny] For extroverts, even body-doubling works: "Just having another living, breathing human in the room creates a sense of peace, enough of a sense of peace that these tasks no longer feel so annoying." [tim] Focusmate ($5/month) is the digital version. [tim]

"If you do nothing else I teach you, do this." [c1]

#### Inbox Zero [7/19]
Sources: c1, c2, gcw1, gcw2, tim, twist, er

Mochary adopts David Allen's Inbox Zero wholesale but compresses it into a vivid metaphor and a strict protocol.

"Think of your combined inboxes as a single triage room at a hospital. Some cases that come in are urgent, others not so much. It is critical to notice the urgent cases immediately... If you use the triage room as a waiting room as well, then a new patient can enter the room, sit down in a chair, and bleed out from his stab wound before you even realize he is there." [gcw1]

**The protocol:**
1. Check inbox only twice a day (morning and afternoon). [gcw1]
2. If an email takes less than 2 minutes, do it immediately. [gcw1]
3. If it takes more than 2 minutes, write a Next Action and file the email. [gcw1]
4. Repeat until inbox is at zero. [gcw1]

"If you check your email incessantly, multiple times an hour, you are wasting hours of productivity." [gcw1] A full inbox is an open loop that drains cognitive energy all day. [c1]

#### GTD -- Getting Things Done [6/19]
Sources: c1, c2, gcw1, gcw2, tim, er

Mochary adopted David Allen's system as his organizational backbone and adapted it for CEOs who face a volume of inputs that no other system can handle.

**The adapted system:**
1. Process every single item in your inbox daily (all inboxes: email, Slack, text, to-dos). [gcw1]
2. If the action takes less than 2 minutes, do it immediately. [gcw1]
3. If more than 2 minutes, write down the required action and place it on one of these lists: Next Actions (separated by context: Computer, Calls, Outside, Home), Waiting For (delegated items with person, action, and date), Someday/Maybe, Agenda (items to discuss with people you meet regularly), Projects (multi-step serial actions). [gcw1]
4. Review cadence: Daily for Next Actions and Waiting For. Weekly for Someday/Maybe, Agenda, and Projects. [gcw1]
5. Keep a copy of your 10-Year Vision and quarterly OKRs on the Goals list (Mochary's addition). [gcw1]

"The key is to not have to think about what needs to be done again once the Next Action has been written down." [gcw1]

Mochary's adaptation for nighttime anxiety: "Keep a notepad by your bed. Write down whatever it is that you woke up thinking." Then faithfully follow GTD during the day so the mind trusts the system. [c2] The trust loop is critical -- "if you skip the weekly review, the whole system collapses within two weeks." [c1]

#### First 2 Minutes of Actions [4/19]
Sources: c1, c4, gcw1, er

This framework eliminates the gap between commitment and action, which Mochary identifies as the single largest source of productivity loss.

"If it takes less than 2 minutes: Do it immediately. If it takes more than 2 minutes: Estimate the time required. Create a calendar event with the action as the title." [c1]

Mochary traces the escalation of urgency through Frank Slootman's *Amp It Up*: In meetings, team members commit to deadlines like "I'll do this by next week." Slootman's challenge: "Why can't you get it done by tomorrow?" Mochary takes it further: "Why not right now?" [c1]

"The hardest part of any task is starting." [c1]

At the end of every meeting, do the first two minutes of each committed action right there. If it involves someone else and you already have a meeting with them, add it to that agenda. If not, message them now (if less than 2 minutes) or block calendar time. Nothing leaves as a floating to-do. [c1]

#### On Time [6/19]
Sources: c1, c3, gcw1, gcw2, er, ll

Mochary elevates punctuality from a courtesy to a moral obligation -- not through idealism, but through a specific reframe.

"Each minute they are away from their work is a minute of productivity you have stolen from them. This action is not only disrespectful but also counterproductive." [c1]

The framing is deliberate: not "wasting" time but "stealing" it. [c1] Practical implementation:
- The moment you realize you will be late, notify the other person immediately, before the meeting starts. [c1]
- Schedule 25- and 50-minute meetings to build natural buffers. [gcw1]
- During every meeting, phone goes face-down. No checking messages. [gcw1]
- "Being present means that you are composed, prepared, and focused on the subject matter. It can take a few minutes to 'get present' -- prepare the agenda, research the topic and the attendees." [c1] Arrive 15 minutes early for external meetings. End current work 5 minutes before internal ones. [c1]

#### Impeccable Agreements [6/19]
Sources: c1, c3, gcw1, gcw2, er, ll

"A very common cause of inefficiency in startups is sloppy agreements." [gcw1]

An impeccable agreement has two properties: "(1) precisely defined, and (2) fully agreed to (which almost always means written) by all relevant people." [gcw1] "Precisely defined" means "a successful follow-through of the agreement can be judged by an objective third party." [gcw1]

**The contrast:**
- Bad: "We'll start back up again after lunch." [gcw1]
- Good: "It is 12:04 pm now. We will start the meeting again at 1:00 pm. We all agree to be in our seats and present prior to 1:00 pm." [gcw1]

Consequences for breaking agreements are straightforward: "Apologies are not needed. All that is required is that they only make agreements that they can commit to, and that they meet all of the agreements they make." [gcw1] If someone continues to fail: "There is only one consequence that makes sense: They can no longer be part of the company." [gcw1]

Verbal agreements are not impeccable because "we all understand words a little bit differently." [c3] The standard must be written.

#### Writing vs. Talking (Written-First Culture) [7/19]
Sources: c1, c4, c8, gcw1, gcw2, lenny, naval

"If you want the most effective and efficient decision-making process, require that anyone who wants to discuss an issue write it up, along with the desired solution, ahead of time." [gcw1]

The efficiency case: "Team meetings that take 3 hours when done verbally can take 30 minutes (and be more effective) when done in writing." [gcw1]

The equity case: "Only the most forward people speak up and get heard." [gcw1] Written prep "ensures that everyone has a voice, not just those who are bold." [c4]

The durability case: "Whenever you find yourself saying something for a second time (to a second audience, or in a second situation), it is highly likely that you will end up saying it again and again in the future. To vastly improve the quality of the communication, and reduce the amount of time that you spend communicating it... write it down." [gcw1]

But writing alone is insufficient. "Good communication is not verbal only, and good communication is not written only. Really good communication is both written and verbal." [tim] Writing clarifies thinking and is faster to consume for facts. Speaking captures nuance and emotion. Both are required. [c4]

Mochary practices this himself: "I forget everything within 6 seconds of saying it. So I have to write things down." [twist] And: "Every time I found we'd come up with a solution, we'd implement it, it worked, and then the next CEO would come along and have that same exact problem, I would simply write up the solution." [ll]

#### Stories

**The Hospital Triage Room (Inbox Zero).** Mochary's most vivid productivity metaphor: your inbox is a triage room, not a waiting room. If you let it accumulate, someone will "sit down in a chair, and bleed out from his stab wound before you even realize he is there." [gcw1] The metaphor works because it reframes inbox management from an administrative chore to a life-or-death clinical practice.

**Frank Slootman's "Bias to Action" and Mochary's Escalation.** From *Amp It Up* by Frank Slootman: team members commit to "next week" deadlines. Slootman pushes to "tomorrow." Mochary pushes to "right now, in this meeting." [c1] This story sets up the First 2 Minutes framework and captures Mochary's philosophy that procrastination is the enemy, not complexity.

**The Brex Energy Audit.** Matt ran the energy audit with Enrique and Pedro from Brex. "It was revolutionary for them and it caused them to change how the two of them operated together. They realized one really enjoyed the internal meetings and the other really enjoyed the external meetings. So like, well, great, let's just do that." Then Brex asked Matt to run it with all their managers. [lenny] This illustrates how personal productivity extends into team redesign.

**Mochary's Own GTD Journey.** He tried every productivity system and settled on GTD because "it's the only one that handles the volume a CEO faces." [gcw1] The notepad-by-bed method and the insistence on weekly review come from his own experience: "If you skip the weekly review, the whole system collapses within two weeks." [c1]

**The Live Coaching of Tim Ferriss (Action Forcing).** In the Tim Ferriss interview, Matt demonstrates the complete productivity cycle in real time: deep acknowledgment of Tim's emotional state, driving toward a falsifiable prediction, converting to a single concrete action (Tim will ask three friends by Friday to organize group social activities), and Matt commits to following up. "That's the whole process." [tim] The action is always small, concrete, and time-bound.
