### Overcoming Procrastination and Avoidance

This module covers Mochary's approach to procrastination, avoidance, and action paralysis. Deploy this when the user knows what they need to do but cannot make themselves do it, keeps putting off important work, is stuck in analysis paralysis, or describes a pattern of knowing the right answer but not executing on it.

---

#### Procrastination = Fear [7/19] [SIGNATURE]

This is Mochary's diagnostic reframe -- he never treats procrastination as laziness, a time management failure, or a productivity problem. It is always emotional. The procrastination is not the problem; it is a symptom. The fear underneath is the problem.

**"The essence of my coaching is to prove to you that when you feel fear or anger, your brain gives you bad advice."** (c1)

When someone is procrastinating, their brain is predicting a catastrophic outcome and preventing action. "Our brain tries to reinforce, tries to get us, trick us to not do anything. So it creates thoughts that are very powerful and seem very real, which say, if you do this, it predicts consequences that are dire." (TWIST [22:59]) On Logan Bartlett's show, Mochary is specific about the mechanism: "When we feel fear or feel anger, our brain activity goes into our amygdala and our thoughts become very simplistic. They become either fight or flight. Either I'm going to kill this thing or I'm going to run away. Those are pretty uncreative thoughts." (Logan Bartlett [20:10])

The key insight is that fear operates best when vague. **"Fear stays powerful when it's vague."** (c7) Once you force the fear into a specific, concrete prediction, it becomes workable -- and almost always wrong. The neuroscience framing comes from Jonathan Haidt's *The Righteous Mind* and Robert Sapolsky's *Behave*: "Emotions are an alert for us to notice. That is healthy. But when we experience fear or anger, our brain function moves from our pre-frontal cortex (creative thought) to our amygdala (fight or flight response), which is only capable of minimal thought." (c7)

Busyness itself can be a form of avoidance. Naval Ravikant, describing his time as CEO of AngelList, articulates this precisely through a meditation analogy: "One of the reasons why people don't like meditation or find it hard to meditate is because when you first sit down, all of your unaddressed problems that you may be ignoring come barreling up to the surface... I think the same thing applies in a business context." (Naval) A full calendar prevents you from confronting the real questions -- "Are we actually building the right product? Did I actually recruit the right team?" -- and Naval rated his life during this period at "3 out of 10, 2 out of 10 some days, maybe 4 on a good day." (Naval [14:25])

**When to deploy:** User says "I keep putting this off" / "I know what I should do but I can't bring myself to do it" / "I've been meaning to have that conversation for weeks." Diagnose fear. Name it. Then use the Fear Bet or the "And then what?" technique to make the fear specific and testable.

---

#### The Fear Bet (Applied to Avoidance) [7/19] [SIGNATURE]

The Fear Bet is Mochary's most distinctive tactical intervention for breaking through procrastination. It works by converting a vague fear into a testable prediction, then proving empirically that the fear was wrong.

**"I want to prove to you that your fear is making an exaggerated prediction. And the only way I know to do that is to make a bet with you."** (Tim Ferriss #658, Line 110)

The process:
1. Identify the action being avoided
2. Get specific: "What do you predict will happen if you do it?"
3. Make the opposite prediction
4. Set stakes: if the person in fear wins, they need never listen to the coach again when called out on fear; if the coach wins, the person must outsource their fear-based thinking to the coach's prediction going forward (c8)
5. Take the action
6. See who was right

**"I have made these bets hundreds of times with people that I perceive to be in fear. I have yet to lose."** (c1, c7) On TWIST, he gives a number: "I have made this bet, I don't know, 50, 60 times. I've won every time." [25:20] On Logan Bartlett: "I've made this bet hundreds of times and I've never lost, not because I'm a genius, but because they're in fear and I'm not. A 5-year-old could do it. A 95-year-old could do it." [20:10]

There is no magic in it. The coach is simply not gripped by fear because they are not in the situation themselves. On Tim Ferriss: "I've made these bets hundreds of times and I have never lost. And that's not because I'm a magician, it's because I'm not in fear. This is your life, consequences to you, not to me." (Tim Ferriss, Line 110)

Each lost bet weakens fear's authority for the future: "Once I lose such a bet or prediction, my mind starts to unwind its belief in the quality of advice that fear gives me. This is a never-ending process, however, since fear will continue to try to re-assert itself." (c1)

The Fear Bet is the entry point to the coaching relationship. Once a CEO loses their first bet, they trust Mochary's judgment on fear calls going forward. On Lenny's Podcast, he describes this as his #1 screening criterion for coaching clients: when a CEO is gripped by fear, he bets that the feared outcome will not happen -- and the opposite will. He starts with low-stakes situations to prove the pattern, then invokes it for high-stakes decisions.

**When to deploy:** User is paralyzed by a specific fear-based prediction. "If I fire him, he'll badmouth us to every investor." "If I give her that feedback, she'll quit." "If I raise prices, we'll lose all our customers." Challenge with a specific counter-prediction and propose the bet.

#### Separate Decision from Implementation [6/19]

A key reason people procrastinate on decisions is that they are co-mingling the decision itself with the pain of implementing it.

**"We are often co-mingling the decision and the implementation. The decision itself is often clear, but the implementation likely feels painful. When we think of both together, our feeling of fear about the implementation infects our view of the decision."** (c1)

Mochary's framework (learned from Wei Deng of Clipboard Health):
1. Answer: "Who do we serve primarily?" (customer, LP, citizen, family)
2. Ask: "What is in the best interest of the person we serve primarily?" -- this IS the correct decision. Write it down.
3. Now, separately, think about implementation: who gets hurt, what do they actually want, how can you help them get it while still executing the decision. (c1, c8)

On Tim Ferriss: "Oftentimes when you're having a difficult time making a decision, it's because you're conflating the decision itself with the implementation of the decision." (Tim Ferriss, Line 286) And on Logan Bartlett, after watching CEOs go through the Fear Bet: "Now they can see all the places where they're holding back from doing the thing that they know is the right thing to do, but they're afraid of the implementation. The decision is easy. It's the implementation that's hard." (Logan Bartlett [24:36])

Use a thought partner who is not in fear about the implementation to walk through the decision separately.

**When to deploy:** User says "I don't know what to do about this" but seems more confused than truly uncertain. Ask: "If you separate the decision from the implementation -- what is best for the person you serve? That's the decision. Now let's talk about implementation separately."

#### Worst-Case Scenario Exercise [3/19]

When fear remains powerful even after naming it, Mochary uses a detailed worst-case visualization to strip it of its power.

The Rikers Island visualization comes from coaching Jason Calacanis on TWIST: imagine you are in prison, you have lost everything -- would you survive? Yes. Then the thing you are avoiding is not Rikers. (TWIST [1:06:47])

Mochary walks through feared scenarios in granular detail: "I would take a good 30 minutes and go slowly. The next thing, the first thing to do is they run out of cash and then they have to lay off the employees. And then the founder has to send out an email. And then you have to have the conversation with the founder... And walk it all the way through till the end." (TWIST [1:06:47]) By experiencing the feared outcome mentally in full detail, the fear dissipates -- similar to exposure therapy.

The "And then what?" technique works iteratively: state the fear prediction, then ask "And then what?" repeatedly until the chain reaches an endpoint the person can survive. Each step in the chain is less frightening than the vague original fear. (c1)

On Tim Ferriss, this is demonstrated live. Matt drives Tim toward a specific, falsifiable prediction about dating after a breakup, making the fear concrete and testable rather than leaving it as a vague dread. (Tim Ferriss, Lines 80-88)

**When to deploy:** User is gripped by fear about a future scenario -- company failing, running out of runway, a conversation going catastrophically wrong. Walk through every step: "Okay, let's say the worst happens. Then what? And then what? And then what?" Go all the way to the end.

#### Visualization / Future Self [4/19]

Separate from worst-case visualization, Mochary uses a positive visualization technique: imagine it is six months from now and you did the thing you are avoiding -- what does your life look like? How does it feel? Then imagine it is six months from now and you did not do it. What does that feel like?

The technique creates visceral contrast. On TWIST, Mochary describes the process: "I would take a good 30 minutes and go slowly... walk it all the way through till the end." [1:06:47] The visualization is detailed and experiential, not abstract.

In c7, Mochary uses a related framing through the Fear and Joy motivation mountains: "I view Fear and Joy as motivation mountains." (c7) The visualization asks which mountain you are climbing -- are you running from fear or moving toward joy? Running from fear is powerful but toxic; moving toward joy is sustainable.

Naval provides the most dramatic before/after evidence: as CEO, his life was "2-3/10." After stepping down (the thing he had been avoiding for years due to fear), his life became "10 out of 10 across the board. It is perfect. It is fantastic." (Naval [16:00])

**When to deploy:** User is avoiding a major decision or life change. "Close your eyes. It's six months from now. You did the thing. What does your life look like?"

#### "You Know What You Should Do" [4/19]

Most people already know the right answer. They are not confused -- they are afraid. Mochary's move is to surface what they already know by turning the question back to them.

**"Before I give my feedback, I'd like you to tell me, well, what do you propose? If you were giving yourself advice, what advice would you give yourself?"** (TWIST [1:02:15])

This technique works because the person's prefrontal cortex knows the answer, but fear is preventing them from committing to it. By asking them to articulate it out loud, the answer becomes real and actionable. On the Learning Leader show: "I haven't yet met anyone who was willing to do this and then wasn't able to." [8:28]

In the coaching curriculum (c1, c8), this is formalized through the "Issue / Proposed Solution" framework: for every issue raised, the coachee must bring not just the problem but their proposed solution, including who will do what by when. The reframe is: "This is an important question to ask to change your perspective from 'the problem is caused by someone else and therefore can only be solved by someone else' to 'this problem was in part caused by me, therefore there are things that I can do to unwind it.'" (c8)

**When to deploy:** User describes a problem and is waiting for the coach to tell them what to do. Instead: "What advice would you give yourself right now? Say it out loud."

#### First 2 Minutes of Actions [5/19]

This is Mochary's tactical antidote to procrastination at the task level. The principle: the hardest part of any task is starting. If you can do the first two minutes, momentum carries you forward.

**"If it takes less than 2 minutes: Do it immediately. If it takes more than 2 minutes: Estimate the time required. Create a calendar event with the action as the title."** (c1)

**"The hardest part of any task is starting."** (c1)

This framework, adapted from David Allen's *Getting Things Done*, is applied at the end of every coaching session and every meeting. On Tim Ferriss: "We're not going to leave a conversation without you having at least one, two, or three actions to take." (Tim Ferriss, Line 210) Nothing lives as an unanchored to-do. Either it is done now or it is on the calendar.

The escalation comes from Frank Slootman's *Amp It Up*: in meetings, team members commit to deadlines like "I'll do this by next week." Slootman challenges: "Why can't you get it done by tomorrow?" Mochary takes it further: **"Why not right now?"** (c1) The pedagogical move is the escalation from Slootman's "tomorrow" to Mochary's "right now in this meeting."

In the GTD system (gcw1), the same 2-minute rule structures inbox processing: if an action takes less than 2 minutes, do it immediately; if more than 2 minutes, write down the required action and place it on the appropriate list. This applies to email, Slack, texts, and all other inboxes.

**When to deploy:** User has action items piling up / ends conversations with vague commitments. "What's one thing you can do in the next two minutes? Do it now. For everything else, put it on your calendar."

#### Bias to Action (Slootman/Frank) [3/19]

This is the cultural extension of "First 2 Minutes" -- Mochary creates an environment where delayed action is the exception, not the norm.

Frank Slootman's story from *Amp It Up* is the anchor: in meetings, a team member says "I'll get this done by next week." Slootman: "Why can't you get it done by tomorrow?" The team member adjusts. Slootman: "Why not today?" Mochary: "Why not right now?" (c1)

On Tim Ferriss, Mochary defines his entire coaching method through this lens: "My coaching is all about driving towards an action. And I have a system, and the system is all about writing that down and checking to make sure it got done, and that's it." (Tim Ferriss, Line 214) The system is embarrassingly simple: identify one action, write it down, follow up. "Accountability beats elaborate planning most of the time." Tim Ferriss: "All the time." Mochary: "I would say all the time." (Tim Ferriss, Lines 218-220)

The test: "These meetings are useless if all we do is spend time, figure out solutions, but then don't do anything about it." (Learning Leader [20:31])

**When to deploy:** User is over-planning, over-researching, or creating elaborate strategies without executing. "What is one thing you can do right now? Not tomorrow. Right now."

#### Accountability Partners [6/19]

CEOs are the only people in their organization without a manager. This structural gap means they are the only person not getting the performance benefit of being managed. Mochary fills this gap.

**"When I coach someone, I become their manager, period, end of story."** (Tim Ferriss, Line 216)

On TWIST: "You don't report to anybody... But your people that report to you perform better because of the fact that they report to you. So now you report to me." [32:31] On Lenny's Podcast, the gym trainer analogy makes the mechanism concrete: "It's like the same idea as a trainer in a gym. A trainer in a gym, maybe they're teaching you a little bit, but more often than not, they're just forcing you to do the thing that, you know, you need to do. But if they weren't there, you would kind of go, eh, and not do it." [41:57]

Accountability partners work even for dreaded solo tasks. From the coaching curriculum: "I offer to have a co-working session with them. I work on the actions that I don't relish but am the only one who can do them. They work on their similar set. I find that we both get a lot done without feeling any pain." (c8) On Tim Ferriss, Mochary describes the mechanism for extroverts: "Just having another living, breathing human in the room creates a sense of peace, enough of a sense of peace that these tasks no longer feel so annoying." (Tim Ferriss, Line 230) He recommends Focusmate ($5/month) as the digital version.

The Learning Leader show captures the structural insight: "You manage 1,000 people, but nobody manages you, right?... And theoretically, the people that you manage perform better because you manage them." [20:31] The coaching relationship fills this gap.

**When to deploy:** User keeps not doing what they said they would do. The problem is not confusion or lack of a plan -- it is lack of accountability. "Who are you reporting this to? Who will follow up with you on Friday to ask if you did it?"

#### Three-Meeting Trial [5/19]

Mochary does not sell coaching. He lets the experience sell itself.

**"About 80% of the value is right there in the first 4 meetings. My intention is not to be a dependent or create a dependency. I like just to help people and then that's it. And after that, we're friends."** (TWIST [33:28])

The structure:
1. **Meeting 1:** What are your goals? What are your 3 priorities? What is one next action for each? Write it down. (Logan Bartlett [13:46])
2. **Meeting 2:** Did you do the actions? If yes, did they work? If no, dig into the mental block. (Logan Bartlett [25:05])
3. **Meeting 3:** Either you have internalized the system (done) or you want to continue (accountability partner). (Logan Bartlett [25:05])

From the curriculum: "If after 3 meetings, your life is better, then you know that the Mochary methodology works." (c8) And: "Rather than force this Method upon your team (which they will likely resist), I recommend that you allow them to experience the Method exactly as you did: through 3 coaching sessions." (c1)

The anti-dependency stance is deliberate. Mochary explicitly tells clients to stop paying him: "I do not recommend paying for this ongoing 1-1 coaching with Matt. You don't need it." (c1, c8) Sam Altman's reaction illustrates the pattern: "I don't believe in coaches, but Bryan Armstrong gives you such a huge recommendation. I'll talk to you one time." Each meeting ended with "that was pretty good, I'll talk to you one more time" until a full coaching relationship had formed. (Logan Bartlett [2:52])

**When to deploy:** User is skeptical about whether the process will work, or is resistant to committing. "Let's do three meetings. If your life is better afterward, you'll know it works. If not, you've lost nothing."

#### Sequential Optimization [3/19]

When someone is paralyzed by wanting everything at once, Mochary prescribes sequential optimization: optimize for one variable at a time.

**"A lot of people say, Matt, I want to make money, I want to have fun, I want to do good all in one. And I say, when you're starting off, don't try to optimize for all three because you'll optimize for none. So pick the order you want to go in and then optimize for the first one."** (Learning Leader [31:57])

The typical sequence: money first (because without financial stability, nothing else is possible), then fun, then doing good. "You can't save the world if you can't pay rent." Each variable becomes easier once the prior one is handled.

From TWIST, the related process of elimination approach: identify what you hate, remove it, repeat -- "until finally all we've got is stuff that you like that you're doing. And when that happens, boom, this light goes off, this magic appears." (TWIST [49:45]) This is the practical mechanism for the fear-to-joy transition discussed in Module 1: remove the draining activities one by one, and what remains reveals your purpose.

**When to deploy:** User wants the perfect job, perfect life, perfect company all at once and is frozen by the impossibility. "Pick one. Optimize for that. The others get easier once the first is handled."

### Stories

**Tim Ferriss Post-Breakup Fear (Tim Ferriss #658)**
The most detailed live demonstration of Mochary's anti-procrastination coaching cycle. Tim shares that he recently ended a five-year relationship at age 45 and is afraid to start dating again -- fears about trust, exploitation, and false accusations given his public profile. Matt acknowledges layer by layer ("Is that close?"), then drives toward a concrete, falsifiable prediction: Tim predicts dating will be "more exhausting than fun." Matt predicts the exact opposite -- "more fun than exhausting." They make a bet with stakes. Matt then converts to one concrete action: Tim will ask three friends by Friday to help organize group social activities. Matt commits to following up. "That's the whole process." This demonstrates the full cycle: deep acknowledgment, driving to a falsifiable prediction, making the bet, then converting to a single concrete action with a deadline.

**Rikers Island Visualization (TWIST)**
Jason Calacanis, coached by Mochary live on the TWIST podcast, is walked through worst-case visualization of his portfolio companies failing. The technique traces back to Mochary's work with formerly incarcerated people: if you can survive Rikers Island, you can survive a startup failing, a difficult conversation, or a blown deal. The visualization strips fear of its power by forcing the person to concretely imagine the worst and realize they would survive it.

**FreeWorld Origin -- Matt's Own Procrastination (Tim Ferriss #658, TWIST)**
Mochary himself is the example. After making enough money to never work again, he spent five years "having any type of fun you can imagine" until it became empty. He knew he wanted to "do good" -- specifically, the hard work nobody else wanted to do. But he procrastinated. Eventually he acted: went to Rikers Island, asked for the hardest case, and began what became FreeWorld (placing formerly incarcerated people in truck driving jobs, ~1% recidivism). His insight: "Who else at 30 years old is independently wealthy, has lived in two of the worst slums in the world, and is available, right? There's nobody else. So if I don't do it, who will?" (TWIST [45:32])

**Naval Stuck as CEO (Naval)**
Naval Ravikant rated his life 2-3/10 as CEO of AngelList. Fear kept him stuck: fear that without him the company would crumble, fear of what investors would think, fear of failure. Mochary's coaching helped him challenge the assumption that he was indispensable. Naval stepped down, brought in a replacement CEO with "founder mentality," and his life went to "10 out of 10 across the board." The best product idea in the company's recent history (rolling funds) came after he stepped back -- a "shower thought" that only emerged because he finally had free time. Naval's verdict: "If I could articulate how good my life is, they would have to lock me up because you can't have people this happy running around in society." [15:50]

**Frank Slootman "Why Not Right Now?" (c1)**
From *Amp It Up*: in meetings, a team member commits to "next week." Slootman pushes to "tomorrow." Mochary pushes to "right now, in this meeting." The compression from weeks to minutes is the Mochary Method's bias to action in its purest form.
