### Product Development and Engineering

This module covers how Mochary coaches CEOs through product strategy, finding product-market fit, building new products inside scaled companies, managing engineering teams, and increasing development velocity. Product development is not Mochary's primary domain -- his core expertise is leadership, emotional regulation, and management systems -- but he has synthesized distinctive frameworks from three practitioners whose thinking deeply influenced him: Marty Cagan (Inspired), Rahul Vohra (Superhuman), and Wei Deng (Clipboard Health). The engineering philosophy comes primarily from Aaron White (Blissfully/Vendr). What makes Mochary's product/engineering guidance distinctive is that it sits atop his emotional and organizational operating system: customer discovery uses the same "repeat-back" listening technique he applies to feedback and conflict resolution, product decisions follow the same "separate decision from implementation" framework, and engineering speed is treated as a function of talent density and energy management, not process.

#### Customer Discovery as an Ongoing Practice [5/19]
Sources: c9, gcw1, gcw2, er, lenny

Customer discovery is not a phase -- it is a permanent operating discipline. Mochary treats it as the foundation of all product work and applies his universal listening framework directly to customer conversations.

> "There are many ways to create a company, but only one good one: To deeply understand real customers (living humans!) and their problem, and then solve that problem." (gcw1)

> "To live the customer problem, you must sit with the customer, ask them about their lives, and observe their daily routine, on a regular and constant basis." (gcw2, Ch. 17)

> "If you actively listen to your customers' pain, they will trust you to decide which solution will best erase that pain. If you do not listen to your customers' pain, then they will do the thinking and make demands about what the solutions should be, no matter how impractical." (gcw2, Ch. 17)

The customer discovery process uses the same repeat-back technique Mochary deploys everywhere else in his method:

> Talk to lots and lots and lots of potential customers. Ask: "What are your core problems and frustrations related to [your area]?" Repeat back to each: "I think I heard you say..." then "Is that right?" -- keep repeating until "Yes." (c9)

From the EnterpriseReady interview, Mochary offers his Pain-Wallet-Gap framework for selecting which customer segment to serve:

> "Pick the group that has the most pain, the biggest wallet able to pay for the solution to that pain, and the least good solution available to them today. And then I simply solve their pain for them." (er, [49:45])

A critical warning against building without discovery:

> "When I hear 'We have built a new product and are ready to launch it, but we haven't yet figured out how to position it to customers,' I know that the Product Development process has gone far off the rails." (c9)

> "If you have built a product, and don't yet know exactly which individual decision-makers have the problem that this product solves, then you have done things in reverse order." (c9)

The customer-first sequence: (1) discover customer problems via conversations, (2) segment customers and problems into buckets, (3) pick your beachhead -- the segment with the most customers, the most money to spend, the worst current alternatives, and the easiest for you to implement, (4) build a product that solves that segment's problem, (5) positioning becomes obvious: "State the problem that the customer has. Then... 'If you have this problem, we have your solution.'" (c9)

Mochary also references April Dunford's positioning framework (via Regina Gerbeaux): position against the current status quo, identify differentiators, find 2-3 value themes, define best-fit customers, and select the market category that makes your value obvious. (c9)

#### Product-Market Fit Signals and Timing [4/19]
Sources: c9, gcw1, gcw2, er

Mochary is emphatic about not scaling before achieving genuine product-market fit, a mistake he considers one of the most common and most deadly.

> "Startups don't usually fail because they grow too late. They usually fail because they grow too early (ie- before they have achieved Product Market Fit)." (gcw1)

> "I STRONGLY RECOMMEND not going beyond 6 team members before you have PM Fit." (gcw2, Ch. 30)

> "One of the bigger mistakes that young companies make is thinking that they have PM Fit before they really do." (gcw2, Ch. 30)

> "Do not try to scale... until you have proof that customers believe your solution is indeed better." (gcw2, Ch. 30)

The correct signal is pull, not push: customers actively seeking you out, angry when the product is down, telling others about it without being asked. Mochary's beachhead strategy aligns with this: find a small segment with "a particularly difficult problem for whom your solution is indeed 10x better than the broad non-specific legacy solution" (gcw2, Ch. 30).

From the Rahul Vohra / Superhuman framework (cited in c9): focus on users who "truly love your product and would feel great sadness if they no longer had access to it. They are your power users and evangelists. Double down on them, and those like them!"

> "Rather than try to dumb this product down into something that's generic like everything else, why don't I just focus on the people who have a problem that only I can solve and then go find many more people with that same problem?" -- Rahul Vohra, via c9

The Slack model for controlled scaling also appears in the EnterpriseReady interview:

> "I wanna copy the Slack model. And what Slack did, at least my understanding is, is that they had a limited number of users for quite a long time... and just kept iterating and making the product better and better and better. Didn't have to worry about DevOps, didn't have to worry about security." (er, [14:15])

#### Innovation Teams / Internal C-Corps (Wei Deng Model) [4/19]
Sources: c9, lenny, er, ll

This is one of Mochary's most distinctive product frameworks. When a scaled company needs to build new products, the main organization's immune system will kill innovation unless you create structural separation.

> "Achieving product-market fit for a new product when you only have 2-3 people in your company (i.e., a YC startup) is hard. Doing it when your company has hundreds of people is damn near impossible." (c9)

> "The only way to successfully build a new product within a scaled company is to recreate the early-stage building mode." (c9)

The core tension: as companies scale, their DNA shifts from experimentation to brand protection. Scaled-company DNA (protect, harden, stabilize) is fundamentally incompatible with new-product DNA (experiment, break, iterate). The solution is not cultural -- it is structural:

> For each new product team: (1) Create a team reporting directly to the CEO, outside EPD. (2) Create a new corporate entity (C-corp, 100% owned by parent company). (3) Give it a new name and brand. (c9)

> "The separate company doesn't really add anything functional but it adds great symbolism... The team feels like they're a true YC start-up with three to five members per team, someone who's designated as the CEO, and they have their own entity (bank account, etc)." (c9)

Wei Deng at Clipboard Health added a distinctive twist -- competing parallel teams:

> "I created 5 C-corps in the last 2 months." -- Wei Deng, via lenny [54:30-58:37]

> Two competing teams per product: one engineering-focused, one customer-relationship-focused using manual/off-the-shelf solutions. "I just see which one makes more progress faster." -- Wei Deng, via lenny

A critical lesson from Microsoft: initially, only the "winning" team was rewarded. People became reluctant to join product teams for fear of being on the losing side. Both Microsoft and Wei Deng now reward both teams for effort: "It turns out fear of losing is stronger than joy from winning." (c9)

On autonomy vs. equity as motivator for innovation teams:

> "I don't think it matters at all. I think that what really motivates people is building shit that gets used in the world... They have ownership, not equity ownership, ownership over decision-making, ownership over creation. That's what I think people want." (lenny, [59:10])

#### The Two-Step Moat [2/19]
Sources: er, gcw1

Mochary's moat framework goes beyond single defensible advantages:

> "You create a two-step process that is basically impossible to replicate." (er, [53:04])

The model: build a base product that gives you a unique asset (e.g., a hospital platform that provides access to doctor eyeballs), then use that asset to serve a premium customer segment no one else can reach (e.g., pharmaceutical companies who need those eyeballs). The moat is the compound step, not either step alone.

> "Here's a really easy way to create a moat. You have a base product that works, and then you go talk to these customers and realize... they have a problem that I can solve, but you could only solve it if you had a platform in hospitals." (er, [53:04])

This connects to the ongoing customer discovery practice: the two-step moat emerges from continuously asking not just "who are my current customers?" but "who do I WANT as my customers?" after initial PMF is achieved.

#### Speed Over Polish in Product Development [5/19]
Sources: c9, ll, er, lenny, gcw2

Mochary's product development philosophy, informed by Marty Cagan, centers on one principle: get to user feedback as fast as possible.

> "User feedback is far more valuable than design. So, skip design and get to user feedback as fast as you can." (c9)

The "New Way" of product development (per Marty Cagan, via c9):
1. Talk to customers, find the top common problem with the least good solution.
2. Build a prototype and get it into real users' hands in days. "Don't waste time designing the solution. Instead, slap together a quick and dirty prototype and get it in the hands of users for their reactions."
3. Iterate -- another prototype in another few days.
4. "Keep iterating like this until users are loving the prototype. Then build that!"

The key infrastructure requirement:

> "The key is to create a prototyping space that isn't connected to your production site, so that the team building your main product doesn't require the code to be stable before releasing it." (c9)

From the Learning Leader interview, Aaron White (Blissfully/Vendr) provides the sharpest articulation of why speed matters more than the current product:

> "When you create software, you actually haven't done anything. All you've really done is create a prototype for the rest of the world to copy... So really what you need is not to create software, but you need to create the fastest software development team on the planet." (ll, [15:17])

#### The Master Builder Model for Engineering [3/19]
Sources: c9, gcw1, gcw2

Mochary's engineering philosophy is deliberately contrarian: the instinct to hire more engineers and get a VP of Engineering to manage them is often wrong.

> "Your engineering team is not producing fast enough. And what they are producing is laden with bugs that need to be fixed. How to correct this? The answer seems obvious: hire more engineers and get a VP of Engineering that knows how to actually manage them. Right?! Oftentimes ... wrong!" (c9)

The master builder analogy: a senior developer who assembles the full system -- foundation, floors, walls, roof, electrical, plumbing -- knows how everything interconnects. Adding junior builders creates overhead that can be net negative:

> "If a junior builder gets added to the team, he will be given a task (like building a wall) that needs to be reviewed, which takes the master builder's time. That review might reveal something wrong that needs to be fixed, which takes more of the master builder's time." (c9)

> "Much more insidiously, the master-builder may well find the process of reviewing and fixing other people's work draining, leaving her with far less energy to create herself." (c9)

> "Having junior team members may be net negative to overall productivity." (c9)

The prescription: hire only senior developers whose code is clean and requires little review. "While they cost 3x, they actually produce the proverbial 10x... mainly because the junior engineers produce close to net zero." (c9)

For junior engineers, create a separate training program: "Do not sap the energy and time of your senior developers with this process. Have it happen off to the side, where the teachers are only those who actually derive energy from the process of teaching and mentoring." (c9)

The analogy to professional sports: "Major League Baseball has the same issue. For that reason, they have farm teams. But you don't see the star major league player being required to mentor the minor league players. Don't ask yours to, either." (c9)

#### Rubber Duck Channels (Aaron White / Blissfully) [2/19]
Sources: c9, ll

Aaron White's system at Blissfully (later acquired by Vendr specifically because of engineering productivity) provides Mochary's most concrete framework for engineering excellence.

> "One of the primary tenets of creating excellence in an engineering org is speed. Speed from idea to prototype. Speed from getting stuck on a problem to getting help. Speed from having feedback to give to actually giving that feedback. Speed from realizing that someone doesn't meet the talent bar to letting them go (with kindness)." (c9)

The Rubber Duck Channel system:
1. Each engineer gets their own Slack channel.
2. They write out engineering problems they face -- often this leads to the solution by itself (the classic "rubber ducking" practice made public).
3. Senior engineers can quickly see when others are stuck and offer suggestions.
4. The whole team learns from the most senior engineers' problem-solving approaches.
5. Those who don't use the channels don't get immediate help and often struggle to keep up.
6. Anyone who struggles receives feedback within 24 hours. If they don't fully repair within 2 weeks, Aaron fires them well -- often within days.

The system serves as a 3-in-1 tool: problem-solving, mentorship, and performance evaluation all happen through the same mechanism. (c9)

> Aaron White feels he "knows within 2 weeks whether someone will be a great addition to the team. All that are not, he lets go within days." (c9)

> "Aaron is the founder and CEO of Blissfully, which was purchased by Vendr, because Ryan Neu (CEO of Vendr) deemed Blissfully to be one of the most, if not the most, productive engineering orgs Ryan had ever seen." (c9)

#### Fewer People, Better Output [5/19]
Sources: lenny, er, logan, c9, gcw1

Mochary's belief that fewer people produce better absolute output applies directly to product and engineering teams:

> "With fewer people in the organization, things work better. That's the big realization that most people never discover." (lenny)

> "Each additional person, they just require time and overhead. Time to get informed about the issue, time to be heard about their opinions, time to share the problems that they're experiencing and feel cared about. Just time. And the fewer people you have, the less consensus you need, the less it just goes quicker." (logan, [1:01:05])

> "I think it's almost an intellectual laziness that we go through and just say, let's just hire bodies." (er, [8:46])

> "To date it hasn't been done of keeping a team less than 20 people and having it be worth $10 billion, but it doesn't mean that it can't exist. And I think it's just no one's tried. So I'm going to try." (er, [8:46])

This connects directly to the Master Builder Model: the instinct to throw more engineers at a slow codebase is almost always wrong. A small team of senior developers, each operating in their zone of genius, will outperform a larger team weighed down by coordination overhead and junior-engineer review cycles.

### Stories

**The Hospital-to-Pharma Pivot (Two-Step Moat)** -- from er, c9. A unicorn company built an AI platform for hospitals but discovered hospitals are "mostly not-for-profit... really slow bureaucratic decision-making process. Most of them are near bankrupt." CEO asked: who do I WANT as customers? Pharmaceutical companies -- "private, make decisions instantly, and they have gobs of money." The insight: to solve pharma's problem, you need eyeballs of every doctor in the country. "You know what? I've got that because I have this platform in hospitals." Pharma companies prepaid for the solution. "Six months ago, he thought he'd never get past $2-3b in value. Now he's confident he'll get to $100b." *Use when:* a company has initial PMF but is serving low-value customers, or when someone is stuck thinking about their existing product rather than what their platform uniquely enables.

**Wei Deng's Five C-Corps** -- from lenny, c9, ll. Matt wrote up his internal-startup theory but no one was implementing it. Then Wei Deng told him: "Oh yeah, that's what I do. I created 5 C-corps in the last 2 months." She added the twist of two competing teams per product -- one engineering-focused, one customer-relationship-focused. "I just see which one makes more progress faster." One of the C-corps was Billterms: "It works! Customers aren't trying to push requests about the base Clipboard product, because they aren't talking to Clipboard, they're talking to Billterms." *Use when:* a scaled company is trying to innovate but new products keep dying in the main org, or when a CEO asks how to create "startup energy" inside a large company.

**Aaron White's Blissfully -- The Most Productive Engineering Org** -- from c9, ll. Aaron White built Blissfully with a radical approach: Rubber Duck channels, talent density (firing within days if someone doesn't meet the bar), and speed as the meta-metric. Ryan Neu, CEO of Vendr, acquired Blissfully specifically because he considered it "one of the most, if not the most, productive engineering orgs" he had ever seen. Aaron's insight: "When you create software, you actually haven't done anything. All you've really done is create a prototype for the rest of the world to copy. So really what you need is not to create software, but you need to create the fastest software development team on the planet." *Use when:* an engineering team feels slow, or when a CEO is considering hiring more engineers rather than improving the existing team's velocity.

**Wee Deng's Competing Teams Validation** -- from ll. Wee Deng suspected her engineering team was underperforming but couldn't prove it ("I'm not an engineer. I couldn't figure it out"). She hired an entirely new small team to do the exact same work. "Sure enough, the new smaller team way outperformed the old big team. And then she knew." She expanded to 3 parallel teams. The original team's performance improved dramatically "simply because they see what the others are doing that's working and they simply copy it." *Use when:* a non-technical founder suspects engineering underperformance but lacks the domain expertise to evaluate quality directly.
