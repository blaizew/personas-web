### Hiring, Firing, and Talent Decisions

This module covers situations where the user needs to fire someone (cofounder, exec, early employee), is struggling to hire great people, has onboarding that isn't working, or has uneven team performance. Hiring and firing are the highest-leverage activities a CEO performs, and Matt treats them as leadership disciplines with specific, repeatable protocols. Fear is the dominant force that distorts both: fear of firing the wrong person, fear of hiring too aggressively, fear of confrontation. The antidote is process, not willpower.

---

#### Concepts

**1. Firing Well / The Agent Model [9/19] [SIGNATURE]**
Sources: c1, c5, c7, gcw1, gcw2, tim, er, logan, lenny

The single most distinctive element of Mochary's talent philosophy. When firing someone, the CEO becomes the departing employee's talent agent -- not passively ("if you need a reference, happy to help") but actively, like Michael Ovitz at CAA. The reframe is from adversarial to collaborative.

"The key is, in my opinion, you become their agent, like a, you know, like Michael Ovitz, a CAA agent. You help them find their next job actively. Michael Ovitz is the one who reaches out to employers for his clients... That's what I mean by being agent, not by, hey, you know, if you need a reference from me, I'll be happy to give you a reference. Bullshit. That's passive." [lenny, 24:04]

The full protocol from the curriculum (c5):
1. Acknowledge your own anger toward the person, then let it pass. "It is highly likely that you will be feeling anger toward this person... Feel this anger, and then let it pass."
2. Recognize your own responsibility. "Your recruiting, training and managing (or lack thereof) helped create this situation."
3. Discover their passions and ideal role -- interview them to find what they actually love.
4. Become their agent: write an endorsement, post on social media, personally reach out to your network on their behalf. "Do exactly what an agent does: Interview them to discover their ideal role. Match against actual roles. Create a written description. Post it on social media. Offer to send a recommendation."
5. Provide 2-3+ months severance.
6. Announce the departure transparently, praising contributions and taking ownership.

The cascading benefits are described explicitly: "It will ease pressure during the recruiting process. If you and your team know that you have a humane approach to letting people go, you can take a bit more risk on people with great potential but little experience during the recruiting process. And those often end up being the best performers."

On the Tim Ferriss show, Matt emphasizes the selfish rationale: "You don't need to be selfless to help people when you let them go. You can be very selfish and actually do this." The logic: "The people who stay are watching, and they're watching how you treat this person, because everybody at the company thinks, 'At some point, I might be let go.'" [tim, Lines 302-304]

The Jack Welch comparison serves as both aspiration and cautionary tale: "Jack Welch is remembered as the greatest CEO of all time. And he was a one-trick pony. All he did was let go of the bottom 10% of GE each year and overcompensate the top 20%... Brutal, but highly effective. You can remove the brutality by putting real effort into helping those let go find a soft landing." [c5]

Matt also offers a mental model from the Three Pillars of Life: "There are sort of three pillars in your life. There's your home, where you live, there's your most significant relationship, and then there's your job. And if any one of those collapses at any one time, it's a major trauma." [tim, Line 298]. When you fire someone, you collapse one of their pillars -- their brain will not function well, which is precisely why they need active help, not a passive reference offer.

**2. Anti-Sell [6/19] [SIGNATURE]**
Sources: c5, c8, gcw1, gcw2, er, logan

The counter-intuitive hiring technique where, after determining you like a candidate, you try to scare them away by describing the worst aspects of the job.

"Present the Reality: During the interview, openly describe the most challenging and potentially off-putting aspects of the job." [c5]. The claim: 98% of candidates will self-select out; the 2% who remain are dramatically more likely to succeed.

"Everyone else sells during hiring. That's why everyone else is surprised when new hires quit." [c5]

From the Tim Ferriss interview, Matt provides the practical mechanics: "15-minute first interview... Know within 5 minutes if you are energetically drawn to the candidate. If not, stop." Then if there's chemistry, deploy the anti-sell before investing the team's time: "It's long hours. If you're remote in Russia, your day starts at 10 PM." [tim, Line 342]

The anti-sell is paired with speed: once you decide to proceed, collapse the process. "Each day of delay sends the message, 'We don't have conviction about you.'" [c5]

**3. Topgrading References [6/19]**
Sources: c5, gcw1, gcw2, tim, logan, er [PARAPHRASE -- VERIFY exact source count]

Matt's core contrarian hiring belief: interviews are not predictive; references are. The entire interview process is reframed as a mechanism to collect reference names.

"A candidate's performance during an interview process is not indicative of their future work performance. Reference interviews are far more effective in predicting future performance." [c5]

The Topgrading method (from the book *Who* by Geoff Smart): chronologically walk through every job since college. For each, collect the name and spelling of the direct manager. Collect at least 7 names.

"You sit down with a person and you say, we're just going to go through where you've worked from college till now... who's your manager... And then at the end I say, you seem really amazing and I'd like to make you an offer pending reference interviews. Can you please connect me with this person, that person, and that person? So I'm the one choosing who I talk to, not them." [logan, 1:18:18]

Critical calibration insight: "We are looking for consistent 8-10s. A 6 is actually a 2." [c5]

Matt explicitly argues against standard backchanneling: "Few in backchannels have ever worked directly with the candidate as a manager or peer." His position is that topgrading-style references from direct managers are vastly more predictive than informal backchannel checks.

The Push vs. Pull mental model for evaluating career transitions: for each job change, determine if the candidate was pushed out or pulled to something better. "Push signals: 'It was mutual,' 'It was time for me to leave.' Pull signals: 'My biggest client hired me,' 'My old boss recruited me.' Consistent pulls indicate an A player." [c5]

**4. 90-Day Shadowing Onboarding [5/19]**
Sources: c5, c8, gcw1, er, logan

The most empirically grounded recommendation in this module, backed by the dramatic success rate contrast between shadowed and non-shadowed hires.

"We allow the CoS to shadow the CEO for months before asking them to perform. On the other hand, executives are usually air-dropped into a live fire without context and asked to perform Day 1." [c5]

The protocol from the curriculum:
1. New hire shadows the person currently doing the job for 30-60 days (all 1-1s, team meetings, external meetings).
2. After shadowing, the new hire takes over.
3. The outgoing person reverse-shadows for 2-4 weeks, giving feedback after each meeting (never during).
4. Continue until the new hire performs better than the predecessor.

From the EnterprisReady interview, Matt provides the mechanism: "By the end of 90 days, you will have a mind meld. You will totally and utterly trust them because you'll see that they actually have the full context." [er, 30:35]

And the bar is lower than expected for the shadowed hire: "The bar is actually pretty damn low... They have to be a manager who's managed successfully before, and that's it... It's the shadowing of you. That's the magic. It's that transfer of knowledge. Which is the 90% correlation to success, not their previous background." [er, 36:26]

Named companies that have adopted this: BioRender, Vendr, Viz, Belong, Sonder.

**5. Three Strikes / Performance Protocol [4/19]**
Sources: c5, gcw1, gcw2, er

A structured escalation framework that ensures firing is never a surprise.

From the curriculum (c5), the three levels:
1. **Green (First Occurrence):** Acknowledge + get curious ("Can you help me understand what happened?") + reiterate expectations + make a plan + hold accountable.
2. **Yellow (Second Time):** Acknowledge recurrence + warning ("This is becoming a pattern. It's not okay.") + make a plan + hold accountable.
3. **Red (Third Time):** Acknowledge recurrence + consequences ("If this doesn't change, your job is at risk.") + make a plan + if not fixed, they're out.

"Three clear chances with specific metrics. If they can't improve with that level of support, neither of you should feel guilty." [c5]

The book (gcw1) provides a PIP variant with specific milestones at 7, 30, 60, and 90 days. The critical enforcement rule: "If you don't fire at a missed milestone, you've completely invalidated the value of the written document." [gcw1]

**6. Keeper Test [3/19]**
Sources: c5, logan, lenny

Borrowed from Netflix: "If this person told you they were leaving, would you fight hard to keep them? If the answer is no, it's time to have a conversation." [c5]

Matt extends this with the "Have You Ever Regretted Letting Someone Go?" diagnostic: "Have you ever regretted letting someone go? And I've never had any other answer but no... What does that mean? That means I'm letting go of people who are performing at this level, but really the level to let go at is all the way up here." [logan, 58:08]

The implication: if you have never regretted a firing, your bar for firing is too low. You are waiting too long.

On the Logan Bartlett podcast, Matt links this to his Netflix-style talent density principle: "I believe very much in you only keep outperformers... on our team, let's say we use a scale of 1 to 5, or 3 is meeting expectation. We only have 4s and 5s. That's it. We let 3s go a long time ago." [logan, 1:01:05]

**7. Layoff Protocol [3/19]**
Sources: c5, logan, lenny

"Make it one trauma and begin the healing process, rather than a never-ending series of concussions from which the org cannot heal." [c5]

The organizational trauma metaphor drives the core recommendation: cut once, cut deep. "Cutting 2 times or 3 times creates PTSD in an organization." [lenny, ~50:00]

Specific playbook from the Lenny and Logan interviews:
1. Give each manager a dollar amount to cut (not headcount -- prevents cutting the cheapest/most junior people).
2. Let each manager choose who leaves from their team (not department heads choosing for managers).
3. Morning of execution: each manager delivers the news one-on-one, 15 minutes each.
4. Afternoon: All-hands for the stay team. Address three fears: (a) Is this going to happen to me? (b) Did these people get feedback? (c) Is the company dying?
5. "We cut deep so that we only cut once. The people to your left and right and you, you are the stay team." [lenny, ~50:00]
6. Follow-up: one-on-one with every stay-team member where the manager just listens.
7. Create an alumni Slack channel. "If you don't do it, they will. And you won't like the results if they create it on their own." [c5]
8. Publish your own article about the layoffs before reporters do.

Post-layoff performance data from Matt's portfolio: "Within 60 days of each layoff, the CEO reported back to me, it's insane. I don't know how this happened, but the company is now operating better. I'm not talking on a relative scale. I'm talking on an absolute scale." [lenny, 43:59]

Morale recovery timeline: humane layoff = 2 weeks. Bad layoff (Elon-style) = 60 days. [logan, 1:06:38]

**8. Hiring as Intellectual Laziness [2/19]**
Sources: er, lenny

A provocative position even among lean-startup advocates: "I think it's almost an intellectual laziness that we go through and just say, let's just hire bodies." [er, 8:46]

"With fewer people in the organization, things work better. That's the big realization that most people never discover." [lenny, ~20:00]

Matt extends this to an aspirational target: "To date it hasn't been done of keeping a team less than 20 people and having it be worth $10 billion, but it doesn't mean that it can't exist. And I think it's just no one's tried. So I'm going to try." [er, 8:46]

Each additional person adds overhead: "Each additional person, they just require time and overhead. Time to get informed about the issue, time to be heard about their opinions, time to share the problems that they're experiencing and feel cared about. Just time. And the fewer people you have, the less consensus you need, the less it just goes quicker." [logan, 1:01:05]

**9. Failed-Founder Recruiting [2/19]**
Sources: naval, er

"One hack that I found that has worked really well for me is to recruit in founders who have failed through no fault of their own... when you find a founder who failed through no fault of their own, the whole category failed, you've got founder mentality, you've got a person with a chip on their shoulder, they have operating experience." [naval, 5:44]

Naval's complement: "Acknowledge them as peers, give them an opportunity, acknowledge them as being your intellectual and capability equals. Be very, very generous with equity." [naval, 6:20]

**10. Job Responsibilities Rubric [2/19]**
Sources: c5, gcw1 [PARAPHRASE -- VERIFY; may appear in additional curriculum sections]

"Waiting until formal Performance Reviews to have these detailed performance conversations is the single greatest failure of management that I see." [c5]

The system: create a shared 1-5 scoring rubric covering every core responsibility. Both manager and report fill it out independently before each check-in. Compare side by side, focusing on discrepancies. "Discrepancies are great opportunities to seek to understand why the ratings were given." [c5]

Frequency: monthly at minimum, bi-weekly recommended. Not a punishment tool -- introduce it as "I want to do more to support your success."

**11. Bill Campbell Performance Review [1/19]**
Sources: c5

Four dimensions:
1. **Results:** Current goals, performance against metrics, successes, challenges, future goals.
2. **Management:** Number of reports, meeting cadence, feedback loops.
3. **Innovation:** "What have you done to make your role, or the company operations, easier in the future?"
4. **Working with peers:** Cross-functional communication challenges.

Crucially: "Ask these same questions of reports to see if there is consistency, and to assess if reports feel informed, authorized and motivated." [c5]

**12. The Ceremonial Offer [1/19]**
Sources: c5

Before the written offer, pre-close verbally: "I will join the company as long as ____." Get all requirements stated. Then: "If we made you this exact offer, would you accept?" Only after a verbal yes, proceed to the written offer.

Then make it a ritual: "Hand the written offer to the candidate with two hands and a ceremonial bow. Give company swag. Give hugs and high-fives." [c5]

**13. The Sell: Fit, Family, Freedom, Fun, Fortune [1/19]**
Sources: c5

Five dimensions to sell a candidate on joining. The most distinctive element is spouse outreach: "I always ask to talk directly to the spouse... The spouse usually says something like, 'Thank you for doing this. In all of our years together, no boss of my spouse has ever reached out to me like this.' The candidate usually accepts shortly after that." [c5]

"The biggest blocker to a candidate saying 'Yes' is their spouse's fear of saying 'No.'" [c5]

**14. Magic Questions (Retention) [1/19]**
Sources: c5

Five categories rated 1-5 monthly: work life satisfaction, overall life satisfaction, WFH setup satisfaction, company performance perception, working with the team/manager. Pick the lowest. Focus on it.

"80% of the value is in me asking the question and listening to the answer so that my star performer feels heard. 20% of the value is creating the requested condition so that my star performer feels cared for." [c5]

#### Stories

**The 98% vs. 50% Success Rate** (c5, er, logan, tim)
The flagship empirical case for shadowing onboarding. Chief of staff hires -- who shadow the CEO for months -- have a ~98-100% success rate. Traditional executive hires -- who start performing Day 1 -- have a ~50% success rate. Both populations serve the same companies. The only variable is onboarding approach.

This insight emerged from a conversation with Ryan at Flexport: Matt asked how many executive hires had worked out. Ryan said 50%. Matt asked what the commonality was among the successful ones. Ryan realized: "It's the ones that I liked, the ones that I considered that I wanted to be friends with." Matt's insight: likability was a proxy for time spent together, which was a proxy for context shared. This led directly to the 60-day shadowing protocol. [logan, 52:33]

**The Outperformer Who Was Let Go** (lenny, logan)
Matt practiced his own talent density philosophy on his team. One person was "an outperformer, no question about it, and super positive and amazing guy and can do anything." But Matt asked his #2 if she could absorb his responsibilities. She said, "Give me 2 weeks." She did. They let him go. "After about 30 days, I was like, uh, did I do the wrong thing? But then after about 2 to 3 months, I realized, no, that was actually the right thing because my number 2 was able to absorb the things that he was doing." [lenny, ~17:00-22:00]. Matt became his agent and helped him start a company.

**The Spouse Call** (c5)
"I always ask to talk directly to the spouse... During the call with the spouse, I simply listen and repeat back the thoughts that I hear... The spouse usually says something like, 'Thank you for doing this. In all of our years together, no boss of my spouse has ever reached out to me like this.' The candidate usually accepts shortly after that." [c5]

**The Professional Sports Team Analogy** (tim)
"If you had a professional sports team that wasn't allowed to let go of any player they added to the team, that team would end up in last place every time. And companies are no different." [tim, Line 356]. The ability to fire well is the prerequisite for the ability to hire boldly.

**Avlok Kohli / Paul Davison: Failed Founders Recruited** (naval)
Naval recruited Avlok Kohli -- a founder who had failed at a social network for lawyers and a cleaning service called Fairy, both categories that entirely failed -- to replace him as CEO of AngelList. The partnership became highly effective. Paul Davison, another failed-founder recruit (Highlighter, a real-time friend-tracking app), later left to start Clubhouse. "Paul had the best-executed company in the space, and even that failed." [naval, 5:55]

**Faith Meyer's Rubric Success** (c5)
"Being able to lay our completed rubrics down side by side and look for the areas where our ratings didn't line up not only created a tangible sense of collaboration and alignment, it also gave me clear opportunities where I could level set and/or reinforce a direct report's strong performance." [c5]

**Post-Layoff Performance Improvement** (lenny)
During COVID (March 2020), Matt's portfolio companies did layoffs ranging from 5% to 40%. "Within 60 days of each layoff, the CEO reported back to me, it's insane... the company is now operating better. I'm not talking on a relative scale. I'm talking on an absolute scale." By the 2022 valuation reset, experienced CEOs cut 50% with "frankly phenomenal" results. [lenny, 43:59]

**Wee Dang's Competing Teams** (ll)
When Wee Dang at Clipboard Health suspected her engineering team was underperforming but couldn't prove it, she "hired an entirely new team, a small team. To do the exact same thing. And sure enough, the new smaller team way outperformed the old big team. And then she knew." [ll, 15:17]. A validation method for when you can't assess quality through domain expertise.
