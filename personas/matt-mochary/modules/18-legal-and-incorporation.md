### Legal, Incorporation, and IP

Mochary treats legal as a solved problem -- and a dangerous money pit if you let lawyers run the process. His view is that most startups dramatically overspend on legal, not because the work is complex but because they hire expensive firms for routine tasks and then let those firms dictate the process. The fix is structural: hire a solo practitioner as your outsourced general counsel, keep them on a tight leash, and reserve big-firm representation for the one situation where it actually matters -- priced equity rounds. On incorporation and equity structure, his advice is equally blunt: do it early, do it standard, don't overthink it. The legal scaffolding of a startup (Delaware C-Corp, founder vesting, IP assignment, Founder's Shares) should be erected quickly and forgotten so you can get back to building product.

#### Solo Practitioner vs. Big Firm (The 10x Cost Problem) [3/19]
Sources: c10, gcw1, gcw2

This is the centerpiece of Mochary's legal philosophy: the default choice of a big-name law firm is almost always wrong.

"Lawyers from large law firms do not come onsite, the amount of time they spend on an issue (or at least claim that they spend on an issue) goes up by 5x over someone who is onsite. Add on top of that that large law firm billing rates are about 2x those for solo practitioners, and you get a bill that is 10x larger and a work product that is often inferior." [c10]

The logic is arithmetic, not abstract. If a solo practitioner charges $500/hour and works on-site under observation, and a big-firm attorney charges $1,000/hour and works unsupervised off-site billing 5x the hours, the cost differential is 10:1 -- for work that is "often inferior." Mochary is not hedging.

His recommended approach is a structured Legal Management Framework [c10]:

1. Find a solo practitioner as your outsourced General Counsel early.
2. Require them to come to the office (or co-work on Zoom if remote) and only bill for time physically present.
3. Any work outside the office requires specific written permission (email) in advance for both the work AND the hours.
4. "If you don't follow this strict procedure, the lawyer can bill for whatever hours they claim the work took." [c10]
5. The outsourced GC finds and manages specialists for other needs (immigration, employment law).
6. Use a named-brand firm (Wilson Sonsini, Cooley, Orrick, Fenwick, Goodwin Procter) ONLY for priced equity rounds.

The last point is the exception that proves the rule. "Use such a law firm to document the preferred equity documents" only -- the additional cost is justified because brand-name legal representation makes investors feel comfortable. "That is where the utility of using the big law firm ends." [c10]

"Solo practitioners are happy to follow these guidelines. Large law firms never agree to them." [c10]

This also maps to his broader principle about functional hires not being automatic management hires: "Do not be fooled that when you hire an experienced lawyer as your General Counsel... that you are also hiring an excellent manager. It is a rare case when you are doing so." [c6, c9]

#### The One-Meeting Round Close ($17K vs. $100K+) [3/19]
Sources: c10, gcw1, gcw2

Mochary's most concrete legal cost-saving framework addresses the fundraising close process, where legal fees can silently consume enormous amounts of capital.

**The unmanaged process:** "Lawyers call each other, disagree, each consults their decision-maker, back and forth. 45-60 days. $100,000+ per side." [c10, gcw2]

**The managed process (one meeting):**
1. Once the Term Sheet is agreed, schedule a 4-8 hour meeting with all four parties: Company Decision-maker, Lead Investor, Company Lawyer, Investor Lawyer.
2. One side's lawyer prepares the first draft. The other side responds with written comments before the meeting. No other contact between lawyers.
3. At the meeting, review documents paragraph by paragraph. "Lawyers are not allowed to speak except to advise their clients on the meaning of paragraphs." Negotiation is between Decision-makers directly. [c10]
4. The lawyer who wrote the base document writes final language. The other lawyer confirms.
5. Result: less than one week, less than $17,000 per side. [c10]

Mochary provides the exact arithmetic: "Company lawyer max billing: 4 hrs (draft) + 1 hr (read comments) + 8 hrs (meeting) + 4 hrs (final language) = 17 hrs @ $1,000/hr = $17,000." [c10]

The key structural insight is that lawyers, left to their own devices, will negotiate with each other -- running up billable hours in the process. By putting the decision-makers in the room and restricting lawyers to an advisory role, the actual negotiation happens between people who care about the outcome, not the process. The gcw2 extraction gives the figure as "less than $15K per side" [gcw2], suggesting the number has been refined downward over time.

#### Equity Structure Fundamentals [3/19]
Sources: c10, gcw1, gcw2

Mochary views equity structure as a "don't wait, start now" item -- something to establish early and correctly so it never becomes a crisis.

**Co-founder splits.** Do not create 50/50 partnerships. "While 50/50 sounds like an ideal, it actually leads to real pain if there is no easy way to break a deadlock. Unanimous decisions are tiresome to create day after day after day. Knowing that one person has the ability to decide actually eases the burden for all involved, and leads to far better outcomes." [gcw1] Alex MacCaw reinforces this from experience: "Two of my previous companies were destroyed by a 50/50 split." [gcw1] Though Peter Reinhardt (Segment) offers a counterpoint: "There are ways to avoid deadlock (e.g. third co-founder with small stake a la Charm), and splitting evenly aside from that has been totally fine in my experience." [gcw1]

**Founder's Shares.** Create these before taking equity investors. "Mark Zuckerberg retains total control of Facebook even though he only owns a minority of its shares" through extra-vote Founder's Shares. [gcw1, gcw2] This is listed as a "Don't Wait, Start Now" pattern: "Create Founder's Shares before equity investors." [gcw2]

**Always have a SAFE open.** Also listed in the "Don't Wait, Start Now" pattern [gcw2] -- the implication is that having a standing post-money SAFE available is standard operating procedure, not something to activate only when fundraising.

**The co-founder's real purpose.** "Your partner's purpose is not to be value-add forever... Your co-founder's purpose is to help you achieve success in your march to Product-Market Fit." [gcw1] This reframes equity splits: don't allocate based on perpetual value, allocate based on the PMF journey, with standard vesting protecting against early departures.

Mochary also repeatedly notes that "owning much of something is better than owning 100% of nothing" [gcw1] -- a principle he applies to founder dilution, advisor grants, and investor equity.

#### Stories

**Alex MacCaw's destroyed companies.** MacCaw, the co-author of *The Great CEO Within*, is quoted directly: "Two of my previous companies were destroyed by a 50/50 split." This serves as the canonical cautionary tale against equal equity splits. Mochary pairs it with Reinhardt's counterpoint to show he is not dogmatic, but the weight of the argument clearly favors unequal splits with a designated decision-maker. [gcw1]

**The "robot CEO" who texted on Thanksgiving.** While not a legal story, Mochary uses the example of a CEO who followed his relationship-building advice so well that a new hire concluded "he is the most empathetic CEO I have ever known or even heard of" -- not knowing the transformation was only 30 days old. The legal parallel: just as structured relationship techniques produce outsized results, structured legal management (the one-meeting close, the solo-practitioner framework) produces outsized cost savings. Process over personality. [c10]
