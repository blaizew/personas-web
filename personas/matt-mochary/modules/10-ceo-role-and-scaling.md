### The CEO Role and Scaling

The CEO role is the most shape-shifting position in any company. Matt Mochary returns to this theme relentlessly because he sees founders make the same mistake: they fuse their identity with a specific version of the CEO job and refuse to let it evolve. The result is burnout, organizational dysfunction, and -- paradoxically -- worse outcomes for the company they are trying to protect. This module covers the non-outsourceable functions of the CEO, the frameworks for stepping down or restructuring the role, the Chief of Staff and COO hiring patterns, the dangerous transition from 20 to 40 employees, the system-over-talent principle, and the evolving model of CEO operations that Mochary now sees converging across his most successful clients.

---

#### Concepts

**1. CEO Non-Outsourceable Functions [8/19]**

The CEO role is commonly defined as: setting vision/mission/values, ensuring product solves customer problems, maintaining stakeholder relationships, reporting to board, hiring/managing the exec team. Mochary's reframe is sharp: "These are indeed CEO responsibilities. But it doesn't mean that they all need to get done by the CEO. The CEO must ensure that they all get done by someone." (c6) He reduces the truly non-outsourceable functions to three or four: (1) create and maintain positive relationships with key stakeholders, (2) report to the board and investors, (3) hire the exec team, and (4) future visioning of the company and its products. Everything else can and should be delegated. "I posit that only someone who loves a function can be truly good at it, that includes you. If you are not passionate about any of these functions, you will not be good at them." (c6)

The mental model he uses is CEO as Fireman: "A fireman lives in the firehouse, ready to respond to a fire whenever it comes. Most of the time, there is no fire to respond to, so the fireman waits. The most enterprising ones use that downtime to do something productive. The best CEOs that I coach do the same." (c6, c8) Francis Davidson of Sonder reinforces: "When all the systems are green, you sit and watch. When one of the systems turns yellow or red, you can step in and operate it yourself." (c6) The prescription is to keep large swaths of open time -- at least 25% of the calendar -- for firefighting, visioning, and the functions only the CEO can do.

From the Logan Bartlett interview: "It's the responsibility of the CEO to make sure these things get done. Not that the CEO has to do them herself." [33:30] (logan) And from EnterpriseReady: "Almost every founder that I know loves product, and that's it... I know maybe 2 founders that enjoy doing that [operations], and the other 98 that I know hate it. And if you hate something, you're not going to do it well." (er)

**2. CEO Stepping-Down Framework [8/19] -- SIGNATURE**

This is Mochary's most distinctive organizational scaling framework. The systematic process: (1) Energy Audit your current role. (2) Identify your Zone of Genius. (3) Hire someone to own everything outside it. (4) Shadow them. (5) Step back. "Most founders resist this because their identity is fused with their company. That's the fear mountain talking." (c6, taxonomy)

The decision framework for whether to step down: Do you love people management, hiring, sales, and fundraising? If no -- if the only thing you love is Product -- hire a CEO. If you love some but not all, hire a COO/President/Chief of Staff. After 90 days, they will likely be able to do any of the roles that you do not love. (c6)

On the upside of stepping down: "The joke is that you still get to own all of your equity, that equity will become even more valuable, and you get to build yourself an ideal life." (c6) And more directly: "Name yourself Chairman. Go off and build your ideal life." (c6)

From EnterpriseReady: "I posit you'll create way more value by staying in founder mode... because you'll create the next level, the next solution." (er) The paradox: stepping down as operator does not mean disengaging -- it means freeing yourself to do the creative, visionary work that only you can do.

**3. COO / Chief of Staff Pattern -- Three Tiers [6/19] -- SIGNATURE**

Mochary considers this his single strongest recommendation, backed by data: "The 98% success rate of my CoS placements vs. 50% for traditional exec hires is the strongest evidence I have for any single recommendation." (taxonomy) From Logan Bartlett: "What we found was, is that chief of staff, there was like a 100% success rate on how they turned out. Meanwhile, the executives that were getting hired was like a 50% success rate." (logan)

He defines three tiers of the role (c6):

- **Tier 1 (Junior Exec Ops):** Supports you, no ambition to run a department. Handles logistics, follow-ups, meeting prep. Entry point.
- **Tier 2 (Senior Exec Ops):** Ambitious, wants to run a department, eventually becomes COO. Background: "Several years at a top management consulting firm (Bain, BCG, and McKinsey). I am partial to Bain, as I find x-Bain consultants also have excellent financial modeling skills." (c6)
- **Tier 3 (Very Senior):** Strategic thought-partner, crafts future vision alongside the CEO.

The training protocol is absolute: "Give your Exec Ops unfettered access to all the information that you receive. It would be best to have the Exec Ops sit beside you from morning till night, with full access to your emails, calls, meetings, etc. By seeing what decisions you make, based on what information you receive, she will soon be able to think like you, and then she can truly be an extension of you (and magic will happen)." (c6)

The hiring advice is action-biased: "Don't wait for perfection here. Pick someone good... It's the shadowing that is most important as no one will perform on Day 1." (c6) And a critical branding note from Logan Bartlett: "We should call it something other than Chief because there are Chief of Staffs who start to think of themselves as a C-level exec, and they are not. They are an assistant to the CEO, period, end of story." [47:47] (logan) Hence the rename to "Exec Ops."

The causal mechanism behind the 98% vs 50% success rate is not experience -- it is context transfer. From the Logan Bartlett interview: "The bar is actually pretty damn low... They have to be a manager who's managed successfully before, and that's it... It's the shadowing of you. That's the magic. It's that transfer of knowledge. Which is the 90% correlation to success, not their previous background." (er) And: "The CEO is not smarter than everybody else. The CEO just sees all the information, and he or she is the only one. If everyone else could see all that information, they would make the same decisions or, frankly, better ones." (er)

**4. Office of the CEO / Smoke-Jumpers [3/19]**

After the initial Exec Ops hire proves out (typically 2-3 months), the next step is expansion: "Have the Exec Ops hire 1-2 more CoS types ('Members of the Office of the CEO')." (c6) These become the CEO's rapid-response team. "These are your smokejumpers. Don't ever be left without 1-2 available to you." (c6)

The model at scale: Francis Davidson (Sonder) has a CoS who runs all OKRs and roadmap planning, with a team of 10 smoke-jumpers who get dispatched into struggling departments. (c6) The Biz Ops variant is similar: staff are "mini-CEOs" from top consulting firms who can parachute into problem areas. Critical rule: "It is critical that the Biz Ops person report to that Manager the entire time." Otherwise, they are viewed as a spy. (c6)

**5. Founder Mode vs. Operator Mode [5/19]**

"Founder mode is high-touch, high-intuition, willing to break process. Operator mode is systematic, delegation-heavy, process-driven." (taxonomy) Both are valuable at different stages. The danger is twofold: founders who refuse to shift to operator mode, and operators who kill founder energy. "The best CEOs learn to switch between modes." (taxonomy)

The most vivid illustration is Naval Ravikant. Matt gave Naval the systematic CEO playbook (what became The Great CEO Within), and Naval looked at it and said: "I looked at it and I was like, this is the right manual. Here's a really smart person who's figured this out and systematized it as much as possible. And I looked at it and I was like, I don't want to do this." [3:55] (naval) Rather than force himself into operator mode, Naval hired operators and stepped into a purely creative/strategic role.

The message to founders who hate operations: "You create way more value by staying in founder mode... because you'll create the next level, the next solution." (er) The operational system should be run by a COO or Chief of Staff, not by the founder grinding through tasks they despise.

**6. The 20-to-40 Danger Zone [3/19]**

"One of the most dangerous transitions a company makes is from less than twenty team members to more than forty." (c6, gcw1, gcw2) Below 20, informal communication works. Above 40, without a management system, "you will hire great people, but they won't know what to do, and you will be frustrated by their lack of output." (c6)

The prescription: implement a formal management system. The good news: "The good news is that the same system that allows your company to operate well with 25 people will also work well with 25,000. Neither the system nor the amount of overhead will change. It is a one-time hit." (c6, gcw1) That one-time hit is about 20% of the work week: "The overhead -- twenty percent of the standard work week -- can feel tremendous to a startup CEO... But without this one-day-per-week investment, a larger team will never fully know what to do." (gcw1)

**7. System Over Talent -- Warriors vs. Cavaliers [5/19]**

This is one of Mochary's most-repeated mental models. The NBA analogy: "The Golden State Warriors were an underperforming team under Mark Jackson. Once Steve Kerr joined and brought an effective system, the same roster of players became dominant and won the championship." (c6, gcw1, gcw2, c8) By contrast, Cleveland (LeBron) and Houston (Harden) relied on individual talent with no system. "Even against remarkable talent, the system inevitably wins." (c6, gcw1)

The company parallel: "Many members of Facebook's original leadership team continue to be on the leadership team today. This is because Sheryl brought a management system to the company that elevated all who used it. By contrast, Twitter has not developed a similarly effective system." (c6, gcw1) The lesson: Zuckerberg's smartest early move was not hiring domain executives with competing management styles, but hiring one person (Sandberg) to bring a unified methodology. "The problem with bringing the executive to just one department is they bring their style and their methodology in that department... and now you've got competing styles." (er)

Implementation options (c6):
- **Option 1:** Hire a COO who was a manager at a large, well-managed company (200+ employees) -- ideally Google, Facebook, or Amazon.
- **Option 2:** Hire an x-CEO as a "1 day a week CEO" to implement the system in 8-12 weeks. Have them watch you run it for two weeks, then run it yourself.

"One of the measures that you have successfully implemented this system is that your original team members continue to perform as excellent managers even as their departments grow massively." (c6)

**8. Company Stages [3/19]**

Mochary's stage model (c6, gcw1, gcw2):
- **Pre-product (0-10 people):** Founder does everything. "The environment feels like a team in battle. Chaos is expected." (gcw1)
- **Post-product (10-50):** Hire first managers. This is where the 20-to-40 danger zone lives.
- **Scaling (50-200):** Hire executives, CEO steps back. Implement formal management system.
- **Growth (200+):** CEO is coach/strategist, not operator.

"The CEO job at each stage is so different that it's almost a different job." (c6)

**9. CEO Refresh Compensation [1/19]**

A practical topic most coaches avoid: "Yes, there is such a thing. Investors hate it. But in the end, they grant it. Because it's market." (c6) The advice: "Don't ask for this amount yourself. For many board members, especially less experienced ones, they will feel offended by this ask. Better to hire a good compensation firm to present the market data." (c6) As of late 2020, market for CEO refresh: 3% with 4-year vesting.

**10. Enterprise to Early Stage Comparison [1/19]**

A framework for executives transitioning from large companies to startups (c6). Key contrasts:
- Enterprise: clear hierarchy, explicit swim lanes. Startup: leaders wear multiple hats.
- Enterprise: sufficient resources. Startup: resource scarcity, ruthless prioritization.
- Enterprise: certainty and evidence, consensus. Startup: urgency and unknowns, alignment over consensus.
- Enterprise: specialists rewarded for depth. Startup: generalists rewarded for range.
- Enterprise: Coach/Manager role. Startup: Player-Coach role.

**11. Evolving CEO Method -- No 1-1s, War Rooms, Single-Threaded Leaders [3/19]**

Mochary identifies an emerging pattern (as of 2025) across his most successful CEOs -- Sam Chaudhary (ClassDojo), Jensen Huang (NVIDIA), Brian Chesky (Airbnb), Zach Perret (Plaid), Alberto Rizzoli (V7), Harry Qi (Motion) (c6, c1, c8):

1. "Train people to act like you" (via shadowing).
2. "Get rid of 1-1s to force issues and information into the group" -- frees up 10-15 hours per week.
3. Use freed time for daily standups, weekly unblocking meetings for the top 10-20 projects.
4. "Put one single-threaded leader (not an exec!) in charge for each of these projects." (c6)

This is presented not as Mochary's opinion but as empirical convergence across his portfolio. It represents an evolution beyond the standard 1-1 model he taught for years.

#### Stories

**Naval Ravikant's Stepping-Down Story (naval)**
The canonical example. Naval was burning out as CEO of AngelList -- "It's an incredibly lonely job. And I'd done it for a long time and I was burning out." [1:33] He rated his life "3 out of 10, 2 out of 10 some days, maybe 4 on a good day" as CEO. [14:25] He brought in Kevin Laws as COO, then recruited Avlok Kohli (a founder who failed when the entire category failed -- "failed through no fault of their own"). Naval stepped back to focus on strategy and creativity. His best contribution, rolling funds, came as "a shower thought" while on the beach during free time. After stepping down: "My life is a 10 out of 10 across the board. It is perfect. It is fantastic. And it's because I got to engineer my life exactly the way that I want to, with no compromises. None." [16:00] And memorably: "If I could articulate how good my life is, they would have to lock me up because you can't have people this happy running around in society." [15:50]

**The Totality Origin Story (logan, er, twist)**
Matt's own company Totality was "sort of the precursor to AWS." They "hired 280 people in the first 18 months, raised $130 million, spent most of it... No one knew what they were doing ever. It was just complete chaos." (logan) It sold to Verizon for a good financial outcome, but the operational experience was terrible. "My partner and I, Michael Carrier, we had no idea what we were doing, and we frankly did a terrible job of running the company." (er) This drove Matt's entire coaching career: the question became "Is there a way I could have done this better?"

**Ryan at Flexport -- The 50% Executive Success Rate (logan)**
Matt asked Ryan at Flexport how many executive hires had worked out. Ryan said 50%. Matt asked what the commonality was among the successful ones. Ryan realized: "It's the ones that I liked, the ones that I considered that I wanted to be friends with." Matt's insight: likability was a proxy for time spent together, which was a proxy for context shared. This led directly to the 60-day shadowing protocol. "By the end of 90 days, you will have a mind meld. You will totally and utterly trust them because you'll see that they actually have the full context." (er)

**The Zuckerberg / Sheryl Sandberg Decision (er)**
Instead of hiring individual domain executives with competing management styles, Zuckerberg hired one person to manage the entire exec team and teach a unified methodology. This is Mochary's model case for System Over Talent. "This function was Sheryl Sandberg's gift to Facebook. You deserve the same." (c6)

**Francis Davidson / Sonder -- The Smoke-Jumper Model (c6)**
Davidson's CoS runs all OKRs and roadmap planning and has a team of 10 smoke-jumpers who parachute into struggling departments. This is the Office of the CEO pattern at full maturity.

**Chris Mansi / Viz.ai -- Promoting EA to CoS (c6)**
Promoted an EA to Chief of Staff who did not have the typical GSB/HBS/McKinsey background: "She did not have the typical GSB/HBS/McKinsey background, but we did the 30-day shadowing period, and it's been amazing." This demonstrates that the shadowing is the variable, not the pedigree.
