### Board Management

Most CEOs dread board meetings. Mochary understands why -- and thinks the dread is largely justified. His view of boards is unromantic: they exist primarily for legal protection, and most of them actively hurt the companies they oversee. His solution is not to fight this reality but to engineer around it -- minimize the damage, maximize the rare value boards can provide, and turn the relationship from adversarial to coaching-oriented. He offers two distinct operating models depending on trust level, a structured feedback exercise he has personally run at companies like Confluent, Reddit, and Sonder, and a set of conversational techniques for managing board member egos without capitulating to bad advice.

---

#### The Default Reality of Boards [7/19]
Sources: c9, c3, c6, c8, gcw1, gcw2, lenny

Mochary does not begin with aspiration. He begins with diagnosis: most boards are broken.

"When a board of directors and a CEO function well, board members act as lieutenants to the CEO. The board also holds the CEO accountable, and if the CEO becomes dysfunctional, the board fires her and hires another. This is the proper role of an effective board. Unfortunately, most boards are not functional." [c9, gcw2]

The dysfunction has a structural root -- an information asymmetry that cannot be solved by better communication alone. The CEO is immersed in operations 20+ days per month. Board members see only what gets shared in periodic meetings. "The board members, therefore, cannot possibly have the full context. Yet many feel the need to give advice. And once you give that advice, because most have large egos, they want you to follow it." [c9]

This creates a dangerous dynamic: "Because boards can hire and fire the CEO, if enough board members get their feelings bruised from unfollowed advice, the CEO may find herself looking for a new job." [c9, gcw2]

Boards have clear legal utility -- fiduciary oversight, corporate veil protection. Without proper board meetings, "someone who the company has injured can sue the organization and 'pierce the corporate veil,' making the owners individually liable." [c9] But beyond that legal function, the utility drops sharply: "unfortunately, that is usually where the utility of a board of directors and board meetings ends." [c9]

In his coaching framing, Mochary puts it even more bluntly: "You report to no one. Yes, you report to the board, but not really. You don't share with them everything, and therefore they are not your true thought partners." [c8, c9] And in the context of meeting effectiveness: "Most meetings don't declare a purpose, and therefore become arenas for people to hear themselves talk. I call those ... Board Meetings." [c9, c4]

The CEO cannot outsource the board relationship. It is one of three or four functions that must remain with the CEO personally: "Create and maintain positive relationships with key stakeholders, report to the board and investors, hire the exec team, future visioning of the company and its products." [c6]

#### The Minimal Board Model (Low Trust) [4/19]
Sources: c9, gcw1, gcw2, c3

When trust between the CEO and board is low -- which Mochary implies is the default state -- the goal is defensive: "Make board members feel informed and heard so that they don't make trouble for you" while using "the least amount of your time to achieve the above." [c9]

**Board composition.** Keep the board to an odd number of seats and as few seats as possible. "The ideal number is three. Try very hard not to go beyond five." [c9, gcw2] An even number creates deadlock risk: "Even on simple issues, can cause existential crisis." [c9] Too many members requires excessive maintenance -- "each wants 1-1 conversations and airtime." [c9]

**Board observers.** They "are supposed to observe only during a board meeting and not talk unless asked a direct question by a board member. Enforce this behavior." [c9, gcw2]

**Written pre-reads.** Give board members all information they need in writing before the meeting -- complete financials, metrics/KPIs, the good and the bad, accomplishments since last meeting, plans for next period, and significant challenges. Then: "Do a test run with a trusted non-board member who asks questions where things are unclear; answer all their questions and add answers to the board presentation." [c9] The result: "Present this final version to the board -- they will likely have no further questions." [c9]

**The homework deflection.** After the presentation, give each board member a very specific assignment. "Don't worry, none of them will actually do the homework. But they also are then not likely to give you unsolicited advice. This is the true goal of the homework." [c9, gcw2] This is a deliberate misdirection: it appears collaborative but its true function is preventing advice that the CEO would then feel pressured to follow.

**The summarize-and-confirm technique.** When a board member does give advice: "take notes, summarize what the board member said, and ask if you got it right. Once they say 'Yes, that's right,' thank them for their advice and let them know that you will seriously consider it in formulating your go-forward plan. This will make the board member feel that you heard them, which is primarily what they are after." [c9] This is the same reflect-back technique Mochary uses throughout his method -- feedback, conflict resolution, fundraising -- applied specifically to managing board egos.

#### Board Meeting Best Practice (High Trust) [3/19]
Sources: c9, gcw1, gcw2

When trust is established, Mochary shifts from defensive board management to extractive -- actively pulling value from the board rather than just neutralizing them.

"The best board meeting is one that gives board members enough information to allow them to be helpful to you. Be transparent. Be vulnerable. If you do, the board will reward you with rich and valuable feedback." [c9]

**The Strategic vs. Operational Filter.** Board members are pattern-matchers with cross-portfolio visibility. "Most board members have far more visibility than you as to what other companies have experienced when tackling forward-looking strategic questions or operations at other companies. By contrast, you have much more context than your board members on internal operating issues." [c9] The rule: "Stick to the questions where your board has more visibility than you do." [c9] Ask the board about expansion, adjacent products, market timing, layoff strategies. Keep internal operating issues to yourself.

**Pre-meeting protocol (7+ days before):**

1. Share information in writing at least 3 days in advance, preferably 7 days. Format: memo or deck. "My preference is a memo, as it lends itself more to reading." [c9]

2. Include a short Loom video (<5 minutes) walking through the key ideas. "Some CEOs are now starting to include short Loom videos (less than 5 minutes) that run through all of the most important ideas in the memo. Board members LOVE this as they can watch at 1.5x speed." [c9] This replaces the CEO's opening walk-through in the meeting itself.

3. Content structure: Update (KPIs with Green/Yellow/Red labels, OKRs, the good, the not good, and what you plan to do about it), Issues (1-2 strategic questions with proposed solutions), Requests (specific action requests -- warm introductions, network leverage), Feedback ("How could this board meeting be more effective?" and "How could I as CEO be more effective?"). [c9]

4. Collect board member questions at least 2 days before. Write up answers within 48 hours.

5. Hold 1-1 phone calls with each board member just before the meeting to ensure they have ingested the information and to elicit their questions/concerns "so that they come to the board meeting already feeling heard (this is critical for creating trust)." [c9]

**During the meeting:**

1. **Get everyone present.** Each board member shares one fun thing experienced since the last meeting (30 seconds or less). "If there are more than 6 people present, the verbal sharing becomes too long. In that case, I limit the sharing to only board members, not observers, lawyers nor execs." [c9]

2. **Answer questions** received about the board packet.

3. **Invite the executive team** for 2-3 minute verbal department summaries and strategic issue participation.

4. **Unpack 1-2 strategic issues.** Give each board member equal air time. If willing to write, use the Issue/Proposed Solution template. "Allow Board observers and lawyers to write, but not to speak unless you specifically ask for their input." [c9]

5. **Confirm action items.** Each board member confirms willingness to do requested actions.

6. **Closed-door session.** All non-board members leave. Written feedback collected.

7. **Closing ceremony.** Each board member thanks one other person in the room for something they did. "Once a person is thanked, they can't be thanked again (so that everyone gets thanked for something)." The CEO thanks last, since she has something to thank each person about. [c9]

8. **Social time.** Informal meal before or after the meeting. "Board observers and lawyers are usually not invited to this gathering." [c9]

#### The Board Feedback Protocol (80 Minutes) [3/19]
Sources: c9, c3, c8

This is Mochary's signature board exercise -- a structured session where board members give the CEO raw, written feedback in a facilitated environment. It applies the same feedback principles (5 A's, written-first, gamified vulnerability) used throughout his method, adapted specifically for the board-CEO relationship.

The core problem it solves: "As Board Members, we want to share our knowledge and network with the founder to help her succeed (and therefore make our investment more valuable). But we don't want to offend or embarrass that founder, so we rarely give our full critical feedback, and certainly not in a board meeting group setting." And: "As a CEO, we want to get full value (and therefore the full thoughts) from our advisors and board members. But we often forget to ask for full critical feedback specifically, and rarely do we ask for it in writing. Both the withholding from the board members and not asking for feedback from the founder cause inefficiency in the communication." [c9]

**Pre-work:** All participants do pre-reads (the 5 A's framework, the feedback document). The exercise can optionally begin before the meeting. [c9, c8]

**The exercise (80 minutes):**

1. Each board member creates a private scratchpad (blank Google Doc). [10 min]
2. Each writes what actions the CEO has done that they LIKE, and what steps they WISH THAT the CEO would do (or not do). The framing is deliberately competitive: "Please give the rawest WISH THAT feedback that you can think of. Yes, it's a competition!" [c9] Most important feedback goes at the top.
3. All paste feedback into a shared doc. [c9]
4. Each person reads their LIKEs to the CEO. [5 min] "The intent here is to generate joy and celebrate all the good in this relationship." [c9]
5. Each person reads their top WISH THAT aloud. [5 min] "We can't really understand the full message until we read it AND hear it spoken as well." [c9]
6. All read through the remainder of WISH THAT feedback. [5 min] Each person adds +1 comments to feedback from others they agree with.
7. CEO picks the "juiciest WISH THAT" and fully unpacks using the 5 A's: Ask, Acknowledge, Appreciate, Accept (or not), Action. [10 min]
8. CEO continues unpacking other WISH THATs until time expires. [50 min] "If there is enough time, it is ideal to unpack each board member's top issue (so that each feels heard)." [c9]

**Matt's role in the room:** "Think of him as a lion-tamer. Most likely, the lion will stay in the cage, and the CEO will not need Matt. But if he is required, we'll all be glad that he's there." [c9, c3, c7, c8] He is present to intervene only if the CEO gets triggered and drops below the line.

**Follow-up cadence:** CEO asks for written feedback at the end of every board meeting going forward. Treats all feedback as a gift. Reports back on actions taken at the beginning of the next board meeting. If board members are open to it, the CEO gives written feedback to each board member in subsequent 1-1 interactions. [c9]

**A critical qualification:** "All need to recognize that board members are highly qualified to give feedback in some areas (the effectiveness and efficiency of the board meeting, their interaction with the CEO) and less qualified in other areas (how to run the company). It doesn't mean that board members shouldn't give feedback in areas they are not as qualified in; it just means the CEO will be putting that feedback through a more stringent filter to see whether it resonates with him/her." [c9, c3]

#### Board Transparency and the Fear Bet [2/19]
Sources: lenny, naval

One of Mochary's most repeated coaching stories involves a CEO who was afraid to share bad news with the board. This is where the board management module intersects directly with Module 1 (Fear and Anger).

"A CEO discovered a problem in the business and hadn't told the board. The next fundraise was 6-12 months away and they needed existing investors to participate. Knee-jerk reaction: don't tell them." Mochary's response: "I think that's fear. I think if you actually tell them, the exact opposite of what you think is going to happen is going to happen. You think you're going to lose their trust. I think you're going to gain their trust." The CEO shared transparently. "Every single time that's happened, the board members have said, this is fantastic. I love this honesty. Thank you so much." [lenny]

The underlying principle from Naval Ravikant captures why CEOs default to hiding: "It's an incredibly lonely job... You can't confess to your board of directors or they'll lose faith in you and fire you eventually. And you can't confess to your employees because they'll stop following you." [naval] Mochary's coaching explicitly contradicts this conventional wisdom -- his bet is that transparency with the board builds trust, and he claims to have never lost that bet.

#### Boards as the Death of Great Investors [1/19]
Sources: logan

Mochary also addresses boards from the investor side. "Each board seat consumes ~3 hours/quarter minimum. At 15+ boards, a third of the investor's time is gone. Combined with a third for internal firm management and a third for personal life, there is zero time left for sourcing new deals." [logan] His advice to investors: proactively prune boards, especially of companies that are not succeeding. The reputation hit they fear does not materialize: "the next founder that wants you on their board doesn't think that they're going to fail." [logan]

#### CEO Compensation and the Board [1/19]
Sources: c6

On CEO equity refreshes: "Yes, there is such a thing. Investors hate it. But in the end, they grant it. Because it's market." Mochary's tactical advice: "Don't ask for this amount yourself. For many board members, especially less experienced ones, they will feel offended by this ask." Instead: "Better to hire a good compensation firm to present the market data." [c6]

#### Stories

**The CEO Who Wouldn't Tell the Board (lenny).** A CEO hid a business problem from the board because he feared losing their trust before a fundraise. Mochary made the fear bet: "I think the exact opposite of what you think is going to happen is going to happen." The CEO shared transparently. Board members responded with gratitude: "This is fantastic. I love this honesty." Mochary reports this outcome has repeated every single time he has coached a CEO through this scenario.

**Board Feedback at Confluent, Reddit, Sonder, Mattermost, Viz (c9).** "I have done this exercise at Confluent, Reddit, Sonder, Mattermost, Viz, etc. And each of those boards and CEOs have found it to be worthwhile. If anything, they've wanted it to be longer." The full version takes 80 minutes; a quick-and-dirty version at 50 minutes "rarely feels as successful as the full version." [c9]

**Naval Ravikant on the Loneliness of the CEO Role (naval).** Naval describes the CEO job as "incredibly lonely" and says a coach fills the gap that no other relationship can -- not the board, not employees. Matt's board management framework is partly a response to this loneliness: if you can make the board a coaching team rather than a tribunal, you gain thought partners you desperately need.

**Boards as the Death of Great Investors (logan).** Mochary counsels investors to prune their board seats aggressively. At 15+ boards, investors have no time left for sourcing. The fear of reputational damage from leaving a board is unfounded because "the next founder that wants you on their board doesn't think that they're going to fail."
