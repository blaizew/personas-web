### Company Culture and Remote Operations

Culture is one of the most misunderstood concepts in company-building. CEOs spend days wordsmithing value statements for their website, then tolerate behaviors that contradict every word. Mochary cuts through this with a simple reframe: culture is not what you say -- it is what you do, what you reward, what you punish, and most importantly, what you ignore. Remote work compounds the challenge by removing the ambient social infrastructure that offices provide by accident. Mochary's response is not to retreat to in-person mandates but to be fiercely intentional about building culture in a distributed world -- while holding one of his strongest convictions: if you go remote at all, go remote-only. Hybrid is the worst of all worlds.

---

#### Concepts

**1. Culture = Behavior, Not Words** [5/19: c10, gcw1, gcw2, er, lenny]

Mochary's definition of culture strips away the aspirational veneer: "Culture is the unspoken set of rules that people in a group follow when interacting with each other." It is revealed through action, not declaration. The behaviors a CEO tolerates become the real values -- regardless of what is posted on the wall.

A critical nuance that most founders miss: you do not get to invent your values from scratch. "One misnomer CEOs sometimes have is thinking they get to choose the values. By the time you're 30 or so employees your company has a set of values whether you like it or not. It's now your job to codify what's already there." Values are descriptive, not prescriptive. They emerge from the people you have already hired and the behaviors you have already rewarded.

This reframe has practical teeth. Once values are codified, they must drive personnel decisions: values "should clearly define the behaviors that you want all team members to exhibit. Once created, you should hire for these behaviors and fire those unwilling to do these behaviors." Values that do not influence hiring and firing are decoration.

Mochary provides two concrete methods for values creation:

- **Method 1 (Aspirational, small companies):** Complete the sentence: "The rest of you in the company can make all of the decisions from now on, as long as you ...."
- **Method 2 (Existing culture, larger companies):** Each Leadership Team member names a non-LT person who exhibits a desired behavior, naming the person AND the behavior. Select 3-5 examples. These are now your values -- grounded in real people doing real things.

Culture also requires active protection against politics. "Politics are created when someone successfully lobbies the CEO or their manager for some kind of benefit. Others see this, so they in turn lobby." The antidote: "The only way to prevent politics is to never allow lobbying to be successful, and the only way to do this is to have a written policy about as many situations as possible." Written policies eliminate the discretionary space where politics breed.

**2. Radical Transparency** [5/19: c10, gcw1, gcw2, er, lenny]

Transparency is not a nice-to-have in Mochary's model -- it is a structural necessity. The argument is both practical and psychological: "Your team members are smart. When there are problems, they know it. Hiding negative information from them does not make them feel better. If anything, it makes them more anxious." The reason: "Our imaginations are much more powerful than reality." People will imagine scenarios far worse than the truth.

The prescription is direct: "Share all relevant information with your team, both negative and positive. This will give them great comfort and enable them to use their brilliance and talents to adapt."

Mochary also reframes the CEO's informational advantage as a structural problem, not a personal one: "The CEO is not smarter than everybody else. The CEO just sees all the information, and he or she is the only one. If everyone else could see all that information, they would make the same decisions or, frankly, better ones." This is an argument for radical context-sharing: the bottleneck is information asymmetry, not intelligence asymmetry.

The cultural aspiration is captured in a quote Mochary borrows from Ben Horowitz: "A good culture is like the old RIP routing protocol: bad news travels fast, good news travels slow."

**3. Remote-First Principles (Anti-Hybrid Conviction)** [4/19: c2, c10, gcw2, er]

This is one of Mochary's strongest and most emphatic positions in the entire curriculum. He ranks organizational effectiveness models explicitly:

1. In-person (one or several offices) -- most effective
2. Remote-only -- second most effective
3. Hybrid -- least effective

The conclusion is unequivocal: "If you are going to be remote at all, be remote-only." And: "The temptation for many CEOs who make this shift to remote work will be to go with a hybrid approach: a central office with remote options. Please don't do it."

His predicted cascade of hybrid failure unfolds in six steps:

1. Leadership keeps working from HQ.
2. Culture degrades from "remote first" to "remote allowed."
3. Remote workers don't get promoted at equal rate (less visible).
4. Productive remote employees leave for all-remote companies.
5. Hybrid companies blame lack of productivity on remote (not on hybrid being hard).
6. Yahoo/IBM-like moves to force everyone back.

The core argument: "Hybrid creates a culture of the ins (in person) and the outs (remote), which breeds mistrust and low morale." Remote workers become second-class citizens. The best ones leave. Information asymmetry compounds. "Those at In-Person companies will enjoy communing with all of their co-workers. Those at Remote First will enjoy being first-class citizens in a unified culture. Remote workers at Hybrid companies will not."

Mochary draws heavily on Sid Sijbrandij and GitLab's model of "intentionally remote" -- designed-from-scratch remote culture rather than forced remote. GitLab's informal communication tactics include coffee chats (25-minute calls with no agenda via Donut bot), mixer calls (two people each invite someone else), Juice Box Chats (family members meeting), team social calls, AMA sessions, talent shows, coworking calls (screen-share while working silently together), and leading with empathy ("How are you?" to start every call).

He also cites Brian Armstrong's experience at Coinbase: "Within 30 days, Brian declared to me that they resolved all of Coinbase's recruiting challenges. Going remote was the single answer!" The recruiting advantage of remote is transformative: "We get to start recruiting for open positions from anywhere in the world. It is hard to describe just how beneficial this is until you experience it."

Remote work also drives one of Mochary's other core principles -- written-first communication: "Remote-only forces all communications to be written and available equally to everyone." This eliminates the information asymmetry that hybrid breeds.

**4. The Four Pillars of Remote Well-Being** [4/19: c2, c7, c10, er]

"I posit that humans need four things to feel mentally balanced: Connection to nature, Connection to tribe, Blood flow, Uninterrupted workspace." Pre-COVID, going to an office delivered all four accidentally. Remote work demands intentionality.

1. **Connection to nature:** 5-minute walk outside at the start and end of the day. Get outside at least every 2 hours. Close eyes and face the sun for 1 minute. Desk in front of a window.
2. **Connection to tribe:** 1-minute hugs with family members. In-person gatherings at least once weekly. Purpose: laughter.
3. **Blood flow:** Minimum effective dose. Mochary's threshold: 3 minutes on a treadmill at 4.0 speed. "My goal is to make this step so easy (i.e., short and slow) that there is no reason for me not to do it each day."
4. **Uninterrupted workspace:** Dedicated room far from kids' activities, with a lockable door. If unavailable: co-working space. Company should provide a rent stipend.

Critically, the manager's role expands in a remote environment: "The manager is a coach whose role is to help their report unpack and solve the issues that the report faces, whether they appear in their work or personal world." When reports are unhappy, the cause is often not the job but the remote setup. Managers should ask about home environment and daily routines proactively.

**5. Building Human Connection Remotely (Five-Settings Trust Model)** [3/19: c7, c10, lenny]

Mochary identifies six elements that create genuine human connection, with an emphasis on how to engineer them deliberately in distributed teams:

1. You know what is important to me (superficially: name, spouse, kids, hobbies; deeply: dreams, traumas, fears, shame).
2. You recognize and call out emotions/thoughts I am experiencing.
3. You know what is essential to the organization (Vision, OKRs, Product Roadmap).
4. You intend to take care of issues important to me and the org.
5. You are competent (insightful, organized, skilled).
6. You create laughter and fun.

The trust mechanism has a specific threshold: "The magic number is usually five. Once I see you in five different settings or physical locations, I usually tell myself that I know your character." Remote teams must engineer diverse interaction contexts -- not just work meetings but social calls, offsites, co-working sessions, and informal check-ins -- to reach this five-settings threshold.

The tactical protocol for maintaining connection is deceptively simple: collect personal information about each team member (spouse name, children, pets, hobbies, hometown), then send short messages letting people know you are thinking of them. "Thinking of you. Hope you, Dan, Cindy, and Luke are doing well." Mochary anticipates skepticism: "You are likely thinking: 'Matt, this is such an obvious ploy. My people will see right through it.'" His response: "Many of my coachees still send me messages like this. I know exactly what they are doing. Hell, I'm the one who showed them how to do it. And yet, I appreciate it every time."

**6. Calendar Cadence** [3/19: gcw2, c6, lenny]

"Every company needs a heartbeat." The cadence matters more than the content -- consistency creates the rhythm the organization depends on. Mochary prescribes a specific structure: weekly team meetings, monthly all-hands, quarterly offsites, annual planning.

Meeting discipline is non-negotiable: only 25- and 50-minute meetings, ending on time. Every meeting uses the ACT framework -- Accountability (did you do what you said?), Coaching (what problems arose?), Transparency (peer and upward feedback). This is the visible expression of culture.

**7. OKR Creation and AORs** [3/19: c6, c10, gcw2]

OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) provide the alignment infrastructure. Mochary specifies: "3 and 3. Three Objectives, with three Key Results for each Objective." The cascade runs on a strict one-week timeline (Admin Week):

- Monday: Leadership Team creates company OKRs (2 hours)
- Tuesday: Departments create department OKRs. CEO approves by end of day.
- Wednesday: Teams create team OKRs.
- Thursday: Sub-teams.
- Friday: Individuals.

A critical principle: "Keep in mind that each person should develop their OKRs (that align with the company's) rather than you dictating each person's OKRs. That way, each person feels personally invested." And the common failure: "A common mistake is to create Department OKRs, without creating Company-level OKRs. This omission leads to silo-ed fiefdoms that do not work together nor trust each other."

AORs (Areas of Responsibility) are the complementary tool. Apple "pioneered AORs in Silicon Valley." The concept addresses the tragedy of the commons: "When several people share responsibility for an action or process, often that action doesn't get done well or at all." Implementation takes 15 minutes in a team meeting -- CEO fills out her section first as an example, everyone writes theirs, then the team identifies conflicts (two people own the same thing, or nobody owns something). The goal: "A well-run company has no single point of failure."

**8. Impeccable Agreements Scaled to the Organization** [4/19: c1, c10, gcw1, gcw2]

The individual productivity principle -- make clear commitments, meet them -- scales to become an organizational culture principle. Two criteria: commitments must be "precisely defined" (a third party could judge compliance) and "fully agreed to" (almost always written).

The consequence structure is absolute. First violation: point it out immediately. No apology needed -- only a recommitment. Continued failure: "There is only one consequence that makes sense: They can no longer be part of the company." A company where agreements are routinely broken is a company where trust is routinely broken.

#### Stories

**The Robot CEO Who Became the Most Empathetic Leader** [c7, c10]: A CEO with self-admittedly low emotional intelligence asked Mochary for help being more empathetic. Mochary gave him the personal connection protocol -- collect personal info on team members, send brief "thinking of you" messages weekly. Thirty days later, at a two-day offsite, the entire exec team turned to Mochary: "How did you do it? He used to be a robot. And then about 30 days ago, he suddenly turned into this super-caring, super-compassionate guy." The newest hire was stunned: "You mean he wasn't like this two months ago? I just went through the recruiting process with him and concluded that he is the most empathetic CEO I have ever known or even heard of." Demonstrates that culture is built through deliberate, repeatable behaviors -- not personality transformation.

**The CEO Whose Imposter Syndrome Built Great Culture** [ll]: A CEO Matt coaches receives outstanding Glassdoor reviews. When asked why, she said: "I think it's that I feel that I'm so unqualified to be where I am that I'm just so grateful that anybody wants to work with me. And so I let them know all the time just how amazed and grateful I am." Mochary's reflection: "I would want to work at a company like that too, where I got thanked and appreciated by the CEO constantly." Imposter syndrome, channeled as genuine gratitude, becomes a powerful culture-building force.

**Brian Armstrong and Coinbase Going Remote-First** [c10]: When Armstrong declared Coinbase remote-first in May 2020, the results were immediate: "Within 30 days, Brian declared to me that they resolved all of Coinbase's recruiting challenges. Going remote was the single answer!" Meanwhile, Mochary and Sijbrandij predicted that Twitter, Square, Facebook, and Shopify -- all of which went hybrid -- would struggle with the cascade of second-class citizenship and attrition.

**The Great Reshuffle Prediction** [c10]: Mochary predicted a sorting that plays out over years: CEOs declare unified culture (Remote First or In-Person First). Workers self-select. "Both pure cultures recruit as many great people as they lose." Hybrid companies avoid short-term pain but suffer long-term as their best remote workers leave for all-remote companies, and eventually their in-person workers leave too as the company underperforms and equity value drops.
