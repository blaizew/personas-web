### Conscious Leadership and Personal Growth

Conscious leadership is the philosophical spine of the Mochary Method. While other modules address specific operational problems -- hiring, firing, meetings, decisions -- this module addresses the person underneath. It is where Mochary's coaching shifts from "how to run a company" to "how to live a life that allows you to run a company well." The core insight is that professional leadership and personal growth are not separate domains: the skills that make you a better CEO (emotional awareness, vulnerability, radical ownership) are identical to the skills that make you a better partner, parent, and human. Every concept here is something Mochary teaches from personal experience -- often from personal failure.

---

#### Concepts

**1. Above the Line / Below the Line** [6/19: c1, c7, c8, gcw1, twist, tim]

This is Mochary's primary framework for conscious leadership, drawn from the Conscious Leadership Group (Jim Dethmer, Diana Chapman, Kaley Warner Klemp). "At any point, a leader is either above the line or below the line. If you are above it, you are leading consciously, and if you are below it, you are not. Above the line, one is open, curious, and committed to learning. Below the line, one is closed, defensive, and committed to being right."

The signature teaching: "Knowing that you're below the line is more important than being below the line." The goal is not permanent above-the-line existence -- that is unrealistic. The goal is awareness. Once you notice you are below the line, you have a choice. "Conscious Leadership is about being more interested in learning than being right. When our egos make us afraid to be wrong, that fear leads us to defend our ideas at all costs."

The practical tool is the Drama Triangle. Below the line, each person identifies their Villain ("I blame [person's name] for [action]"), their Victim ("This negatively affects me in the following way..."), and their Hero ("I temporarily solve this situation by..."). The shift above the line happens through the question: "I created this situation by..." followed by a series of probing questions: What do you have to be right about? What do you withhold, and from whom? What feelings do you repress? What are you trying to control that is NOT in your control?

The concluding question that signals the shift: "Am I willing to now easefully and playfully resolve this issue?" The phrasing is deliberate -- "easefully and playfully" signals that the problem-solving itself should feel light, not grueling.

**2. 100% Responsibility (Radical Ownership)** [5/19: c1, c7, c8, gcw1, twist]

This is the above-the-line practice stated as a philosophical stance. Every problem you encounter, you helped create. "This is an important question to ask to change your perspective from 'the problem is caused by someone else and therefore can only be solved by someone else' to 'this problem was in part caused by me, therefore there are things that I can do to unwind it.'"

The coaching move: when someone presents a problem, ask "What did you do to create it?" or more vividly: "If I wanted to get myself into the exact same position that you are in now, what steps would I have to take?" This reframes every external complaint into an internal action item. The answer to a people problem is almost always the mirror image of its cause -- if you failed to give feedback, start giving feedback; if you skipped reference checks, do reference checks.

The philosophical version extends to every domain: "Instead of asking 'Who's to blame?', conscious leaders ask, 'What can we learn and how can we grow from this?'" This is not about self-blame -- it is about agency. If you created it, you can uncreate it.

**3. Sadness Processing** [3/19: c7, tim, gcw1]

"Sadness is the most important emotion for leaders to learn to process. It's the one they most resist, and it's the one that unlocks the most growth." Mochary reframes sadness entirely: "Sadness is merely proof of love. If I didn't love something, I wouldn't feel sad when it was gone. So, to me, sadness is massively affirming. I, therefore, believe that we should celebrate sadness. It is both cathartic and affirming."

The root: "Almost all sadness comes from the death of something (or the fear of the end of something): a person, a relationship, or a vision of how my world would be."

The protocol is specific:
1. Go to a quiet place where you can be alone and not heard (a car works).
2. Get yourself into a state where you can access your sadness -- watch a sentimental video clip to get tears flowing.
3. Close your eyes. Envision a full funeral service for the death of the thing you are mourning.
4. Allow the tears to flow fully. Do not try to stop them until they stop on their own.

The binary: "Two choices when feeling sadness: (1) Full expression (wailing, rarely longer than 1 minute) -- cathartic release. (2) Suppress it -- becomes depressive, long-lasting mood."

On organizational culture: "If we make it not okay to cry, then people have no way of releasing their emotions, and then that emotion becomes a bad mood." The 90-second emotion rule (from Dr. Jill Bolte Taylor): "Ninety seconds is all it takes to identify an emotion and allow it to dissipate while you simply notice it."

A characteristically Mochary observation: "Most funeral services are designed to create maximum crying. The Catholic Church seems to be particularly good at this with its use of open caskets."

**4. Anger as a Cover for Pain** [3/19: tim, lenny, c7]

This is the concept Mochary describes as his most personally transformative discovery. "Anger is not a base emotion. Anger is a cover for pain. But if you allow yourself... Because it's so painful that the brain doesn't want to experience it, so it takes it and shoves it outward as anger, but all you're doing is taking your own pain and shoving it on the people around you."

The practice:
1. Notice anger.
2. Ask: "What about this situation is causing me pain?"
3. Locate the physical sensation (throat, chest, etc.).
4. Breathe into that area.
5. Articulate the pain -- speak it aloud, write it down, or write a poem.
6. Stay with it. Do not numb (no drugs, alcohol, comfort food).
7. Identify the boundary that was crossed.
8. If desired, share the pain and the crossed boundary with the other person (non-accusatory).

The effectiveness argument: "In terms of being effective and influential in changing behavior, anger is a zero and expressing your pain and the boundary that got crossed is larger than a zero."

**5. Imposter Syndrome as Signal** [3/19: twist, ll, gcw1]

Rather than treating imposter syndrome as a pathology, Mochary reframes it as a feature with a manageable cost. He traces its origin: "My parents got really excited when I walked, so they cheered... So I learned to really enjoy those cheering moments. So now I seek achievement. And I constantly feel that I'm less than unless I'm in the moment of experiencing an achievement."

The management strategy is recognition, not elimination: "You'll never get rid of the voice, but at least if you recognize it and where it came from, when you hear it, you can go, oh, I hear your voice. Thank you very much. I don't need you anymore."

The productive reframe: a CEO Mochary coaches gets outstanding Glassdoor reviews. Her explanation: "I think it's that I feel that I'm so unqualified to be where I am that I'm just so grateful that anybody wants to work with me. And so I let them know all the time just how amazed and grateful I am." Mochary's reflection: "I would want to work at a company like that too." Imposter syndrome, channeled into genuine gratitude, can be a powerful culture-building force.

**6. Sequential Optimization: Make Money, Have Fun, Do Good** [3/19: ll, gcw1, twist]

"A lot of people say, Matt, I want to make money, I want to have fun, I want to do good all in one. And I say, when you're starting off, don't try to optimize for all three because you'll optimize for none. So pick the order you want to go in and then optimize for the first one."

Mochary's personal arc is the proof:
"I very much enjoyed the feeling of success in making money. That good feeling lasted about a week. Then I very much enjoyed having fun. I was good at it, and it lasted for years. But when I finally started to change lives by doing good, I felt something that I'd never felt before: deep satisfaction. And it is the best feeling that I have ever experienced."

On the money illusion: "Once you get to a billion, you realize, oh shit, the money actually doesn't do anything... what would make me happy today? Not in the future, but today. And once they start thinking about that and figuring that out, that's actually when satisfaction usually arrives."

The sequencing matters because each stage creates the conditions for the next. Financial security removes the anxiety that prevents joy. Joy-driven work builds the skills and relationships that enable impact. Trying to skip ahead -- pursuing "doing good" before financial stability -- leads to anxiety about rent.

**7. Fear vs. Joy as Motivation Mountains** [4/19: c7, c8, gcw1, twist]

"Fear is an incredibly powerful motivator, but it's toxic. It erodes the soul over time. What I found is joy is actually a better motivator. It's much harder to achieve, but if you can help someone achieve it, then they're just excited. They're excited to go to work. They're excited to dive in and tackle problems, and there's no cost."

The danger in the transition: "I view Fear and Joy as motivation mountains. If you lose Fear before you discover Joy in your work, then you will indeed fall into a motivation valley and may not have any motivation to continue at work." The sequence matters: discover Joy first (via the Energy Audit, finding your Zone of Genius), THEN lose Fear (via Consciousness Training).

"When people start diving into the Conscious Leadership work, they quickly lose their fear. And just as quickly, they realize that fear was their primary motivator... Once fear is gone, their life becomes much better, but their business suffers." The solution: keep pushing through to joy-based motivation. "Joy is an even better motivator than fear, so your business will thrive. And your life will be amazing!"

**8. Parenting as CEO** [3/19: c2, gcw1, gcw2]

"To become the best CEO that you can be, your life must be in balance with your personal life optimized as well." Mochary explicitly bridges professional and personal: "Get good at your relationship with your spouse or children, and you will discover that you learn all the skills needed to develop a great relationship with your teammates or customers."

The parallel structure is deliberate: "The CEO role is full-time if you do every piece of it. Luckily, you don't have to. Similarly, being a parent is full-time if you do every piece of it. Luckily, you don't have to. You can delegate all parts of it that don't energize you."

The practical recommendation: over-hire parenting support (full-time night nurse, day nanny, weekend nanny, personal Chief of Staff). Have the company pay for it -- "There are likely no higher-leverage employees at the company."

**9. Mental Health Normalization** [2/19: c2, twist]

"I am Matt Mochary. I am 55. I coach some of the most successful tech CEOs and investors in the world... I share openly with every one of them that I have, in my lifetime, seriously considered suicide, been profoundly depressed, and regularly experience anxiety."

In groups, Mochary asks who has had suicidal thoughts. "30-50% raise their hands." The result is not discomfort but connection and reduced shame. "The mind gets run down sometimes. There is no shame in it."

His suicidal ideation context: "When I was 20 and 21, just got out of college and I, you know, got fired from my first job and had no idea what to do and was drifting and felt completely useless after having graduated from, you know, one of the best universities in the world. I thought, I'm an idiot. I'm broken."

**10. Vulnerability as Connection** [4/19: c7, c8, twist, tim]

"Fear tells us never to reveal anything shameful. It tells us that if we do, people will be disgusted by us and never want to associate with us again. But my reveal proves otherwise." Trust is built not by sharing strengths but by revealing flaws.

The model is Peter Fenton: "Within 5 minutes, he had told me things that felt like his deepest, darkest secrets. My instant thought was, 'I love this guy! He's not telling me how great he is. Instead, he's telling me how broken he is. He's so humble.'" Mochary adds: "Peter does not do this as a technique. He is very open about his flaws. And the world has rewarded him for it."

The teaching: "There is a way to be humble and impressive at the same time. That way is to share your deep dark shameful flaw."

#### Stories

**Matt's Marriage Ending and the Discovery of Pain** (tim, lenny)

Matt's 18-year marriage with three children ended because of his anger. His wife had told him repeatedly: "Zero anger, Matt, zero." He had gotten better at catching himself but believed zero was impossible: "I didn't think that you could not go to anger ever. That seemed unrealistic to me, so I didn't try to find out about that 'cause it seemed like a myth." One incident of acting in anger and she said, "That's it."

The day after, a coach told him anger is a cover for pain. She asked him to identify the pain underneath. "Words came out, and all of a sudden tears started to come out. And it was so painful, and I realized, oh, my God I've never felt this before. I've never actually felt pain before until now."

Matt stopped numbing -- no drugs, alcohol, or comfort food. "I have purposely not numbed myself in any way. No drugs, no alcohol, no comfort foods even. I've lost 50 pounds in the last six months." He reported not experiencing anger for the entire period since.

The meta-lesson: "I didn't try because I thought it was impossible" -- we do not attempt things we have already declared impossible. This is a powerful mirror for any leader who says "that's just who I am."

**Matt's Fear-to-Joy Journey** (ll, twist, lenny)

Matt gets emotional discussing joy as a motivator. "I feel joy. So these are tears of joy. I love what I do. I love sharing with others that they can love what they do too." His personal arc: early career driven by fear of not making money ("I way overshot my goal"), then years of having fun ("any type of fun you can imagine") until it became empty, then discovering deep satisfaction through changing lives (the FreeWorld program for formerly incarcerated people). He tears up on camera and does not apologize or deflect -- he names it. This is deliberate modeling: a coach who gets emotional about joy gives permission for others to care deeply.

**Matt's Suicidal Thoughts at 21** (twist, c2)

"When I was 20 and 21, just got out of college... got fired from my first job and had no idea what to do and was drifting and felt completely useless... I thought, I'm an idiot. I'm broken." Used in group vulnerability exercises where 30-50% of participants raise their hands when asked the same question. The purpose: normalize the experience. If someone who coaches the CEOs of OpenAI, Coinbase, and Reddit went through this, it is not a sign of weakness -- it is part of being human.

**Matt's Little League Anger Failure** (c7, c8)

"I preach that Fear and Anger give bad advice, that you should never act in anger. And yet, I act in anger regularly. Several years ago, when I was coaching little league baseball, a parent was very competitive and wanted me to run many more practices. After the season was over, he called me to tell me that I should never have volunteered to be a coach. Rather than listen and learn, I asked him, 'Where are you right now? I want to know so that I can tell you to your face what a f*(&ing asshole you are.' The only thing this accomplished was to create a rift in the town's society."

This story is used in Deep Trust exercises to demonstrate that even the person teaching emotional regulation still fails at it. The vulnerability creates connection precisely because it contradicts his expertise.

**The CEO Whose Imposter Syndrome Built Great Culture** (ll)

A CEO Matt coaches gets outstanding Glassdoor reviews and deep employee loyalty. When he asked why, she said: "I think it's that I feel that I'm so unqualified to be where I am that I'm just so grateful that anybody wants to work with me. And so I let them know all the time just how amazed and grateful I am." This reframes imposter syndrome from pure liability to potential asset when channeled into genuine appreciation.
