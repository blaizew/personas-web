### Decision-Making

Most organizations do not have a decision-making problem. They have a decision-clarity problem. People know what the right answer is -- they just cannot bring themselves to execute it, because they are conflating the decision with the pain of implementation. Or they have no formal process, so decisions happen through the loudest voice, through consensus paralysis, or through avoidance. Mochary's decision-making system addresses all three failure modes: it separates decision from implementation, provides escalating methods for different buy-in requirements, and assigns exactly one person to every decision.

---

#### Separate the Decision from the Implementation [7/19]

This is Mochary's most counterintuitive and most powerful decision-making principle. When someone says "I don't know what to do," the problem is almost never that the right answer is unclear. The problem is that the right answer is painful to implement, and the fear of implementation is infecting their view of the decision itself.

"We are often co-mingling the decision and the implementation. The decision itself is often clear, but the implementation likely feels painful. When we think of both together, our feeling of fear about the implementation infects our view of the decision." [c1]

The framework, which Mochary explicitly attributes to Wei Deng of Clipboard Health:

1. Answer: "Who do we serve primarily?" (customer, LP, citizen, family)
2. Ask: "What is in the best interest of the person we serve primarily?" That answer is the correct decision. Write it down.
3. Now -- separately -- think about the implementation: who gets hurt, and what do they actually want? Design the implementation to address that.

"She separates the decision from the implementation, meaning she thinks about who is the stakeholder here that I'm solving for. And almost always in a company, you're solving for the customer. So what would the customer want to see happen? That's the decision." [lenny, attributed to Wee Dang]

"Oftentimes when you're having a difficult time making a decision, it's because you're conflating the decision itself with the implementation of the decision." [tim]

The teaching is direct: if you feel confused about a decision, you are almost certainly co-mingling two separate questions. Step one is always cognitive separation. Use a thought partner who is not in fear about the implementation to walk you through the process.

#### The Three Methods of Decision-Making [4/19]

Not all decisions need the same process. Mochary teaches three methods, each trading speed for buy-in:

**Method 1 (Unilateral):** The decision-maker decides and announces. Fast. Minimal buy-in. Appropriate for low-impact decisions (holiday party venue, office furniture).

**Method 2 (Consultative):** The decision-maker creates a written straw man proposal, shares it with the team, invites written and verbal feedback, then decides. "Optimal for the vast majority of important decisions." [c4]

**Method 3 (Full Brainstorm):** No straw man. The team brainstorms from scratch, then a straw man is created async, shared for feedback, and decided. Maximum buy-in, most time-consuming. "For major, core issues (Company 10-Year Vision, Company Goals, Product Roadmap)." [c4]

"Not surprisingly, the greatest benefits require the most work. If you want more buy-in and a better decision, you need to take more time in making the decision." [gcw1]

The key teaching: consensus is almost always wrong for startups. It is slow, produces mediocre compromises, and nobody feels accountable. The default should be Method 2 -- consultative, not consensus. The decision-maker hears everyone, but ultimately decides alone.

"This is not a consensus decision. The Decision-Maker creates the buy-in and confirms that she hears all ideas but ultimately does not accommodate all ideas." [c4]

#### RAPID Framework [6/19]

When a team is too large for all voices in one room, or when consensus cannot be reached within five to twenty minutes, Mochary deploys the RAPID framework, adapted from Bain & Company and introduced to him by Emilie Choi at Coinbase.

- **R** (Recommend) -- the person who identified the issue and proposed the solution
- **A** (Agree) -- colleagues whose input is vital (typically Legal)
- **P** (Perform) -- those who will enact the decision
- **I** (Input) -- those whose input is worth considering
- **D** (Decide) -- the single decision-maker

The critical variable is D. Every decision has exactly one D. Not two. Not a committee. "Most organizational dysfunction comes from unclear D. Two Ds means zero Ds." [taxonomy, c4, gcw1]

The process:
1. R writes up the Issue + Proposed Solution + names all RAPID roles
2. R solicits written input from A, P, and I
3. R schedules a Decision Meeting
4. At the meeting, D reads the document and asks questions
5. If questions can be answered in five minutes, D decides. If not, D assigns another written round with specific questions and assignees (one mulligan allowed)
6. D publishes the decision with Next Actions, each with a DRI and due date

For Type 1 decisions (irreversible), the CEO is the D. For Type 2 decisions (reversible), someone other than the CEO should be the D. [c4, gcw1]

#### Type 1 / Type 2 Decisions [5/19]

Borrowed from Bezos's 2016 shareholder letter and central to Mochary's framework:

- **Type 1:** Consequential and irreversible. One-way doors. Must be made methodically, carefully, slowly. The CEO decides.
- **Type 2:** Changeable and reversible. Two-way doors. Should be made quickly by the person closest to the information. Delegate to reports.

"Decisions are almost never Type 1." -- Mike Vernal, Sequoia [c4]

"As organizations get larger, there seems to be a tendency to use the heavy-weight Type 1 decision-making process on most decisions, including many Type 2 decisions." [gcw1]

The teaching: ninety percent of decisions are Type 2. Treating them as Type 1 creates bottlenecks and trains your reports to escalate everything. "Each time there is a decision to be made, rate it as Type 1 or Type 2." [c4] Default to pushing Type 2 decisions down to the people closest to the work.

#### The IPS Template (Issue / Proposed Solution) [6/19]

Every issue brought to a meeting must follow this format:

1. State the Issue clearly
2. "What I did to help create this situation" (100% responsibility)
3. A Proposed Solution -- bold, declarative, with actions, DRIs, and due dates
4. A Loom video describing the above (written + verbal captures both fact and nuance)

The critical rule on proposed solutions: "Even if they only have 10% confidence in their answer, they should guess that their answer is the right one. And they should phrase the Proposed Solution in very bold, directive terms." [c4, gcw1]

"This may seem aggressive, but creates a flag in the sand which generates a much more productive discussion and a quicker decision-time, which ultimately is more important than appearing to be humble." [gcw1]

The inclusion of "What I did to help create this situation" is a distinctive Mochary innovation. It simultaneously removes blame from others, reveals self-created pathways to resolution, and models accountability culture. "If you make an honest effort to declare how you contributed to creating the situation, then others will not feel blamed, and you will likely see a way to unwind the situation without relying on others to do so." [c4]

#### The Loudest Voice in the Room [4/19]

A structural problem Mochary warns about repeatedly:

"As CEO, your voice will be the 'loudest in the room.' ... Once people hear your perspective, some percentage will naturally alter their own views to more closely match yours. This % is much higher than you might imagine." [gcw1]

This means the CEO must speak last, or not at all until others have committed to their positions. The solutions:

1. **Written votes before the CEO speaks.** Have everyone write their position in a shared doc before the CEO shares theirs.
2. **Simultaneous reveal.** Thumbs-up or thumbs-down at the same time -- no sequential voting.
3. **The Challenger Role.** Rotate one team member per meeting as the designated critical voice. Junior team members in this role sets a powerful example. [c4]
4. **Amazon's junior-speaks-first rule.** "Apparently at Amazon, they require the most junior people to speak and ask questions first. Also becomes a great way to show off junior talent." [gcw1]

The concept was inspired by a conversation with Anthony Ghosn, CEO of Shogun, and CJ Reim (Amity Ventures) pointed Mochary to Robert F. Kennedy's *13 Days* about the Cuban Missile Crisis as the definitive example of this problem and its solution. [gcw1]

#### DRIs (Directly Responsible Individuals) [4/19]

Every action, metric, goal, project, meeting, and decision needs exactly one DRI. Not two. Not a committee. One person who owns the outcome.

"Stating: 'One of you, please get this done.' ... will result in no one doing the task." [c4]

Common pushback and Mochary's responses:
- "They don't have authority" -- Someone as DRI is better than no one, even if that person doesn't have full authority to influence. A junior DRI escalates via Clean Escalation to the Apex Manager.
- "They'll over-optimize" -- The DRI reports; the Manager is the Decision-Maker on direction.

The DRI system also includes a reporting obligation: do not allocate resources until the project's intended impact is defined and a DRI assigned. Kill projects ruthlessly on a quarterly cadence. [c4]

#### The Bezos Written Decision Method [3/19]

Amazon's approach, which Mochary endorses and adapts:

"He requires that anyone who wants to bring up an issue or proposal must write up the item fully prior to the decision meeting... The meeting is then spent reading the write-ups." [gcw1]

"Though time-consuming for the sponsor, this method yields extraordinarily thoughtful decisions in a short amount of time." [c4]

The written-first method solves three problems simultaneously: it ensures prepared thinking, eliminates the extrovert advantage, and compresses meeting time. "Written prep also ensures that everyone has a voice, not just those who are bold." [c4] "While the prep does take a while, the other participants' ability to ingest the material goes up by 5x, making the synchronous meeting itself that much shorter and the quality of the outcome that much greater." [c4]

#### The "I Intend To..." Framework [3/19]

From David Marquet's *Turn the Ship Around*, adapted by Mochary for startups:

"Marquet simply asked the department heads to let him know what those decisions were before they performed the action, so that Marquet could ask questions or give feedback... he had them always say 'I intend to....' That way, if Marquet wasn't available to give feedback, the department would go ahead and act anyway." [c1]

Mochary applied this to his own team: "I feel fully informed, but I am no longer the bottleneck. And the quality of decisions, actions, and buy-in from the team has gone up dramatically." [c1]

The mechanism trains people for leadership. By saying "I intend to..." before acting, reports are forced to anticipate cross-functional impacts proactively -- developing the judgment that makes them ready for more responsibility.

#### Clean Escalation [3/19]

When two people cannot resolve a disagreement, escalation must be structured, not political:

1. Person A goes to Person B directly, states the problem and proposed solution
2. If they agree, done
3. If not, they jointly request a meeting with their Apex Manager (the person with fire/hire authority over both)
4. Both prepare written positions in advance
5. All read both documents at the meeting
6. Apex Manager asks questions, then decides
7. One mulligan allowed (Apex Manager requests more info)
8. At the second meeting, decision is final

"This action is a 'dirty escalation.' Escalation because they have escalated the issue up the org chart to you. Dirty because you are only hearing one side of the story." [c4]

The critical teaching: escalation is not failure. Going behind someone's back is failure. The CEO must refuse to hear one-sided complaints. "Once you do [say 'no' to dirty escalations], your company and your life will become cleaner!" [c4]

---

#### The "Ask Twice" Framework [2/19]

From Naval Ravikant, who credits Mochary for teaching him the technique:

"When you want to find the answer to something, like a reason why somebody is doing something, like let's say somebody says no... you ask them the reason, they'll give you a reason. And then you ask them like, well, are there any other reasons? ...They'll give you a second reason. The second reason is usually the correct one." [naval]

"Usually, if you want to get the truth out of somebody in almost any context, ask it a couple of times or find out a couple of times. Usually, whatever the last thing they say is, is the real truth." [naval]

Simple technique for getting past surface-level reasoning in any decision-relevant conversation.

#### Stories

**David Marquet and the worst submarine in the Navy.** Marquet was assigned to command the worst-performing submarine in the US Navy and did not know how anything on it operated. Out of necessity, he could not make decisions himself. He asked department heads to make decisions using "I intend to..." If Marquet was unavailable, they acted anyway. The sub went from worst to best performing in the fleet, and from the lowest to the highest percentage of crew promotions -- because department heads had been training themselves to think like commanders. [c1]

**Jeff Bezos and Amazon's written method.** Bezos requires anyone bringing an issue to write it up entirely before the meeting. Meeting time is spent reading, then deciding. Amazon also uses the junior-speaks-first rule, and now holds exec team meetings in an auditorium where VPs sit in stadium-seating, silently observing. [c4, gcw1]

**The Cuban Missile Crisis.** CJ Reim pointed Mochary to Robert F. Kennedy's *13 Days* as the definitive example of the loudest-voice problem. During the Crisis, Kennedy ensured that the most junior advisors spoke first so their views would not be contaminated by senior voices. [gcw1]

**The startup that could not fire because nobody was the D.** No one had explicit authority to fire an underperformer, so the person stayed for six months past their useful life. The root cause: unclear decision authority. The fix: assign a D for every personnel decision via RAPID. [c4]

**Wei Deng at the Sequoia CEO retreat.** Mochary was coaching ten CEOs for three days. Wei Deng, CEO of Clipboard Health, consistently had the best answers -- "people wanted to hear from way more than from me, and me too." She shared the separate-decision-from-implementation framework, which Mochary now teaches as a core method. "Her answers were insanely good." [tim]

**Emilie Choi and Coinbase.** Emilie Choi, VP of Corporate and Business Development at Coinbase, introduced Mochary to the RAPID framework developed by Bain Consulting. He has used it as a core tool ever since. [gcw1]
