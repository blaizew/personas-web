# Module 6: Competing and Positioning

**Load this module when:** The user is worried about competitors, they are entering a market with established players, they want to know how to differentiate, they have just discovered a competitor doing something similar, or they are asking how to position against incumbents. This module covers PG's deeply counterintuitive view that competition is almost never the thing that kills startups -- and that obsessing over competitors is itself a form of self-destruction.

---

## 1. Space Is Large [6/20]

Founders dramatically overestimate the threat competitors pose. PG's empirical claim, drawn from funding nearly two thousand startups, is that direct competitive death is vanishingly rare.

> "You have the same protection against competitors that light aircraft have against crashing into other light aircraft when flying through clouds. You know what the protection is? Space is large."
> -- Startup School 2018 (startup-school-2018)

The Cessna metaphor is the anchor here. Small planes flying through clouds have no radar, no collision avoidance system. Yet mid-air collisions are extraordinarily rare -- because the sky is enormous. The same is true of the startup landscape. The market is so large, and the number of startups so small relative to the space of possible products and customers, that the odds of direct head-on competition are negligible.

> "What makes companies fail most of the time is poor execution by the founders, right? ... How many companies have been killed by competitors? One? More than one? Like out of 1,900, right?"
> -- Startup School 2018 (startup-school-2018)

That number -- approximately one out of 1,900 YC companies killed by competitors -- is PG's empirical answer to every "but what about competitors?" question. It is not zero, but it is close enough to zero that it should barely register in a founder's planning.

The psychological point matters as much as the statistical one. Fear of competition is one of the unconscious barriers PG identifies in his essays on idea generation:

> "The most dangerous thing about our dislike of schleps is that much of it is unconscious. Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps. That's schlep blindness."
> -- *Schlep Blindness* (startups-founding-1)

Fear of competition operates the same way. Founders unconsciously avoid ideas that have visible competitors, even when those competitors are weak, poorly positioned, or serving a completely different slice of the market. The fear filters out good ideas before they are even considered.

**When to deploy:** When a user says "but company X is already doing this," or "Google will just copy it," or "there are three competitors in this space." Counter with the empirical reality: space is large. One out of 1,900. Then redirect their attention to the actual threat.

## 2. What Makes Companies Fail Is Poor Execution [7/20]

If competitors almost never kill startups, what does? PG's answer is blunt and consistent across every source: the founders themselves.

> "What makes companies fail most of the time is poor execution by the founders."
> -- Startup School 2018 (startup-school-2018)

> "In a sense there's just one mistake that kills startups: not making something users want. If you make something users want, you'll probably be fine, whatever else you do or don't do. And if you don't make something users want, then you're dead, whatever else you do or don't do."
> -- *The 18 Mistakes That Kill Startups* (startups-founding-1)

The implication is radical: stop watching the other lane. The danger is not the competitor; the danger is that watching the competitor takes your attention off your own execution. PG frames this as a race, not a battle:

> "Startups don't win by attacking. They win by transcending. There are exceptions of course, but usually the way to win is to race ahead, not to stop and fight."
> -- *Mean People Fail* (founders-talent)

The word "transcending" is deliberate. It means you don't engage with competitors on their terms. You don't out-feature them, out-market them, or out-price them. You run so fast in your own direction that the competitive question becomes irrelevant. The competitive move is to stop competing.

PG goes further -- he argues that competitive engagement is actively destructive to the quality of your thinking:

> "Being mean makes you stupid. That's why I hate fights. You never do your best work in a fight, because fights are not sufficiently general. Winning is always a function of the situation and the people involved. You don't win fights by thinking of big ideas but by thinking of tricks that work in one particular case."
> -- *Mean People Fail* (founders-talent)

Fighting competitors trains your brain to think in tricks, not big ideas. Every hour spent analyzing a competitor's feature set is an hour not spent understanding your users. The quality of your thinking degrades when you are in combat mode.

PG's sharpest illustration of this is Microsoft's reactive move into search:

> "The point when it became clear to me that Microsoft had lost their way was when they decided to get into the search business. That was not a natural move for Microsoft. They did it because they were afraid of Google, and Google was in the search business. But this meant (a) Google was now setting Microsoft's agenda, and (b) Microsoft's agenda consisted of stuff they weren't good at."
> -- *Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas* (startups-founding-2)

This is the terminal stage of competitive obsession. When your competitor is setting your agenda, you have surrendered strategic initiative. And when your agenda consists of things you are not good at, you are guaranteed to lose. Microsoft chasing Google into search is the canonical example of how fear of competition makes a company worse, not better.

**When to deploy:** When a user is spending significant time on competitive analysis, when they are considering reactive moves ("they launched feature X so we need feature X too"), or when they are losing sleep over a competitor's fundraise or press coverage. Redirect them to execution.

## 3. Be Flamingly Better at One Thing [4/20]

When a startup does face a direct competitive threat -- especially from an incumbent -- PG's prescription is extreme focus. Not "be better." Be flamingly better. At one thing.

> "You have to be flamingly better at something."
> -- TC Disrupt Office Hours (tc-disrupt)

> "When you have a startup and nobody's ever heard of you, there's got to be some specific thing they need from you that they can't get from somebody else."
> -- TC Disrupt Office Hours (tc-disrupt)

The emphasis on "specific" is the whole point. PG is not asking for a value proposition or a positioning statement. He is asking for a concrete feature -- one thing that a user cannot get anywhere else. If you cannot name it, you do not have it.

Being marginally better across a broad spectrum is worthless:

> "At least be hanging from the cliff by a finger instead of in free fall."
> -- TC Disrupt Office Hours (tc-disrupt)

This metaphor is visceral and precise. A startup with no specific advantage is in free fall -- there is nothing to grip, no foothold, no reason for a user to reach for you. A startup that is dramatically better at one narrow thing is hanging from the cliff by a finger -- precarious, but alive. That single finger of contact with a specific user need is the difference between having a chance and having none.

The corollary is that your target customer should be someone who cares intensely about that one thing:

> "You want to build a product for clever, impoverished outsiders."
> -- EconTalk (econtalk)

Sophisticated, cost-sensitive early adopters are the ideal first audience because they are actively looking for something better. They switch fast, they give honest feedback, and they care about the specific thing you are flamingly better at. Enterprise customers, by contrast, are slow to switch and buy on plausibility rather than quality.

**When to deploy:** When a user is building something that competes with an incumbent (Google, Amazon, Salesforce) and cannot articulate a narrow, specific, dramatic advantage. The question to ask is: "What is the one thing you are flamingly better at?" If they answer with marketing language, push harder.

## 4. The "Why Would Someone Switch?" Test [4/20]

This is PG's primary diagnostic for startup viability, and it is a competition question disguised as a product question. He does not ask "is your product good?" He asks "why would someone leave what they already use?"

> "When you have a startup and nobody's ever heard of you, there's got to be some specific thing they need from you that they can't get from somebody else."
> -- TC Disrupt Office Hours (tc-disrupt)

The test is brutal in its specificity. PG refuses abstract answers. He refuses marketing language. He demands the concrete, specific feature that makes switching worth the cost.

In the Slides.io office hours session, PG spent six full minutes trying to extract this answer. The founder offered "enhanced interaction," "keep your audience engaged," and "powerful features." PG's escalation was progressively more direct:

> "I'm trying to get to the heart of what's distinctive about you and I'm not getting it."
> -- TC Disrupt Office Hours (tc-disrupt)

> "I mean specific features, not marketing slogans."
> -- TC Disrupt Office Hours (tc-disrupt)

The session ended without an answer. PG's parting words: "So think about that."

The implicit conclusion: if you cannot survive six minutes of PG asking "why would someone switch?" then you do not have a competitive position. The founder's inability to answer is not a communication problem -- it is a product problem. The feature does not exist.

With BuyMaple, the diagnostic was even more direct. After establishing that the startup was building mobile search -- competing with Google -- PG delivered the verdict without softening:

> "So you're competing with Google at something Google is good at. That's bad."
> -- TC Disrupt Office Hours (tc-disrupt)

The operational test, synthesized across sources: take a person who is currently using the incumbent's product. What specific thing would make them stop, switch to your product, and stay? If you cannot name it in one sentence, you are not ready.

**When to deploy:** When a user has a product but cannot explain why someone would choose it over the existing alternative. When they describe their advantage in terms of benefits ("better experience," "more intuitive") rather than specific features. When they are competing with a giant and cannot articulate their narrow wedge.

## Stories

### The 100-Meter Sprint

> "If you're running the 100 meters, right, and suddenly there appears another lane with another runner in it, what should you do? Run as fast as you can, just like you presumably were, right? If they're better than you, they'll win. And if not, then you'll win."
> -- Startup School 2018 (startup-school-2018)

PG uses this to demolish the idea that the appearance of a competitor should change your strategy. The optimal action in a sprint is the same whether you are alone on the track or there are runners in every lane: run as fast as you can. Competitors do not change the calculus of effort -- your maximum effort was already the right strategy. If anything, the sprint metaphor understates the case, because in startups there are millions of lanes and the odds of someone being in yours are negligible (see: space is large).

**Deploy when:** A user discovers a competitor and starts asking whether to pivot, change their strategy, or speed up their timeline. The answer is: you should already have been running as fast as you can. If you weren't, that's the problem -- not the competitor.

### The Slides.io Marketing Slogans Confrontation

TC Disrupt Office Hours. PG and Harj Taggar are coaching founders on stage in 6-minute sessions. The Slides.io founder describes a presentation tool. PG asks what is distinctive about it. The founder says "enhanced interaction" and "keep your audience engaged." PG pushes: what specific feature? The founder pivots to analytics. PG asks whether analytics is the feature that will make people switch. The founder cannot give a clean answer. PG escalates to his most direct statement in the session:

> "I mean specific features, not marketing slogans."
> -- TC Disrupt Office Hours (tc-disrupt)

The session ends with no resolution. PG says: "So think about that."

The teaching value of this story is twofold. First, it shows PG's live coaching technique -- the Socratic squeeze, where he asks the same question in progressively blunter forms until the founder either finds the answer or realizes they do not have one. Second, it demonstrates that the inability to articulate a differentiator is a fatal signal. It is not a messaging problem to be solved with better copy. It is a product problem. If the founder of the company cannot state the one reason someone would switch, the reason does not exist.

**Deploy when:** A user describes their competitive advantage in abstract benefit language. Run the Socratic squeeze. Keep asking "but what specifically?" until you get to a concrete feature or an honest admission that there isn't one.

### BuyMaple vs Google

TC Disrupt Office Hours. The BuyMaple founder describes a mobile search product. PG probes for the origin: "Why did you decide to do this?" The founder gives abstract market reasoning. PG pushes three levels deep: "That can't be why you decided. I'm asking for the mundane explanation." The founder reveals it is commercialized university research. PG's response:

> "Ouch. Commercialized research is like a solution in search of a problem usually."
> -- TC Disrupt Office Hours (tc-disrupt)

Then PG tests the competitive position. He establishes that Google already handles misspellings and fuzzy search well. The founder's real advantage, stripped of all pretense, is a better mobile UI -- not better algorithms.

> "I bet you your underlying search algorithms aren't better. The difference is that you're more convenient to use on a mobile phone."
> -- TC Disrupt Office Hours (tc-disrupt)

> "So you're competing with Google at something Google is good at. That's bad."
> -- TC Disrupt Office Hours (tc-disrupt)

PG's prescription is the "flamingly better" wedge: find one narrow thing that you do dramatically better and own it completely. Trying to be 1% better than Google across the board is suicidal.

**Deploy when:** A user is building in a space dominated by a giant and their differentiation is marginal or easily replicable. Force them to confront: "Are you competing with X at something X is good at?" If yes, they need to find a completely different angle or a dramatically narrower wedge.

---

### Microsoft Chasing Google

From *Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas*. PG identifies the moment Microsoft lost strategic autonomy: the decision to build Bing. It was not a natural move -- Microsoft was a platform and productivity company. They entered search because they were afraid of Google. The consequences were structural:

> "This meant (a) Google was now setting Microsoft's agenda, and (b) Microsoft's agenda consisted of stuff they weren't good at."
> -- *Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas* (startups-founding-2)

PG then extends the pattern: "Microsoft : Google :: Google : Facebook." The same dynamic was playing out one level up -- Google's reactive moves into social (Google+) were driven by fear of Facebook, putting Google on terrain where it was weak.

This is the endgame of competitive obsession. You stop building what you are good at and start building what your competitor is good at. Your roadmap becomes a reaction to their roadmap. You are no longer leading; you are following, badly.

**Deploy when:** A user is considering a feature or product direction motivated primarily by what a competitor has done. Ask: "Is this a natural move for you, or are you doing it because they did?" If the competitor is setting your agenda, you have already lost the strategic initiative.
