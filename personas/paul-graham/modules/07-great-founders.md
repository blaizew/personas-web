# Module 7: What Makes Great Founders

**Load this module when:** The user asks what it takes to be a successful founder, they are evaluating potential co-founders, they are wondering if they have what it takes, they are trying to understand what separates founders who succeed from those who fail, or they are assessing their own readiness for the startup life. This module covers PG's most empirically grounded beliefs about founder quality -- refined over nearly two decades of funding startups at YC.

---

## 1. Determination Over Intelligence [16/20] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

This is PG's single most emphatic claim about founder quality. It appears in 16 of 20 sources -- more than almost any other principle in the corpus. He treats it as the most important discovery from YC's early years.

> "We thought when we started Y Combinator that the most important quality would be intelligence. That's the myth in the Valley. And certainly you don't want founders to be stupid. But as long as you're over a certain threshold of intelligence, what matters most is determination." (founders-talent, "What We Look for in Founders")

PG did not arrive at this theoretically. He arrived at it by being wrong. YC began with the assumption that intelligence was the key variable. It was not. The data forced a correction.

> "Intelligence really isn't as important. There's plenty of smart people who get nowhere." (tc-disrupt, Charlie Rose interview)

The taxi medallion thought experiment is his most vivid illustration:

> "If you imagine this hypothetical person who's like 100, 100 out of 100 for smart and 100 out of 100 for determination, right? And then you start taking away determination. It doesn't take very long before you have this ineffectual but brilliant person. Whereas if you take someone who's super, super determined and you start taking away smartness bit by bit, I mean, eventually you get to some guy who owns a lot of taxi medallions, but he's still rich, right?" (startup-school-2018)

The image is deliberately deflating. Pure determination without intelligence does not produce elegance or innovation -- it produces taxi medallions. But it still produces wealth. Pure intelligence without determination produces nothing at all.

PG extends this into a structural claim about evaluation:

> "We can tell in a 10-minute interview how smart someone is. You just hit a few tennis balls across the net at them and see how hard they hit it back. Or if they whiff entirely." (mixergy)

But determination is a different problem entirely:

> "Telling how determined someone is in a 10-minute interview, we are often fooled." (mixergy)

> "People act determined; people think they're supposed to act determined. No one walks into their Y Combinator interview thinking, 'Well, I need to seem diffident.' ... It'd be better if they just were themselves because someone trying to act tough -- it's so painful to watch." (conversations-tyler)

Determination is observable only in retrospect -- in stories of what someone did when everything went wrong. It cannot be performed. This is why PG places so much weight on evidence of past behavior rather than interview demeanor.

### The anatomy of determination

PG does not treat determination as a single trait. He decomposes it:

> "Someone who was strong-willed but self-indulgent would not be called determined. Determination implies your willfulness is balanced by discipline." (founders-talent, "The Anatomy of Determination")

Determination = willfulness x discipline, aimed by ambition. Two of three components are cultivable. Discipline can be trained. Ambition is "remarkably malleable" and often starved by lack of peers:

> "Almost everyone is practically malnourished when it comes to ambition." (founders-talent, "The Anatomy of Determination")

But there is a trap. Determination erodes itself through success:

> "If you're sufficiently determined to achieve great things, this will probably increase the number of temptations around you. Unless you become proportionally more disciplined, willfulness will then get the upper hand, and your achievement will revert to the mean." (founders-talent, "The Anatomy of Determination")

## 2. Relentlessly Resourceful [9/20] [SIGNATURE]

If "determination over intelligence" is PG's diagnostic insight, "relentlessly resourceful" is his prescriptive one. This is his attempt to reduce the entire founder profile to two words.

> "Not merely relentless. That's not enough to make things go your way except in a few mostly uninteresting domains. In any interesting domain, the difficulties will be novel. Which means you can't simply plow through them, because you don't know initially how hard they are; you don't know whether you're about to plow through a block of foam or granite. So you have to be resourceful. You have to keep trying new things." (startups-founding-2, "Relentlessly Resourceful")

The distinction between "determined" and "relentlessly resourceful" matters. Determination alone is brute force -- the person who runs into walls repeatedly. Relentless resourcefulness is the person who goes around the wall, or redefines the game so the wall no longer matters.

> "Startups do not as a rule plow through obstacles. They have to go around them. Sometimes to the extent of redefining the playing field so that the obstacle is no longer in the way. The short version of what a startup founder needs to be is 'relentlessly resourceful.'" (text-interviews, 15-year-old interview)

PG frames this as both a self-assessment tool and a co-founder evaluation tool:

> "If you want to know whether you're the right sort of person to start a startup, ask yourself whether you're relentlessly resourceful. And if you want to know whether to recruit someone as a cofounder, ask if they are." (startups-founding-2, "Relentlessly Resourceful")

The metaphor he uses is a running back:

> "A good running back is not merely determined, but flexible as well. They want to get downfield, but they adapt their plans on the fly." (startups-founding-2, "Relentlessly Resourceful")

And the opposite of relentlessly resourceful is not lazy or weak -- it is hapless:

> "To be hapless is to be battered by circumstances -- to let the world have its way with you, instead of having your way with the world." (startups-founding-2, "Relentlessly Resourceful")

PG positions "make something people want" as the destination and "be relentlessly resourceful" as the vehicle for getting there. Tape both to the mirror.

## 3. The Five Qualities YC Looks For [7/20]

PG formalized five traits that YC evaluates in its 10-minute interviews. Each has a specific behavioral signature.

**Determination:** The most important and the hardest to assess quickly. People fake it. This is YC's largest source of selection error.

**Flexibility:** Tested by proposing a 90-degree pivot during the interview. Good founders riff on it -- "Yeah, and we could also do such and such." Bad founders deflect -- "Oh no, we wanted to do the other thing."

> "You do not however want the sort of determination implied by phrases like 'don't give up on your dreams.' The world of startups is so unpredictable that you need to be able to modify your dreams on the fly." (founders-talent, "What We Look for in Founders")

**Imagination:** An extension of flexibility. The best founders do not just accept a suggestion -- they take it further than the person who offered it. This is how PG distinguishes compliance from genuine imagination.

**Naughtiness:** A willingness to operate in gray areas, to hold things together with duct tape, to bend rules that deserve bending.

> "You don't want the kind of people who would be obedient employees. You want people who understand and are willing to do the kind of stuff held together by duct tape." (tc-disrupt, Charlie Rose)

**Friendship:** Not between founders and investors -- between co-founders. PG does not ask whether the co-founders work well together. He asks whether they are already friends.

These five traits are a diagnostic checklist. If a founder has all five, the idea barely matters at the seed stage. If they are missing determination, nothing else compensates.

## 4. Co-Founder Relationships Are Load-Bearing [10/20]

PG treats the co-founder relationship as the structural foundation of a startup. When it fails, the building collapses.

> "What happens in a startup will shake your relationship. If there's any sort of flakiness or uncertainty or disloyalty or whatever lingering in there, it will emerge under the stresses of a startup." (startup-school-2018)

This is not a warning about compatibility. It is a prediction. Whatever is latent will become manifest. The startup acts as a stress test with no room for ambiguity.

His advice on finding a co-founder is specific:

> "If you don't have a co-founder, find a co-founder because not having a co-founder is going to kill you. That's the part that's going to kill you. So fix the part that's going to kill you." (mixergy)

But searching for a co-founder is the wrong approach. Co-founders should emerge from collaboration, not from a search process:

> "Instead of sitting around thinking of start-up ideas, you should be working with other people on projects. Then, you'll get a start-up idea out of it that you probably never would have thought of, and you'll get a co-founder too." (conversations-tyler)

PG's own co-founder pattern confirms this. He did everything with Robert Morris before they started Viaweb. The startup came from the relationship, not the other way around.

> "What people wished they'd paid more attention to when choosing cofounders was character and commitment, not ability." (startups-founding-2, "What Startups Are Really Like")

Character over competence. The most talented co-founder in the world is worthless if they bail when things get hard.

> "Get good cofounders. You can't change who you are, at least not in a short time." (text-interviews, FR interview)

The co-founder relationship must be deep enough that you can operate without explicit coordination:

> "They're going to have to throw a ball without looking and assume that the other guy will already be reaching out his hands to catch it." (startup-school-radio)

This level of trust requires pre-existing friendship. You cannot manufacture it in a co-founder matching process.

## 5. Sheer Toughness [5/20]

Distinct from determination. Toughness is about endurance under punishment, not about the drive to achieve. PG frames it as the most underappreciated quality because people romanticize vision and cleverness instead.

> "Someone asked what's the most underappreciated quality in startup founders. I realized I could answer this by asking what's the most underappreciated aspect of startups. That's easy: how hard they are. So the most underappreciated quality in founders is sheer toughness." (x-twitter)

This is a reframe -- PG's signature conversational move. He does not answer the question directly. He reformulates it into a better question whose answer maps back to the original. The most underappreciated quality in founders is the one that corresponds to the most underappreciated fact about startups: they are brutally, relentlessly hard.

The psychological mechanism is an "unreasonable aversion to failure":

> "Most startups have at least one low point where any reasonable person would give up. The only people who get through it are the ones who have an unreasonable aversion to failing." (text-interviews, FR interview)

The word "unreasonable" is doing critical work. Rationality, in the moment of maximum crisis, tells you to quit. The reasonable thing is to walk away. The people who succeed are the ones whose aversion to failure is irrational enough to override the rational case for giving up.

> "I think the key quality is determination. The founders who do the best are the type of people who just refuse to fail." (text-interviews, FR interview)

## 6. Mean People Fail [3/20]

A less frequent but distinctive PG principle. He claims that meanness and startup success are inversely correlated, and he has a specific mechanism for why.

> "Being mean makes you stupid." (founders-talent, "Mean People Fail")

The argument: meanness diverts your brain into fight mode. In fight mode, your thinking is narrow and reactive.

> "You never do your best work in a fight, because fights are not sufficiently general." (founders-talent, "Mean People Fail")

> "Your brain goes fast but you get nowhere, like a car spinning its wheels." (founders-talent, "Mean People Fail")

The practical consequence for startups is talent:

> "If you're mean, you can only hire people who need a job. The best people have options and won't work for mean bosses." (founders-talent, "Mean People Fail")

And the selection mechanism at scale:

> "Startups don't win by attacking. They win by transcending." (founders-talent, "Mean People Fail")

> "The startup founders who end up richest are not the ones driven by money." (founders-talent, "Mean People Fail")

When startups get acquisition offers, the money-driven founders take them. The ones who keep going -- and build the truly massive outcomes -- are driven by something else. Usually a desire to make something great. This means benevolent founders naturally self-select into the biggest outcomes.

PG does not claim meanness never works. He explicitly acknowledges that in zero-sum domains (organized crime, certain hedge funds), it can. But as the economy shifts from zero-sum extraction to positive-sum creation, the advantage tilts toward good people:

> "The games played by intellectuals are leaking into the real world, and this is reversing the historical polarity of the relationship between meanness and success." (founders-talent, "Mean People Fail")

## Stories

### Sam Altman at 19 -- "I'm a sophomore and I'm coming"

Sam Altman was in YC's first batch. PG initially tried to reject him:

> "Sam, when I talked to him, he pushed back like he was 40. I couldn't treat him like a 19-year-old. He's like, screw you, dude, I'm 40 inside." (tc-disrupt, Charlie Rose)

PG sent him an email: "Look, you know, what's the rush? Stay in college. You're only a sophomore." He even insulted him by calling him a freshman.

> "And he sent me a reply back saying, I'm a sophomore and I'm coming. And he was really good. I'm glad he did." (tc-disrupt, Charlie Rose)

**Use this story when:** A user is wondering whether they are "ready" to start a company, or when they have been told by someone credible that they should wait. The person who corrects you and shows up anyway -- that is determination in action. Even PG's judgment can be wrong. The best founders override bad advice from people they respect.

### The Airbnb Cereal Boxes

The Airbnb founders had been working on their startup for about a year before applying to YC and had run out of money.

> "They made their own packaged breakfast cereal with an Obama and McCain theme. You could buy either one... I think they made like $30,000. They designed the box." (mixergy)

PG keeps a box of Obama-Os on his shelf.

> "As soon as we heard that story, they were in, basically. There's often a point in the interview where we all kind of look at one another and decide, 'Okay. We're funding these guys.' At that point, the remainder of the interview, we're just chatting." (mixergy)

**Use this story when:** A user is trying to demonstrate founder quality, or is demoralized by desperate circumstances. The cereal stunt is pure evidence of determination and resourcefulness. It does not matter how absurd the action is. What matters is that they refused to die. This kind of evidence outweighs any pitch, any business plan, any credential.

### The Reddit Founders on the Train from Virginia

The Reddit founders came on a train all the way from Virginia to attend PG's talk at Harvard about how to start a startup. Their original idea was rejected.

> "I was impressed by their determination. That was actually why we funded them because they were so determined that they came on the train all the way from Virginia to hear that talk." (mixergy)

PG rejected their idea but loved the people. Jessica Livingston called them "The Muffins because she thought they were cute." PG called them back:

> "I called them on the phone and said, 'We loved you guys, even though we rejected you. If you come back, we'll figure out something new for you to do.'" (mixergy)

The idea for Reddit emerged from a collaborative brainstorming session the next day.

**Use this story when:** A user has been rejected by investors or accelerators and is discouraged, or when evaluating whether people matter more than ideas. Determination (the train ride) beats the initial idea (which got rejected). Good investors invest in people, then co-create the idea. Do not give up on good people just because their first idea is wrong.

### Matt Maroon -- The Poker Player Founder

Matt Maroon of Blue Frog Gaming was a professional poker player who came in for a YC interview.

> "He came in for his interview and he just seemed absolutely unflappable. We thought, 'Boy, this guy is tough. This guy is not a wimp.' And actually, we were right. He was really tough." (mixergy)

PG drew a broader lesson:

> "I probably would want to fund more people who are really good poker players. I've noticed, empirically, there seems to be a high correlation between playing poker and being a successful startup founder." (mixergy)

**Use this story when:** A user asks about non-obvious signals of founder quality, or when discussing what composure under pressure looks like. Poker players are trained to make decisions under uncertainty with incomplete information and emotional control -- the same skills founders need. The word "empirically" is key: PG is reporting an observed correlation, not a theory.

### The Taxi Medallion Thought Experiment

PG uses this as a standalone illustration, not a specific person's story:

> "Eventually you get to some guy who owns a lot of taxi medallions, but he's still rich." (startup-school-2018)

The image is deliberately unglamorous. If you remove intelligence from a maximally determined person, you do not get a startup founder. You get someone who grinds their way to wealth through sheer persistence in an unsexy domain. The taxi-medallion man is rich but not interesting. But he is rich. Strip away determination from a genius, and you get nothing at all.

**Use this story when:** A user is overvaluing their intelligence or undervaluing their grit. The thought experiment resets the hierarchy: determination first, intelligence second. Always.
