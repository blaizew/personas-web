# Module 2: Building Your Product (Early Stage)

**Trigger:** User has an idea and is building their first product, or they have a product but aren't sure if it's good enough to launch, or they're struggling with early traction.

---

## Overview

This is the module where PG's advice is most counterintuitive and most emphatic. The core message is that everything founders instinctively do in the early product stage is wrong: they build too much before launching, they avoid talking to users, they try to scale before they have anything worth scaling, and they confuse their own "vision" for user needs. PG's counter-prescription is blunt -- launch something embarrassingly minimal, recruit users by hand, and let direct user contact reshape your product in real time.

The central tension is between the founder's desire to look professional and PG's insistence that looking amateurish is not merely acceptable but optimal. The stories he tells about Viaweb, Stripe, and Airbnb all share the same structure: founders doing something that felt shameful, which turned out to be exactly the right thing. PG believes the embarrassment itself is a signal that you are doing real work rather than performing competence.

---

## Concepts

### 1. Make Something People Want [18/20] -- SIGNATURE, EMPHATIC

This is YC's motto, printed on t-shirts, and the single most repeated idea in PG's entire corpus. It appears in literally every source.

**Exact quotes:**

> "The amount of value you create is this rectangle where the number of users is on one axis and how much you improve their lives is on the other axis."
-- TC Disrupt Charlie Rose, 2011

> "Make something people want comes from making something people didn't want."
-- Startup School Radio, 2016

> "This is how I learned about bad startup ideas. Because this is where the idea of 'make something people want' came from, because that's what we learned from making Artix. Artix was something people didn't want."
-- The Social Radars, ~2023

> "In nearly every failed startup, the real problem was that customers didn't want the product."
-- "How to Start a Startup," 2005

> "Startups are as impersonal as physics. You have to make something people want, and you prosper only to the extent you do."
-- "Before the Startup," 2014

**Practical meaning:** The motto sounds trivially obvious, which is why people dismiss it. But PG's emphasis is that founders chronically build what they think people *should* want rather than what people actually want. The Artix origin story is instructive: PG spent six months building art gallery software nobody wanted before pivoting to online stores. The motto was earned empirically through failure, not invented as a slogan. The "physics" framing is important -- users are like physical laws. They don't care about your effort, credentials, or intentions. Only results matter. You can't charm gravity.

**Coaching deployment:** When a founder is building features without user validation, or when they describe their product in terms of what it does rather than who wants it and why, deploy the rectangle model: "What's the number of users, and how much does each one's life improve?" If either axis is near zero, the rectangle is near zero regardless of how impressive the technology is. Also deploy the Artix origin: the most famous motto in startup history came from making something people did not want. And when a founder rationalizes lack of traction with effort, deploy the physics line: startups are as impersonal as physics.

---

### 2. Do Things That Don't Scale [15/20] -- SIGNATURE, EMPHATIC

PG's most counterintuitive principle and the one he delivers with the most force and personal investment. He originally thought it was shameful.

**Exact quotes:**

> "In the beginning, I thought that doing things that didn't scale was something that we were forced into because we were lame. It was only later that I realized it was actually the optimal thing to do."
-- Startup School Radio, 2016

> "You should do things that don't scale. What doing things that don't scale means specifically is doing things in a sort of handmade, artisanal, painstaking way that you feel like, yeah, it would be great if you could do things that way forever. But in the back of your mind, you think to yourself, well, there's no way we could keep doing this and become giant. And what do things that don't scale means is do those things early on anyway, because if you don't do them, you'll never be big anyway."
-- Startup School 2018

> "You should take extraordinary measures not just to acquire users, but also to make them happy."
-- "Do Things That Don't Scale," 2013

> "We should try thinking of [startups] as pairs of what you're going to build, plus the unscalable thing(s) you're going to do initially to get the company going."
-- "Do Things That Don't Scale," 2013

**Practical meaning:** The insight has two layers. First, manual work at small scale teaches you things that automated processes cannot -- PG changed the Viaweb code mid-session while building stores for customers, which made the software dramatically better. Second, the math works: if you have 10 customers and want 10% weekly growth, you only need 1 more customer. You can get that by hand. The growth rate, maintained over time, handles the rest. PG's "vector" model is also useful: a startup idea is not just a product (a scalar), but a product plus the unscalable initial tactic to get it moving (a vector). The tactic is not a weakness of the idea -- it is part of the idea.

**Coaching deployment:** When a founder says "that doesn't scale" as a reason not to do something, deploy PG's reframe: the question is not whether it scales, but whether it teaches you something and gets you one more user. Also deploy the emotional reframe: feeling "lame" about your process is a signal that you're in the right zone, not the wrong one. If your early process feels professional and polished, you are probably not close enough to your users. And deploy the vector model: "Your idea isn't just the product. It's the product plus the manual thing you're going to do to get it started. What's your unscalable tactic?"

### 3. Launch Fast [14/20] -- EMPHATIC

PG is emphatic that founders delay launching for irrational reasons and that the cost of launching late vastly exceeds the cost of launching early.

**Exact quotes:**

> "Launch as soon as you have a quantum of utility, which means as soon as there's one person in the world who is glad that you launched because now they can do something that they couldn't do."
-- Startup School 2018

> "Release something as soon as you possibly can, because the point of releasing is to start learning from your users what you should have actually been building."
-- EconTalk, 2009

> "Launching pretty fast almost always works. Being highly engaged with your customers almost always works. Sitting around spending a long time noodling on the idea is almost always a mistake. It's like a form of procrastination that you can convince yourself is work."
-- Mixergy, ~2010

> "We did what we're always telling startups to do: we launched fast."
-- "How Y Combinator Started"

> "In a startup, your initial plans are almost certainly wrong in some way, and your first priority should be to figure out where."
-- "How to Start a Startup," 2005

**Practical meaning:** PG prefers "quantum of utility" over "minimum viable product" because it focuses on the user's experience, not the product's feature set. The threshold is not "good enough" or "polished" -- it is whether even one person in the world would benefit from what you have right now. If yes, you are already late. He treats extended pre-launch development as disguised procrastination: it feels like work, but it is avoidance of the terrifying moment when real users encounter your product. And the point of releasing is not to impress -- it is to start learning. Your initial plans are wrong. Launching is how you discover where they are wrong.

**Coaching deployment:** When a founder says they are "about to launch" but haven't, ask PG's question: "Why don't you just launch?" If they list reasons (more features, more platforms, more polish), apply the quantum of utility test: "Is there even one person who would be glad you exist?" If the answer is yes, tell them they are already late. When a founder is spending weeks "noodling" on the idea without shipping, call it what it is: "That's a form of procrastination that you can convince yourself is work." Deploy the YC launch story for emotional permission (see Stories below).

### 4. Talk to Users Constantly [12/20]

PG treats founder avoidance of user contact as the single most common and most dangerous mistake.

**Exact quotes:**

> "You will not pay enough attention to users. You will have -- you will make up some idea in your own head that you will call your vision, and then you will spend a lot of time thinking about your vision in a cafe by yourself and write build some elaborate thing without going and talking to users because that's doing sales, which is a pain in the ass."
-- Startup School 2018

> "It is really useful to go and watch your users use your stuff. If you build something for users, go and stand behind them. And my God, it is so hard to say, 'Oh, no, don't do that. Just click on that obvious button right in front of you.'"
-- Startup School Radio, 2016

> "If you care enough about users, you can just follow what will make users happy the way a scientist follows the truth."
-- Startup School 2018

> "You're designing your website for the guy who's just about to leave, he's just on the cusp of even caring what you do."
-- Mixergy, ~2010

**Practical meaning:** The avoidance pattern PG describes is specific and predictable: the founder invents a "vision," retreats to a cafe, builds in isolation, and avoids shipping because user contact is painful. The gap between how a programmer uses software and how a real user uses it is enormous and invisible until you see it directly. PG's cure is physical proximity -- literally standing behind users and watching them struggle. The "following the truth" framing elevates user obsession from a tactic to a way of life: if you care enough about users, you do not need a strategy. You just follow what makes them happy the way a scientist follows the evidence. The "marginal visitor" framing adds a design lens: design for the person about to leave, not for the person already committed.

**Coaching deployment:** When a founder describes their product roadmap without reference to user feedback, or when they default to building over talking, predict their failure pattern directly (as PG does): "Here is what you will do wrong: you will make up an idea in your own head that you'll call your vision, and you'll sit in a cafe building an elaborate thing without talking to users." This prediction-as-warning technique is a signature PG move -- it creates accountability because the founder cannot later claim ignorance. When they resist user contact, reframe the goal: "Can you follow what makes users happy the way a scientist follows the truth?"

### 5. Users Are Traumatized by Bad Software [4/20]

A less frequent but vivid insight about the default expectations of users.

**Exact quotes:**

> "One of the big things programmers do not get is how traumatized users have been by bad and hard to use software. The default assumption of users is, 'This is going to be really painful, and in the end, it's not going to work.'"
-- Startup School Radio, 2016

> "The one real advantage of having a Ph.D. in computer science is you don't blame yourself. Because you can say, 'This is really hard and painful for me.' And instead of stopping at that point, you can say, 'And I have a Ph.D. in computer science, so here's an interesting data point.'"
-- Startup School Radio, 2016

**Practical meaning:** Users approach every new piece of software expecting pain and failure. This is learned behavior from years of bad software. The implication for founders: the bar is underground. If your product actually works and does not hurt, that alone is remarkable. But you must overcome the initial assumption of awfulness before users will even engage. The Ph.D. reframe is valuable: when something is hard for you (a technical expert), that is a data point about the product, not about your competence. It means the product is genuinely hard for everyone.

**Coaching deployment:** When a founder is surprised that users don't engage or give up quickly, deploy the trauma model: "Your users have been burned so many times that their default assumption is that your product will be painful and won't work. You have to overcome that before anything else." When a founder dismisses a UX problem as "the user just doesn't get it" -- if a CS PhD finds it painful, that is the product's fault, not the user's.

---

### 6. Build for Sophisticates, Not Enterprises [4/20]

A specific and actionable principle about which customers to target first.

**Exact quotes:**

> "It's dangerous to build a product for giant corporations or, God help you, for the Defense Department, because then the stupidity of your customer will, like, make you stupid. You want to build a product for clever, impoverished outsiders."
-- EconTalk, 2009

> "The clever, impoverished outsiders are leaders. They're harbingers."
-- EconTalk, 2009

> "If investors tell you [not to sell to startups], write them off as idiots. Selling to startups is the best thing you can do."
-- X/Twitter

**Practical meaning:** Smart, cost-sensitive early adopters sharpen your product because they demand quality and will switch to competitors without sentiment. Enterprise and government customers dull your product because their buying process rewards plausibility over quality, and their inertia kills iteration speed. "Clever, impoverished outsiders" are harbingers of mainstream demand -- they adopt first because they need the value most and have the least loyalty to incumbents. Newly founded startups are among the best early customers because they adopt fast, give honest feedback, and grow with you.

**Coaching deployment:** When a founder is chasing enterprise deals or government contracts as their first customers, deploy the warning directly: "The stupidity of your customer will make you stupid." Redirect toward the smallest, smartest users who will actually push back on the product and force improvement. When investors pressure a founder to "go upmarket" too early, PG's advice is blunt: "Write them off as idiots."

## Stories

### Viaweb Manual Selling -- "So Lame"

> "We made an online store builder. We would go to would-be customers and say, would you like to use our easy online store builder? And they would say, No. And we would say, 'But you want an online store, right?' And they would say, 'Yeah.' ... 'What if we used our software for you to make an online store and then you could have it?' ... And they'd say, 'All right.' And so we'd like, 'Ah, we can't get anyone to use our software, but at least they're willing to let us use it for them.' And it seemed so lame."

But PG learned enormously from using his own software to build stores for customers:

> "I would change the software in the middle of using it and then go back to working on their website. And boy, did that make the software much better that I had to use it."

*Source:* startup-school-2018-extraction

*Teaching application:* Deploy when a founder is embarrassed about their manual or amateurish process. The "lame" feeling is the signal, not the problem. PG's realization -- that the shameful manual process was actually optimal -- is the emotional core of "do things that don't scale." Also demonstrates extreme dogfooding: building for real customers with your own tool, fixing bugs in real time.

---

### The Men's Shirts Collar

> "I learned how to sell men's shirts. I had to digitize all these damned images of men's shirts, right? And I got these beautiful images, and I made this whole site with all these pictures of these shirts. And I learned from the shirt guy that all they want is pictures of the collar. I had to go and redo them all, the whole site, hundreds of shirts. I still remember those shirts so well."

*Source:* startup-school-radio-extraction

*Teaching application:* Deploy when a founder is building what they *think* users want versus what users actually want. The gap between the technologist's assumption (beautiful full-body photos) and the domain expert's knowledge (just show the collar) is the kind of thing you can only learn by doing unscalable manual work for real customers. No amount of user surveys would have revealed "just the collar."

---

### Stripe "Collison Installation"

> "Stripe is one of the most successful startups we've funded, and the problem they solved was an urgent one. If anyone could have sat back and waited for users, it was Stripe. But in fact they're famous within YC for aggressive early user acquisition. At YC we use the term 'Collison installation' for the technique they invented. More diffident founders ask 'Will you try our beta?' and if the answer is yes, they say 'Great, we'll send you a link.' But the Collison brothers weren't going to wait. When anyone agreed to try Stripe, they'd say 'Right then, give me your laptop' and set them up on the spot."

*Source:* startups-founding-1-extraction (Do Things That Don't Scale essay, 2013)

*Teaching application:* Deploy when a founder has a product that requires any setup or integration and is relying on documentation or onboarding flows to get users started. The Collison installation is the purest form of "do things that don't scale" -- don't send a link. Set them up right now, on their machine, while they are in front of you. Even Stripe, which solved an urgent, obvious problem, did aggressive manual installation. That is how high the bar is.

---

### Airbnb's Rolling Suitcases

> "Airbnb is a classic example of this technique. Marketplaces are so hard to get going that you should expect to take heroic measures at first. In Airbnb's case, these consisted of going door to door in New York, recruiting new users and helping existing ones improve their listings. When I remember the Airbnbs during YC, I picture them with rolling suitcases, because when they showed up for Tuesday dinners they'd always just flown back from somewhere."

PG adds the critical detail about how fragile the outcome was:

> "Airbnb now seems like an unstoppable juggernaut, but early on it was so fragile that about 30 days' worth of going out and engaging in person with users made the difference between success and failure."

*Source:* startups-founding-1-extraction (Do Things That Don't Scale essay, 2013)

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone thinks a marketplace should grow organically, or when they feel their manual efforts are too small to matter. Thirty days of in-person effort made the difference between Airbnb and nothing. The rolling suitcases are the image to remember: founders who are constantly in transit between where they live and where their users are.

---

### Readmill "Just Launch" Intervention

The Readmill founder (Henrik) had 2,000+ beta users, growing organically through quote sharing, but hadn't launched publicly because he wanted to support more devices and partner integrations first. PG asked:

> "Why don't you just launch it?"

After the founder explained his reasons, PG delivered:

> "The point to launch is when anybody in the world would say, 'Oh, I'm glad these guys launched because now the world is better by that one little amount.' And clearly, if people are recommending this and sharing quotes, it's doing something they couldn't do otherwise. So you should launch."

The founder immediately said "Okay, let's launch."

*Source:* tc-disrupt-extraction (Office Hours, 2011)

*Teaching application:* Deploy when a founder is delaying launch for non-essential reasons. The test is stark: does anyone benefit from what exists today? If 2,000 people are already using it and recommending it, you are not "about to launch" -- you are late. Also note the coaching technique: PG doesn't argue. He states the principle, applies it to the founder's specific situation, and the conclusion becomes undeniable.

### Fixing Bugs While Users Reproduce Them

> "Sometimes, somebody would report a bug to us. And we would say, 'Oh, can you reproduce it?' And while they were reproducing it, we would fix it. And then, they wouldn't be able to reproduce it, because we would have pushed the fix while we were talking to them on the phone. And they would say, 'Oh, I guess I'm imagining it.'"

*Source:* startup-school-radio-extraction

*Teaching application:* Deploy as a vivid illustration of the speed advantage of SaaS and tight founder-user feedback loops. This story also reveals PG's playful personality -- they found it funny, not burdensome, to fix bugs in real time. Use when a founder is complaining about the pace of customer support or bug reports; reframe it as a superpower rather than a burden.

---

### YC Launched in Less Than a Week

> "We launched YC probably in less than a week of having the idea. Of course, launching it was merely posting a website. And we didn't have any software in the beginning. People -- we just had this ASCII form that people would fill out and reply to us by email. The first couple batches, the applications were by email. We would print them out and pass them around amongst the partners, and like we would all write grades on the top, like we were grading papers. And then we would sort this heap of papers, and that was the application process."

*Source:* startup-school-2018-extraction

*Teaching application:* Deploy to demolish the "we need to build more before launching" excuse. YC itself -- arguably the most important institution in startup history -- launched with email-based applications, printed paper, and handwritten grades. If that is good enough for YC, whatever the founder has right now is good enough for their users. The first YC dinner in California had paint still wet on the walls (startup-school-radio-extraction). The most sophisticated institution started with the least sophisticated tools.
