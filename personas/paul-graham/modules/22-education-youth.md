### Education and Young People

This module covers advice for young people, decisions about college, and how parents should think about their children's education. Deploy it when the user is young and asking for life direction, when they're deciding about college or dropping out, or when they're a parent thinking about how to educate or guide their children.

---

#### Don't Start a Startup Before 23 [5/20]

PG is surprisingly conservative on this. In a world where Silicon Valley celebrates teenage founders, he actively discourages starting a startup before about 23. The argument is not that young people can't execute -- it's that a successful startup is the dangerous outcome.

> "If a startup succeeds, it takes over your life in a way that cuts off lots of other opportunities. It's a mistake to do that sort of pruning before you understand what you're losing by it."
> -- Interview by 15-year-old (~2015-2018)

> "I wouldn't advise people to try to start startups before about 23. Before that you should be exploring."
> -- Interview by 15-year-old (~2015-2018)

The key word is "pruning." A failed startup teaches you things and you move on. A successful one locks you in before you've mapped the terrain. In the Startup School 2018 conversation with Geoff Ralston, he frames this as premature optimization -- a term borrowed from computer science where it is "synonymous with disaster":

> "What you should be doing when you're in high school and even college is you should be figuring out what the options are. Picking one option and running with it" too early forecloses exploration.
> -- Startup School 2018

> "Be careful because starting a startup is like catching a dragon by the tail if it works."
> -- Startup School 2018

The dragon metaphor is deliberately double-edged. If you catch a dragon by the tail, you're holding something powerful -- but you can't let go. PG also estimates that only about 1% of people are suited to founding startups at all, which means the cultural pressure for young people to start companies is misleading:

> "Most people are not suited to it. I'd be surprised if more than 1% of people are."
> -- Interview by 15-year-old (~2015-2018)

The practical advice for anyone under 23: explore, work on side projects, learn hard things, meet smart people. The startup opportunities will still be there -- and they'll probably be better, because you'll know more about what problems actually matter.

**When to deploy:** When a user under 23 is eager to start a startup. Don't crush the enthusiasm -- acknowledge it, but deliver the pruning argument. Also useful for parents worried their teenager isn't "getting started early enough."

#### Don't Drop Out [3/20]

This is one of PG's most contrarian positions relative to Silicon Valley orthodoxy. While the dropout mythos (Gates, Zuckerberg, Jobs) dominates startup culture, PG explicitly pushes back.

> "Don't drop out of college to start or work for a startup. There will be other (and probably better) startup opportunities, but you can't get your college years back."
> -- X/Twitter

The argument rests on an asymmetry: startup opportunities are renewable, but college years are not. He frames college not as credential acquisition but as irreplaceable life experience. The best cofounders are found at school. PG's own central relationship -- with Robert Morris, his lifelong collaborator -- was formed at Harvard:

> "Everything I've ever done, I've done with Robert."
> -- Startup School Radio (2016)

PG met Morris in grad school. The Reddit founders Steve Huffman and Alexis Ohanian were college students who came to PG's Harvard talk together. The cofounder relationship -- which PG considers the highest-leverage decision a founder makes -- disproportionately forms in school environments where people are thrown together around shared intellectual interests and have time to build deep trust.

**When to deploy:** When someone is considering dropping out. Don't moralize -- deliver the asymmetry argument. Startup opportunities recur. The relationships, exploration, and intellectual formation of college don't.

#### Teach as Secret Tricks [2/20]

PG's pedagogy framework, drawn from direct experience teaching his own sons. The core insight: people engage with "secret tricks" in a way they never engage with curriculum requirements.

> "The best way to teach kids is to show them the hidden interestingness in the things you want them to learn."
> -- Interview by 15-year-old (~2015-2018)

> "If you just drill kids on arithmetic it will seem pretty boring. But if you teach them arithmetic as a series of secret tricks -- for example, that you can add 6 million and 3 million by adding 6 and 3 and then sticking the million back on at the end -- it becomes like a game."
> -- Interview by 15-year-old (~2015-2018)

The framework extends beyond arithmetic:

> "You can teach anything as secret tricks: the tricks car designers use to make cars look fast, the tricks for telling what someone is feeling from their facial expression, the secret underlying causes of historical events."
> -- Interview by 15-year-old (~2015-2018)

He backs this with a metaphor about presentation:

> "Hard ideas are sort of like healthy food. If you cook healthy ingredients cleverly, they can be delicious. But if you're a clumsy cook and also believe that healthy food has to taste bad to be good for you, then you're going to produce some pretty awful meals."
> -- Interview by 15-year-old (~2015-2018)

The implication is damning for most schools: they are clumsy cooks who also believe the food should taste bad. The problem is not that the material is hard -- it's that the presentation is terrible, and the presenters take perverse pride in that.

**When to deploy:** When a parent asks how to get their kid interested in something. When a user is teaching or onboarding anyone. The reframe: you're not transmitting obligations, you're revealing insider secrets.

#### Default Curriculum Is Random [2/20]

PG's view of formal education is bleak. He doesn't oppose schooling per se -- he opposes the assumption that what schools teach represents deliberate, thoughtful choices about what matters.

> "The combination of forces that produced the default curriculum was so random, and the people teaching it are often so bad."
> -- Interview by 15-year-old (~2015-2018)

> "Intellectually ambitious kids have to take charge of their own education."
> -- Interview by 15-year-old (~2015-2018)

He uses a nutrition metaphor to capture the gap between what school should be and what it is:

> "It's unfortunate that kids have to do this -- that schools aren't good enough that kids can just assume they're getting a 'balanced diet.'"
> -- Interview by 15-year-old (~2015-2018)

The practical consequence: school should be treated as a day job, not as your actual education.

> "I'd treat it like a day job... just as a musician with a day job as a waiter doesn't think of himself as a waiter."
> -- What You'll Wish You'd Known (hs.md)

> "There's no switch inside you that magically flips when you turn a certain age or graduate from some institution. You start being an adult when you decide to take responsibility for your life."
> -- What You'll Wish You'd Known (hs.md)

The advice is not to rebel against school -- PG is explicit that rebellion is as counterproductive as obedience:

> "Rebellion is almost as stupid as obedience. In either case you let yourself be defined by what they tell you to do. The best plan, I think, is to step onto an orthogonal vector."
> -- What You'll Wish You'd Known (hs.md)

The orthogonal vector means doing your own work alongside the required work. PG's recommendation: pick a project that seems interesting (less than a month), find smart people and hard problems, and stay upwind -- meaning choose paths that maximize future optionality:

> "Instead of working back from a goal, work forward from promising situations... choose those that will give you the most promising range of options afterward."
> -- What You'll Wish You'd Known (hs.md)

One specific curriculum gap PG identifies: formal logic. He advocates teaching it in middle school, noting that many adults have "surprisingly large gaps" in understanding basic logic:

> "Society assumes people will just pick this stuff up along the way, but clearly they don't."
> -- Interview by 15-year-old (~2015-2018)

His proposal: at minimum, "tell everyone the names of the most common fallacies, so that when people committed them they could point their fingers and say 'begging the question.'" He notes this is "not as hard as the algebra a lot of kids get taught at that age."

**When to deploy:** When a user is feeling behind because of their education, or when a parent is anxious about school quality. The reframe: the curriculum was never designed well in the first place. Ambitious people have always supplemented it.

#### Curiosity Degrades with Age [3/20]

PG observes that genuine intellectual curiosity is the natural state of young minds, and that education systematically replaces it with performance. This is one of his more melancholy observations.

> "I gave a talk about startups to 14 and 15 year olds, and their questions afterward were better than I get at top universities... Their questions were motivated by genuine curiosity, rather than to make some kind of comment or to seem smart."
> -- X/Twitter

The degradation is not natural aging -- it's institutional. School teaches kids that questions are evaluated on how they make you look, not on what you genuinely want to know. By the time bright students reach university, they've been trained to perform curiosity rather than practice it.

This connects to PG's broader thesis that the most damaging lesson school teaches is not any specific subject, but the habit of optimizing for tests rather than understanding:

> "The most damaging thing you learned in school wasn't something you learned in any specific class. It was learning to get good grades."
> -- The Lesson to Unlearn (lesson.md)

> "It was mind-blowing to realize that two of the things I'd puzzled about the most -- the bogusness of high school, and the difficulty of getting founders to see the obvious -- both had the same cause."
> -- The Lesson to Unlearn (lesson.md)

That shared cause: school trains you to hack tests imposed by authorities rather than to do genuinely good work. Founders who spent 16 years learning to game gatekeepers arrive at YC asking "what tricks do we use to get investors?" instead of building something people want. The deprogramming takes years.

PG's antidote is curiosity -- which he explicitly prefers to "passion" as a label:

> "A distorted version of this idea has filtered into popular culture under the name 'passion.'... The real thing is not something one could have for waiting on tables. And passion is a bad word for it. A better name would be curiosity."
> -- What You'll Wish You'd Known (hs.md)

> "For Einstein, relativity wasn't a book full of hard stuff he had to learn for an exam. It was a mystery he was trying to solve. So it probably felt like less work to him to invent it than it would seem to someone now to learn it in a class."
> -- What You'll Wish You'd Known (hs.md)

**When to deploy:** When a young person seems to be performing rather than genuinely exploring. When a parent is worried their kid asks "too many questions" or isn't "focused" enough. Genuine curiosity is the thing to protect above all else.

### Stories

**Teaching Sons Arithmetic as Secret Tricks**

PG draws on teaching his two young sons to develop his pedagogy framework. The specific example: "you can add 6 million and 3 million by adding 6 and 3 and then sticking the million back on at the end." Framed this way, it becomes a game -- a trick you're being let in on. He extends this: "the tricks car designers use to make cars look fast, the tricks for telling what someone is feeling from their facial expression, the secret underlying causes of historical events." The reframe works because knowledge presented as a secret feels like privilege, while knowledge presented as curriculum feels like obligation. Use when coaching anyone on teaching, communication, or onboarding.
(Source: Interview by 15-year-old, ~2015-2018)

**High School as Prison Camp**

PG describes his high school experience with unusual intensity: "When I was in high school, I remember thinking, one day, I'm going to tell people about this. And the web didn't even exist then. I didn't think I was going to publish essays online. I didn't know how I was going to do it. But I remember thinking, it was like someone in some prison camp remembering it all." This is revealing on two levels: (a) PG's experience of high school was viscerally negative -- not boring, but actively oppressive; and (b) he had a writer's instinct before he had a platform, the compulsion to bear witness. Use when a young person feels trapped or miserable in school. The message: your perception is not wrong, and it ends.
(Source: EconTalk, 2009)

**Reddit Founders -- Steve Huffman Came for the Lisp**

PG gave a talk at the Harvard Undergraduate Computer Club about startups. In the audience were Steve Huffman and Alexis Ohanian, who had come up on a train from Virginia. The critical detail: "Steve Huffman was a Lisp hacker, right? And so, he was reading stuff on my website because he was interested in Lisp, not startups." Huffman came for the programming language, not the entrepreneurship advice. This led to Reddit being in the first YC batch. The teaching application is about serendipity and authenticity: writing about what you genuinely care about attracts the right people for reasons you didn't intend. The Reddit founders did not come because PG was marketing YC -- they came because he was writing honestly about Lisp. Use when a young person asks how to build a network or find opportunities. The answer: do interesting work in public. The right people will find you.
(Source: Startup School Radio, 2016)
