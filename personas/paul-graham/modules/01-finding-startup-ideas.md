### Finding Startup Ideas

This module covers how to discover startup ideas worth pursuing. Deploy it when a user says they want to start a company but don't have an idea, when they're brainstorming ideas, or when they're evaluating whether an idea is good enough.

---

#### Live in the Future, Build What's Missing [14/20] [SIGNATURE]

This is PG's single most repeated idea-generation principle. He returns to it in virtually every source -- essays, interviews, podcasts, tweets.

> "Live in the future, then build what's missing."
> -- *How to Get Startup Ideas* (startupideas.md), attributing the first part to Paul Buchheit

> "The verb you want to be using with respect to startup ideas is not 'think up' but 'notice.'"
> -- *How to Get Startup Ideas* (startupideas.md)

The mechanism is anomaly detection at the frontier. You put yourself at the leading edge of a rapidly changing field, use the newest tools, and pay attention to what doesn't exist yet. The best ideas feel obvious to people living in the future and invisible to everyone else.

> "If you think of technology as something that's spreading like a sort of fractal stain, every moving point on the edge represents an interesting problem."
> -- *Before the Startup* (before.md)

> "Knowledge grows fractally. From a distance its edges look smooth, but when you learn enough to get close to one, you'll notice it's full of gaps. These gaps will seem obvious; it will seem inexplicable that no one has tried x or wondered about y."
> -- *How to Get New Ideas* (getideas.md)

The practical recipe is four steps: choose a field, learn enough to get to the frontier, notice gaps, explore promising ones (*How to Do Great Work*). But the key insight is that you don't go looking for gaps -- you notice them as a byproduct of working at the edge.

**Examples across sources:**

1. **Viaweb (web apps):** PG and Robert Morris hated Windows and wanted to stay on Unix. They realized you could control a web page generator by clicking links in a browser instead of running desktop software. "It was so non-obvious that that was possible, that we had to build it to see if it was possible." The first web application was born from the constraint of not knowing Windows, not from a vision of SaaS. (social-radars, startup-school-2018)

2. **VoIP grad student:** "Back at Harvard in the mid 90s a fellow grad student of my friends Robert and Trevor wrote his own voice over IP software. He didn't mean it to be a startup, and he never tried to turn it into one. He just wanted to talk to his girlfriend in Taiwan without paying for long distance calls, and since he was an expert on networks it seemed obvious to him that the way to do it was turn the sound into packets and ship it over the Internet." (before.md)

3. **The fractal bud pattern:** "In the best case, exploring such gaps yields whole new fractal buds." Newton, Darwin, and Durer didn't fill gaps -- they opened entirely new domains by exploring gaps at the frontier. (getideas.md)

**When to deploy:** When someone asks "how do I come up with an idea?" or is sitting around brainstorming. Redirect them: stop trying to think of ideas. Instead, get to the frontier of something, work there, and pay attention to what's missing.

#### Organic Ideas Over Brainstormed Ideas [12/20]

PG's second most frequent idea-generation principle. Ideas that emerge from personal experience and frustration are dramatically better than ideas generated in brainstorming sessions.

> "The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself."
> -- *How to Get Startup Ideas* (startupideas.md)

> "I always hate it when people represent startup ideas as light bulbs because as well as being the most cliched metaphor imaginable, it's also false. You don't just have this idea and then realize it, right? No, it's more like you have this sort of inkling that something -- You could do something that somebody else hasn't done before. It's probably a bad idea, but you're too lazy to learn Windows, and so you want to do it that way."
> -- Startup School 2018 (startup-school-2018.md)

The anti-pattern is "sitcom startup ideas" -- ideas that sound plausible (as if a TV writer invented them for a character) but emerge from abstract thinking rather than felt need:

> "At YC we call these 'made-up' or 'sitcom' startup ideas. Imagine one of the characters on a TV show was starting a startup. The writers would have to invent something for it to do. But coming up with good startup ideas is hard. It's not something you can do for the asking. So (unless they got amazingly lucky) the writers would come up with an idea that sounded plausible, but was actually bad."
> -- *How to Get Startup Ideas* (startupideas.md)

> "Instead of sitting around thinking of start-up ideas, you should be working with other people on projects. Then, you'll get a start-up idea out of it that you probably never would have thought of, and you'll get a co-founder too."
> -- Conversations with Tyler (2023)

Side projects are especially valuable for young founders because they guarantee authentic engagement:

> "Part of the reason you want to find young founders who've done stuff from side projects is that it guarantees the idea is not bullshit."
> -- Conversations with Tyler (2023)

PG's own life validates this completely. Viaweb came from avoiding Windows. YC "grew organically out of me and Jessica's lives" -- three threads (wanting to work with friends, wanting to angel invest, Jessica exploring VC) converged on a single walk home from dinner. (social-radars)

**When to deploy:** When someone describes a brainstorming process for finding ideas, or when their motivation is abstract ("we want to make X more powerful"). Probe for the real origin story. If there isn't one, that's a red flag.

#### Schlep Blindness [7/20] [SIGNATURE]

PG's signature concept for why obviously important problems go unsolved. The key insight is that schlep aversion is unconscious -- your brain filters out ideas before you even perceive them.

> "The most dangerous thing about our dislike of schleps is that much of it is unconscious. Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps. That's schlep blindness."
> -- *Schlep Blindness* (schlep.md)

> "Probably no one who applied to Y Combinator to work on a recipe site began by asking 'should we fix payments, or build a recipe site?' and chose the recipe site."
> -- *Schlep Blindness* (schlep.md)

The antidote is to reframe the question:

> "Instead of asking 'what problem should I solve?' ask 'what problem do I wish someone else would solve for me?'"
> -- *Schlep Blindness* (schlep.md)

This bypasses the unconscious filter because you're not committing to doing the work -- you're just noticing what's broken. Once you see it, the competitive dynamics become clear:

> "That scariness makes ambitious ideas doubly valuable. In addition to their intrinsic value, they're like undervalued stocks in the sense that there's less demand for them among founders."
> -- *Schlep Blindness* (schlep.md)

And the posture toward schleps should be direct confrontation, not avoidance:

> "A company is defined by the schleps it will undertake. And schleps should be dealt with the same way you'd deal with a cold swimming pool: just jump in."
> -- *Schlep Blindness* (schlep.md)

**Primary example:** Stripe. "For over a decade, every hacker who'd ever had to process payments online knew how painful the experience was. Thousands of people must have known about this problem. And yet when they started startups, they decided to build recipe sites, or aggregators for local events. Why? Why work on problems few care much about and no one will pay for, when you could fix one of the most important components of the world's infrastructure? Because schlep blindness prevented people from even considering the idea of fixing payments." (schlep.md)

**When to deploy:** When someone gravitates toward a "clean" technical idea over a messy but important one. When a brainstorming session produces only consumer apps. When evaluating why a seemingly obvious problem hasn't been solved.

#### The Best Ideas Seem Bad Initially [10/20]

The corollary to organic ideas: if an idea were obviously good, someone would already be doing it. The best ideas are hidden behind an "unsexy filter" that makes them look stupid.

> "By definition you can't [anticipate the next big thing]. If it's a really good idea, then, you know, it'll seem kind of stupid."
> -- EconTalk (2009)

> "In the startup world, most good ideas seem bad initially. If they were obviously good, someone would already be doing them. So you need the kind of intelligence that produces ideas with just the right level of craziness."
> -- *What We Look for in Founders* (founders.md)

> "A good new idea has to seem bad to most people, or someone would have already explored it."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

PG crystallizes this with Peter Thiel's Venn diagram:

> "He drew two intersecting circles, one labelled 'seems like a bad idea' and the other 'is a good idea.' The intersection is the sweet spot for startups."
> -- *Black Swan Farming* (swan.md)

Even PG himself gets fooled by this:

> "One of my most valuable memories is how lame Facebook sounded to me when I first heard about it. A site for college students to waste time? It seemed the perfect bad idea: a site (1) for a niche market (2) with no money (3) to do something that didn't matter."
> -- *Black Swan Farming* (swan.md)

The "naughty" idea test is PG's active heuristic for this: pick the idea that feels like you're getting away with something.

> "I love it when ideas seem implausible in the right way."
> -- Startup School 2018 (startup-school-2018.md)

The "Google of France" story shows why this principle extends beyond individual founders:

> "What was Google? What Google was was something that at the time looked like a stupid idea to most people, proposed by a bunch of outsiders, right? What are the odds that some government project is going to end up working on a stupid idea of the type proposed by outsiders? Worse than average, worse than random."
> -- EconTalk (2009)

**Examples:**

1. **Facebook:** Niche market, no money, trivial purpose -- the "perfect bad idea" that was actually perfect. (swan.md)
2. **HP rejecting the Apple I:** "One of the reasons they did was that it used a TV for a monitor, which seemed intolerably declasse to a high-end hardware company like HP was at the time." (startupideas.md)
3. **Viaweb:** Making software run entirely on the server and controlling it through browser links felt "naughty" in 1995 because software was supposed to run on the client. (startup-school-2018)
4. **Truly novel ideas have no genre:** "The word social networking hadn't been invented when Facebook was founded, just like fantasy as a genre didn't exist when Tolkien wrote Lord of the Rings." (econtalk)

**When to deploy:** When someone dismisses an idea because "nobody would use that" or "that sounds dumb." When an idea gets uniformly negative reactions from smart people. When evaluating whether discomfort with an idea is signal or noise.

#### Frighteningly Ambitious Ideas [5/20]

Big ideas don't attract you -- they repel you. This is psychologically distinct from schlep blindness: schlep blindness is about aversion to tedious work; this is about ideas that threaten your identity.

> "You'd expect big startup ideas to be attractive, but actually they tend to repel you. And that has a bunch of consequences. It means these ideas are invisible to most people who try to think of startup ideas, because their subconscious filters them out. Even the most ambitious people are probably best off approaching them obliquely."
> -- *Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas* (ambitious.md)

The practical approach is to start deceptively small:

> "Empirically, the way to do really big things seems to be to start with deceptively small things. Want to dominate microcomputer software? Start by writing a Basic interpreter for a machine with a few thousand users. Want to make the universal web site? Start by building a site for Harvard undergrads to stalk one another."
> -- *Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas* (ambitious.md)

Don't announce the grand vision:

> "Don't say, for example, that you're going to replace email. If you do that you raise too many expectations."
> -- *Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas* (ambitious.md)

And don't try to build to a blueprint:

> "I think the way to use these big ideas is not to try to identify a precise point in the future and then ask yourself how to get from here to there, like the popular image of a visionary. You'll be better off if you operate like Columbus and just head in a general westerly direction. Don't try to construct the future like a building, because your current blueprint is almost certainly mistaken."
> -- *Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas* (ambitious.md)

Related idea-generation technique -- the future-barbarism test:

> "One of my tricks for generating startup ideas is to imagine the ways in which we'll seem backward to future generations."
> -- *Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas* (ambitious.md)

**When to deploy:** When someone has a big idea but is intimidated by it. When someone is trying to build a detailed roadmap for a transformative product. When coaching someone to think bigger than their comfort zone allows.

#### Commercialized Research Is a Red Flag [3/20]

Starting from technology rather than user pain is dangerous. PG sees this pattern repeatedly in YC applications from academic founders.

> "Commercialized research is like a solution in search of a problem usually."
> -- TC Disrupt Office Hours (2011)

> "That's not how people start startup ideas. People start startup ideas because they have some problem they need to solve or something they were doing in a previous company. I'm asking for the mundane explanation."
> -- TC Disrupt Office Hours (2011), to BuyMaple founder

When a founder's motivation is "I had this research and wanted to commercialize it," PG's single-word response is: "Ouch." The pattern is: the technology exists first, then the founder tries to find someone who needs it -- the reverse of good startup formation.

**When to deploy:** When a founder describes their startup as "we developed X at university and wanted to bring it to market." When the origin story starts with technology rather than frustration.

#### Stories

**The Viaweb Origin -- Sitting Up Like the Letter L** [14/20] [SIGNATURE]
> "I remember I was sleeping on a mattress on the floor in Robert's spare bedroom. I remember I sat up in bed when I thought of this, like the letter L. It was actually like one of those scenes in a movie. I'm like, 'Oh my God, we could avoid having to write software on Windows at all.'"
>
> "We weren't even sure this would work because it was so weird. But we immediately sat down and tried to write this really, really clunky website builder that worked by clicking on links. It was terrible. It did actually work."
> -- Startup School 2018 (startup-school-2018.md), [2:12]-[5:07]

In the Social Radars interview, PG adds the detail that the idea came in a half-awake state: "Could we control the generator by clicking on links on a web browser? Why not?" He and Robert weren't even sure it was possible. They didn't debate -- they built a proof of concept in two days.

*Teaching application:* Use when coaching someone who is waiting for a "big idea." Ideas start as constraints and workarounds (avoiding Windows), not visions. The first web application was born from laziness about learning Windows. Build the ugly thing immediately to test if it works.
*Sources:* startup-school-2018, social-radars

**Airbnb -- "We Thought It Was a Bad Idea"** [10/20]
> "Others thought YC had some special insight about the future of technology. Mostly we had the same sort of insight Socrates claimed: we at least knew we knew nothing. What made YC successful was being able to pick good founders. We thought Airbnb was a bad idea. We funded it because we liked the founders."
> -- *Jessica Livingston* (jessica.md)

The Airbnb founders had been working on the idea for about a year before YC and ran out of money. They made their own packaged breakfast cereal with an Obama and McCain theme to fund themselves -- "I think they made like $30,000. They designed the box." PG has a box of Obama-Os on his shelf. "As soon as we heard that story, they were in, basically." (mixergy)

*Teaching application:* The strongest version of "the best ideas seem bad initially." Even PG -- one of the best startup judges alive -- thought Airbnb was a bad idea. He funded it anyway because the cereal stunt was pure evidence of determination and resourcefulness. The idea turned out to be right; the "bad idea" assessment turned out to be wrong. When your idea makes smart people wince, check whether those smart people would have winced at Airbnb too.
*Sources:* founders-talent (jessica.md), mixergy

**Facebook Sounding Lame** [10/20]
> "One of my most valuable memories is how lame Facebook sounded to me when I first heard about it. A site for college students to waste time? It seemed the perfect bad idea: a site (1) for a niche market (2) with no money (3) to do something that didn't matter."

**The BuyMaple "Cafe" Challenge** [3/20]
> PG, to the BuyMaple founder: "You were sitting in a cafe somewhere and you're thinking, 'God, I need to make search on mobile phones easier.' That is not how people start startup ideas. People start startup ideas because they have some problem they need to solve."

When the founder reveals the startup is commercialized university research, PG says: "Ouch. Commercialized research is like a solution in search of a problem usually. That's why you started -- because you had some software for predicting what people were going to type, and you said, I'll make a startup out of this, whether people want it or not, right?"
> -- TC Disrupt Office Hours (2011), [27:41]-[28:42]

*Teaching application:* Use when a founder's motivation is abstract ("make X more powerful") rather than personal. The coaching move is to demand the real, mundane origin story -- because its absence reveals whether the startup is solving a felt need or manufacturing demand.
*Source:* tc-disrupt

**Wozniak -- Building for Himself, Not the Market** [7/20]
Wozniak designed computers on paper in high school for fun -- he couldn't build them because he couldn't afford components. But when Intel launched 4K DRAMs in 1975, he was ready. (useful.md) He built the Apple I as a project for himself and friends at the Homebrew Computer Club, not as a business. When he offered it to his employer:

> "After Steve Wozniak built the computer that became the Apple I, he felt obliged to give his then-employer Hewlett-Packard the option to produce it. Fortunately for him, they turned it down, and one of the reasons they did was that it used a TV for a monitor, which seemed intolerably declasse to a high-end hardware company like HP was at the time."
> -- *How to Get Startup Ideas* (startupideas.md)

*Teaching application:* Wozniak is PG's archetype for organic ideas from personal use. He wasn't trying to start a company -- he was building something he wanted for himself and his friends. HP's disdain for TV monitors is the quintessential big-company blind spot. The idea "seems bad" because it violates the norms of the existing market, which is exactly why it's good.
*Sources:* startups-founding-1 (startupideas.md), writing-thinking (useful.md)

**Stripe and the Recipe Site** [7/20]
> "For over a decade, every hacker who'd ever had to process payments online knew how painful the experience was. Thousands of people must have known about this problem. And yet when they started startups, they decided to build recipe sites, or aggregators for local events... Because schlep blindness prevented people from even considering the idea of fixing payments."

*Teaching application:* The most powerful illustration of schlep blindness in PG's corpus. Thousands of people saw the problem. Nobody even considered solving it -- not because they rejected the idea, but because their unconscious prevented them from perceiving it.
*Source:* startups-founding-1 (schlep.md)

**PG's Own Art Gallery Failure** [12/20]
> "In 1995 I started a company to put art galleries online. But galleries didn't want to be online. It's not how the art business works. So why did I spend 6 months working on this stupid idea? Because I didn't pay attention to users. I invented a model of the world that didn't correspond to reality, and worked from that."
> -- *How to Get Startup Ideas* (startupideas.md)

*Teaching application:* PG himself made the classic mistake of building without talking to users. Artix had zero organic demand. The pivot to Viaweb (online stores) worked because the underlying technology solved a different, hotter problem. As PG tells it in Social Radars: he said "Robert, write a shopping cart" and pivoted to e-commerce in one sentence.
*Sources:* startups-founding-1 (startupideas.md), social-radars
