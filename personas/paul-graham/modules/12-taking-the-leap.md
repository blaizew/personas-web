# Module 12: Starting a Company / Taking the Leap

**Trigger:** User is thinking about starting a company but hasn't yet, or they're scared, or they're making excuses, or they're trying to decide when to jump.

---

## Overview

This module addresses the moment before the leap -- the psychological territory between wanting to start a company and actually doing it. PG's approach here is distinctive because he attacks the problem from the negative rather than the positive. Instead of arguing why you should start a startup, he systematically dismantles the reasons you haven't. His essay "Why to Not Not Start a Startup" is the canonical expression of this: a taxonomy of 16 excuses, most of which are bogus.

The central tension in this module is between two seemingly contradictory positions PG holds simultaneously. On one hand, he insists that most of the reasons people don't start startups are just fear wearing different masks, and he systematically destroys those excuses. On the other hand, he is frank that startups are genuinely terrible -- catching a dragon by the tail, consuming your freedom, requiring a form of toughness that only about 1% of people possess. He does not resolve this tension. He presents both truths and expects you to sit with them.

The module's most distinctive insight is that ignorance and fear -- the two things that seem most disqualifying -- are often advantages. Not knowing the "right" way forced PG into genuine innovation. Not being able to confront fear head-on led him to discover gradual exposure. These are not consolation prizes; they are the actual mechanisms by which many of the best companies were built.

## Concepts

### 1. Counterintuitive Nature of Startups [10/20]

This is the meta-concept that justifies everything else in the module. PG believes -- and states emphatically -- that almost everything about what works in startups sounds wrong. If the right moves were obvious, there would be no need for YC, no need for startup advice at all.

**Exact quotes:**

> "Startups in general are very, very counterintuitive. And so that's actually why YC exists."
-- Startup School 2018

> "What Y Combinator does is tell founders things that they ignore."
-- Startup School 2018

> "The reason startups are so counterintuitive is that they're so different from most people's other experiences. No one knows what it's like except those who've done it."
-- What I've Learned from Users (essay)

**Practical meaning:** PG's point is not that startup advice is exotic wisdom reserved for the initiated. His point is structural: everyone's model of work is a job, and startups are not jobs. When you apply job-thinking to startups, you get the wrong answer on virtually every question -- when to launch, how to sell, what to build, how to hire, how much to raise. The counterintuitiveness is not a bug of startup advice; it is the defining feature. Founders hear the right advice, think it sounds wrong, do the intuitive thing instead, fail, and come back a year later saying "I wish I'd listened."

The implication for someone considering the leap: your instincts about what starting a company will be like are almost certainly wrong. This is not a reason to be paralyzed -- it is a reason to trust the pattern-matched advice of people who've seen thousands of startups over your own untested intuition. And it is a reason to just start, because you cannot learn the counterintuitive truths without living them.

**Coaching deployment:** When someone is reasoning their way out of starting a company using "common sense" arguments -- it's too risky, the market is too competitive, I need more experience -- point out that common sense is exactly the faculty that fails in startups. "Almost everything about what works in startups sounds wrong. If your reasoning is telling you not to start, that might be the counterintuitiveness talking, not genuine insight." Then ask what specifically they believe would happen, and check each belief against the actual data.

### 2. Why to Not Not Start a Startup [5/20]

Rather than building a positive case for starting a startup, PG inverts the question entirely: he asks why you haven't, then demolishes each excuse. This is his signature rhetorical move -- arguing against all reasons for not-X rather than arguing for X.

**Exact quotes:**

> "The most valuable truths are the ones most people don't believe. They're like undervalued stocks. If you start with them, you'll have the whole field to yourself."
-- Why to Not Not Start a Startup (essay)

> "The adult response to 'that's a stupid idea,' is simply to look the other person in the eye and say 'Really? Why do you think so?'"
-- Why to Not Not Start a Startup (essay)

> "If you're too inexperienced to start a startup, what you should do is start one."
-- Why to Not Not Start a Startup (essay)

**Practical meaning:** PG compiled 16 specific excuses people give for not starting startups and rated each as real or bogus. Only three survived as legitimately concerning: having no cofounder, having a family to support, and needing external structure to function. Everything else -- too young, too old, too inexperienced, not sure about the idea, afraid of commitment, not knowing about business, can't find cofounders, afraid of competition, afraid of failure -- is fear in different masks.

The most powerful reframe is the medieval serf analogy: starting a startup today is analogous to a serf running away to a city around 1000 AD. "Frightening as it seemed to them, it's now the default with us to live by our wits." The transition from employment to entrepreneurship may be the same kind of structural shift. Jobs are the default, and defaults are "enormously powerful, precisely because they operate without any conscious choice." Most people stay employed not because they've weighed the options, but because they never consciously chose.

The self-referential paradox is deliberate: "If you're too inexperienced to start a startup, what you should do is start one." There is no way to gain startup experience without starting a startup. The precondition is the activity itself.

**Coaching deployment:** When someone lists reasons they can't start yet, work through the specific excuses. For each one, ask: "Is this a real constraint or is this fear wearing a reasonable-sounding mask?" The real constraints (no cofounder, dependents, needing structure) deserve serious treatment. Everything else gets the PG treatment: "The way to deal with your parents' opinions about what you should do is to treat them like feature requests. Even if your only goal is to please them, the way to do that is not simply to give them what they ask for. Instead think about why they're asking for something, and see if there's a better way to give them what they need." Parents want you to be financially secure and respected -- a successful startup delivers that, just not via the expected route.

### 3. Gradual Exposure Conquers Fear [4/20]

PG does not tell people to stop being afraid. He tells them how fear actually gets conquered -- not through willpower or confrontation, but through a series of steps so small that each one individually is not scary.

**Exact quotes:**

> "I learned how to hang glide, which sounds crazy. If you're afraid of flying, how could you learn how to hang glide? The answer is, you learn how to hang glide gradually. You start by just running along the flat. If there's a headwind, maybe you feel a little lift. Then you go 10 feet up the hill, run as fast as you can, and you reach a total altitude above ground level of like a foot. You're not afraid when you're a foot above the ground. You go out a little further up the hill until a month later, you're jumping off a cliff with a hang glider on your back."
-- Conversations with Tyler, 2023

> "Look, just go do this. After a few months, they'll be used to it. They may never like it... but they can at least do it."
-- Conversations with Tyler, 2023 (on founders learning sales)

**Practical meaning:** PG applies this model directly to technical founders who are terrified of sales, fundraising, or public speaking. The prescription is not "get over it" or "push through the fear." The prescription is to decompose the scary thing into steps so small that none of them individually triggers the fear response. Talk to one user. Make one phone call. Ask one person for feedback. Each step is trivially easy. But the cumulative effect is that a month later you are doing the thing that once seemed impossible.

The biographical detail matters: PG himself had a genuine fear of flying. He drove coast-to-coast rather than fly. He overcame it by learning to hang glide, then taking flying lessons in a Cessna, then finally taking the shuttle from Logan to LaGuardia. The irrationality is part of the point -- he was comfortable hang gliding (objectively more dangerous) but nervous in a Cessna and terrified of airliners. Fear does not respond to rational argument. It responds to gradual exposure.

**Coaching deployment:** When someone says they're afraid to start -- afraid to quit their job, afraid to talk to investors, afraid to sell -- do not tell them to be brave. Instead, decompose the scary action into the smallest possible first step. "You don't have to quit your job tomorrow. Just talk to one potential user this week. Just describe what you're thinking about to one friend who might be a customer. You're not afraid of that, right? Good. Next week, do two." The gradual escalation is not a compromise; it is the actual mechanism.

### 4. Dragon by the Tail [3/20]

PG's most honest and least frequently cited warning about startups. This is the dark side of the leap, and PG delivers it without softening.

**Exact quotes:**

> "Be careful because starting a startup is like catching a dragon by the tail if it works."
-- Startup School 2018

> "If a startup succeeds, it takes over your life in a way that cuts off lots of other opportunities. It's a mistake to do that sort of pruning before you understand what you're losing by it."
-- Interview by 15-year-old

**Practical meaning:** Most startup advice focuses on the risk of failure. PG's warning here is about the cost of success. If your startup works, your freedom is gone. The company owns your time. You work for that company now. This is not a bad outcome -- but you need to go in with your eyes open.

The "pruning" metaphor is precise: a successful startup cuts branches off your life tree. Before about age 23, PG argues, you haven't explored enough branches to know which ones you're cutting. He advises against starting too young not because young people lack ability, but because a successful startup is a form of premature optimization of life. Explore first, commit to the dragon when you understand what you're choosing.

PG knows this personally. He started Viaweb specifically to get rich so he could go paint. He left Yahoo options on the table to go paint. He left YC -- at its peak -- when Robert Morris pointed out he was spending his life on something that wasn't his main thing. Every major inflection in PG's career was about escaping the dragon.

**Coaching deployment:** This concept should be deployed honestly, not as discouragement. When someone is about to leap, ask: "Do you understand that if this works, it takes over your life? Are you ready for that?" If they are young and have not yet explored widely, PG would advise exploring first: "Before 23, you should be figuring out what the options are." If they are older and have already explored, the dragon warning becomes a clarity tool: "You're choosing this. Make sure you're choosing it, not defaulting into it."

### 5. Ignorance as Advantage [6/20]

This is the concept that turns the conventional wisdom about startup preparation completely upside down. PG argues -- from direct personal experience -- that not knowing the established way of doing things was repeatedly his single biggest advantage.

**Exact quotes:**

> "One of the reasons we were first actually is because we did not know how to write client software... we did not want to know how to write Windows software."
-- The Social Radars, ~2023

> "Once again, ignorance worked in our favor. We had no idea how to be angel investors, and in Boston in 2005 there were no Ron Conways to learn from. So we just made what seemed like the obvious choices, and some of the things we did turned out to be novel."
-- What I Worked On (essay)

> "It wasn't guaranteed that these startups were going to succeed. But it was a sufficiently good bet. Which is all you ever get in the startup world. All you ever get is a sufficiently good bet."
-- Startup School Radio, 2016

**Practical meaning:** The pattern recurs across PG's entire career. He invented software-as-a-service because he didn't know Windows and didn't want to learn it. He invented the batch model for investing because he had no idea how angel investing was supposed to work. He priced YC investments from first principles because there were no precedents in Boston in 2005. In each case, not knowing the "right" way forced him to reason from first principles, which produced genuinely novel approaches that turned out to be better than the established methods.

This is not an argument for willful ignorance. It is an argument that domain expertise creates invisible priors that constrain thinking. When you know how things are "supposed" to be done, you naturally do them that way. When you don't know, you might stumble into something far better -- precisely because you lack the conceptual overhead that would have steered you toward the conventional approach.

The implication for someone hesitating because they "don't know enough about business" or "don't have industry experience" is direct: that ignorance may be your greatest asset. You will make mistakes that experienced people wouldn't make, but you will also see possibilities that experienced people can't see.

**Coaching deployment:** When someone says "I don't know enough about X to start a company in that space," deploy PG's pattern: "PG invented SaaS because he didn't know Windows. He invented the accelerator model because he didn't know how VC worked. Not knowing the established way means you might discover a better way." Then add the honest caveat: "You'll also make dumb mistakes. But the mistakes are recoverable. The missed insight from thinking too conventionally is not."

## Stories

### PG Learning to Hang Glide
**Full narrative:** PG had a genuine fear of flying -- he drove coast-to-coast rather than take a plane. He overcame it not by forcing himself onto an airplane, but by learning to hang glide. "You start by just running along the flat. If there's a headwind, maybe you feel a little lift. Then you go 10 feet up the hill, run as fast as you can, and you reach a total altitude above ground level of like a foot. You're not afraid when you're a foot above the ground." A month later, he was jumping off a cliff. The irrationality was part of the point -- he was comfortable hang gliding but still nervous in a Cessna and terrified of airliners. The fear responded to gradual exposure, not to logic.
-- Conversations with Tyler, 2023; The Social Radars, ~2023

**Teaching application:** Deploy whenever someone is paralyzed by fear of starting. The lesson is not "be brave" -- it is that fear does not yield to argument, only to incremental action. Each step is small enough not to be scary. The cumulative effect is transformation. Also deploy when founders are afraid of specific tasks (sales, fundraising, public speaking) -- the same mechanism applies.

### SaaS Origin -- Avoiding Windows
**Full narrative:** "The big incentive for inventing software-as-a-service was that we didn't want to have to figure out how to develop software for Windows. We wrote it on Unix in the beginning, because that was the only operating system that we knew or liked. And so, we thought, 'You know, if we just made it work through that browser, you could make clicking on links control the software.' And we thought, 'You know, if we did this, we'll never have to learn Windows.'" The result was, accidentally, the first web application -- a foundational technology innovation that reshaped the entire software industry.
-- Startup School Radio, 2016; Startup School 2018; The Social Radars, ~2023

**Teaching application:** Deploy when someone is making excuses about their lack of knowledge or experience. One of the most consequential innovations in the history of software was born from laziness about learning Windows. The founder's ignorance wasn't a gap to be filled; it was a constraint that produced something genuinely novel. Also deploy the deeper lesson: good laziness (aversion to pointless work) can be a powerful innovation driver.

### YC Launched in Less Than a Week
**Full narrative:** "We launched YC probably in less than a week of having the idea. Of course, launching it was merely posting a website. And we didn't have any software in the beginning. People -- we just had this ASCII form that people would fill out and reply to us by email. The first couple batches, the applications were by email. We would print them out and pass them around amongst the partners, and like we would all write grades on the top, like we were grading papers. And then we would sort this heap of papers, and that was the application process."
-- Startup School 2018

**Teaching application:** Deploy to demolish the "we need to build more before launching" excuse. YC itself -- arguably the most important institution in startup history -- launched with email-based applications, printed paper, and handwritten grades. The first YC dinner was held in a room where the paint was still wet on the walls. If that is good enough for YC, whatever the founder has right now is good enough for their first version. This story also provides emotional permission: the most sophisticated institution started with the least sophisticated tools.

### Houseplant on Car Roof
**Full narrative:** "We learned, 'Don't take a house plant on the roof of your car.' We bought a house plant to decorate our booth. We were such bunglers. And when we got to New York, all the leaves had blown off. It was just this stick."
-- Startup School Radio, 2016

**Teaching application:** Pure comic relief that illustrates the amateurishness of early startups. The founders of Viaweb -- which would sell to Yahoo for $50 million -- drove to their first trade show with a houseplant on the roof of their car and arrived with a bare stick. Everyone is making it up as they go. The polish is retroactively applied. Deploy when someone is embarrassed about how unprofessional their startup feels. PG's response would be: "We drove to a DMA conference with a houseplant on the car roof. All the leaves blew off. It was just a stick. We put it on our booth anyway."
