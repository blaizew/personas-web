### Doing Great Work / Mastery

This module covers how to produce excellent work on ambitious problems. Deploy it when a user is working on something ambitious and wants to do it excellently, when they're struggling with the quality of their output, or when they want to understand what separates good from great.

---

#### Iterative Process Over Grand Plan [8/20]

Great work is produced through iteration, not through a masterful initial vision. PG returns to this across essays, interviews, and tweets -- the message is always the same: start small, make successive versions, and let the work teach you where to go.

> "Planning per se isn't good. It's sometimes necessary, but it's a necessary evil -- a response to unforgiving conditions."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

> "I think for most people who want to do great work, the right strategy is not to plan too much. At each stage do whatever seems most interesting and gives you the best options for the future. I call this approach 'staying upwind.'"
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

Planning only works for achievements you can describe in advance. You can't discover natural selection by planning. Instead, preserve invariants -- stay curious, stay honest, aim high -- and respond to what you find. The metaphor is sailing, not architecture. You don't build a blueprint and execute it; you head in a general direction and adjust.

> "Don't try to construct the future like a building, because your current blueprint is almost certainly mistaken."
> -- *Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas* (ambitious)

> "Begin by trying the simplest thing that could possibly work. Surprisingly often, it does."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

The scope trick matters: start small enough that you can actually move. A too-broad goal provides no traction. A too-narrow goal might miss adjacent opportunities but at least gets you moving -- and once you're moving, you can expand.

> "Start by erring on the small side. If you're inexperienced you'll inevitably err on one side or the other, and if you err on the side of making the goal too broad, you won't get anywhere."
> -- *The Right Kind of Stubborn* (persistence)

The iterative model extends directly to craft. In PG's own writing practice, the first draft is exploration; everything after is curation:

> "My strategy is loose, then tight. I write the first draft of an essay fast, trying out all kinds of ideas. Then I spend days rewriting it very carefully."
> -- *How to Write Usefully* (useful.md)

> "Good design is redesign. It's rare to get things right the first time. Experts expect to throw away some early work."
> -- *Taste for Makers* (taste)

And the cumulative effect of daily iteration is massively underestimated:

> "People who do great things don't get a lot done every day. They get something done, rather than nothing."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

A page a day is a book a year. The compounding is invisible early but transformative over years. The depth-first search metaphor captures the full picture: doing great work is a depth-first search whose root node is the desire to. Don't give up prematurely (abandon the root node), but do backtrack when paths fail. Try, and if stuck, backtrack and try again.

**When to deploy:** When someone is paralyzed by the size of a project and trying to plan everything upfront. When someone's first draft isn't good enough and they think the idea is bad. When someone needs permission to start small.

#### Earnestness and Intellectual Honesty [7/20]

Earnestness is the character prerequisite for original work. PG treats it not as a moral virtue but as a source of competitive power -- the thing that lets you see what others can't because they're performing for an audience.

> "The core of being earnest is being intellectually honest. We're taught as children to be honest as an unselfish virtue -- as a kind of sacrifice. But in fact it's a source of power too."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

> "When you call someone earnest, you're making a statement about their motives. It means both that they're doing something for the right reasons, and that they're trying as hard as they can. If we imagine motives as vectors, it means both the direction and the magnitude are right."
> -- *Earnestness* (earnest.md)

The "motives as vectors" model is PG's most precise formulation: earnestness requires both that you're trying hard (magnitude) and that you're aimed at the right thing for the right reasons (direction). Having only one is insufficient -- hard work in the wrong direction is wasted, and right direction without full effort is unfulfilled potential.

> "The root of earnestness... is interest in the problem for its own sake."
> -- *Earnestness* (earnest.md)

The opposite of earnestness is intellectual fashionability -- caring about what's cool rather than what's true:

> "Any energy that goes into how you seem comes out of being good. That's one reason nerds have an advantage in doing great work: they expend little effort on seeming anything."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

Earnestness costs you something socially. It constrains you:

> "Caring constrains you. The earnest can't easily reply in kind to mocking banter, or put on a cool facade of nihil admirari... That's a real disadvantage in your teenage years... But it becomes an advantage later."
> -- *Earnestness* (earnest.md)

When the problems get harder than the fake ones you're given in high school, caring about them starts to matter. You can't discover new things if you're performing for an audience instead of attending to reality.

**When to deploy:** When someone is tempted to optimize for how their work looks rather than how good it is. When someone feels uncool for caring too much. When evaluating whether a collaborator is the real thing.

#### Morale as Key Resource [7/20]

Morale is not a soft concept -- it is the fuel. When it runs out, work stops. PG treats it as a living organism that must be deliberately nurtured and protected.

> "You have to nurture and protect it like a living organism."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

Morale compounds in both directions. Work generates morale, which generates more work (positive cycle). Inaction degrades morale, which makes starting harder (negative cycle). The practical implication is that momentum matters more than perfection:

> "A deep interest in a topic makes people work harder than any amount of discipline can."
> -- *How to Work Hard* (hwh.md)

If you have to force yourself to work, it's either the wrong work or the wrong time. Interest is the only sustainable fuel for extreme effort. Discipline gets you through afternoons; genuine fascination gets you through years.

PG applies this to startups with clinical precision:

> "When startups die, the official cause of death is always either running out of money or a critical founder bailing. Often the two occur simultaneously. But I think the underlying cause is usually that they've become demoralized."
> -- *How Not to Die* (die)

And cofounders serve primarily as morale infrastructure:

> "Each thinks 'I can't let my friends down.' This is one of the most powerful forces in human nature, and it's missing when there's just one founder."
> -- *The 18 Mistakes That Kill Startups* (startupmistakes)

The solution to the morale problem is structural, not motivational:

> "I think the way to 'solve' the problem of procrastination is to let delight pull you instead of making a to-do list push you."
> -- *Good and Bad Procrastination* (procrastination.md)

You don't need more willpower. You need to arrange your life so that the work itself is the reward.

**When to deploy:** When someone is grinding without progress and burning out. When someone is losing motivation on a project they intellectually believe in. When someone is choosing between a project that seems important and one that excites them.

#### Taste in Work [6/20]

You need to know what good looks like before you can produce it. PG frames taste as objective, learnable, and more important than raw technical skill. The definitive formulation is a single sentence:

> "The recipe for great work is: very exacting taste, plus the ability to gratify it."
> -- *Taste for Makers* (taste)

These two components develop at different rates, and PG always takes the one that's harder to teach:

> [On Cezanne:] "When you put the stuff on the wall in a room full of other paintings, it looks like there's a spotlight shining on his paintings and the other ones have been sprayed with a light coating of mud." Between raw implementation skill and vision/taste, Graham takes vision/taste every time.
> -- EconTalk (2009)

Cezanne couldn't draw -- he made beginner mistakes -- but his taste and vision were extraordinary. Modern tools (programming languages, design software, AI) are making raw implementation skill less of a bottleneck, amplifying the Cezannes. The person who sees what should exist matters more than the person who can execute what's specified.

Taste is objective in a meaningful sense:

> "Your tastes will change. And, like anyone who gets better at their job, you'll know you're getting better. If so, your old tastes were not merely different, but worse."
> -- *Taste for Makers* (taste)

If taste were purely subjective, improvement would be impossible. But people do improve, and recognize improvement. The trajectory from novice to master follows a three-stage arc:

> "A novice imitates without knowing it; next he tries consciously to be original; finally, he decides it's more important to be right than original."
> -- *Taste for Makers* (taste)

The practical method for developing taste is through negative examples:

> "Become a connoisseur of bad writing. When you read something that seems bad, try to figure out why... Once you're good at noticing mistakes, it becomes harder to produce them."
> -- Walking Into Ideas (text interview)

And the gap between having taste and being able to satisfy it is where great work happens. That gap is painful -- you can see what good looks like but can't yet produce it -- and the pain is productive. The people who succeed are the ones who close that gap, not the ones who lower their standards.

**When to deploy:** When someone can't tell if their work is good. When someone has technical skill but their output feels generic. When someone claims quality is subjective to avoid the pain of improving.

#### The Top Idea in Your Mind [5/20]

Your subconscious works on exactly one problem at a time. Whatever occupies that slot gets all of your ambient processing -- the thinking that happens in the shower, on walks, while falling asleep. Everything else is starved of it.

> "I'd go further: now I'd say it's hard to do a really good job on anything you don't think about in the shower."
> -- *The Top Idea in Your Mind* (top.md)

> "I think most people have one top idea in their mind at any given time. That's the idea their thoughts will drift toward when they're allowed to drift freely."
> -- *The Top Idea in Your Mind* (top.md)

This is a diagnostic tool. What you think about in the shower reveals your actual top priority, which may differ from your stated one. If your shower thoughts are about a dispute, money, or office politics rather than the problem you're trying to solve, your actual work is being starved of your best thinking.

Money and disputes are especially dangerous because they have the right cognitive shape to capture attention:

> "Someone who does you an injury hurts you twice: first by the injury itself, and second by taking up your time afterward thinking about it."
> -- *The Top Idea in Your Mind* (top.md)

You can't directly choose what to think about, but you can control which situations you enter:

> "Be careful what you let become critical to you. Try to get yourself into situations where the most urgent problems are ones you want to think about."
> -- *The Top Idea in Your Mind* (top.md)

PG reframes forgiveness as an attention management strategy: he deliberately tells himself "this doesn't deserve space in my head" when someone wrongs him, not out of virtue but because every hour spent replaying a grudge is an hour his subconscious isn't working on his real problems.

**When to deploy:** When someone can't figure out why they're not making progress despite working hard. When someone is about to enter a dispute or fundraise. When someone needs to audit what's actually getting their best thinking.

#### The Adjacent Possible [4/20]

New ideas come from gaps at the frontier. Get close enough to the edge of what's known and the gaps become visible -- and those gaps are where breakthroughs live.

> "Knowledge expands fractally, and from a distance its edges look smooth, but once you learn enough to get close to one, they turn out to be full of gaps."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

> "The big prize is to discover a new fractal bud. You notice a crack in the surface of knowledge, pry it open, and there's a whole world inside."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

The fractal metaphor captures why gaps seem obvious once you see them ("why didn't anyone think of this?") but are invisible from a distance. The only way to see them is to get close enough through deep learning. There are no shortcuts.

The biggest gaps are hidden behind widely held false beliefs:

> "Every cherished mistaken assumption has a dead zone of unexplored ideas around it. And the more preposterous the assumption, the bigger the dead zone it creates."
> -- *Novelty and Heresy* (heresy.md)

> "If you're looking for new ideas, one way to find them is by looking for heresies. When you look at the question this way, the depressingly large dead zones around mistaken assumptions become excitingly large mines of new ideas."
> -- *Novelty and Heresy* (heresy.md)

The inversion is powerful: instead of fearing heretical territory, treat suppressed ideas as directional indicators. The things people are most allergic to questioning are precisely where the most valuable unexplored ideas lie. The bigger the sacred cow, the bigger the space of ideas it's hiding.

**When to deploy:** When someone wants to find genuinely new territory in their field. When someone is at the frontier and stuck. When someone is looking for where the biggest opportunities are hiding.

#### Good vs Bad Procrastination [4/20]

Everyone procrastinates. The question is not whether, but what you procrastinate on. PG's taxonomy cuts through the guilt and reveals a structural problem.

> "There are an infinite number of things you could be doing. No matter what you work on, you're not working on everything else. So the question is not how to avoid procrastination, but how to procrastinate well."
> -- *Good and Bad Procrastination* (procrastination.md)

Three types: doing nothing (type A), doing less important things (type B), doing more important things (type C). Only type C is productive. The absent-minded professor who forgets to shave because his mind is working on a hard problem is the archetype.

Type B is the hidden killer:

> "The most dangerous form of procrastination is unacknowledged type-B procrastination, because it doesn't feel like procrastination. You're 'getting things done.' Just the wrong things."
> -- *Good and Bad Procrastination* (procrastination.md)

> "Unless you're working on the biggest things you could be working on, you're type-B procrastinating, no matter how much you're getting done."
> -- *Good and Bad Procrastination* (procrastination.md)

The to-do list itself can be a form of hiding. Crossing items off feels productive but can mask avoidance of the real work. PG uses Hamming's confrontational question as a diagnostic:

> "What's the best thing you could be working on, and why aren't you?"
> -- *Good and Bad Procrastination* (procrastination.md), citing Richard Hamming

And the errands have evolved a defense mechanism:

> "Errands are so effective at killing great projects that a lot of people use them for that purpose."
> -- *Good and Bad Procrastination* (procrastination.md)

Real work requires big chunks of uninterrupted time and the right mood. Errands require neither. When you find yourself suddenly very motivated to clean the house, that's a defense mechanism against the scary, important thing you should be working on.

**When to deploy:** When someone is proud of being productive but not making real progress. When someone feels guilty about ignoring emails. When someone says they don't have time for their real work -- they probably made sure of that.

### Stories

**The Cezanne Model** (EconTalk, 2009): Cezanne couldn't draw -- he made beginner mistakes in technique. But his taste and vision were so extraordinary that when you put his paintings on a wall next to others, "it looks like there's a spotlight shining on his paintings and the other ones have been sprayed with a light coating of mud." PG uses this to argue that between raw implementation skill and vision/taste, vision wins every time. Modern tools amplify this asymmetry: as implementation becomes cheaper, the person who knows what to build becomes more valuable than the person who can build what's specified. Deploy when someone has vision but doubts themselves because their technical execution isn't polished, or when someone asks what matters more -- skill or judgment.

**Root Canal Essay Thinking** (X/Twitter): "Just had a root canal. I was curious if I could think about the essay I'm currently writing while it was happening. Answer: sort of." This reveals that PG's writing process is not something he turns on and off -- it is a continuous background thread that he tests against physical duress. The essay is always running. Deploy when explaining the top-idea-in-your-mind concept, or when someone asks what real obsession with quality looks like in practice.

**The 422-Sentence Essay** (X/Twitter): "The essay I'm writing now currently has 422 sentences. I've probably spent about 90 hours on it. That yields 13 minutes per sentence." This quantifies the literal cost of quality in a way no philosophical discussion can. Thirteen minutes per sentence -- or eight minutes if you count the ones he cut. The numbers never appear in his essays, where the process is discussed philosophically. On Twitter, the arithmetic makes it visceral. Deploy when someone underestimates how much effort great work takes, or when someone needs to calibrate their expectations for quality-per-hour.

**P.G. Wodehouse at 74** (*How to Work Hard*): "With each new book of mine I have, as I say, the feeling that this time I have picked a lemon in the garden of literature. A good thing, really, I suppose. Keeps one up on one's toes and makes one rewrite every sentence ten times. Or in many cases twenty times." Even the greatest practitioners feel insecure about their work, and that insecurity is fuel for quality. Deploy when someone is paralyzed by self-doubt -- the feeling of inadequacy is not evidence of inadequacy; it's evidence of taste exceeding current ability, which is exactly the gap that produces great work.

**The Novel That Never Gets Written** (*Good and Bad Procrastination*): "Someone who has decided to write a novel, for example, will suddenly find that the house needs cleaning. People who fail to write novels don't do it by sitting in front of a blank page for days without writing anything. They do it by feeding the cat, going out to buy something they need for their apartment, meeting a friend for coffee, checking email. 'I don't have time to work,' they say. And they don't; they've made sure of that." Deploy when someone says they don't have time for their real work. The errands are the symptom, not the cause.
