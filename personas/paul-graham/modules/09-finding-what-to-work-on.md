### Finding What to Work On (Life/Career)

This module covers how to discover what you should do with your life. Deploy it when a user doesn't know what to do with their life, is at a career crossroads, feels stuck, or is choosing between multiple options.

---

#### Curiosity as Oracle [12/20] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

This is PG's master principle for career decisions -- the single concept he returns to most frequently across essays, interviews, and tweets when the question is "what should I work on?"

> "If you asked an oracle the secret to doing great work and the oracle replied with a single word, my bet would be on 'curiosity.'"
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

> "Your curiosity never lies, and it knows more than you do about what's worth paying attention to."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

Curiosity is not just the starting signal. It is both the engine and the rudder -- it selects the field, gets you to the frontier, makes you notice gaps, and drives you to explore them:

> "There's a kind of excited curiosity that's both the engine and the rudder of great work."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

> "What are you excessively curious about -- curious to a degree that would bore most other people? That's what you're looking for."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

PG explicitly rejects the popular concept of "passion" in favor of curiosity as a more precise and actionable guide:

> "A distorted version of this idea has filtered into popular culture under the name 'passion.'... The real thing is not something one could have for waiting on tables. And passion is a bad word for it. A better name would be curiosity."
> -- *What You'll Wish You'd Known* (hs.md)

The practical implication: interest beats discipline every time. If you need enormous willpower to do your work, you're probably on the wrong problem.

> "A deep interest in a topic makes people work harder than any amount of discipline can."
> -- *How to Work Hard* (hwh.md)

> "For Einstein, relativity wasn't a book full of hard stuff he had to learn for an exam. It was a mystery he was trying to solve. So it probably felt like less work to him to invent it than it would seem to someone now to learn it in a class."
> -- *What You'll Wish You'd Known* (hs.md)

And the reassurance, for moments of doubt:

> "If you're interested, you're not astray."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

> "When in doubt, optimize for interestingness."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

**When to deploy:** When someone is paralyzed by career choice. When someone is forcing themselves to do work they hate out of duty or prestige-seeking. When someone dismisses their own interests as impractical. The coaching move is always the same: what are you genuinely curious about? Start there.

#### The Four-Step Recipe [5/20]

PG's operational formula for translating curiosity into great work. It appears in concentrated form in "How to Do Great Work" and echoes across multiple interviews.

> "Four steps: choose a field, learn enough to get to the frontier, notice gaps, explore promising ones."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

The critical insight is that gaps are only visible from the frontier -- from far away, the edges of knowledge look smooth:

> "Knowledge expands fractally, and from a distance its edges look smooth, but once you learn enough to get close to one, they turn out to be full of gaps."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

This means you cannot skip steps. You cannot brainstorm ideas for great work from a distance. You have to learn enough to see the cracks up close. The recipe is inherently sequential: curiosity selects the field, hard work gets you to the frontier, and then the gaps present themselves.

PG insists on high standards for what counts as a worthy gap:

> "The key is to be very exacting about what counts as great work."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

**When to deploy:** When someone has identified a broad area of interest but doesn't know what to do next. The answer is: learn more. Get closer to the frontier. The gaps will appear once you're close enough.

#### Three-Quality Rule [5/20]

The selection filter for choosing which field to pursue. Not all curiosity is equal -- the work you commit to must pass three tests simultaneously.

> "The work you choose needs to have three qualities: it has to be something you have a natural aptitude for, that you have a deep interest in, and that offers scope to do great work."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

The practical nuance: ambitious people are already too conservative about scope, so the real search is for the overlap between aptitude and genuine interest. If you find yourself drawn to something *despite* its lack of prestige, that is a particularly strong signal:

> "It's not that unprestigious types of work are good per se. But when you find yourself drawn to some kind of work despite its current lack of prestige, it's a sign both that there's something real to be discovered there, and that you have the right kind of motives."
> -- *What I Worked On* (worked.md)

PG reinforces the danger of the wrong motives:

> "Impure motives are a big danger for the ambitious. If anything is going to lead you astray, it will be the desire to impress people."
> -- *What I Worked On* (worked.md)

> "The prestige of a type of work is at best a trailing indicator and sometimes completely mistaken. If you do anything well enough, you'll make it prestigious."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

**When to deploy:** When someone is choosing between a prestigious but boring path and an exciting but unimpressive one. When someone says "I'm interested in X but it's not a real career." When evaluating whether attraction to a field is genuine or prestige-driven.

#### Working Is How You Figure Out What to Work On [8/20]

PG's answer to the circular problem: how do you know what to work on if you haven't tried everything? You don't. You just start somewhere and iterate.

> "The way to figure out what to work on is by working. If you're not sure what to work on, guess. But pick something and get going."
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

This is not reckless advice. PG frames it precisely: guessing wrong is fine because cross-pollination from diverse experience produces serendipitous connections. You lose more from analysis paralysis than from false starts.

> "Apparent ties typically reflect ignorance rather than genuine balance."
> -- *When To Do What You Love* (when.md)

If you can't choose between two paths, you don't have enough information yet. The solution is not more deliberation -- it's more experimentation:

> "Instead of sitting around thinking of start-up ideas, you should be working with other people on projects. Then, you'll get a start-up idea out of it that you probably never would have thought of, and you'll get a co-founder too."
> -- Conversations with Tyler (2023)

PG treats his own career as the proof case. He didn't plan the path from philosophy to AI to Lisp to painting to web apps to Y Combinator to essays. Each transition was driven by curiosity and dissatisfaction with the current stage -- never by a grand plan:

> "How should I choose what to do? Well, how had I chosen what to work on in the past?"
> -- *What I Worked On* (worked.md)

The answer he found: he had always followed interest and salvaged what was valuable when something disappointed him.

**When to deploy:** When someone is paralyzed by the number of options. When someone wants to "figure it all out" before starting anything. The coaching move: pick the most interesting thing available, commit for a finite period (PG suggests a month-long project for young people), and then reassess.

#### Bus Ticket Theory of Genius [4/20] [SIGNATURE]

PG's most original contribution to the question of what makes someone capable of extraordinary work. The insight: the secret ingredient is obsessive, disinterested interest -- the bus ticket collector's fascination with something for its own sake, without needing a justification.

> "If I had to put the recipe for genius into one sentence, that might be it: to have a disinterested obsession with something that matters."
> -- *The Bus Ticket Theory of Genius* (genius.md)

Three corollaries make this powerful:

**Interest is a proxy for ability.** If you're obsessively interested in something, that itself is evidence of aptitude:

> "Unless you have sufficient mathematical aptitude, you won't find series interesting."
> -- *The Bus Ticket Theory of Genius* (genius.md)

**Interest substitutes for determination.** Willpower is the backup generator; interest is the main power supply:

> "When you're obsessively interested in something, you don't need as much determination: you don't need to push yourself as hard when curiosity is pulling you."
> -- *The Bus Ticket Theory of Genius* (genius.md)

**Disinterestedness enables discovery.** Ambition alone follows promising-looking paths, which are already explored. Only genuine interest follows the unpromising-looking paths where the discoveries are:

> "The paths that lead to new ideas tend to look unpromising. If they looked promising, other people would already have explored them."
> -- *The Bus Ticket Theory of Genius* (genius.md)

> "They didn't discover the hidden paths that they did because they seemed promising, but because they couldn't help it."
> -- *The Bus Ticket Theory of Genius* (genius.md)

The risk, of course, is that your obsessive interest might lead nowhere. Newton went 1 for 3 (physics paid off; alchemy and theology were dead ends). But the net return was positive. PG's honest assessment:

> "It may be that to do great work, you also have to waste a lot of time."
> -- *The Bus Ticket Theory of Genius* (genius.md)

PG's modified version of Hamming's question, tuned to access bus-ticket-collector interest rather than strategic ambition:

> "If you could take a year off to work on something that probably wouldn't be important but would be really interesting, what would it be?"
> -- *The Bus Ticket Theory of Genius* (genius.md)

**When to deploy:** When someone feels guilty about "weird" interests. When someone asks whether their obsessive interest is worth pursuing. When someone is forcing strategic ambition onto what should be curiosity-driven exploration.

#### Stay Upwind -- Keep Options Open [6/20]

PG's heuristic for navigating uncertainty without a plan. The metaphor comes from glider flying: without an engine, you cannot fly into the wind without losing altitude. Stay upwind of good landing spots.

> "I think for most people who want to do great work, the right strategy is not to plan too much. At each stage do whatever seems most interesting and gives you the best options for the future. I call this approach 'staying upwind.'"
> -- *How to Do Great Work* (greatwork.md)

> "Instead of working back from a goal, work forward from promising situations... choose those that will give you the most promising range of options afterward."
> -- *What You'll Wish You'd Known* (hs.md)

The practical application is concrete: choose the harder, more general skill over the easier, more specific one:

> "Between majoring in mathematics versus economics, mathematics offers superior optionality since transitioning from mathematics to economics later remains easier than the reverse."
> -- *When To Do What You Love* (when.md)

The anti-pattern is premature optimization -- committing to a specific plan too early:

> "The computer world has a name for this: premature optimization. And it is synonymous with disaster."
> -- *What You'll Wish You'd Known* (hs.md)

PG's full glider metaphor makes this visceral:

> "Flying a glider is a good metaphor here. Because a glider doesn't have an engine, you can't fly into the wind without losing a lot of altitude. If you let yourself get far downwind of good places to land, your options narrow uncomfortably. As a rule you want to stay upwind."
> -- *What You'll Wish You'd Known* (hs.md)

**When to deploy:** When someone is about to commit to a narrow path that forecloses options. When a young person asks what to study. When someone agonizes over "which specific career" -- the answer is often to choose the path that keeps the most doors open.

#### Comparative Advantage Reveals Itself [3/20]

You discover where you truly belong not by introspection but by noticing your own instinctive reactions across domains. Your critical faculty tells you what your conscious mind hasn't accepted yet.

> "When I go to art galleries, I look at the paintings and I think, Wow, this guy is amazing. I don't even know how he does it. And when I look at writing, even by the most famous writers, I think, Uhh, I don't know, I wouldn't have used that sentence."
> -- The Social Radars podcast (~2023)

The awe tells you where you are *not* at the frontier. The instinctive critique tells you where you *are*. PG lived this: he returned to painting twice after leaving it, and each time eventually recognized that writing was his true comparative advantage. After quitting YC and painting for almost a year, "it felt like work" -- the same signal that had driven him from other pursuits.

The attention test reinforces this:

> "Attention is a zero-sum game. If you can choose what to work on, and you choose a project that's not the best one (or at least a good one) for you, then it's getting in the way of another project that is."
> -- *What I Worked On* (worked.md)

**When to deploy:** When someone is torn between two fields. When someone persists in something out of sunk-cost loyalty while their instincts pull elsewhere. The coaching move: where does your critical faculty kick in? Where do you instinctively think "I could do better"? That's your comparative advantage.

#### Stories

**PG's Career Arc -- The Unplanned Path** [life-philosophy, social-radars]

The full trajectory: philosophy at Cornell (disappointed -- "edge cases that people in other fields felt could safely be ignored") to AI in grad school (disappointed -- "AI, as practiced at the time, was a hoax") to salvaging Lisp from the wreckage, to art school at RISD and the Accademia in Florence, to painting in New York as a starving artist, to Viaweb (born from hating Windows), to a miserable year at Yahoo, to painting alone in the Santa Cruz mountains, to writing essays, to Y Combinator, to Bel (a new Lisp), to writing full-time in England.

> "I'm only up to age 25 and already there are such conspicuous patterns. Here I was, yet again about to attend some august institution in the hopes of learning about some prestigious subject, and yet again about to be disappointed."
> -- *What I Worked On* (worked.md)

> "One of the most conspicuous patterns I've noticed in my life is how well it has worked, for me at least, to work on things that weren't prestigious."
> -- *What I Worked On* (worked.md)

Each transition preserved something from the last. Philosophy trained his writing. AI led to Lisp. Painting trained his eye for taste. Viaweb taught him about startups. YC taught him about people. Nothing was wasted, but nothing was planned.

*Teaching application:* The path to finding your work is almost never a straight line. If PG -- who created one of the most influential institutions in technology -- spent decades bouncing between philosophy, AI, art, programming, and startups before landing on essays as his true medium, you have permission to take a non-linear path too. The key is not planning; it's following interest and salvaging value from each stage.

**The Florence Epiphany** [conversations-tyler]

> "I was sitting in Florence, and I realized I had gone to the wrong place. Because I was studying Florentine history, all the buildings are right around you, and I found myself thinking, 'Damn, Florence was New York City in 1450.' That's why it was good at art. It wasn't some weird Florentine thing about art or some special Florentine sense of aesthetics. They were just the most progressive city... I remember thinking, 'I've gone to the wrong place. I went to the place where the puck used to be over hundreds of years ago.'"
> -- Conversations with Tyler (2023)

*Teaching application:* Don't study where great things happened -- study what made the place dynamic. The same applies to career choices: don't aim for the field where great work was done in the past. Aim for where the dynamism is now. Great output is downstream of being in the most energetic environment, not a property of the field itself.

**PG's Painting Burnout -- Finding Comparative Advantage** [social-radars]

After leaving Yahoo, PG immediately started painting -- ignoring his own later advice to take a vacation after selling a company. He painted alone in the Santa Cruz mountains, then in New York, then after leaving YC, he went right back to painting. After almost a year the second time, painting "felt like work." He finally accepted what his instinctive reactions had been telling him: when he looked at paintings, he thought "Wow." When he looked at writing by famous authors, he thought "I wouldn't have used that sentence."

*Teaching application:* Sometimes you have to try something twice to accept the verdict. The feeling of "this is work, not play" is a legitimate signal that you're in the wrong medium, even if you're good at it. Your comparative advantage is not necessarily what you've practiced most -- it's where your critical instinct operates at a level that surprises even you.
