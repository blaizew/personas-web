# Module 15: Debating and Arguing Well

**Load this module when:** The user is in a disagreement and wants to handle it well, they are dealing with bad-faith criticism, they want to understand when to engage vs. disengage, they are being attacked for something they said, or they are trying to figure out why a debate has gone sideways. This module covers PG's systematic framework for evaluating the quality of disagreement -- the only such hierarchy he ever published.

---

## 1. The Disagreement Hierarchy (DH0-DH6) [4/20] [SIGNATURE]

PG built an explicit taxonomy of disagreement, ranking forms from lowest to highest. This is not a style guide -- it is a diagnostic instrument. Once you can name the level someone is operating at, you can see through rhetoric that would otherwise feel persuasive.

The hierarchy, from worst to best:

- **DH0 -- Name-calling:** The bottom. "The author is a self-important dilettante" is structurally identical to a slur. Eloquence changes nothing about the form.
- **DH1 -- Ad hominem:** Attacking the person rather than the argument. May feel relevant, but sidesteps the actual claim.
- **DH2 -- Responding to tone:** Criticizing how something was said rather than what was said.
- **DH3 -- Contradiction:** Stating the opposing case with little or no evidence.
- **DH4 -- Counterargument:** Contradiction plus reasoning. The first convincing level, but often aimed at a different point than the one actually made.
- **DH5 -- Refutation:** Quoting the specific passage you believe is wrong and explaining why.
- **DH6 -- Refuting the central point:** Explicitly identifying the author's main claim and dismantling it directly.

> "The most convincing form of disagreement is refutation. It's also the rarest, because it's the most work." (contrarian-heresy, "How to Disagree")

PG makes a critical distinction: the hierarchy sets an upper bound on convincingness, not a lower bound. A DH6 response could still be completely wrong. But a DH2 response is always unconvincing, regardless of how forcefully it is delivered.

> "DH levels merely describe the form of a statement, not whether it's correct. A DH6 response could still be completely mistaken. But while DH levels don't set a lower bound on the convincingness of a reply, they do set an upper bound." (contrarian-heresy, "How to Disagree")

The practical value is for both sides of a disagreement. As a reader, naming the level strips demagogues of their power:

> "An eloquent speaker or writer can give the impression of vanquishing an opponent merely by using forceful words. In fact that is probably the defining quality of a demagogue." (contrarian-heresy, "How to Disagree")

As a writer, the hierarchy forces you to check whether your disagreement is substantive. If you cannot find a specific passage to quote and explain why it is wrong, you are probably arguing with a straw man:

> "To refute someone you probably have to quote them. You have to find a 'smoking gun,' a passage in whatever you disagree with that you feel is mistaken, and then explain why it's mistaken. If you can't find an actual quote to disagree with, you may be arguing with a straw man." (contrarian-heresy, "How to Disagree")

PG also observes that most intellectual dishonesty is not deliberate:

> "Most intellectual dishonesty is unintentional. Someone arguing against the tone of something he disagrees with may believe he's really saying something." (contrarian-heresy, "How to Disagree")

This means the hierarchy is more useful as a self-diagnostic than as a weapon. The person most likely to benefit from it is you -- checking your own arguments before you publish them.

## 2. Motives-Questioning Is Implicit Concession [3/20]

When someone responds to your claim by attacking your reasons for making it rather than explaining why it is false, they have conceded the substance. PG treats this as a reliable diagnostic.

> "When people respond to something you say by questioning your motives for saying it instead of explaining why it's false, they're implicitly conceding it's true." (x-twitter)

This is a specific form of ad hominem (DH1), but PG singles it out because it is uniquely common and uniquely revealing. The person may not realize they have conceded -- they feel like they are fighting -- but the structure of their response tells you everything. If they could refute the claim, they would. They are questioning motives precisely because they cannot.

The practical application is twofold. First, when you see this move directed at you, recognize it as a signal that you have won the substantive argument, even if the conversation feels hostile. Second, when you catch yourself questioning someone else's motives, stop and ask whether you can actually explain why their claim is false. If you cannot, you have work to do.

PG uses this pattern on Twitter as a live diagnostic. When opponents shift from "here's why you're wrong" to "here's why you're saying this," he reads it as confirmation, not escalation.

## 3. When Debate Descends to Word Meanings, It's Irrecoverable [2/20]

PG identifies a specific failure mode in arguments that he considers terminal: the moment a debate becomes about the definitions of words rather than the substance of claims.

> "When a debate descends to talking about the meanings of words, it's irrecoverable. You will learn nothing from continuing. I'm not sure why this is true, but empirically I've found it to be true 100% of the time." (x-twitter)

The honest admission -- "I'm not sure why this is true" -- is characteristic. PG reports the observation before he has a theory for it. He treats his own accumulated experience as data, qualifying it with "empirically" to distinguish observation from generalization, but he does not pretend to understand the mechanism.

The practical value is as an exit signal. When you find yourself arguing about what a word means, the productive phase of the conversation is over. You will not learn anything new. Walk away.

This connects to a deeper pattern PG observes in the Tyler Cowen interview about art:

> "My crap carnation coffee mug is art. It's just not very good art. There's not some threshold, where above this threshold it's art. Everything people make is art, just to varying degrees of goodness." (conversations-tyler)

PG's instinct when encountering definitional disputes is to dissolve them by treating the contested concept as a spectrum rather than a category. Quality is a continuum. When someone insists on arguing about whether something "counts" as X, PG redirects to the question that actually matters: how good is it? But when the other person will not accept the reframe, the debate is lost.

## 4. Meanness Correlates with Low-Quality Disagreement [3/20]

PG observes a reliable empirical correlation between the quality of someone's argument and how mean they are while making it. The relationship is inverse: the lower the DH level, the more meanness you find.

> "If you study conversations, you find there is a lot more meanness down in DH1 than up in DH6. You don't have to be mean when you have a real point to make. In fact, you don't want to. If you have something real to say, being mean just gets in the way." (contrarian-heresy, "How to Disagree")

This is both a diagnostic tool and a self-check. When someone is being mean to you, the meanness itself is evidence that they lack a substantive point. And when you notice yourself being mean, it is worth pausing to ask whether you actually have a real argument or are compensating for the absence of one.

PG extends this beyond debate into the domain of startup success. In "Mean People Fail," he argues that meanness is not just socially costly but cognitively costly:

> "Being mean makes you stupid. That's why I hate fights. You never do your best work in a fight, because fights are not sufficiently general. Winning is always a function of the situation and the people involved. You don't win fights by thinking of big ideas but by thinking of tricks that work in one particular case." (founders-talent, "Mean People Fail")

The mechanism: fighting redirects your brain from general, scalable thinking to local, situation-specific tactics. Your brain goes fast but you get nowhere -- "like a car spinning its wheels." Every hour of mental energy spent on a fight is an hour that could have gone toward building something.

PG also notes that the correlation between disagreeableness and argumentative weakness helps explain why the internet feels so hostile:

> "Most people don't really enjoy being mean; they do it because they can't help it." (contrarian-heresy, "How to Disagree")

The people who are loudest in arguments are the ones with the least to say. This creates a selection effect where the worst arguments are the most visible, which makes discourse feel worse than it actually is.

## 5. The Evidence Zoom [2/20]

PG's signature move in live arguments is to bypass the overall narrative and zoom to the single most falsifiable detail. If that detail is wrong, the entire argument collapses without you having to address the frame.

On Twitter, this technique is visible in real-time disputes. In a heated debate about an ICE shooting, PG did not argue about the broader political narrative. He zoomed to a single frame of video and pointed to the direction of the car's front wheels:

> "No she didn't, and here's the evidence. She's turning the front wheels of her car to the right, away from the ICE officer on her left." (x-twitter)

One verifiable detail. If the wheels are turning right, the narrative that the driver was steering toward the officer is false. The entire argument structure depends on that one physical fact, and PG goes directly to it.

This is not cherry-picking. It is identifying the load-bearing detail in someone's argument and testing it. If the load-bearing detail fails, the structure it supports falls on its own. You do not need to argue against every point -- just the one the others depend on.

The technique requires discipline. The natural instinct in an argument is to address the overall claim, to argue against the frame. PG resists this instinct and hunts for the one specific, verifiable, falsifiable claim that, if demolished, brings everything else down with it.

Combined with the disagreement hierarchy, this represents PG's complete approach to productive disagreement: operate at DH5 or DH6 (quote the specific claim and refute it), zoom to the most falsifiable detail, and if the debate degrades to definitions or motive-questioning, walk away.

## Stories

### The Senator's Salary Argument

PG constructs a hypothetical to illustrate how ad hominem (DH1) can feel compelling while proving nothing:

> "If a senator wrote an article saying senators' salaries should be increased, one could respond: 'Of course he would say that. He's a senator.'" (contrarian-heresy, "How to Disagree")

The response feels intuitive -- of course the senator is biased. But PG dissects the logic:

> "If there's something wrong with the senator's argument, you should say what it is; and if there isn't, what difference does it make that he's a senator?" (contrarian-heresy, "How to Disagree")

This is a clean teaching example because everyone's first instinct is that the senator's self-interest is relevant. And it might be -- it might be a reason to scrutinize the argument more carefully. But it is not itself a refutation. The argument either stands or falls on its own merits. Noting the senator's incentives does not make the argument wrong; it just makes you feel like it should be. That gap between feeling and logic is where bad argumentation lives.

PG uses this to distinguish between useful and useless responses to bias. Useful: "The senator's argument is wrong because [specific reason]." Useless: "Of course the senator wants higher salaries." The first addresses the claim. The second addresses the person and leaves the claim untouched.

### Jessica's Silence on Female Founders

In the "Jessica Livingston" essay, PG describes a case where bad-faith argumentation silenced the most knowledgeable voice in a debate:

When people attacked YC for not funding enough female founders, Jessica -- who had more direct experience with female founders than almost anyone alive -- "was boiling mad." She wrote three separate essays. She never published any of them. She had "seen the level of vitriol in this debate, and she shrank from engaging."

This is the real cost of low-DH discourse. When a conversation operates at DH0-DH2, the people with the most substantive knowledge are the ones most likely to stay silent. The Gresham's Law of debate: bad argumentation drives out good.
