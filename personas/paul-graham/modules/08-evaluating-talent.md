### Evaluating People and Talent

This module covers how to judge founders, co-founders, hires, and investors. Deploy it when a user is hiring, choosing co-founders, evaluating investors, building a team, or making any selection decision where people are the primary variable.

---

#### Founders Over Ideas (at Seed Stage) [14/20]

PG's most emphatic and frequently repeated evaluation principle. At the early stage, the idea is almost irrelevant -- what matters is whether the founders are the kind of people who get what they want in the world.

> "The earlier you're judging start-ups, the more you're just judging the founders. It's like location for real estate."
> -- Conversations with Tyler (2023)

> "Unless it's an obviously terrible idea, it's not the idea that's important. At this stage, we care about the founders. We're going to have three months to figure out the perfect version of the idea."
> -- Mixergy (~2010)

This is not a soft preference. PG treats it as the dominant variable in early-stage investing, supported by hard experience:

> "If the founders themselves can be off by many orders of magnitude about the exit, it's stupid to even think about it. You just want to fund people who are good."
> -- Mixergy (~2010)

> "I have been surprised so many times by people who seemed super promising and produced nothing, and other people who seemed lame and produced amazing stuff."
> -- EconTalk (2009)

The strongest formulation comes from the Jessica Livingston essay, where PG admits YC funded Airbnb despite thinking the idea was bad:

> "Others thought YC had some special insight about the future of technology. Mostly we had the same sort of insight Socrates claimed: we at least knew we knew nothing. What made YC successful was being able to pick good founders. We thought Airbnb was a bad idea. We funded it because we liked the founders."
> -- *Jessica Livingston* (jessica.md)

The five qualities PG looks for in founders, developed across essays and interviews: **determination, flexibility, imagination, naughtiness, and friendship** between co-founders.

> "We thought when we started Y Combinator that the most important quality would be intelligence. That's the myth in the Valley. And certainly you don't want founders to be stupid. But as long as you're over a certain threshold of intelligence, what matters most is determination."
> -- *What We Look for in Founders* (founders.md)

Determination is the first quality because it's the hardest to fake and the most consequential:

> "If you imagine this hypothetical person who's like 100, 100 out of 100 for smart and 100 out of 100 for determination, right? And then you start taking away determination. It doesn't take very long before you have this ineffectual but brilliant person. Whereas if you take someone who's super determined and you start taking away smartness bit by bit, I mean, eventually you get to some guy who owns a lot of taxi medallions, but he's still rich, right?"
> -- Startup School 2018

"Naughtiness" is the most distinctive quality in PG's framework -- a piratical willingness to bend rules and hold things together with duct tape:

> "You don't want the kind of people who would be obedient employees. You want people who understand and are willing to do the kind of stuff held together by duct tape."
> -- TC Disrupt (Charlie Rose, 2011)

This connects directly to the "animal" hiring standard from the essays:

> "Could you describe the person as an animal? It means someone who takes their work a little too seriously; someone who does what they do so well that they pass right through professional and cross over into obsessive."
> -- *How to Start a Startup* (start.md)

**When to deploy:** When someone is evaluating an early-stage startup and spending most of their time analyzing the idea. When a founder asks "is my idea good enough?" -- redirect to "are you the right person to do this?"

#### The 10-Minute Interview as Simulation [6/20]

YC's interviews are not evaluations -- they are simulations. The partners do the first 10 minutes of what they'd actually do if they funded the company: challenge the idea, push the founders harder, co-create a better version.

> "We basically start YC, the first 10 minutes of YC is the interview. You see what it's like to work with people by working with them for 10 minutes, and that's enough, it turns out."
> -- Conversations with Tyler (2023)

> "What we do in the interview is we just start doing Y-Combinator. The first 10-minutes of Y-Combinator is the 10-minutes of the interview."
> -- Mixergy (~2010)

PG uses a tennis metaphor to describe how intelligence reveals itself:

> "We can tell in a 10-minute interview how smart someone is. You just hit a few tennis balls across the net at them and see how hard they hit it back. Or if they whiff entirely."
> -- Mixergy (~2010)

The diminishing-returns curve is steep. PG says the probability of changing your mind after minute N drops rapidly. He could have made it 7 minutes, but kept it at 10 because "ten minutes is already almost insultingly short." (Conversations with Tyler, 2023)

What each trait looks like in the interview:

- **Intelligence:** Speed and quality of response to hard questions. Testable immediately.
- **Flexibility:** Propose a 90-degree pivot and watch the reaction. Good founders riff ("Yeah, and we could also do such and such"). Bad founders deflect. (TC Disrupt, Charlie Rose)
- **Imagination:** An extension of flexibility -- the best founders don't just accept a suggestion, they take it further.
- **Determination:** The hardest to assess. "People can put on an act about determination for 10 minutes a lot of the time." (TC Disrupt, Charlie Rose). PG says determination is only observable in retrospective narratives -- "something went wrong, and instead of giving up, they persevered." (Conversations with Tyler, 2023)

The interview is also a "reverse negotiation" -- founders often walk out thinking their idea is better than they thought, because the partners actively expand the idea rather than poking holes.

And questions, not pitches:

> "We don't like it when people pitch us. We don't have time to be pitched. What we do is we ask them questions."
> -- EconTalk (2009)

**When to deploy:** When someone is designing an interview process for hiring or selection. The key insight is: don't interrogate people. Simulate the actual work. See what it's like to collaborate for 10 minutes.

#### Jessica Livingston as Social Radar [7/20]

PG openly admits he is bad at judging character and defers almost entirely to Jessica on people decisions. This is operational self-awareness, not false modesty.

> "I have bad judgment of character. I'm not good at judging people. Really, I'm kind of bad at it. I know I'm bad at it. But Jessica, she is very rarely wrong."
> -- Mixergy (~2010)

> "A lot of people don't realize... I think a lot of them think Jessica is some kind of secretary... What happens after the interview is they walk out. Basically, I'll turn to Jessica and say, 'Okay. Should we fund them?'"
> -- Mixergy (~2010)

The division of labor at YC was explicit. PG assessed technical depth -- "I can tell if people know what they're talking about when they come and talk about some technical idea." Jessica assessed character. During interviews, the three male cofounders peppered applicants with technical questions while Jessica mostly watched:

> "What does the Social Radar say?"
> -- *Jessica Livingston* (jessica.md)

PG's essay about Jessica makes the claim in its strongest form:

> "Much of what's most novel about YC is due to Jessica Livingston. If you don't understand her, you don't understand YC."
> -- *Jessica Livingston* (jessica.md)

> "She's one of those rare individuals with x-ray vision for character."
> -- *Jessica Livingston* (jessica.md)

The practical lesson is not "find a Jessica." It is: know what you're bad at evaluating, and recruit someone who covers that gap. PG's self-awareness about his own weakness is what made YC's selection system work.

**When to deploy:** When someone is making people decisions solo and is overconfident in their own judgment. When someone underestimates the role of character assessment in hiring or investment decisions. The person who says the least in the room may be learning the most.

#### Trust Asymmetry [3/20]

A subtle and important epistemological claim about the limits of character assessment. PG treats this as a structural vulnerability in any selection process.

> "Intelligent people can judge other intelligent people, but trustworthy people cannot judge other trustworthy people. In fact, trustworthy people are often fooled by untrustworthy people."
> -- Startup School 2018

The implication is directional: when a smart person tells you someone else is smart, you can trust that assessment. When a trustworthy person tells you someone else is trustworthy, you should verify independently. Honest people are especially vulnerable to dishonest people because they project their own good faith onto others.

This is why PG needed Jessica at YC -- not because he's naive, but because the skill of detecting untrustworthiness is a separate faculty from intelligence, and one he acknowledges he lacks.

**When to deploy:** When someone is relying on a referral to assess character. When building a selection process. The system must include a trustworthiness check that doesn't rely on the candidates' own presentation, because trustworthy evaluators are systematically overconfident about detecting dishonesty.

#### Side-Project Heuristic [4/20]

For young, inexperienced founders, side projects are the most reliable signal of authentic engagement. The project itself is less important than what it proves about the founder.

> "Part of the reason you want to find young founders who've done stuff from side projects is that it guarantees the idea is not bullshit."
> -- Conversations with Tyler (2023)

PG is careful to scope this heuristic. It applies primarily to young founders who don't have a track record:

> Older founders "can do things like start supersonic aircraft companies... and actually get it right."
> -- Conversations with Tyler (2023)

The underlying logic connects to "organic ideas over brainstormed ideas" -- a side project is proof that the founder's interest is real, not manufactured for a pitch. They were working on this before anyone was watching.

PG's archetype for this is Robert Morris, whose character he trusted precisely because Robert's life was a series of projects driven by pure curiosity -- reconnecting Harvard to the internet (getting kicked out in the process), writing the internet worm out of curiosity, editing operating system source code because he didn't like what it did. (startup-school-2018, social-radars)

**When to deploy:** When evaluating young founders with no professional track record. The question is not "what have you built?" but "what have you built when nobody was paying you to?"

#### Obsessive False-Negative Tracking [4/20]

YC treats missed winners as injuries to be studied, not mistakes to be forgotten. This is systematic error correction applied to talent selection.

> "YC has a list of all the companies that we've missed, that have applied to YC and we've turned down, and they've gone on to be successful. We spend a lot of time, like someone thinking about past injuries."
> -- Conversations with Tyler (2023)

The most vivid example is the spam company incident, where a single evaluator's blind spot buried a future public company (see Stories below). The fix was structural -- add a second reader to every application the first reader grades poorly.

YC's rejection emails embed the error-correction loop directly:

> "Tell us if you succeed so we can fix our process."
> -- TC Disrupt (Charlie Rose, 2011)

And PG holds this posture about YC's overall accuracy:

> "Oh, god, yes. Yes, we think of ourselves as just utterly terrible at picking startups."
> -- Mixergy (~2010)

This is not false modesty. It is the operational principle that keeps the system improving. The moment you think you're good at selecting people, you stop finding your blind spots.

**When to deploy:** When someone runs any selection process -- hiring, admissions, investing. The advice is: track your false negatives obsessively. When patterns emerge, fix the system, not the person who missed it.

#### Stories

**The Spam Company False Negative** [6/20]
> "There was this company doing email, if you wanted to send mass emails. This was back when we were reading the applications. If the first reader gave it a sufficiently low grade, it would never be seen by anybody else. The first reader was Robert Morris. He was designated wet blanket of YC. He gave this application a C, with the comment 'spam company.' Nobody ever saw it again. They ended up going public."
> -- Conversations with Tyler (2023)

After discovering this, PG changed the software immediately: "After that, every application had to be seen by at least two people." The lesson is not "Robert Morris was wrong" -- it is that a single gatekeeper is a single point of failure in any evaluation system. Build redundancy. Fix the process, not the person.
*Sources:* conversations-tyler, tc-disrupt

---

**Wufoo Nervousness** [3/20]
The Wufoo founders (Kevin Hale et al.) were "so nervous" during their YC interview that opinions were divided on whether to fund them.

> "After the interview, reactions were divided about whether to fund them. And I said, 'No, they're not stupid. They were just nervous.' It's true. That was it. They were just nervous."
> -- Mixergy (~2010)

PG already knew their prior work (Particletree) was good. The idea for Wufoo's form builder was partly crystallized during the 10-minute interview itself -- Kevin initially resisted the idea of being "in the form business." PG's active intervention was to reduce noise (nervousness) so signal (ability) could be detected. YC now deliberately tries to calm applicants before interviews for exactly this reason: "If people are nervous, all it does is add noise to the interview. We need all the signal strength we can get." (Mixergy)
*Source:* mixergy

**Five Founders Profiles** [4/20]
PG's essay "Five Founders" profiles the people he uses as reference points when advising startups: Steve Jobs (design as strategic weapon), TJ Rodgers (clarity of thought that changes how you think), Paul Buchheit (responsible for Gmail, AdSense, and "Don't be evil" -- versatility and judgment clustering in one person), Sam Altman (unstoppable force of will), and David Heinemeier Hansson (it's better to make a small number of users love you than a large number kind of like you).

> "There are a few people with such force of will that they're going to get whatever they want."
> -- *Five Founders* (5founders.md), about Sam Altman

> "On questions of design, I ask 'What would Steve do?' but on questions of strategy or ambition I ask 'What would Sama do?'"
> -- *Five Founders* (5founders.md)

The essay reveals PG's evaluation values through his choices. Each founder embodies a different dimension of what PG considers exceptional: taste (Jobs), intellectual depth (Rodgers), versatile judgment (Buchheit), raw determination (Altman), and product philosophy (DHH).
*Source:* founders-talent (5founders.md)

**"Kicked Out of School" Recruiting** [3/20]
At Viaweb, PG turned academic failure into a recruiting signal. The story starts with Robert Morris:

> "Harvard's Internet connection had died of bit decay -- which showed how unimportant the Internet was back in the mid-'80s. The Internet connection died and no one complained for a year. But then Robert, just out of curiosity, decided to resuscitate it and he didn't do any classwork, and he got bad grades, and got kicked out."
> -- Startup School Radio (2016)

PG and Trevor used this as a literal recruiting technique:

> They posted flyers at Harvard: "Did you get kicked out for working on some project of your own? We'd like to hire you." It yielded a programmer who made significant money from stock options.
> -- Startup School 2018

The insight: the trait that gets a hacker kicked out of school -- obsessive focus on projects over coursework -- is exactly the trait that makes them valuable at a startup. Conventional credentials are a poor proxy for the kind of intensity startups require.
*Sources:* startup-school-2018, startup-school-radio, social-radars

**Airbnb Cereal Boxes** [10/20]
The Airbnb founders ran out of money and made Obama and McCain themed breakfast cereal -- "I think they made like $30,000. They designed the box." PG has a box of Obama-Os on his shelf.

> "As soon as we heard that story, they were in, basically. There's often a point in the interview where we all kind of look at one another and decide, 'Okay. We're funding these guys.' At that point, the remainder of the interview, we're just chatting."
> -- Mixergy (~2010)

The cereal stunt is pure evidence of determination and resourcefulness. It doesn't matter that selling branded cereal has nothing to do with renting out air mattresses. What matters is that they refused to die.
*Source:* mixergy, founders-talent

---

**Sam Altman Overrides PG's Rejection** [3/20]
PG tried to talk 19-year-old Sam Altman out of joining YC's first batch:

> "I sent him an email saying, look, you know, what's the rush? Stay in college. You're only a sophomore."

Altman's response:

> "I'm a sophomore and I'm coming."

> "Sam, when I talked to him, he pushed back like he was 40. I couldn't treat him like a 19-year-old."
> -- TC Disrupt (Charlie Rose, 2011)

The teaching application is twofold: determination looks like ignoring reasonable-sounding advice from authority figures, and even the best evaluators (PG himself) can misjudge people. The founders who override bad advice are often the best founders.
*Source:* tc-disrupt
