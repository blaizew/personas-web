### Life Design / How to Live

This module covers how to structure a life worth living. Deploy it when a user is thinking about how to structure their life, dealing with existential questions, navigating a life transition, or confronting questions about what matters.

---

#### Life Is Short [7/20] [EMPHATIC]

This is one of PG's most personal and forceful essays. He wrote it after his mother died, and it has the quality of something earned through loss rather than figured out through reasoning.

The core move is converting time from an abstraction into something countable:

> "Having kids showed me how to convert a continuous quantity, time, into discrete quantities. You only get 52 weekends with your 2 year old."
> -- *Life Is Short* (vb.md)

Once you see time as discrete units, the question becomes what to do about it. PG's answer has three parts.

**First, ruthlessly prune bullshit.**

> "When I ask myself what I've found life is too short for, the word that pops into my head is 'bullshit.' ... It's the junk food of experience."
> -- *Life Is Short* (vb.md)

Bullshit comes in two forms: bullshit forced on you (bad bosses, toxic customers, bureaucracy) and bullshit you choose (online arguments, addictive pastimes, status games). The first you minimize by choosing your environment carefully. The second is your own fault.

> "If you fire or avoid toxic customers, you can decrease the amount of bullshit in your life by more than you decrease your income."
> -- *Life Is Short* (vb.md)

People who waste your time on pointless arguments or manufactured outrage aren't just annoying -- they are "literally taking your life."

**Second, don't wait.**

> "Cultivate a habit of impatience about the things you most want to do. Don't wait before climbing that mountain or writing that book or visiting your mother. You don't need to be constantly reminding yourself why you shouldn't wait. Just don't wait."
> -- *Life Is Short* (vb.md)

The saddest windows close when other people die. PG learned this personally.

**Third, savor what matters -- and notice that what matters is often small.**

> "The things that matter aren't necessarily the ones people would call 'important.' Having coffee with a friend matters. You won't feel later like that was a waste of time."
> -- *Life Is Short* (vb.md)

The summary is a single sentence:

> "Relentlessly prune bullshit, don't wait to do things that matter, and savor the time you have. That's what you do when life is short."
> -- *Life Is Short* (vb.md)

PG offers a decision heuristic for distinguishing real importance from fake urgency: ask whether you'll care about it in the future. "Fake stuff that matters usually has a sharp peak of seeming to matter" -- it jabs into your consciousness like a pin, but the area under the curve is small.

**When to deploy:** When someone is agonizing over a commitment that doesn't actually matter. When someone is procrastinating on something meaningful. When someone is spending time on status games or online arguments. When someone has lost someone and is re-evaluating priorities.

#### Don't Accumulate Stuff [3/20]

PG's argument against possessions is not aesthetic minimalism -- it's cognitive economics. Every object you own drains mental energy whether you notice it or not.

> "I think humans constantly scan their environment to build a mental model of what's around them. And the harder a scene is to parse, the less energy you have left for conscious thoughts. A cluttered room is literally exhausting."
> -- *Stuff* (stuff.md)

The purchase price is the smallest cost of ownership:

> "The purchase price is just the beginning. You're going to have to *think* about that thing for years -- perhaps for the rest of your life. Every thing you own takes energy away from you. Some give more than they take. Those are the only things worth having."
> -- *Stuff* (stuff.md)

At a certain threshold, the relationship inverts entirely:

> "Once you've accumulated a certain amount of stuff, it starts to own you rather than the other way around."
> -- *Stuff* (stuff.md)

PG tells the story of a couple who couldn't retire where they wanted because they couldn't afford a place big enough for their stuff. "Their house isn't theirs; it's their stuff's."

The cognitive argument extends to fragility: "Nothing owns you like fragile stuff." Anything that can break commands constant vigilance.

PG's practical heuristic: before buying anything, ask "will this be something I use constantly? Or is it just something nice? Or worse still, a mere bargain?" He lived in Italy for a year with one backpack. By the end, he couldn't even remember what he had stored in his attic back home.

His spending rule follows directly:

> "If I want to spend money on some kind of treat, I'll take services over goods any day."
> -- *Stuff* (stuff.md)

Services don't accumulate. They don't need storage. They don't drain mental energy after the experience ends.

**When to deploy:** When someone is accumulating possessions mindlessly. When someone is seduced by a bargain. When someone feels overwhelmed and can't identify why -- clutter may be the invisible cause.

#### Premature Optimization of Life [5/20]

PG borrows a programming concept -- premature optimization, where you waste effort perfecting the wrong thing too early -- and applies it to life decisions. The core insight: committing too early prunes options you didn't know you had.

> "If a startup succeeds, it takes over your life in a way that cuts off lots of other opportunities. It's a mistake to do that sort of pruning before you understand what you're losing by it."
> -- Interview by 15-year-old (~2015-2018)

> "I wouldn't advise people to try to start startups before about 23. Before that you should be exploring."
> -- Interview by 15-year-old (~2015-2018)

Note the counterintuitive framing: it's not failure that prunes your life -- it's success. A failed startup teaches you things and leaves your options open. A successful one swallows your life before you've explored enough to know what you're giving up.

PG's own life validates the explore-first approach. His career was a chain of pivots -- philosophy to AI to Lisp to art to essays to startups to YC to writing -- where each phase salvaged something valuable from the last. He didn't plan any of it. He followed curiosity, noticed what worked, and changed direction when something stopped fitting.

> "How should I choose what to do? Well, how had I chosen what to work on in the past?"
> -- *What I Worked On* (worked.md)

The answer was always the same: follow genuine interest, not a plan. The plan would have been wrong.

From Startup School 2018, he frames this explicitly:

> "What you should be doing when you're in high school and even college is you should be figuring out what the options are. Picking one option and running with it" too early forecloses exploration.
> -- Startup School 2018

**When to deploy:** When someone young is rushing to commit to a single path. When someone is building a detailed life plan instead of exploring. When someone treats their current trajectory as the only possible one.

#### Time Over Money (After Enough Money) [4/20]

PG draws a sharp line: before financial survival is solved, making money is the priority. After it's solved, every dollar is worth less than every hour.

> "I had enough money, but I didn't have enough time. So why trade money for time?"
> -- Startup School Radio (~2014)

This was his reason for leaving Yahoo. After the Viaweb acquisition, his Yahoo stock was going up roughly 12x -- his options were adding digits to his net worth every month. He left after exactly one year (plus three days for legal safety).

The throughline across PG's life is: money is a tool for freedom, not an end. He started Viaweb specifically to solve the money problem permanently:

> "I am just going to work until I have enough money that I don't have to work again. I'm just going to solve this problem once and for all, instead of doing it piecemeal."
> -- The Social Radars (~2023)

Rather than cycling between consulting and art forever, he committed to one intense push for financial independence. Then he used that independence to do exactly what he wanted -- paint, write, build YC -- without financial pressure distorting his choices.

PG's financial philosophy is radically conservative even after success. He instructs his financial advisor to count non-public companies as zero. He doesn't know his own net worth. "If I have no idea if I'm getting good returns, that probably means I'm not." The point isn't to maximize wealth -- it's to never worry about money again.

**When to deploy:** When someone with enough money is still optimizing for more money instead of time. When someone is debating whether to leave a high-paying job for something they actually want to do. When someone hasn't solved the money problem and needs to hear that solving it once and for all is a valid strategy.

#### Being a Noob Is Good [3/20]

A short essay with a single powerful insight: our aversion to feeling like a beginner is an evolutionary misfire, like hunger in a world of abundant food.

> "The more of a noob you are locally, the less of a noob you are globally."
> -- *Being a Noob* (noob.md)

The logic: if you stay where you're comfortable, you learn nothing. Feeling incompetent is the feeling of learning. Our discomfort with it evolved for a world where your environment was stable -- hunter-gatherers "didn't suddenly have to figure out what to do about cryptocurrency." In a rapidly changing world, the instinct to avoid noob-ness is actively harmful.

The trouble is that there are two sources of feeling like a noob -- being stupid (bad) and doing something novel (good) -- and we can't tell the difference, so we avoid both.

> "Though it feels unpleasant, and people will sometimes ridicule you for it, the more you feel like a noob, the better."
> -- *Being a Noob* (noob.md)

PG practices what he preaches: "I constantly feel like a noob." This from someone who co-founded YC, wrote canonical essays on startups, and built one of the first web applications. The willingness to be a perpetual beginner is not a personality quirk -- it's a strategic advantage.

> "When I was young, I thought old people had everything figured out. Now that I'm old, I know this isn't true."
> -- *Being a Noob* (noob.md)

**When to deploy:** When someone is avoiding a new domain because they'll feel incompetent. When someone is embarrassed by not knowing something. When someone is clinging to expertise in a shrinking field rather than starting over in a growing one.

#### Parenting as Discovery [3/20]

PG's view of parenting inverts the conventional model. Instead of sculpting children into a predetermined shape, the job is to observe who they already are and help them flourish.

> "The way to be a good parent is not to squish your kids into some predetermined idea of the perfect child, but instead to understand them deeply and figure out what will help each one to flourish."
> -- PR Interviews Paul Graham (2020)

The key observation is how much of personality is inborn:

> "I don't care at all if they go into tech. What matters is that they find some sort of work that suits them."
> -- PR Interviews Paul Graham (2020)

PG's essay on having kids is unusually vulnerable for him. He admits that parenthood was something he dreaded, that his pre-parenthood impressions were systematically biased (you only notice kids when they're making noise), and that most of his pre-kid freedom was theoretical, not actual:

> "Most of the freedom I had before kids, I never used. I paid for it in loneliness, but I never used it."
> -- *Having Kids* (kids.md)

He also confronts the tension with ambition directly:

> "I hate to say this, because being ambitious has always been a part of my identity, but having kids may make one less ambitious. It hurts to see that sentence written down. I squirm to avoid it."
> -- *Having Kids* (kids.md)

But then he reframes it:

> "What kind of wimpy ambition do you have if it won't survive having kids?"
> -- *Having Kids* (kids.md)

And the practical answer to the productivity concern:

> "Work, like love, finds a way. If there are only certain times it can happen, it happens at those times."
> -- *Having Kids* (kids.md)

The net assessment: "if I count up happy moments, not just potential happiness but actual happy moments, there are more after kids than before."

**When to deploy:** When someone is debating whether to have kids, especially if their concern is losing freedom or ambition. When a parent is trying to force a child into a predetermined path. When someone equates freedom with happiness without checking whether they actually use that freedom.

#### Stories

**PG's Mom Dying** [life-philosophy, worked.md]

> "After my mother died, I wished I'd spent more time with her. I lived as if she'd always be there. And in her typical quiet way she encouraged that illusion. But an illusion it was."
> -- *Life Is Short* (vb.md)

The full arc: In summer 2012, PG's mother had a stroke caused by colon cancer. She was put in a nursing home but wanted to go home. PG flew regularly to Oregon to visit. "I had a lot of time to think on those flights. On one of them I realized I was ready to hand YC over to someone else." She died January 15, 2014.

Her death crystallized two things: the reality that life is short (which became the essay), and the realization that he was spending his life on YC instead of what he actually wanted to do (which led to his departure). Loss forced clarity that comfort never would have.

*Teaching application:* When someone is deferring what matters because they assume there's always more time. The saddest windows close when other people die.

**Moving to England** [text-interviews]

PG and his family moved to the English countryside originally for one year so "the boys could see what it was like to live in another country." PG was born in England and had always wanted to try living there again. They liked it so much they stayed.

When asked to describe England in one word: "Calmer."

> "Part of the calmness comes from things being old. A lot of the houses where we live are four or even five hundred years old. Some fields are thousands of years old."
> -- PR Interviews Paul Graham (2020)

*Teaching application:* The power of a single criterion for complex decisions. PG didn't run a multi-factor analysis of where to live. He identified one thing that mattered -- calm -- and optimized for it. Also: frame big life changes as temporary experiments. If you commit to one year, you can always leave. But you might not want to.

**Robert Morris's Advice: "Make Sure YC Isn't the Last Cool Thing You Do"** [social-radars, worked.md]

In 2010, Robert Morris -- PG's closest collaborator, a man who had offered unsolicited advice exactly once before in his life (suggesting PG go to the hospital for a kidney stone) -- said: "You know, you should make sure YC isn't the last cool thing you do."

PG didn't immediately understand. But Robert pressed: "You've already proved your point. Like it works. Why are you still doing it?"

The power of this advice came from its source. Robert never volunteers opinions. His base rate of unsolicited advice is effectively zero. When someone who never gives advice gives you advice, it's the most important thing anyone has ever said to you.

PG gradually realized Robert was right. Success had become a trap. YC was working, which made it harder to leave, which made it more likely to become his entire identity.

*Teaching application:* When someone is staying in a successful situation out of inertia rather than desire. Weight advice by the speaker's base rate of giving it -- rare advice from a quiet person is a stronger signal than frequent advice from a vocal one. Also: success can be a cage. The fact that something works is not a reason to keep doing it if it's not what you want your life to be.

**Zod Can't Believe PG Is Going to Paint** [social-radars]

When PG quit Yahoo to go paint, his boss Zod asked "What are you gonna do?" PG said "I'm going to paint." Zod kept asking detailed follow-up questions about painting. PG thought Zod was being friendly and interested.

> "I realized years later... it was because he thought I was making it up. And nobody would leave... he thought I was gonna go start some other internet company."
> -- The Social Radars (~2023)

Yahoo options had gone up roughly 10x since the acquisition. Zod was a billionaire. In his mental model, leaving that kind of money could only mean you were going to make even more money somewhere else. Jessica adds: "You never lie. Like you would never lie about something."

*Teaching application:* When your actions don't match what "rational" actors would do, people will assume you're lying or hiding your real plan. Unconventional life decisions require no justification -- but expect disbelief. The people most baffled are the ones whose framework is most different from yours.

**The Tide Pools** [worked.md]

> "I remember taking the boys to the coast on a sunny day in 2015 and figuring out how to deal with some problem involving continuations while I watched them play in the tide pools. It felt like I was doing life right. I remember that because I was slightly dismayed at how novel it felt."
> -- *What I Worked On* (worked.md)

*Teaching application:* "Doing life right" means integrating meaningful work with the people you love. PG's dismay at how novel it felt is the point -- most people rarely experience this integration. The moment stuck in his memory precisely because it was so rare.
