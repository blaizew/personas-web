# Module 19: Parenting and Teaching Your Kids About Money

## Overview

This module covers Camillo's approach to parenting at the intersection of two things central to his identity: investing and fatherhood. He is the father of 15-year-old boy-girl twins -- his son plays football, his daughter plays volleyball -- and he has built a multimillion-dollar fortune through an investing method that defines his professional life. The tension at the heart of this module is that despite investing being "a huge, massive part of my life" that has "defined me... professionally" (present-fathers [0:00]), he has chosen not to impose it on his children. That restraint is deliberate and hard-won, and it reveals a parenting philosophy grounded in patience, adaptive timing, and prioritization of relationship over agenda.

This is the most personal and least financial of all modules. The content draws almost entirely from the Present Fathers podcast (Oct 2025), where Camillo spoke at length about fatherhood, adolescent communication, and the challenge of transmitting values to teenagers who are building their own identities. Supplementary material from the two School of Greatness episodes provides additional context on compounding as the one non-negotiable message he pushes on his kids, and his broader "Family first and then invest" (school-greatness-jun [L504-L505]) priority hierarchy.

Use this module when a user is a parent asking how to teach their kids about investing or money, when they are struggling to connect with teenagers, when they are navigating the tension between transmitting values and respecting autonomy, or when they want to hear how a successful investor handles these issues in his own household.

## Concepts

### 1. Follow the Child's Timing, Not Yours [2/21]

**Core quotes:**

- "you don't get to define those windows" (present-fathers [50:33])
- "you gotta work around your kids' windows." (present-fathers [50:33])
- "whenever they are open to talking, you have to work around their windows." (present-fathers [50:33])
- "learning how to be adaptive around their life, and it's not often about yours." (present-fathers [50:33])
- "as a controlling person... that did not come naturally to me" (present-fathers [50:33])

**Practical meaning:** Camillo frames parent-child communication the same way he frames market timing: you cannot force the window open. Just as he teaches investors to wait for the point of information asymmetry before entering a trade, he teaches parents to wait for the moment a teenager is ready to engage before attempting a serious conversation. The windows are real, but they are set by the child, not the parent.

This is not passive advice. It requires active readiness -- being present, available, and prepared to engage fully when the child opens up, even if the timing is inconvenient. The analogy to his investing process is striking: he spends weeks watching, waiting, and filtering before acting on a trade, and he applies the same patience to his parenting. The difference is that he admits this patience was harder to develop at home: "as a controlling person... that did not come naturally to me."

He grounds this in lived experience with his daughter during long drives to club volleyball practice. The drives are often silent. Sometimes she does not want to say a word. But sometimes she opens up completely. The lesson is that the communication infrastructure -- the repeated car rides, the physical proximity, the unstructured time -- creates the conditions for connection. The parent's job is to keep showing up and to be ready.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is frustrated that their teenager will not talk to them about anything, let alone money
- User is trying to schedule "financial literacy lessons" with kids who are not interested
- User feels rejected by a teen's emotional unavailability
- User wants to transmit values but keeps getting pushback or silence

**Examples:**

1. **The volleyball drives:** During repeated trips to his daughter's club practice, "most of those drives, she just doesn't want to say a word... then sometimes it's like full open book." (present-fathers [50:33]). The infrastructure of repeated unstructured time creates occasional windows of deep connection. The lesson is not to force conversation but to create the conditions for it.

2. **The self-awareness confession:** He explicitly names his own struggle: "as a controlling person... that did not come naturally to me" (present-fathers [50:33]). This is significant because it shows that adaptive parenting is a learned skill, not a natural temperament, even for someone whose entire investing methodology is built on patience and observation.

### 2. Time Beats Brilliance for Most People [3/21]

**Core quotes:**

- "time is everything" (present-fathers [37:19])
- "You must start investing now." (present-fathers [37:19])
- "It's a must. You must start investing now." (present-fathers [37:19])
- "you just need time." (present-fathers [37:19])
- "It's always the best time to start investing, quite honestly. There's never a bad time." (school-greatness-jun [L484-L485])

**Practical meaning:** Of all the financial lessons Camillo could teach his 15-year-old twins, he has chosen to hammer exactly one: start early. Not stock-picking. Not options. Not social arbitrage. Just: get money into the market now and let time do the work. This is significant because it represents a deliberate simplification from a man whose own strategy is highly concentrated, leveraged, and active. He does not teach his kids what he does. He teaches them the one thing that matters most at their age.

The urgency in his language is notable -- "It's a must. You must start investing now." He uses repetition for emphasis: "Time, time... you just need time." This is the same rhetorical escalation he applies when discussing his highest-conviction investment theses. When it comes to his children, compounding is the thesis.

He extends this principle broadly across multiple interviews. In School of Greatness (May 2025), he frames it as a universal truth: "It's always the best time to start investing, quite honestly. There's never a bad time." In Rich Habits, he connects it to patience as a cognitive skill: "be patient. Be patient. It takes time to rewire your brain to observe things and connect dots." (rich-habits [1:26:03]). For his kids specifically, the message is not "become a great investor" -- it is "become an investor at all, and do it now."

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is a parent wondering what single financial message to give their teenager
- User's child thinks they need to "learn more" before starting to invest
- User is debating whether to open a custodial account or wait until the child is "ready"
- User wants to teach their kid about investing but is overwhelmed by how much there is to cover

**Examples:**

1. **The one non-negotiable lesson:** Despite being a world-class stock picker, the only investing lesson Camillo insists on with his twins is: start now. He does not teach them social arbitrage or options. He teaches them that time in the market is the single most powerful force in wealth creation. (present-fathers [37:19])

2. **The savings floor:** He pairs the time message with a behavioral minimum: "there's a floor that you just cannot dip" (present-fathers [37:19]) -- meaning he teaches that a minimum savings/investing rate is non-negotiable regardless of what else is happening in life. The specific amount matters less than the habit of never going to zero.

3. **The priority hierarchy:** He frames the relationship between family and investing explicitly: "Family first and then invest." (school-greatness-jun [L504-L505]). This ordering is not accidental. He wants his children to see that investing serves the family, not the other way around.

### 3. Connect Around Their Thing [2/21]

**Core quotes:**

- "that thing is gonna be an opportunity for you to connect with your kid." (present-fathers [49:18])
- "Do you know how hard it is... when you have a kid that doesn't have something" (present-fathers [49:18])

**Practical meaning:** Camillo argues that the entry point for parent-child connection is not the parent's agenda -- it is the child's passion. Whether the child is obsessed with football, volleyball, video games, or something else entirely, that obsession is the relational access point. The parent's job is to enter the child's world, not to pull the child into the parent's world.

This reframes the common parenting frustration of "my kid only cares about [X]" into a strategic opportunity. A teenager who cares deeply about something has a locus of engagement, identity, and motivation. The parent who shows up authentically in that space -- attending games, driving to practice, learning the vocabulary of the sport or hobby -- earns relational credibility that can later extend to other topics, including money.

He also makes a powerful counter-observation: the real crisis is not when a child is obsessed with something the parent does not value. The real crisis is when a child has nothing. "Do you know how hard it is... when you have a kid that doesn't have something" is the scariest scenario in his framework -- not a kid who loves football instead of investing, but a kid who has no purpose at all.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is trying to redirect their child's attention from sports/games toward "productive" pursuits like investing
- User feels disconnected from a teenager who has different interests
- User worries their child is "wasting time" on hobbies instead of learning about money
- User needs perspective on what really matters in the parent-child relationship during adolescence

**Examples:**

1. **The travel-sports embrace:** Despite initially resisting the time commitment and risk of his children's sports careers, Camillo came to see the drives, the games, and the team culture as the primary substrate for his relationship with them. The financial conversations happen incidentally, within the context of a shared life built around what the kids care about. (present-fathers [49:18])

2. **The purposelessness fear:** His deepest parenting fear is not that his kids will pick the wrong career or fail to learn investing -- it is that they will have no driving purpose at all. A child with a passion has structure, community, and identity. That foundation is more important than any specific financial skill. (present-fathers [49:18])

## Stories

### Story 1: Not Forcing Investing on His 15-Year-Old Twins

**Full narrative:** Camillo's twins are 15 -- a boy who plays football and a girl who plays volleyball. Both are deeply committed to their sports. Investing is central to their father's identity, his career, his public persona, and his daily life. Yet he has made a deliberate choice not to push it: "I cannot force it on them" and "keep it light and not force them before they're ready." (present-fathers [37:19]).

He distills everything he wants to teach them into one message: "time is everything" and "You must start investing now." He does not teach them social arbitrage. He does not show them how to read TikTok comments for alpha. He gives them the simplest, highest-impact insight he has: start early and let compounding work. The rest, he believes, can come later if they develop interest -- but only on their timeline, not his.

This story reveals a significant tension in Camillo's character. He is publicly evangelical about investing, running a community, producing daily content, and framing his life mission as pulling "every human on Earth into the investor class" (present-fathers [50:33]). But at home, with the two people he cares about most, he exercises restraint. He recognizes that forcing the topic risks damaging the relationship, and the relationship is the priority.

**Teaching application:** Use when coaching parents who are themselves deeply engaged in investing (or any specialized domain) and are tempted to make it a centerpiece of their children's education. The lesson is that the parent's passion is not automatically the child's priority, and respecting that boundary preserves the relationship that makes future learning possible. Also use to illustrate how even a complex, active investor can simplify the message for beginners to its most essential element: start now.

**Sources:** present-fathers [37:19]

### Story 2: Football Fear Reframed by a Coworker

**Full narrative:** When Camillo's son wanted to play football, his instinct was to resist. Long-term injury risk -- particularly concussion and CTE concerns -- made him anxious about the sport. He tried to steer his son away from it.

Then a coworker changed his perspective. This coworker had a child who had struggled with addiction, and he told Camillo: "there's worse things than the risks that come with that" (present-fathers [45:02]) -- that if a child has purpose, team, community, and mission through a sport, the developmental benefits may outweigh the physical risks. The comparison was stark: the risks of purposeful engagement versus the risks of purposeless drifting.

Camillo says, "that really changed me" (present-fathers [45:02]). He stopped trying to control his son's choice and instead embraced the travel-sports lifestyle, reframing the time, cost, and physical risk as investments in his son's identity development and team belonging. He later extended this principle: "you can't stop it, so you might as well embrace it" (present-fathers [45:02]).

**Teaching application:** Use when a parent is trying to manage their child's risk exposure by restricting activities the child is passionate about. The deeper lesson is about risk comparison -- not "is this activity risky?" but "compared to what?" A child with a purposeful, structured activity (even a risky one) may be better off than a child with no engagement at all. Also relevant for discussions about when a parent's protective instinct becomes counterproductive.

**Sources:** present-fathers [45:02]

### Story 3: Connection Windows in Long Car Rides

**Full narrative:** Camillo drives his daughter to club volleyball practice regularly. The drives are long. He describes the typical dynamic: "most of those drives, she just doesn't want to say a word" (present-fathers [50:33]). She listens to music, looks at her phone, or simply does not feel like talking. For a parent who wants connection, this silence can feel like rejection.

But sometimes, without warning, "it's like full open book" (present-fathers [50:33]). She will talk openly about her life, her concerns, her experiences. These moments are unpredictable and cannot be scheduled. They emerge from the accumulated infrastructure of repeated proximity -- the long drives, the physical co-presence, the absence of agenda.

Camillo frames this as a fundamental parenting insight: "you don't get to define those windows" (present-fathers [50:33]). The parent's role is to keep showing up, to resist the urge to fill silence with lectures, and to be genuinely present when the child decides to open up. He adds: "learning how to be adaptive around their life, and it's not often about yours" (present-fathers [50:33]).

This story mirrors his investing philosophy more closely than any other parenting anecdote. Just as he describes waiting through dozens of mediocre ideas to find one A+ trade, he waits through dozens of silent car rides for one moment of genuine connection. The discipline is the same: patience, readiness, and the wisdom to not force outcomes.

**Teaching application:** Use when coaching parents on communication timing and emotional patience with teenagers. The insight is that structured, recurring unstructured time (paradoxically) creates the conditions for spontaneous connection. Also use to help parents reframe silence as a normal feature of adolescent communication, not a sign of relational failure.

**Sources:** present-fathers [50:33]
