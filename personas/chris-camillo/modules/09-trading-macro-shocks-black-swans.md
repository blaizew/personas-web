# Module 9: Trading Around Macro Shocks and Black Swans

## Overview

This module covers how Camillo trades during crises -- pandemics, financial crashes, natural disasters, and geopolitical shocks. He is not a macro investor. He does not forecast recessions or model interest-rate trajectories. But when the world breaks in a visible, fast-moving way, his social-arb method becomes a crisis-trading engine: observe the shock, trace the downstream beneficiaries and casualties, and position before Wall Street's slower machinery catches up.

Three capabilities underpin his crisis trading. First, second-order thinking: not just "who loses?" but "who specifically benefits from this shock?" Second, a deep belief that volatility is the money-making phase, not a reason to retreat. Third, pre-accepting severe drawdowns mentally before they happen, so panic never forces a bad exit.

This module is for coaching situations where a user is facing a crisis -- a market crash, a pandemic, a natural disaster, a geopolitical shock -- and wants to know how to act. They may be paralyzed by fear, tempted to sell everything, or looking for the opportunity hidden inside the chaos.

## Concepts

### 1. Second-Order / Downstream Beneficiary Thinking [8/21]

**Core quotes:**

- "why is Amazon down? Why is Peloton down?... they should be doubling." (alphamind [22:37])
- "Who makes pool equipment? I'm investing in them." (school-greatness-jun [L124])
- "Bud Light sales are down 67%... Coors Light and Miller Lite sales are up 30%." (whales [44:02])
- "Observing the world, seeing a change, connecting the dots to the companies and sectors that are going to benefit... or be harmed." (school-greatness-may [13:49])
- "This is my entire life, seeing something and then being like, 'Who would benefit or not?'" (school-greatness-jun [L44-L45])
- "you can invest in the companies that make the robots, but you could also think about how it's going to change economies and which companies are most likely to benefit." (rich-habits [1:02:17])

**Practical meaning:** When a macro shock hits, Camillo's first move is not to trade the shock itself -- it is to trace the causal chain to its downstream effects on specific companies. COVID hits and the economy freezes -- but people still need to exercise, shop, and entertain themselves. So Peloton, Shopify, Amazon, bicycles, boats, and campers all surge. Texas freezes and every pool in the state breaks -- so pool equipment manufacturers face a six-month order backlog. Bud Light faces a consumer boycott -- so Coors Light and Miller Lite absorb the lost volume with minimal incremental cost, creating outsized margin expansion.

The key insight is that shocks are not uniformly negative. They redistribute demand, attention, and spending in traceable patterns. The investor who asks "who specifically benefits?" before Wall Street finishes writing its macro commentary is the one who captures the trade.

This is not speculation about distant outcomes. It is present-tense observation of behavior changes that are already happening but not yet reflected in stock prices. As he puts it: "90% of what I do is not trying to predict the future. It's trying to decipher the present." (whales [14:33])

**Coaching triggers:**
- User sees a crisis and thinks only in terms of losses, not redistribution
- User is looking at a macro shock and asking "what do I do?" instead of "who benefits?"
- User has identified a crisis but does not know how to connect it to specific investable companies
- User is fixated on the headline losers and missing the second-order winners

**Examples:**

1. **COVID behavior-change basket:** When the pandemic hit, he did not just short the market -- he traced the behavioral shifts. People stuck at home started buying bikes, boats, campers, Pelotons, and shopping on Amazon and Shopify. Wall Street was slow to price these shifts: "I basically had two buckets of investments... [then] made the switch... exactly 2 days after the market hit the bottom." (whales [24:53]) He rotated into "ultra-levered long" positions in these beneficiaries. (whales [24:53])

2. **Texas Freeze to pool equipment:** Living in Texas during the historic freeze gave him immediate on-the-ground data: "All the pipes broke... All the swimming pool equipment broke." (school-greatness-jun [L116-L123]) He checked local constraints -- replacements were "Six months" out because inventory was sold out -- and traced the value chain to manufacturers: "Who makes pool equipment? I'm investing in them." (school-greatness-jun [L124]) Then he ran his standard information-asymmetry check: had Wall Street noticed yet?

3. **Bud Light boycott to Molson Coors:** During the Bud Light consumer backlash, he used data from his own Dallas restaurant: "Bud Light sales are down 67%... Coors Light and Miller Lite sales are up 30%." (whales [44:02]) He then converted volume shifts into financial implications via fixed-cost leverage: "margins are excessive" on the incremental demand flowing to Molson Coors. (whales [44:28])

### 2. Volatility Is the Money-Making Phase [6/21]

**Core quotes:**

- "When it gets scary and it starts to fluctuate, this is when you make all your money." (school-greatness-may [0:40])
- "That's all noise. It's always all noise." (school-greatness-may [2:02])
- "You should be investing always. I would say especially now." (school-greatness-may [2:02])
- "I do have absolute confidence. That the market will generally always have and likely always will recover from most situations." (rich-habits [45:25])
- "The stock market is nothing more than a reflection of all of our work globally." (school-greatness-may [3:33])
- "when something big happens, forget everything else, zone in on it." (farzad [54:26])

**Practical meaning:** Most investors treat volatility as a threat signal -- a reason to reduce exposure, flee to cash, and wait for calm. Camillo treats it as the opposite: volatility is when the biggest mispricings occur, because fear drives prices below rational levels and creates the widest information gaps.

His conviction that markets recover is anchored not in blind optimism but in a structural belief: "The stock market is nothing more than a reflection of all of our work globally." (school-greatness-may [3:33]) As long as humans keep producing, innovating, and working, the aggregate market trends upward. Crashes are temporary dislocations within that long-term trajectory.

But this is not a passive "stay the course" message. When volatility spikes, Camillo goes active. He focuses his entire attention on the crisis: "when something big happens, forget everything else, zone in on it." (farzad [54:26]) He looks for the specific second-order opportunities the shock creates -- and he sizes aggressively into them. The scariest moments produce the widest edges.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is panicking during a market selloff and wants to sell everything
- User asks "should I wait until things calm down?" before investing
- User treats all volatility as a sign that something is fundamentally broken
- User is frozen by macro headlines and not asking "who benefits?"
- User has conviction in a thesis but is letting short-term price movement shake them out

**Examples:**

1. **COVID year, $4.5M to $35M:** By first shorting, then rotating into pandemic beneficiaries during maximum fear, he generated outsized returns: "during the year of the pandemic, I turned a $4.5 million brokerage account into $35 million." (school-greatness-may [43:27]) The edge was not macro prediction -- it was speed in reading behavior change while others were paralyzed.

2. **Tariff-period drawdowns:** During recent tariff volatility: "my account... gets destroyed" and "I lost millions of dollars over the course of a couple weeks." (rich-habits [45:25]) His response: "I don't sell either." (rich-habits [45:25]) Conviction in the underlying theses held, and he stayed positioned.

### 3. Drawdown Tolerance / Pre-Acceptance [5/21]

**Core quotes:**

- "Every time you check your account, reduce it by 70%." (school-greatness-may [17:43])
- "Can I live with it being 50 to 70% lower?" (rich-habits [45:25])
- "Any money that you have invested in risk assets... reduce it by 70%." (school-greatness-may [17:43])
- "You think your account's $100 account and it's a $30 account." (school-greatness-may [18:00])
- "Bucketing money is the most underappreciated thing in all of investing." (present-fathers [4:20])
- "separate what I call risk money from the money that pays for your kids' college education" (opto [49:47])

**Practical meaning:** Camillo's drawdown discipline is not about ignoring losses -- it is about pre-accepting them so that emotional panic never drives decisions. Before allocating any money to risk assets, he runs a mental stress test: "Can I live with it being 50 to 70% lower?" (rich-habits [45:25]). If the answer is no, the allocation is too high.

The "reduce it by 70%" rule is a cognitive tool. When he checks his portfolio, he mentally discounts every dollar in risk assets to 30 cents. If that number is still acceptable -- if he can still pay his bills, fund his kids' education, and maintain his lifestyle with the discounted figure -- then he is properly sized. If seeing the haircut number causes panic, the position is too large.

This connects directly to his risk-bucketing philosophy. The reason he can tolerate 50-70% drawdowns in his risk capital is that it is structurally separated from life capital. His core savings, retirement, and family needs are in the "safe" bucket. The risk bucket is funded from intentional trade-offs, side income, and compounded gains -- money he has psychologically released. As he describes it: "Bucketing money is the most underappreciated thing in all of investing." (present-fathers [4:20])

The combination is what enables crisis trading. You cannot buy into maximum fear if seeing your portfolio drop 40% makes you vomit. Pre-acceptance removes the emotional veto that prevents most investors from acting during the highest-edge moments.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User has never stress-tested their portfolio allocation and panics at first 20% drawdown
- User is asking how much to put in risk assets
- User conflates portfolio size with spending power and cannot tolerate paper losses
- User has experienced a crash and is retrospectively questioning their allocation
- User wants to take bigger positions but has not separated risk capital from life capital

**Examples:**

1. **The 70% mental haircut rule:** Before any risk allocation, he applies the haircut: "Every time you check your account, reduce it by 70%." (school-greatness-may [17:43]) If the portfolio shows $100K in risk assets, he mentally sees $30K. If $30K feels survivable, the allocation is right. If it feels devastating, he is overexposed.

2. **Dot-com drawdown survival:** He recounts young investors during the dot-com crash who "lost like 70%... 70, 80%" and some holdings "went to zero." (school-greatness-may [3:33]) Yet "it didn't take long for the market to come whipping back." He extends this to 2008 and says that now "nothing fazes me anymore." The lesson is that survival is the prerequisite for recovery, and pre-acceptance of severe drawdowns is what keeps you in the game through the worst moments.

3. **Tariff-era millions lost, no selling:** During recent market disruption, he "lost millions of dollars over the course of a couple weeks" (rich-habits [45:25]), but he does not sell. The drawdown was within his pre-accepted range because only risk-bucketed capital was exposed. The thesis had not changed, so the price change was noise.

## Stories

### Story 1: COVID Short-Then-Long Rotation ($4.5M to $35M)

**Full narrative:** In February 2020, Camillo identified COVID severity before Western consensus by translating Chinese medical reports and blogs: "I was translating Chinese newspapers at night into English." (alphamind [22:37]) He saw "it was coming like a freight train" (whales [19:07]) while American media dismissed the risk. He went short: "I heavily and aggressively shorted the market... and I was about 2 weeks early." (alphamind [22:37]) He was "losing money initially as media dismissed the risk" and "repeatedly bought puts too early, and lost 30-40% before market break." (my-first-million [48:59])

He framed the disbelief lag through his Black Swan mental model: "our minds will not allow us to really accept... first-time events... Black Swan." (whales [22:09]) He had been preparing for exactly this moment: "since I read that book, I have literally been preparing my mind every single day for the next big black swan." (alphamind [22:37])

When the crash came, he captured the short leg -- but the bigger trade was the rotation. Rather than holding short indefinitely, he switched aggressively: "made the switch... exactly 2 days after the market hit the bottom" (whales [24:53]) into "ultra-levered long" positions (whales [24:53]) in pandemic beneficiaries. He tracked the behavior shifts in real time: people buying bicycles, boats, campers, ATVs, exercising at home on Pelotons, shopping on Amazon and Shopify. "Wall Street was really slow." (school-greatness-may [43:27])

The Peloton trade was his largest single position. He verified the thesis through repeated operational checks: "I was calling them every day asking them, are you sure there's not supply shortage?" (alphamind [22:37]). His key insight was that a known bear thesis had inverted: Peloton "had stopped advertising" (opto [33:31]) -- the biggest criticism had been unsustainable customer acquisition cost -- yet demand was surging. He placed a "7-figure options trade" (alphamind [22:37]) and made "close to $4 million on just this year alone" on Peloton. (opto [33:31])

Total result: "during the year of the pandemic, I turned a $4.5 million brokerage account into $35 million." (school-greatness-may [43:27]) In another telling: "that year I made $30 million" with "370% annualized return total portfolio." (my-first-million [52:56])

**Teaching application:** Use this story when coaching someone through a regime-change event. The lessons are: (1) prepare your mind for Black Swans before they arrive -- "everything I do is about having a prepared mind" (alphamind [22:37]); (2) be willing to take timing pain before the thesis confirms; (3) the biggest money often comes from the second leg (rotation into beneficiaries), not the obvious first move (shorting); (4) trace behavior changes to specific companies and size aggressively when information asymmetry is wide.

**Sources:** alphamind, whales, farzad, my-first-million, opto, school-greatness-may

### Story 2: Texas Freeze to Pool Equipment Trade

**Full narrative:** During the historic Texas winter storm, Camillo had immediate on-the-ground data because he lived there: "I'm in Texas. I live in Texas." (school-greatness-jun [L122]). He experienced the damage firsthand: "I had a pool. Pool equipment's gone." (school-greatness-jun [L122]). He saw the scale of the problem: "All the pipes broke... All the swimming pool equipment broke." (school-greatness-jun [L116-L123]) He checked with suppliers and learned that replacements were "Six months" out because inventory was sold out nationally. (school-greatness-jun [L123])

Then he applied his standard second-order mapping: "Who makes pool equipment? I'm investing in them." (school-greatness-jun [L124]) Before entering, he ran his information-asymmetry audit: "has the rest of the world caught onto this yet? Has Wall Street?" (school-greatness-jun [L124-L125]) The answer was no -- national media was covering the humanitarian crisis, not the pool-equipment supply chain. He positioned in pool equipment manufacturers before the demand surge showed up in earnings.

In the confessions source, he frames this more broadly: "everyone's pool is busted... the pipes all froze" so replacement demand could lift pool-equipment companies. (confessions [26:57]) He calls it "totally opportunistic" (confessions [26:57]) -- not predicting that Texas would freeze, but rapidly tracing the downstream effects once it did.

**Teaching application:** Use this story when teaching local-information edge during natural disasters or regional events. The lessons are: (1) people on the ground see reality before analysts do; (2) the investable opportunity is often not the obvious victim but the downstream beneficiary (not the homeowner or insurance company, but the equipment manufacturer); (3) supply-chain timelines (six months to replacement) create extended demand windows; (4) always audit whether the market has already noticed.

**Sources:** confessions, school-greatness-jun

### Story 3: Dot-Com Drawdown and Staying In

**Full narrative:** Camillo recounts the experience of living through the dot-com crash as a young investor. People he knew "lost like 70%... 70, 80%" and some holdings "went to zero." (school-greatness-may [3:33]) The emotional devastation was real. But the lesson came from what followed: "it didn't take long for the market to come whipping back." (school-greatness-may [3:33])

He extends this pattern to 2008 and subsequent crashes, building it into an operating principle: "I do have absolute confidence. That the market will generally always have and likely always will recover from most situations." (rich-habits [45:25]) He anchors this confidence in a structural belief about what the market represents: "The stock market is nothing more than a reflection of all of our work globally." (school-greatness-may [3:33]) As long as human productivity grows, the market follows over time.

The result is a battle-hardened emotional baseline: "nothing fazes me anymore." (school-greatness-may [3:33]) Each crash survived builds the psychological capital to stay invested -- or better, to buy aggressively -- during the next one.

**Teaching application:** Use this story when someone is paralyzed by crash memories or currently experiencing their first major drawdown. The lessons are: (1) severe drawdowns are historically normal and recoveries have followed every time; (2) the survivors who stayed in captured the recovery; (3) emotional resilience is built through experience, not through reading about it; (4) the structural case for long-term market recovery is not wishful thinking but a productivity argument.

**Sources:** school-greatness-may, rich-habits
