# Module 18: Building Your Investing Process and Lifestyle

## Overview

This module covers how Camillo's investing method actually fits into a human life. The preceding modules describe what to look for, when to act, and how to size positions. This one answers the practical question: what does the daily and weekly rhythm look like, and who is this method actually for?

The answer is counterintuitive. Despite managing what has grown into tens of millions of dollars, Camillo does not run a trading desk. He makes 2-12 trades per year. His process is a part-time observation practice -- 3-4 hours of nightly social media reading, filtered ruthlessly through a funnel that converts 40-50 observations into one actionable trade -- punctuated by concentrated bursts of decisive action. He does not watch CNBC. He does not read financial newspapers. He does not check stock prices daily. He treats attention as a finite resource and manages it through deliberate behavioral design, including removing apps from his phone to reclaim strategy bandwidth.

The second core message is accessibility. Camillo is emphatic -- across 7 of 21 sources -- that this method is broadly learnable. He lists mailmen, dentists, homemakers, teachers, plumbers, UPS drivers, and delivery drivers as people who can and do practice it. He graduated in the bottom 25% of his high school class. There is no credentialing requirement, no financial background prerequisite, and no minimum capital threshold. The only requirements are curiosity, discipline, and willingness to retrain your pattern-recognition instincts over time.

This module is for coaching situations where a user wants to know how to fit investing into their actual life. They have a day job, a family, limited time. They are asking about daily routines, execution cadence, and whether this is realistic for someone without a finance background.

## Concepts

### 1. Know Your Execution Style [6/21]

**Core quotes:**

- "It's not like I'm trading daily." (moneymasters [12:59])
- "Anywhere between 2 and 12 medium to high conviction trades on an annual basis." (moneymasters [12:59])
- "I'll make between 4 and 8 high conviction trades a year and I don't care what the stock market does." (trends-friends [46:55])
- "I spend on average 3 to 4 hours a night... reading... TikTok comments." (my-first-million [19:14])
- "you will only find one great Social Arb investment for every 40 or 50 things that you discover." (rich-habits [12:10])
- "I've averaged... about 68% annualized returns over the past 17 years." (trends-friends [8:39])

**Practical meaning:** Camillo's investing cadence is the opposite of what most people imagine when they hear "$20,000 to $80 million." He is not day-trading. He is not running screens at dawn. He operates more like a scout than a trader -- scanning constantly, acting rarely.

The daily rhythm is clear: he spends 3-4 hours each evening reading TikTok comments, scrolling social platforms, and absorbing conversational data. This is the observation phase. It runs every single day, 7 days a week, and produces a massive top-of-funnel of behavioral signals. Most of those signals are discarded. The funnel math is explicit: for every 40-50 observations, approximately one passes all filters and becomes an actionable trade.

The trade frequency is remarkably low. Across sources, the number ranges from 2 to 12 per year, with 4-8 being the most commonly stated band. These are not tentative positions. When he trades, he goes big -- 5-10% of his entire liquid portfolio via options on a single idea (my-first-million [48:59]). The discipline is in the waiting, not the trading.

This means the actual lifestyle is: observe constantly (evenings), filter ruthlessly (ongoing), and act decisively (a handful of times per year). The rest of the time, he is not watching the market. The lifestyle is more research analyst than trader, and the research medium is TikTok and real-world conversation, not Bloomberg terminals.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User assumes successful investing requires full-time attention or day-trading activity
- User is overtrading because they feel pressure to "always be doing something"
- User wants to understand the actual time commitment before starting
- User has a day job and thinks investing is only for people who can watch markets all day
- User is conflating frequency with performance

**Examples:**

1. **The nightly TikTok session as "research time":** When Camillo describes spending 3-4 hours a night on TikTok, he is not doomscrolling. He is reading comments on videos about products, services, and cultural shifts -- looking for conversational signals that precede financial data. The comment sections are his Bloomberg terminal. This reframes what "research" looks like: it is not spreadsheets and earnings calls, it is pattern-recognition in lived consumer behavior.

2. **The 4-8 trades per year as a feature, not a bug:** Most investors feel compelled to trade more when they have an opinion. Camillo's discipline is the opposite -- he trades less, not more, as his conviction threshold rises. The 68% annualized return over 17 years was generated by roughly 70 high-conviction trades total (trends-friends [8:39]). That is about 4 per year, and each one was decisive and concentrated.

3. **The integration with a normal life:** Before going full-time, Camillo ran this process while holding a corporate job at eRewards. He made his first million while employed full-time (my-first-million [37:46]). The evening observation sessions and occasional concentrated trades fit around a standard work schedule. This is not a lifestyle that requires quitting your job to pursue.

- "No matter who you are, I guarantee you this is a style of investing that anyone in the world can embrace." (rich-habits [12:10])
- "I graduated in the bottom 25% of my high school class." (my-first-million [1:06:27])
- "I'm the most regular person. I'm the most, underdog investor." (rich-habits [3:21])

- "My life mission is to pull every human on Earth into the investor class. Literally, that's it." (present-fathers [50:33])
- "the data set that I'm most well known for interpreting are those conversational data-sets that quite honestly anyone in the world has access to." (motley-fool L22)
- "You don't need... a degree from... Harvard or Wharton." (words-of-rizdom [57:54]) [PARAPHRASE -- VERIFY exact wording]

**Practical meaning:** Camillo does not position his method as elite knowledge that requires special credentials to access. He does the opposite: he aggressively de-credentials it. He foregrounds his own non-elite academic background, names specific working-class occupations of people in his community who practice the method successfully, and frames the entire public-data research process as available to anyone with internet access.

The accessibility claim is not soft marketing. It rests on a structural argument: the data he uses (TikTok comments, social media conversations, in-store observations) is available to everyone. The edge does not come from proprietary data access -- it comes from interpretation discipline. "Everyone could be looking at the same data as me, but I can gain an edge on everyone else because I feel like I'm the best interpreting that data." (opto [51:23]). The method is public; the skill is in the synthesis.

This connects directly to the lifestyle message: if the data is public and the method does not require financial credentials, then the only barriers are curiosity and time. The observation habit (nightly social media reading) and the filtering discipline (needle-mover test, off-radar check, confounder check) are cognitive skills that can be trained by anyone willing to put in the apprenticeship.

The patience qualifier is important. He explicitly warns that the training period is real: "be patient. Be patient. It takes time to rewire your brain to observe things and connect dots." (rich-habits [1:26:03]). Learnable does not mean instant. Expect a long apprenticeship where pattern-recognition skills are built through consistent repetition, not overnight insight.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User believes investing requires a finance degree or Wall Street experience
- User is intimidated by perceived complexity of markets
- User comes from a non-traditional background and questions whether they can compete
- User is looking for permission to start
- User is early in their learning curve and frustrated by slow progress

**Examples:**

1. **The occupational diversity proof:** Camillo does not just claim the method is for everyone -- he names specific non-finance professionals who practice it: dentists, delivery drivers, pilots, UPS drivers, homemakers, teachers, plumbers (rich-habits [12:10], confessions [22:30], school-greatness-jun [L95]). These are not hypothetical. They are people in his community who surface trade ideas that he sometimes uses himself: "half my investments come from random people in my community that have learned how to do this for themselves." (rich-habits [1:27:31]).

2. **The "rewiring" timeline:** He sets realistic expectations by framing the learning curve as neurological. "Rewire your brain to actually see the things that are right in front of you." (rich-habits [1:26:03]). This implies months to years of practice, not days. The coaching message is: you will miss obvious signals at first. That is normal. The skill compounds over time like the investments themselves.

3. **The bottom-25% credential:** His academic self-disclosure -- "I graduated in the bottom 25% of my high school class" -- functions as both vulnerability and proof-of-concept. If he can build a $60M+ portfolio from $20K with that starting position, the method clearly does not select for academic excellence. It selects for observational discipline, which is a different kind of intelligence entirely.

### 3. Signal Discipline / Avoid Financial Media [8/21]

**Core quotes:**

- "I don't read business papers. I don't watch CNBC." (trends-friends [4:57])
- "Believe in yourself, believe in your own research." (opto [45:44])
- "Getting caught up in market noise, watching financial news and letting that noise impact your investments." (opto [45:44])
- "I literally do not consume financial media at all." (alphamind [22:37])
- "I do not read financial news or consume financial media. I stopped watching CNBC like 15 years ago." (words-of-rizdom [36:17])
- "people just get caught up in just noise... and I'm on the phone with RV dealers." (whales [27:48])
- "I don't look at the stock market... day to day." (trends-friends [46:55])
- "don't forget, have fun with it... I'm reading TikTok comments to discover the next big thing. That's fun." (rich-habits [1:26:03])

**Practical meaning:** This is not casual advice to "tune out the noise." It is a hard operational rule that Camillo has maintained for over 15 years. He does not watch financial television. He does not read financial newspapers. He does not follow market commentary. He does not check stock prices daily. The abstention is total and deliberate.

The logic is structural, not emotional: financial media contaminates independent observation. If you consume CNBC before scanning TikTok, your pattern-recognition is pre-loaded with someone else's narrative. You will see what they told you to see, not what is actually happening. The social-arb method depends on detecting change faster than the market prices it -- which requires that your observation process is upstream of consensus media, not downstream of it.

The replacement is the key. He does not create a vacuum by removing financial media. He fills the time with primary-source observation: TikTok comments, in-store visits, conversations with young investors at his local coffee shop, phone calls to RV dealers and franchisees. The information diet shifts from secondhand interpretation (analyst reports, pundit opinions) to firsthand signal (what are people actually doing, buying, saying, and caring about right now?).

The "fun" element is worth noting. He explicitly frames the observation process as enjoyable: "I'm reading TikTok comments to discover the next big thing. That's fun." (rich-habits [1:26:03]). This is significant because it makes the discipline sustainable. He is not white-knuckling his way through self-imposed media abstinence. He replaced something boring (CNBC) with something genuinely engaging (cultural trend-hunting) -- and the replacement happens to be a better data source.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is paralyzed by conflicting market commentary from financial media
- User's conviction is being shaken by pundit consensus
- User is spending more time consuming opinions than gathering their own observations
- User wants to build an information diet that supports independent thinking
- User feels anxious when disconnecting from real-time market data

**Examples:**

1. **The RV-dealer vs. CNBC comparison:** While financial media was debating macro narratives, Camillo was "on the phone with RV dealers" (whales [27:48]) gathering real-time demand data. The RV dealer call produces primary-source intelligence about consumer behavior. CNBC produces secondhand narrative about what analysts think might happen. One feeds the social-arb process; the other contaminates it.

2. **The 15-year media fast:** This is not a recent experiment. He stopped watching CNBC "like 15 years ago" (words-of-rizdom [36:17]). Over that period, his annualized returns have been in the high-60s percent range. The discipline is not correlated with short-term performance variation -- it is a permanent operating principle that he views as structurally load-bearing.

3. **The replacement, not deprivation, model:** The key coaching insight is that signal discipline is about substitution, not subtraction. You do not just stop watching CNBC and stare at a wall. You replace financial media with primary observation channels -- social platforms where real consumers are discussing real products in real time. The lifestyle change feels like a gain (more interesting, more relevant), not a loss.

### 4. App Deletion as Attention Management [2/21]

**Core quotes:**

- "The easiest time hack was removing apps from my phone." (trends-friends [1:03:33])
- "No Twitter, no Instagram on my homepage... Twitter is just moving it to my desktop has increased... maybe an hour a day." (trends-friends [1:03:56])

**Practical meaning:** Camillo treats attention as a budgetable resource. He applies the same ruthless filtering to his own information consumption that he applies to trade ideas. The specific behavioral move -- removing Twitter and Instagram from his phone's homepage -- recovered approximately one hour per day of strategic bandwidth.

This is a micro-tactic with a macro point: if attention is your edge, then anything that degrades attention quality degrades your edge. Social media apps on a phone are designed for impulse consumption. By shifting access to desktop only, he converts social media from a passive drain into a deliberate research session. He still uses the platforms -- but on his terms, in his research window, with intent.

The broader principle is behavioral design for investor performance. Your environment shapes your behavior. If your phone is configured to pull you into reactive consumption, your observation quality suffers. Reconfiguring the environment is cheaper and more reliable than relying on willpower.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User feels overwhelmed by information overload
- User is losing strategic thinking time to reactive social media consumption
- User wants a practical, immediate tactic for reclaiming focus
- User recognizes their phone habits are degrading their investing discipline

**Examples:**

1. **The one-hour-per-day recovery:** The claim is specific: moving Twitter off the phone homepage and to desktop recovered roughly one hour per day (trends-friends [1:03:56]). Over a year, that is 365 hours -- more than nine 40-hour work weeks. For an investor whose edge depends on deep observation, that recovered time is directly convertible into research quality.

2. **The community pruning parallel:** In the same conversation, Camillo extends the attention-management principle to his community: "The way to grow a community is to cut off, clean up parts of the community or spend more time on what's working." (trends-friends [1:03:56]). The philosophy is consistent across personal and organizational levels: grow by subtracting what does not contribute, not only by adding new inputs.

## Stories

### Story 1: The Coffee-Shop Porch Research Loop

**Full narrative:** Camillo describes a daily operating rhythm that sounds almost mundane: "I have coffee with my dog at a local coffee shop, uh, 7 days a week." (rich-habits [26:15]). But within that routine, he is running a deliberate intelligence-gathering operation. He treats the coffee shop as a primary research site, engaging young investors in conversation about what they are buying, what apps they are using, and what cultural shifts they are seeing.

He connects this grassroots observation to a structural macro thesis: the "great wealth transfer" and the explosion of youth participation in investing are positioning Robinhood and peer apps for long-run growth (rich-habits [26:15]). The coffee-shop conversations are his ground-truth validation for what he is seeing in TikTok comments and social media data. The local and the digital reinforce each other.

The simplicity of the routine is the point. He does not need expensive tools, proprietary databases, or institutional access. He needs a coffee shop, his dog, and genuine curiosity about what young people are actually doing with their money.

**Teaching application:** Use when illustrating low-cost primary research methods. When a user says "I don't have access to the data institutional investors use," this story is the response: the data is sitting across the table from you at any coffee shop, if you know what questions to ask. Also use when validating the principle that macro narratives (wealth transfer, generational investing shift) can be confirmed or refuted through hyperlocal observation.

**Sources:** rich-habits [26:15]

### Story 2: App Deletion Time Hack

**Full narrative:** In his conversation on Trends with Friends, Camillo shares what he calls "the easiest time hack": removing social media apps from his phone (trends-friends [1:03:33]). The specific change was modest -- he moved Twitter and Instagram off his phone's homepage, making them accessible only via desktop. The result was immediate: he recovered approximately one hour per day (trends-friends [1:03:56]).

He frames this in the context of attention allocation for an investor whose primary edge is observation quality. Every hour spent on impulse-driven social media scrolling is an hour not spent on deliberate, directed research. The app deletion is not about digital detox -- it is about reconfiguring his environment so that his highest-value activity (deep social media analysis on desktop, in research mode) is not degraded by his lowest-value activity (reactive phone scrolling).

He extends the principle to community management: "The way to grow a community is to cut off, clean up parts of the community or spend more time on what's working." (trends-friends [1:03:56]). The same subtraction-based growth philosophy applies at personal and organizational scale.

**Teaching application:** Use when a user is discussing executive focus and reclaiming strategy bandwidth. Simple behavioral design -- changing the default environment rather than relying on willpower -- can recover meaningful time. Also use when coaching someone who feels they "don't have enough time to research" -- the time may already exist, trapped inside low-value consumption patterns.

**Sources:** trends-friends [1:03:33], [1:03:56]

### Story 3: The 11-Year-Old Amazon Lesson

**Full narrative:** At a tennis event, an 11-year-old asked Camillo how to invest $500. Rather than suggesting an index fund or a savings account, he proposed full deployment into Amazon -- even with margin -- and framed it as an expected-value calculation: "better than 50/50 chance you will make 4 times your money" versus "1 or 2% chance... you might lose all of your money." (iced-coffee [43:11], [44:04]).

His purpose was not to give specific investment advice to a child. It was to teach probabilistic thinking: "I wanted her to start thinking in probabilities, and I wanted her to start thinking about expected outcomes." (iced-coffee [44:04]). He later privately offered to reimburse any losses, removing the real downside while preserving the educational experience.

The story reveals how Camillo thinks about early investing experiences. The dollar amount is irrelevant. What matters is that a young person starts thinking about risk, probability, and expected outcomes through direct experience rather than textbook abstraction. Small early experiences with real capital compound into lifelong investor identity.

**Teaching application:** Use when encouraging early investing participation regardless of age or capital size. The teaching point is that the psychological formation -- learning to think in probabilities, developing comfort with uncertainty, building an investor identity -- matters far more than the dollar outcome of any first trade. Also use when discussing how to teach investing concepts to beginners: direct experience with small stakes teaches faster than theory.

**Sources:** iced-coffee [43:11], [44:04]
