# Module 5: Going Big on a High-Conviction Position

## Overview

This module covers Camillo's approach to position sizing when conviction is high. He is not a broad-diversification investor. He makes 2-12 trades per year, sizes them aggressively, and uses options to amplify returns on his strongest ideas. The philosophy is simple: if the research is deep enough and the information asymmetry is real, small positions waste the edge. But this aggression has strict prerequisites -- thorough research, risk-capital separation, and probabilistic thinking. Without those, going big is just gambling.

This module is for coaching situations where a user has found a strong thesis and is debating how much to commit. They want to understand conviction-based sizing, the role of leverage, and how to think about asymmetric risk without blowing up.

## Concepts

### 1. Conviction Sizing / Go Big When Right [17/21]

**Core quotes:**

- "Go bigger, go even bigger on my highest conviction trades." (opto [49:47])
- "On a low conviction trade... a couple percent of my portfolio. On a high conviction trade... between 20% of my portfolio and 30%." (opto [23:40])
- "I'm usually investing between 5% and 10% of my entire liquid portfolio into that idea via options." (my-first-million [48:59])
- "I knew the only way that I would get to where I wanted is to be right and go in big." (farzad [19:17])
- "if you just get one thing right and you go in on it, all you really need to be one of the best investors in the world is to get one really big thing right over your entire life." (farzad [54:26])
- "Invest with concentration, not diversification, when you have high conviction." [SECONDARY SOURCE -- VERIFY] (unknown-wizards)
- "having money concentrated in high-growth assets is excruciatingly important." (present-fathers [4:20])

**Practical meaning:** Camillo operates on a conviction ladder. Low-conviction ideas get a couple percent of portfolio. Medium-conviction ideas get moderate sizing. High-conviction ideas -- where he has deep research, confirmed information asymmetry, and a defined trade window -- get 20-30% of portfolio in equity, or 5-10% via options (which can represent equivalent or larger notional exposure due to leverage). On rare occasions (Nintendo Wii), he has gone to 100% of portfolio.

The conviction ladder is not about gut feeling. It maps directly to research depth, information-asymmetry confirmation, and the absence of competing catalysts. The bigger the position, the more verification work is required upstream. As he puts it: "you really have to be comprehensive in your research if you're going to take a levered bet... And you can't, you can't be lazy." (my-first-million [52:56])

**Coaching triggers:**
- User has a strong thesis but is defaulting to a small position because of generic diversification advice
- User is asking how much of their portfolio should go into their best idea
- User has been right on several ideas but undersized every one and made little money
- User needs to understand the difference between conviction and recklessness

**Examples:**

1. **Nintendo Wii (100% of portfolio):** After experiencing the Wii firsthand at E3 and observing intense user excitement, he concentrated fully: "I had 100%, 100% of my portfolio... I bought Nintendo ADRs." (alphamind [12:55]). Made approximately 2.5x in 9-10 months.

2. **Peloton pandemic options (seven-figure trade):** After verifying that Peloton had stopped advertising while demand surged, he placed his largest-ever options trade: "Largest trade I've ever made in my entire life. It was a 7-figure options trade." (alphamind [22:37])

3. **Sphere/Wizard of Oz (massive levered options):** After reading thousands of TikTok comments in the first 48 hours and confirming ticket sellouts, he "opened up a massive levered options position in Sphere" (present-fathers [21:14]) and watched it nearly double in weeks.

### 2. Asymmetric Risk / Expected Value Thinking [10/21]

**Core quotes:**

- "If there's a 51% chance that I make more than 2x my money... I need to do that every time." (alphamind [32:41])
- "there might only be a 1 in 100 chance... but if it happens, you make 500 times your money... you've got to take that every time" (alphamind [36:42])
- "with big returns comes big risk, you just cannot get around that." (moneymasters [8:50])
- "If you're not willing to take real risk, you're simply never going to get rich quick." (iced-coffee [0:00])
- "the ability to take on risk is probably the number one thing that separates the wealth class from everyone else" (present-fathers [4:20])
- "better than 50/50 chance you will make 4 times your money... 1 or 2% chance... you might lose all of your money" (iced-coffee [43:11])
- "there's no such thing as a sure thing." (opto [38:23])

**Practical meaning:** Camillo thinks in expected value, not certainty. He accepts that many trades will lose. What matters is the ratio: if the probability-weighted upside exceeds the probability-weighted downside, the trade is sound even if it fails. This is how he can put a third of his risk capital into a single options trade -- because the asymmetry of the payoff makes the expected value positive even at moderate win rates.

But he never claims certainty. He holds aggression and humility together: "you never, ever, ever -- there's no such thing as a sure thing." (opto [38:23]). The discipline is in doing the math, not in pretending the outcome is guaranteed.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is paralyzed by fear of loss on a well-researched idea
- User does not understand why someone would risk a large sum on a single trade
- User conflates "high risk" with "bad decision" without considering expected value
- User needs help thinking probabilistically rather than in binary win/lose terms

**Examples:**

1. **COVID puts timing pain:** He bought puts on the market two weeks before the crash, losing money initially as media dismissed COVID. He persisted because the expected value was favorable: "I put on the biggest short of my life." (alphamind [22:37]). He lost 30-40% before the thesis paid off and he ultimately made $30 million that year. (my-first-million [52:56])

2. **11-year-old Amazon lesson:** At a tennis event, an 11-year-old asked how to invest $500. Camillo proposed full deployment and framed it as expected value: "better than 50/50 chance you will make 4 times your money... 1 or 2% chance... you might lose all of your money." (iced-coffee [43:11])

### 3. Options as a Leverage Tool [8/21]

**Core quotes:**

- "options trading is really just a tool for leverage." (present-fathers [4:20])
- "It's a tool. It's just a tool." (present-fathers [4:20])
- "To me, an option is just how can I like 10 or 20x my leverage." (farzad [19:17])
- "I only use options for leverage. That's it." (words-of-rizdom [45:33])
- "we have to embrace leverage, but we have to do it in the right way with the right risk bucket." (present-fathers [4:20])
- "I'm usually investing between 5% and 10% of my entire liquid portfolio into that idea via options." (my-first-million [48:59])

**Practical meaning:** Camillo does not use options for day-trading, speculation without thesis, or complex multi-leg strategies. He uses them as a straightforward leverage tool: when he has a high-conviction thesis with a defined time window, options let him express that conviction with more capital efficiency than equity alone. The trade-off is clear -- if the thesis is wrong, the premium is gone. That total-loss risk is why options sizing is lower in percentage terms (5-10%) but can produce outsized returns (200-300%+) when the thesis is correct.

The critical prerequisite is that options are only used inside risk capital -- never from savings, retirement, or family-security funds. And the research bar is higher: "you really have to be comprehensive in your research if you're going to take a levered bet." (my-first-million [52:56])

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is curious about options but treats them as inherently dangerous
- User is already using options but without thesis backing -- pure speculation
- User wants to understand how Camillo gets large returns from concentrated positions
- User needs to understand that leverage amplifies both good and bad decisions

**Examples:**

1. **Elmer's Glue / Newell Brands:** He identified that the slime trend was driving unusual demand for white glue, traced it to Newell Brands (parent of Elmer's), and took a leveraged call position. Despite Elmer's being less than 2% of Newell's revenue, the slow-growth context meant even a small bump was material. His options returned approximately 300% in weeks. (rich-habits [12:10])

2. **Beacon Roofing via hailstorm data:** He tracked Google searches for "roof repair" and saw one season spike to triple historical norms. He took a "very large, very levered call long position" in Beacon Roofing, front-running insurance claim reports that lagged by about a month. (my-first-million [15:26])

## Stories

### Story 1: The Nintendo Wii All-In Bet

**Full narrative:** At an E3 gaming convention, Camillo experienced the Nintendo Wii firsthand and observed intense user excitement: "I saw firsthand the line of people waiting to play with that Wii." (alphamind [12:55]). While the market consensus was that the Wii's underpowered hardware could not compete with Xbox and PlayStation specs, Camillo saw something different -- people were emotionally captivated by the experience, regardless of specifications. He concentrated fully: "I had 100%, 100% of my portfolio... I bought Nintendo ADRs." (alphamind [12:55]). The outcome: "I think I made like 2.5 times my money in... 9 or 10 months." (alphamind [12:55])

**Teaching application:** Use this story when a user has firsthand product or demand evidence that contradicts Wall Street consensus. The teaching point is that direct behavioral observation -- seeing how people actually react to a product -- can justify extreme concentration when the market is anchored on the wrong variable (specs vs experience). Also use when a user asks whether 100% concentration is ever justified; this is Camillo's most extreme example.

**Sources:** alphamind [12:55], opto [23:40], unknown-wizards

### Story 2: The Peloton Seven-Figure Pandemic Bet

**Full narrative:** During COVID, Camillo identified that Peloton had stopped all advertising while simultaneously selling more bikes than ever. The #1 bear thesis on Peloton -- that it would never be profitable because of high customer acquisition costs -- had evaporated overnight. He verified this obsessively: "I was calling them every day asking them, are you sure there's not supply shortage?" (alphamind [22:37]). Once confirmed, he placed his largest-ever trade: "Largest trade I've ever made in my entire life. It was a 7-figure options trade." (alphamind [22:37]). The result: "close to $4 million on just this year alone" from Peloton. (opto [33:31])

**Teaching application:** Use this story when explaining how regime changes can invert a known bear thesis and create asymmetric setups. The key lesson is that Camillo did not just identify the opportunity -- he verified it repeatedly before sizing up. He called the company daily. He checked for supply constraints. The seven-figure options position was the culmination of deep diligence, not a snap decision. Use this story to illustrate the relationship between research depth and position size.

**Sources:** alphamind [22:37], opto [33:31], school-greatness-may [43:27]

### Story 3: The Sphere / Wizard of Oz Trade

**Full narrative:** A contact in Vegas told Camillo the Sphere was generating unusual buzz. He validated by reading "thousands and thousands of comments on TikTok videos" (present-fathers [21:14]) and checking ticket sellout data. His conclusion: "investors are not onto this yet." (present-fathers [21:14]). He took a massive levered options position. In the my-first-million interview, he called it a "monstrously big levered bet" (my-first-million [35:48]). The stock nearly doubled within weeks as awareness spread from retail social media to Wall Street and the company raised guidance.

**Teaching application:** Use this story when explaining end-to-end social arb execution at the conviction-sizing level: signal (local contact + TikTok comment volume) -> validation (ticket sellouts, comment sentiment) -> awareness check (Wall Street not yet on it) -> sizing (massive levered options) -> exit (stock reprices as information reaches parity). This is Camillo's most recent high-conviction trade example and demonstrates the full pipeline from signal to sizing.

**Sources:** my-first-million [35:48], present-fathers [21:14]

### Story 4: The QSR/Tim Hortons Loss (Counter-Example)

**Full narrative:** Camillo bet heavily on QSR (parent of Burger King, Popeyes, Tim Hortons) expecting that the Impossible Whopper and the viral Popeyes chicken sandwich would drive strong earnings. The thesis was partially right -- but he missed a devastating weak link. Tim Hortons posted a severe negative surprise driven by a franchisee revolt at an annual meeting he did not attend. Cost: "a full third of my portfolio." (my-first-million [52:56]). His takeaway was blunt: "you really have to be comprehensive in your research if you're going to take a levered bet... And you can't, you can't be lazy." (my-first-million [52:56]). He specifically regretted not attending the franchisee meeting: "if I was just in... the bar, I would have been well aware." (my-first-million [52:56])

**Teaching application:** Use this story whenever someone is sizing big. It is the essential counter-balance to the conviction-sizing philosophy. Going big works when research is thorough -- but going big with incomplete research is catastrophic. The specific lesson: multi-segment companies have hidden weak links. If you are concentrating on a parent entity, you must understand every major division, not just the one driving your thesis. Also use when a user has experienced a large loss from a concentrated position -- Camillo models how to convert pain into process improvement rather than abandoning the approach.

**Sources:** my-first-million [52:56]

### Story 5: The Jakks Pacific Blowup (Counter-Example)

**Full narrative:** In his short-lived hedge fund, Camillo's first trade was Jakks Pacific around a Frozen toy -- "probably one of the best-selling toys in two decades of the toy industry." (opto [35:35]). The thesis was correct: earnings came in strong and the stock opened up 35% pre-market. Then it reversed violently to down 25% as the largest holder used the earnings pop to exit their entire 10-11% stake. SEC filings later confirmed the dump. Camillo says it "still haunts me." (opto [35:35])

**Teaching application:** Use this story when discussing hidden-liquidity risk and the principle that a correct thesis does not guarantee profits. Even with high conviction and a validated catalyst, unknown ownership dynamics (large holders waiting to exit) can overwhelm the trade. This is an important nuance for anyone learning conviction sizing: your thesis can be right, your timing can be right, and you can still lose.

**Sources:** opto [35:35]
