# Module 7: Using Social Media and Community as Research Tools

## Overview

**Trigger:** The user wants to know how to use social media, online communities, and digital platforms as investment research tools. They are asking about practical workflows for monitoring trends, which platforms to watch, how to extract signal from noise in comments sections, or how to build a research network of observers.

**Core message:** Social media is not a distraction from investing -- it is the primary research laboratory. The richest, most actionable investment data in the world sits in TikTok comments, and it is free. But raw access is not the edge. The edge is interpretation: reading thousands of comments nightly, detecting what people intend to buy before they buy it, and doing this with a discipline most professionals refuse to match. Beyond personal monitoring, the ultimate force multiplier is community -- a distributed network of ordinary people (UPS drivers, physicians, restaurant managers, teachers) who observe the world from positions Wall Street cannot occupy. This is not a stock-tips group. It is a collaborative observation network where insights are shared, not trades.

This module contains four core concepts, three anchor stories, and cross-references to supporting modules.

## Concepts

### 1. TikTok Comments as Primary Alpha Source [12/21]

This is Camillo's most operationally specific research claim: the majority of his current investment ideas originate from reading TikTok comments, not from financial data, news, or analyst reports.

**Exact quotes:**

- "I spend on average 3 to 4 hours a night... reading... TikTok comments." (my-first-million [19:14])
- "I feel that TikTok comments for the past few years have become the richest data set and it's completely free and everybody has access to it." (motley-fool L22)
- "I've migrated almost exclusively to TikTok now. Most of my insights come off of TikTok comments." (motley-fool L22)
- "The goldmine for me of data right now is TikTok comments." (whales [34:12])
- "I read thousands and thousands of comments on TikTok videos. That's like my main source of alpha." (present-fathers [21:14])
- "I spend like three hours a night, sometimes four, just reading conversations on social media." (motley-fool L22)
- "It's not about a video... It's the comments... That's where the gold is." (generation-c [7:42])
- "It's the comments where 10,000 other women are saying, we totally agree, it's already sold out, we're on a waitlist... That's where the gold is." (generation-c [7:42])

**Practical meaning:** The video itself is not the signal -- the comments underneath are. A creator might praise a product, but the alpha is in the thousands of comments below revealing purchase intent, sold-out status, emotional attachment, and competitive comparisons. This is conversational data at its rawest: unfiltered, real-time consumer intent expressed before it becomes a transaction.

The time commitment is non-negotiable. Three to four hours per night, every night, reading comments. This is not casual scrolling -- it is disciplined ethnographic research performed with investor intent. The barrier to entry is not access (anyone has it) but willingness to do the interpretive labor consistently.

**Coaching triggers:** Use when someone asks "Where do you actually find your ideas?" or says they want to start monitoring social media for investment signals. Also use when someone says they spend time on TikTok but never thought of it as research -- reframe their existing habit as a potential edge.

**Examples:**
- The Crocs resurgence trade came "mostly based on TikTok comments" while Wall Street still viewed Crocs as a fad. He observed healthcare adoption and celebrity collaborations making the brand cool with younger demographics. (school-greatness-may [41:19])
- For the Sphere trade, he "read thousands and thousands of comments on TikTok videos" about the Wizard of Oz experience, validated demand through ticketing checks, confirmed "investors are not onto this yet," then opened "a massive levered options position" that roughly doubled in weeks. (present-fathers [21:14])
- He reads comments with a specific lens: "Once you read through 500, 600 comments, it becomes pretty evident that this is a cult brand." (whales [11:39])

### 2. Community / Collaborative Research [13/21]

Camillo's second major research infrastructure is not a tool or platform -- it is people. A distributed community of ~40,000-100,000 ordinary people who observe the world from positions Wall Street analysts cannot occupy.

**Exact quotes:**

- "Half my investments come from random people in my community who have learned how to do this for themselves." (rich-habits [1:27:31])
- "We're like a big hedge fund of just regular people." (school-greatness-jun [L94-L95])
- "My community probably contributes more to my trades than I personally contribute." (whales [17:34])
- "About 65% of those returns were generated from... my community." (whales [17:34])
- "We have UPS drivers in our community... people in our community who work at restaurants and who are physicians, who are scientists." (moneymasters [4:24])
- "The larger our community gets, our combined knowledge base just continues to grow and grow." (moneymasters [4:24])
- "We actually end up sourcing a lot of our trades from our community now." (moneymasters [0:38])
- "My community actually surfaces more ideas for me than I surface for them." (generation-c [41:52])
- "The Dumb Money community is an ecosystem of observers. We collaborate to identify these changes in real time." (medium L24)
- "We don't share stock tips, but rather insights: 'Look what's happening here. How does this change the game board?'" (medium L24)

**Practical meaning:** This is not a Discord where people share ticker symbols and price targets. It is a collaborative observation network with a strict cultural norm: share what you are seeing in the world, not what you are trading. Insights, not signals. A UPS driver notices package volume shifting. A restaurant manager sees a brand's sales collapse overnight. A physician observes a new treatment gaining traction. Each person contributes domain-specific ground truth that no single analyst could replicate.

The community operates on three rules that differentiate it from typical investing forums:

1. **Share observations, not trades.** "We don't really discuss trades... what we discuss... is change trends." (school-greatness-jun [L90-L91])
2. **Independent execution.** "Once we all do our own research... Then we go off and make our own trades." (school-greatness-jun [L94-L97])
3. **No monetization.** "We don't take $1 from our YouTube community... patently rejected FTX money." (whales [17:34]). "We don't charge a dime for anything. We don't even do sponsorship." (school-greatness-jun [L432-L433])

**Coaching triggers:** Use when someone asks how to build or join an investing community. Also use when someone is trying to research alone and hitting limits -- the reframe is that your network IS your research team, and each person's unique vantage point is irreplaceable data. Use when discussing how to structure communities around shared observation rather than stock tips.

**Examples:**
- During the pandemic, community members were calling RV dealers, visiting bike shops, and reporting back real-time demand data while Wall Street relied on lagging credit card reports. "People are focused on the wrong things... and I'm on the phone with RV dealers." (whales [27:48])
- Community fieldwork includes physical store visits, video recording, and aggregated primary research: "We interview stores... take videos... aggregate research." (school-greatness-jun [L94-L95])
- The Bud Light thesis was validated by a community member who owned a restaurant and reported "my Bud Light sales are down 67% in the 3 weeks after this drama hit." (whales [44:02])

### 3. Platform Migration (Twitter to TikTok) [6/21]

Good investors periodically audit and rebuild their information stack as platforms evolve. A data source that generated alpha five years ago may be dead signal today.

**Exact quotes:**

- "Over the years, Twitter has evolved into more of a news and political platform... it's become a lot less valuable for me as a social arb trader. These days I'm more focused on platforms like TikTok." (moneymasters [6:35])
- "In the past, I used to do a lot of this on Facebook... many, many years... on Twitter." (motley-fool L16)
- "I've migrated almost exclusively to TikTok now." (motley-fool L22)
- "I'm probably the world's number one expert in interpreting data on TikTok." (moneymasters [6:35])

**Practical meaning:** Camillo's research platform has migrated three times: Facebook in the early years, then Twitter (where he built TickerTags on the "deca hose" of tweet data), and now TikTok. Each migration happened because user behavior shifted -- Twitter became a news and politics platform where consumer purchase intent largely disappeared, while TikTok became the place where people transparently discuss what they buy, want, and obsess over.

The principle is not "use TikTok." The principle is: your information source has a lifecycle. When users migrate, when content types shift, when the platform's algorithm changes what surfaces -- your signal degrades. The discipline is to notice that degradation and relocate to wherever consumer intent is most transparently expressed right now.

**Coaching triggers:** Use when someone says they rely on Twitter/X for investment research and wonders why signal quality is declining. Use when explaining why investors must periodically audit their information sources for signal decay. Also use when someone is anchored to a single platform -- the meta-lesson is platform-agnostic: follow the signal, not the app.

### 4. Interpretation Over Raw Data [10/21]

Everyone has access to the same social media. The edge is not in seeing the data -- it is in interpreting it better than anyone else.

**Exact quotes:**

- "AI provides the starting point, not the final answer. The real skill lies in human interpretation and subsequent investigation." (medium L20)
- "Everyone could be looking at the same data as me, but I can gain an edge on everyone else because I feel like I'm the best interpreting that data." (opto [51:23])
- "I can show the same thing to 10 people and they'll have 10 slightly different interpretations." (motley-fool L20)
- "You actually have to spend a little bit of time reading a lot of this conversation to extract knowledge from it, and that takes work." (motley-fool L20)
- "At the highest levels, I would literally hold their hand and they just refused to do the work." (motley-fool L18)
- "I've been talking about this now for 14, 15 years, and I have more alpha today than I had 10, 12, 13 years ago." (motley-fool L22)

**Practical meaning:** Public data can still generate alpha because the bottleneck is never access -- it is interpretive labor. Reading 500 comments about Crocs to determine whether enthusiasm is durable or fleeting. Distinguishing genuine purchase intent from ironic commentary. Sensing whether a viral moment will translate to sustained demand or fade in a week. This is manual, time-intensive cognitive work that most people -- including institutions -- simply refuse to do.

This also explains why Camillo's strategy has become more alpha-generative over time, not less, despite being publicly discussed for 15+ years. The method is known; the execution is not replicable without sustained effort. "It is simple, but it's also not." (motley-fool L22)

**Coaching triggers:** Use when someone asks "If everyone can see TikTok comments, where's the edge?" The edge is that almost nobody will spend four hours a night doing the interpretive work. Use when someone has AI tools surfacing social data and assumes the AI output is the final answer -- the reframe is that AI provides the map, but human investigation leads to the treasure (medium L20).

## Stories

### Story 1: Crocs Resurgence via TikTok Comments

**Full narrative:** Wall Street had written off Crocs as a fad -- an ugly shoe that spiked in popularity and would inevitably fade. But Camillo was reading TikTok comments and seeing something entirely different. Healthcare workers were adopting Crocs for their comfort during long shifts. Celebrity collaborations were making the brand aspirational with younger demographics. The comments were not nostalgic -- they were forward-looking purchase intent. He says the Crocs trade came "mostly based on TikTok comments" (school-greatness-may [41:19]). When Crocs acquired HeyDude and the stock sold off on Wall Street skepticism, he went deeper into the comments: "Once you read through 500, 600 comments, it becomes pretty evident that this is a cult brand." (whales [11:39]). The institutional consensus called HeyDude "the dumbest acquisition." The comment-level consumer reality told a completely different story.

**Teaching application:** Use when someone dismisses social data as "noise" or "not real research." The Crocs trade demonstrates that thousands of authentic consumer comments can reveal durable demand shifts that institutional models miss entirely. Also use to illustrate the interpretive gap: Wall Street had access to the same TikTok comments -- they simply did not read them.

**Sources:** school-greatness-may [41:19], whales [10:05], whales [11:39]

### Story 2: Twitter-to-TikTok Migration

**Full narrative:** For years, Camillo's primary research platform was Twitter. He built TickerTags -- a social intelligence company -- on top of Twitter's data firehose ("really a deca hose, 10% of the fire hose of all tweets" -- motley-fool L18), creating a taxonomy of "millions of word combinations" mapped to public companies. He sold that company to Jefferies Bank. But then the platform shifted. "Over the years, Twitter has evolved into more of a news and political platform" (moneymasters [6:35]). The consumer purchase-intent conversations that generated his alpha migrated elsewhere. Rather than clinging to a degrading signal source, he followed the data: "I've migrated almost exclusively to TikTok now" (motley-fool L22). The man who built a company on Twitter data abandoned Twitter when it stopped serving his process. Today he claims "I'm probably the world's number one expert in interpreting data on TikTok" (moneymasters [6:35]) and says he has "more alpha today than I had 10, 12, 13 years ago" (motley-fool L22) -- despite the method being publicly known for over 15 years.

**Teaching application:** Use when explaining why investors must periodically audit their information sources for signal decay. The meta-lesson is about adaptability: the best investor in the room five years ago on Twitter could be the worst today if they never noticed the signal migrated. Also use when discussing how to recognize when a platform's user behavior has shifted enough to warrant rebuilding your research workflow.

**Sources:** moneymasters [6:35], motley-fool L16, motley-fool L18, motley-fool L22

### Story 3: Dumb Money as a Distributed Observation Network

**Full narrative:** What started as a YouTube show seven years ago -- "we're on once a week just literally talking about what we do for fun. Just to try to inspire other people" (motley-fool L14) -- evolved into a 40,000-100,000 person research network that now generates more investment ideas than Camillo himself. "My community probably contributes more to my trades than I personally contribute" (whales [17:34]). The community includes "UPS drivers... people who work at restaurants and who are physicians, who are scientists" (moneymasters [4:24]). Each person contributes ground-level observations from their professional domain -- the kind of data no hedge fund analyst could replicate from a Manhattan desk.

But the community works precisely because of what it does NOT do. "We don't share stock tips, but rather insights: 'Look what's happening here. How does this change the game board?'" (medium L24). Members surface observations, do collaborative research ("we interview stores... take videos... aggregate research" -- school-greatness-jun [L94-L95]), and then "go off and make our own trades" (school-greatness-jun [L94-L97]). The community takes no money from members, runs no courses, and rejected sponsorship deals including FTX. "We don't take $1 from our YouTube community" (whales [17:34]).

The result is what Camillo calls "a big hedge fund of just regular people" (school-greatness-jun [L94-L95]) -- except without the institutional constraints that make actual hedge funds slow and blind.

**Teaching application:** Use when discussing how to build communities around shared observation, not stock tips. The critical design principles are: (1) share insights, not trades; (2) everyone does their own research and makes independent decisions; (3) no monetization of the community itself, which preserves trust and eliminates conflicts. Also use when explaining the compounding nature of a research network -- "the larger our community gets, our combined knowledge base just continues to grow and grow" (moneymasters [4:24]).

**Sources:** whales [17:34], moneymasters [0:38], moneymasters [4:24], medium L24, school-greatness-jun [L94-L95], motley-fool L14, generation-c [19:09], generation-c [41:52]
