# Module 14: Building and Selling a Business

## Overview

This module covers Camillo's experience as a serial entrepreneur and angel investor -- and critically, his hard-won skepticism about entrepreneurship as a wealth-creation path. He has spent decades as a founder, built and sold companies, invested in over 150 early-stage ventures, and ultimately concluded that for most people, building a company is a worse bet than public-market investing. That conclusion does not come from ignorance of the startup world; it comes from deep immersion in it.

The module draws on his founding of TickerTags (a social-data intelligence company sold to Jefferies), his involvement with eCarList (sold to DealerTrack), his aborted hedge fund, and his extensive angel portfolio. It also captures his tactical advice on fundraising, pitching, and the founder-investor relationship. The tension at the core of this module is that Camillo has the credentials and track record to advise founders credibly, yet his strongest recommendation is usually: do not become one unless you have no choice.

Use this module when a user is building a startup, thinking about selling a company, evaluating the founder path versus investing, or asking about fundraising, product-market fit, and the entrepreneurial journey.

## Concepts

### 1. Entrepreneurship Is Over-Romanticized [4/21]

**Core quotes:**

- "For most people, it's insane to go out and start your own company ever." (school-greatness-may [8:34])
- "There are a near infinite number of ways for a founder to fail." (school-greatness-may [9:22])
- "I was working like... 70-hour weeks... those are some of the toughest years of my life." (farzad [41:21])
- "I've had like mixed success as a startup entrepreneur. I've started many companies. The vast majority of them have failed, and the ones that didn't were still torturous." [PARAPHRASE -- VERIFY] (school-greatness-may [9:22], [12:05])

**Practical meaning:** Camillo speaks about entrepreneurship with the authority of someone who has lived it -- and been burned by it repeatedly. He started multiple companies over two decades, and his honest assessment is that most of them failed. Even the successful ones imposed extreme personal cost: years of 70-hour weeks, strain on family time, and returns that underperformed his public-market strategy.

His framing is deliberately contrarian to the cultural narrative. Where most business media celebrates founders, Camillo argues that the expected value of entrepreneurship is negative for most people. He invested in "about 150" private companies with deep due diligence, and reports a failure rate of roughly 75%: "75% of my portfolio that were deeply vetted... Almost all companies will just fail." (school-greatness-may [8:54]). Even with privileged access and operator-level expertise, the base rate of startup failure is devastating.

This does not mean he discourages entrepreneurship universally. He frames it as a calling for a specific type of person -- someone who "will steamroll relationships to make their company a success" (school-greatness-may [9:22]) and cannot function as an employee. For everyone else, he argues that public-market investing gives asymmetric exposure to the world's best operators without bearing the operational risk yourself: "You could just literally just throw some money and just ride their life." (school-greatness-may [9:22]).

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is romanticizing the startup path without accounting for base rates of failure
- User is considering leaving a stable career to start a company primarily for financial reasons
- User needs a reality check on founder life from someone who has been through it
- User is comparing the "glory" of founding versus the discipline of investing

**Examples:**

1. **The personal track record confession:** Camillo openly states he spent decades "torturing yourself as a founder," started many companies, and "almost all of them have failed." Even with access, networks, and capital, entrepreneurship was his lowest-returning activity. (school-greatness-may [9:22], [12:05])

2. **The angel portfolio base rate:** Across ~150 private investments with operator-level diligence, approximately 75% failed. This from a person with decades of startup experience, extensive networks, and the ability to spend months evaluating founders before committing. If that is the failure rate with deep expertise, the rate for a typical first-time founder or investor is worse. (school-greatness-may [8:02], [8:54])

3. **The opportunity-cost reframe:** He explicitly calls his private-market investing "the biggest investment mistake I ever made" -- not because of losses, but because it diverted attention and capital from his public strategy, which returned 60-70%+ annualized. The private portfolio returned "10, 11, 12% annualized." (my-first-million [41:39]; iced-coffee [47:55])

### 2. Build Tools for Your Own Pain First [5/21]

**Core quotes:**

- "I'm a self-directed investor. I built TickerTags for myself." (successful-pitch L71)
- "It was basically institutionalizing my methodology." (alphamind [7:03])
- "I created this company called TickerTags" [to automate] "what I've been doing for ten years manually." (successful-pitch L48; alphamind [7:03])

**Practical meaning:** When Camillo did build a company, it was not from market research or trend analysis -- it was from solving his own recurring pain point. He had spent a decade manually reading 15,000 tweets per night to detect social-data signals for investing. TickerTags was the automation of that exact workflow. He was user zero, and the product's core value proposition was something he had already validated through years of manual execution.

This is his primary filter for evaluating startups as both a founder and an investor. The strongest founders are not those who spotted a "market opportunity" from a pitch deck -- they are the ones who built something because they needed it and could not find an existing solution. The product-market fit question is resolved before the company is even formed: the founder is the market.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is evaluating startup ideas that solve other people's problems rather than their own
- User is trying to build something for a market they do not deeply understand
- User is seeking product-market fit validation through surveys instead of personal use
- User wants to know what kind of company to build

**Examples:**

1. **The TickerTags origin:** He spent a decade doing the work manually -- reading tweets, tracking keyword acceleration, mapping social signals to companies. When he built TickerTags, it was not a speculative bet on "social data analytics." It was the direct automation of a workflow he had already proven generates alpha. (successful-pitch L48, L71)

2. **The manual-to-product escalation:** TickerTags grew to "350,000 tags spanning products, brands, people, places, and cultural movements" and secured a licensing deal with Twitter's Decahose (10% of all tweets). This infrastructure emerged organically from his personal process -- it was not designed top-down. (successful-pitch L47; my-first-million [29:49])

### 3. Investors Back Concept + Team [3/21]

**Core quotes:**

- "They're investing in two things. They're investing a concept that they believe in, but they're also investing people they believe can execute on their vision." (successful-pitch L91)
- "It's about proving that you have the right concept and proving that you have the right team to execute." (successful-pitch L123)
- "first establishing myself as a thought leader in this niche" (successful-pitch L86)

**Practical meaning:** From both the founder and investor side of the table, Camillo frames fundraising as a two-factor test. The concept must be credible -- a real market need with a plausible thesis for why this team can capture value. But concept alone is insufficient. Investors are also evaluating whether the specific people in the room can execute under pressure, adapt to surprises, and sustain focus through the years of grinding that follow seed funding.

He used this understanding strategically when raising capital for TickerTags. Before approaching investors, he spent years establishing credibility through his book (*Laughing at Wall Street*), his audited track record, and his conference speaking. By the time he pitched, the "team" leg of the two-factor test was already established. The pitch was not "believe me" -- it was "look at the evidence."

He also notes that the investor landscape has shifted. Modern early-stage investors are often ex-operators themselves: "Most of us are entrepreneurs ourselves that have had one or more exits." (successful-pitch L119). This means they ask execution-level questions, not just financial-engineering questions. Founders who cannot speak to operational detail will lose credibility fast.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is building a pitch that over-indexes on the idea and under-indexes on team proof
- User has not yet established credibility in their domain before seeking funding
- User is surprised by the practical, operational nature of investor questions
- User needs to understand what investors actually evaluate versus what they say they evaluate

**Examples:**

1. **The thought-leader-first fundraising sequence:** Camillo deliberately built public credibility for years before fundraising for TickerTags. His book, audited returns, and speaking circuit created a proof stack that made the "team" evaluation straightforward. (successful-pitch L86-L87)

2. **Operator-level diligence:** He warns that modern investors are mostly ex-founders who will ask detailed questions about execution, competitive positioning, and operational tradeoffs. Abstract vision statements will not survive their scrutiny. (successful-pitch L119)

### 4. Transparency About Hurdles Is a Trust Signal [3/21]

**Core quotes:**

- "Address your hurdles... transparency is key." (successful-pitch L113)
- "if I find a competitor that you haven't told me about... that's a huge red flag for me." (successful-pitch L113)
- "You should know everyone in your space." (successful-pitch L114)

**Practical meaning:** One of Camillo's sharpest pieces of fundraising advice is to name your risks before your investor discovers them independently. Hiding known weaknesses -- competitors, regulatory hurdles, team gaps -- does not protect you. It destroys trust when the investor finds them on their own, which they will.

He frames competitive intelligence as a non-negotiable founder competence. If a founder does not know every player in their space, it signals weak domain leadership and incomplete preparation. The investor will assume that if you missed a competitor, you may have missed other critical threats.

The deeper insight is about the relationship between transparency and trust in high-stakes negotiations. Investors expect imperfection. They do not expect to be blindsided. A founder who openly names three major hurdles and explains their mitigation strategy builds more confidence than one who presents a frictionless narrative that both parties know is unrealistic.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User is preparing a pitch and tempted to omit known weaknesses
- User does not have a clear map of their competitive landscape
- User is confused about why a technically strong pitch failed to close funding
- User needs to understand that risk disclosure builds credibility rather than destroying it

**Examples:**

1. **The competitor litmus test:** Camillo explicitly states that discovering an undisclosed competitor is "a huge red flag." The failure is not that the competitor exists -- it is that the founder did not know or did not disclose. (successful-pitch L113)

2. **"Living and dying that vertical":** He expects founders to have total domain immersion -- not just awareness of competitors but deep operational knowledge of every adjacent player, substitute, and regulatory shift. (successful-pitch L114)

## Stories

### Story 1: TickerTags -- Build, Struggle, Sale to Jefferies

**Full narrative:** Camillo invested approximately "$1 million" of his own capital and spent "about 4.5 years" building TickerTags, raising an additional "$4 million" from external investors (alphamind [7:03]). The company secured access to Twitter's Decahose -- "10% of the fire hose of all tweets" (motley-fool L18) -- and built a taxonomy of "1.5 million word combinations" mapped to publicly traded companies (my-first-million [29:49]). Major banks and hedge funds became clients.

But institutional adoption hit a wall. "No matter how much data I showed them... They just couldn't wrap their head around it." (alphamind [7:03]). The problem was not signal quality -- it was organizational: "They didn't really have, you know, 20-something-year-old females on staff who were really savvy interpreting... conversational data." (my-first-million [32:43]). Wall Street firms liked the outputs but could not integrate them into existing workflows. He spent "years flying to New York nearly weekly, training the top sell-side banks" (my-first-million [32:43]), but cultural inertia won.

Through TickerTags, he gained a unique vantage point: "that software company allowed me to see behind the curtain of Wall Street" (farzad [32:49]). He saw firsthand that institutional investors "do things kind of in the same way that they've always done things" (my-first-million [32:43]) and that "Wall Street doesn't have the talent anymore. They don't really have the people who are capable of analyzing this stuff." (farzad [37:58]).

He ultimately sold the company to "Jefferies Bank... MScience" (generation-c [12:29]; my-first-million [30:37]). The exit was not a triumphant IPO -- it was the pragmatic conclusion of a company that built a genuinely superior product but could not overcome the adoption barriers of its target market.

In contrast, the retail community adopted the method rapidly. "People in his Discord outside finance -- 'dentists... delivery drivers... pilots' -- could extract value quickly." (confessions [22:30]). The irony is stark: the amateurs could use the tool better than the professionals it was built for.

**Teaching application:** Use this story when discussing how to productize a personal edge, the challenge of selling to institutions that resist change, why organizational culture can override data quality, and the pragmatic reality of exits that are not blockbuster events. Also relevant when discussing the insight that building a product can teach you more about your customers' limitations than about your product's capabilities.

**Sources:** alphamind [7:03], confessions [17:57], [22:30], farzad [32:49], [37:58], generation-c [12:29], motley-fool L16-L18, my-first-million [29:49]-[32:43], opto [6:38], successful-pitch L47-L78

### Story 2: Film-to-Startup Pivot at 21

**Full narrative:** After graduating college, Camillo "moved to LA" and pursued a career in film production, but "that didn't go as well as I had hoped." (successful-pitch L19). In 1999, the dot-com wave was reshaping the economy, and he made a decisive break: "said goodbye" to film ambitions and "got involved in a startup at age 21... and I never left." (successful-pitch L19-L20). He describes this moment as catching "the bug" for entrepreneurship.

He joined CarsDirect during the boom, experienced the bust firsthand, and then spent roughly nine years helping build eRewards into "the largest online research panel company in the world... millions of panelists in 50 countries." (confessions [17:57]; opto [6:38]). That period was his operational apprenticeship -- learning how to build large-scale businesses from the inside before attempting it on his own.

**Teaching application:** Use when advising on decisive career pivots and the importance of letting go of a path that is not working. Also relevant for discussions about the value of operational apprenticeships before founding a company. Camillo did not go straight from college to founder -- he spent years building operator skills inside growing companies first.

**Sources:** successful-pitch L19-L20, confessions [17:57], opto [6:38], farzad [10:03]

### Story 3: Customers as Investors Flywheel

**Full narrative:** Camillo advocates a specific fundraising sequence for early-stage founders: raise from your customers first, then use that traction to attract institutional capital. He argues founders should "plan to raise money from their customers" and, if needed, collect many small checks -- "$500 to $2000" -- through crowd-funding or direct asks (successful-pitch L98). The logic is that customer investment "does the vetting for us as early stage investors." (successful-pitch L100).

When customers put their own money into a product they already use, it accomplishes three things simultaneously: it proves demand, it creates evangelists with financial skin in the game, and it generates traction proof that professional investors recognize. "That's what the real investors want to see." (successful-pitch L100). The customer-investment signals that the product has value beyond the founder's pitch deck.

**Teaching application:** Use when a user is struggling with traditional fundraising channels and needs an alternative sequencing strategy. Also use when a user has strong customer engagement but has not considered converting that engagement into early capital. The key insight is that customer capital is not just money -- it is a validation signal that de-risks the deal for subsequent investors.

**Sources:** successful-pitch L98-L100

### Story 4: The Hedge Fund and the Fork in the Road

**Full narrative:** After the death of a family mentor who urged him toward larger impact through capital management, Camillo raised a "$10 million hedge fund" (farzad [28:39]). But he only got "a total of 2 trades out" before investors flagged a structural conflict: he could not simultaneously run a data company that sold social-intelligence signals (TickerTags) and manage a fund that traded on those same signals. "You can't run a data company that sells this data and run your own hedge fund because you're going to front-run us." (farzad [28:39]).

He shut the fund down after roughly 90 days (opto [35:35]), calling it "the most expensive thing" and "hundreds of thousands of dollars out of my pocket" (farzad [32:49]). The decision was a governance choice: "I can't have it both ways... I'm at this fork in the road." (farzad [28:39]).

He also discovered a deeper incompatibility. His patient, concentrated style -- waiting months for a single A+ setup -- was fundamentally at odds with institutional client expectations for regular deployment and diversification. He frames the experience as a revelation about what he does not want: "I don't like having investors I had to answer to and I felt responsible for." (farzad [41:21]).

**Teaching application:** Use when discussing conflicts of interest in business, the courage to shut something down quickly when the structure is wrong, and the difference between personal-capital investing and institutional-capital management. The lesson is that strategic sacrifice -- walking away from a good opportunity because it conflicts with a better one -- is a sign of clarity, not failure.

**Sources:** farzad [28:39], [32:49], [41:21]; opto [35:35]
