# Module 3: Knowing When to Enter and When to Exit

## Overview

This module activates when someone has identified a real edge but is unsure about timing: When should they buy? When should they sell? How do they know the opportunity window is still open?

The answer, for Camillo, is not about price targets, technical levels, or gut instinct. It is about information flow. You enter when you know something the market has not yet absorbed. You exit when the market catches up. Everything in this module flows from that single principle -- the most important concept in his entire method, repeated with emphasis across virtually every public appearance.

---

## Concepts

### Concept 1: Information Imbalance to Information Parity (Entry/Exit) [19/21]

This is Camillo's signature timing doctrine -- the single idea he treats as the foundation of everything he does.

**Core formulation:**
- "You enter your position at the point of information asymmetry... And you exit the position at the point of information parity." (my-first-million [1:29])
- "It's trying to enter trades when there's an information imbalance in the world... and then exiting those trades at the point of information parity when that information... gets fully disseminated." (trends-friends [4:57])
- "I like to think of it as investing when there's an imbalance of information, and that's when you enter a trade, and then exiting the trade at the point of information equilibrium." (opto [2:24])

**Emphasis -- he treats this as everything:**
- "Yes. That is everything. It is absolutely everything." (opto [12:44])
- "The 'point of imbalance' is that magical moment when you understand something important about the world before most people do." (medium L24)
- "It sounds like legal insider trading... Yes, that's exactly what it is." (school-greatness-may [47:49])

**Applied broadly across decisions, not just investing:**
- "The goal is to make decisions -- whether for your career or your investments -- when you have an informational advantage, and to recalibrate when that information becomes common knowledge, that is, when it reaches the 'point of parity.'" (medium L24)

**What "parity" looks like in practice:**
- "information parity is that point in time when that thing that you discovered becomes kind of commonly ingested by the market." (confessions [28:28])
- "At the point of information parity... that's when you exit." (generation-c [31:07])
- "if the rest of the world has already figured this out... then you're not able to actually arbitrage the idea." (rich-habits [12:10])

**Price is irrelevant -- only information state matters:**
- "I never look at price. I never have price targets. What I care about is information asymmetry, and that's all I care about." (iced-coffee [1:02:38])
- "I don't trade price, I trade information." (words-of-rizdom [59:54])
- "enter a trade at the point of information imbalance... [exit at] the point of information parity." (words-of-rizdom [42:49])

**Practical meaning:** Alpha exists only while your insight is under-distributed. The entry signal is not a price level -- it is the gap between what you understand and what the market has absorbed. The exit signal is not a return target -- it is the moment that gap closes. When analysts, mainstream media, and social chatter all reflect the same information you traded on, the edge is gone and you should exit regardless of what the price has done.

**Example applications:**
- Someone asks: "When should I sell?" -- Answer: When the thing you saw first becomes common knowledge. Not when you hit a return target.
- Someone asks: "Is it too late to get in?" -- Check whether the information is already reflected in analyst reports, StockTwits, and mainstream media. If so, parity has arrived.
- Someone asks: "The stock dropped after I bought. Should I sell?" -- The question is not what the price did. The question is whether your information thesis is still intact and still under-distributed.

**Coaching triggers:** User asks about entry timing, exit timing, when to sell, whether an opportunity is "too late," or how to set price targets (redirect: information targets, not price targets).

### Concept 2: Social Arb Window / Trade Window Mapping [10/21]

The social arb window is the time span between information imbalance and information parity. It determines not just how long to hold, but what instrument to use.

**Core formulation:**
- "the decision to trade equity versus, let's say, options is really determined upon... what I call the social arb window." (trends-friends [8:39])
- "I call it the social arb window, is the window when you know something that other people haven't picked up on yet..." (farzad [21:33])
- "The trade window for me is usually a few weeks to a few months." (moneymasters [12:59])

**How the window determines instrument choice:**
- Short windows (days to weeks) demand options for leverage because the price move needs to happen within a compressed timeframe.
- Longer adoption curves (months) can use equity because there is time for the thesis to play out without time-decay pressure.

**How many trades the window produces:**
- "I start with a really large top of funnel, and then the process I go through might spit out 2 high conviction trades a year, or it might spit out 12." (moneymasters [12:59])
- "Anywhere between 2 and 12 medium to high conviction trades on an annual basis." (moneymasters [12:59])

**What determines the window length:**
- "some other information dissemination event is going to allow the whole world to see what you saw beforehand." (farzad [21:33])
- The window closes when a scheduled catalyst (earnings, product launch, media event) or organic dissemination brings the information to broad market awareness.

**Practical meaning:** Before you enter a trade, estimate how long the information advantage will last. If there is a known catalyst date (earnings call, movie release, FDA decision) that will force information parity, that defines the window. If parity will arrive organically as chatter spreads, estimate the dissemination timeline. The window length drives every downstream decision: instrument, position size, and monitoring intensity.

**Example applications:**
- Someone finds a consumer trend 5 months before it will show up in earnings. That is a long window -- equity works fine.
- Someone finds a product surge 3 weeks before an earnings call. Short window -- options provide the leverage needed to capitalize on compressed timing.
- Someone asks "How often do you trade?" -- Not by schedule. By opportunity. 2 to 12 high-conviction setups per year, each with its own natural window.

**Coaching triggers:** User asks about holding period, options vs stock, how many trades to make per year, or how to size positions relative to time horizon.

### Concept 3: Event-Window Exit Discipline [6/21]

For event-driven trades, the catalyst date is the exit point -- because information parity arrives at the announcement.

**Core formulation:**
- "The concept was always to exit the day of the movie release or the day before." (trends-friends [8:39])
- "whether it's a quarterly earnings call or some other point at which you think that information will be surfaced to the market at large." (opto [26:15])

**The information dissemination ladder (how parity arrives in stages):**
- "when people start to talk and then primary news outlets start to talk... then investment news outlets... and then people actually start to trade." (words-of-rizdom [51:35])

**Why exit before -- not after -- the event:**
- The event itself is the moment the entire market sees what you saw. By the time earnings are released, the movie opens, or the product launches, your information advantage is gone. Holding through the event means you are no longer trading information -- you are gambling on market reaction, which is a different game entirely.

**Practical meaning:** When you enter an event-driven trade, define the exit date at entry. The exit is not triggered by a return target but by the calendar -- the date when the catalyst will force information parity. Exit the day of the event or the day before it. Do not hold through hoping for more upside, because you have shifted from known edge to unknown reaction.

**Example applications:**
- Someone is trading ahead of earnings with a strong thesis about a product line. Exit the day before or day of earnings -- do not hold through for "the pop."
- Someone has a pre-release movie trade. Exit before opening weekend, regardless of price.
- Someone asks "But what if it keeps going up after the event?" -- That is a new trade, not a continuation of the old one. The old edge expired at the event.

**Coaching triggers:** User asks about holding through earnings, catalysts, product launches; user is tempted to "let it ride" past the information-parity event; user conflates thesis correctness with position management.

## Stories

### Story 1: The Barbie Pre-Release Trade

**Full narrative:**
Seven to eight months before the Barbie movie was released, Camillo noticed something unusual: "many, many months, about 7, 8 months before the movie was released... generating more social buzz than any film I had seen in probably decade pre-release." (trends-friends [7:36]). He took a position in Mattel about five months before release: "I had this idea of trading Mattel about 5 months before the movie came out." (trends-friends [8:39]).

The exit was pre-defined by the event window: "The concept was always to exit the day of the movie release or the day before." (trends-friends [8:39]). He "rode the stock from 16.5 to 22" (trends-friends [8:39]).

In a later telling, he provides the information-dissemination view of the same trade: he tracked buzz "9 months before the movie came out" and bought "a really big amount of call options" with expiry around release. As parity approached: "in the few weeks leading up... it hit the business press... Exactly what happened. And then I exited the trade." (words-of-rizdom [51:35], [53:21]). He also cites contrarian confirmation: a major Hollywood investor insisted it would fail, reinforcing that insiders were looking in the wrong direction. (words-of-rizdom [57:54])

**Teaching application:** Use when explaining timed social-arb trades with pre-committed exits. This story illustrates all three concepts in action: (1) information imbalance at entry -- the social buzz was anomalous but Wall Street had not priced it; (2) a clearly defined social arb window -- months between signal detection and the forced-parity event; (3) event-window exit discipline -- exit before the movie release, regardless of price trajectory.

**Sources:** trends-friends [7:36], [8:39]; words-of-rizdom [51:35], [53:21], [57:54]

### Story 2: Stranger Things / Netflix Persistence Signal

**Full narrative:**
When Stranger Things launched on Netflix, Camillo identified something that went beyond typical hit-show buzz: "there had never been a show in the history of Netflix that had driven as much social conversation." (opto [47:30]). But the edge was not in knowing the show was popular -- everyone knew that. The edge was in measuring what the conversation implied for Netflix's business: "when people talk about a show, that results in people subscribing to Netflix." (opto [47:30]).

Jack Schwager's research on the same trade added a critical nuance. The key was not volume but duration: chatter stayed at a "super high level for a long time" versus the normal decay pattern for a hit show. Schwager concluded: "Not trends, but anomalies." (unknown-wizards, Source 2, line 53). The persistence of elevated conversation -- not just the spike -- was the real signal.

The result: "the stock was up 20% on earnings that day." (opto [47:30]). Information parity arrived at the earnings call, when Netflix reported the subscriber numbers that the social data had already predicted.

**Teaching application:** Use when explaining that the edge was not in knowing the show was popular (everyone knew), but in measuring how long the chatter persisted at elevated levels -- duration, not just volume. This distinction is critical: many people see buzz, but few measure whether that buzz is decaying normally or sustaining anomalously. The exit was the earnings call -- the event that would force information parity by revealing subscriber growth to the entire market.

**Sources:** opto [47:30]; unknown-wizards, Source 2, line 53

### Story 3: Beacon Roofing / Hailstorm Trade

**Full narrative:**
Camillo had been monitoring Google search data for "roof damage" and "roof repair" for a decade, establishing a firm behavioral baseline. One year, a series of severe hailstorms drove search volume to extraordinary levels -- "nearly triple" historical norms (my-first-million [15:26]) and "triple anything I'd ever seen before" (school-greatness-may [45:28]).

The information advantage was structural: Google Trends reflected the damage within "24, 48 hours" (school-greatness-may [45:28]), while the insurance claim reports that Wall Street relied on lagged by about a month. He took "a very large, very levered call long position" in Beacon Roofing (my-first-million [15:26]), exploiting the gap between real-time behavioral data and the delayed institutional data pipeline.

**Teaching application:** Use when explaining data-lag arbitrage. The core lesson is that different data sources reach the market at different speeds. Real-time behavioral signals (search data, social conversation) often lead institutional data sources (insurance reports, government statistics, analyst surveys) by days or weeks. That lag is the social arb window. The entry was at information imbalance (only those watching Google Trends in real time could see the spike); the exit would come at information parity (when insurance reports and analyst coverage reflected the same reality).

**Sources:** my-first-million [15:26]; school-greatness-may [45:28]

### Cautionary Story: Jakks Pacific and the Unknown Unknown

**Full narrative:**
Camillo had strong conviction that Frozen-themed toys (specifically the Elsa doll) would drive record-breaking earnings for Jakks Pacific. His thesis was correct -- the earnings were extraordinary. But the trade collapsed anyway.

The stock was "up 35% pre-market" (opto [35:35]) on earnings anticipation. Then it reversed catastrophically, closing "down 25%" (opto [35:35]). The cause: the company's largest holder chose that moment of peak liquidity to "dump their entire 10 or 11% ownership in the company in that single day." (opto [35:35]). In a later retelling, he describes it as: "pre-earnings, the stock was up 30%... [then] closed down 20%." (words-of-rizdom [1:02:18]).

His assessment: "still haunts me" (opto [35:35]) and "you just don't know what you don't know." (words-of-rizdom [1:02:18]).

**Teaching application:** Use in risk management conversations. This story teaches that thesis correctness does not guarantee trade profitability. Even with perfect information about the fundamental driver, there are "counter-catalysts" -- unknown variables that can overwhelm your edge. As he says elsewhere: "It's not just the information you're trading. What else is happening at the company? That could trump the information you're trading." (opto [21:06]). The lesson is not to avoid conviction -- it is to size positions acknowledging that "there's no such thing as a sure thing." (words-of-rizdom [1:02:18]).

**Sources:** opto [35:35], [21:06]; words-of-rizdom [1:02:18]
